<div><b><h1>Cornelius</h1></b></div>
<div><b><h1><br></h1></b></div>
<div><b><h1>Cornelius — Bible Character Study (Acts 10–11)</h1></b></div>
<div><b><h2>1) Story Summary (Chronological)</h2></b></div>
<div>Cornelius enters Scripture as a Roman centurion stationed in <b>Caesarea</b>—a Gentile military officer who nevertheless <b>fears the God of Israel</b>, prays regularly, and gives generously to the Jewish people (Acts 10:1–2). During one afternoon hour of prayer, he receives a <b>vision of an angel</b> who tells him his prayers and alms “have ascended as a memorial before God” and commands him to send men to Joppa to summon <b>Simon Peter</b> (Acts 10:3–6).</div>
<div>Cornelius obeys immediately, dispatching trusted servants and a devout soldier to find Peter (Acts 10:7–8). Meanwhile, God prepares Peter through a vision about clean/unclean animals—teaching him not to call unclean what God has made clean (Acts 10:9–16). When Cornelius’ messengers arrive, the Spirit directs Peter to go with them “without hesitation” (Acts 10:17–23).</div>
<div>Cornelius gathers his household and friends to hear Peter (Acts 10:24, 27). When Peter arrives, Cornelius falls at Peter’s feet in reverence, but Peter raises him, insisting, “I too am a man” (Acts 10:25–26). Cornelius recounts the angelic message and explains they are assembled “to hear all that you have been commanded by the Lord” (Acts 10:30–33).</div>
<div>Peter then preaches Christ: Jesus’ life and ministry, His death on a tree, His resurrection, His role as Judge, and the promise of forgiveness to all who believe (Acts 10:36–43). While Peter is still speaking, the <b>Holy Spirit falls on all who hear</b>, and the Jewish believers are amazed that the Spirit is poured out “even on the Gentiles” (Acts 10:44–46). Peter orders that they be <b>baptized in the name of Jesus Christ</b>, and he remains with them for some days (Acts 10:47–48).</div>
<div>The news reaches Jerusalem, where Peter is criticized for entering Gentile houses and eating with them (Acts 11:1–3). Peter recounts the entire episode—his vision, the Spirit’s command, Cornelius’ angelic vision, and the Spirit’s descent (Acts 11:4–16). Peter concludes that God gave Gentiles the same gift as Jewish believers; therefore no one can stand in God’s way (Acts 11:17). The Jerusalem believers fall silent and glorify God: “Then to the Gentiles also God has granted repentance that leads to life” (Acts 11:18).</div>
<div><b>Where Cornelius’ story begins:</b> Acts 10:1<b>Where it ends (in Scripture):</b> Cornelius’ direct narrative ends with the Jerusalem church’s affirmation of Gentile inclusion (Acts 11:18). Cornelius is not mentioned again by name.</div>
<div><br></div>
<div><b><h2>2) What He’s Known For</h2></b></div>
<div>Cornelius is best known as the <b>first clearly narrated Gentile household</b> to receive the Holy Spirit in Acts, becoming a pivotal figure in the church’s understanding that <b>God includes Gentiles</b> without requiring them to become Jews.</div>
<div><b>Headline descriptors</b><br></div>
<ul>
<li>Roman centurion who feared Israel’s God (Acts 10:1–2)</li>
<li>Devout, prayerful, generous benefactor (Acts 10:2, 4)</li>
<li>Recipient of angelic direction to summon Peter (Acts 10:3–6)</li>
<li>Humble hearer eager for God’s word (Acts 10:25–26, 33)</li>
<li>A “hinge” person in Jew–Gentile gospel expansion (Acts 10:44–48; 11:17–18)</li>
<li>Evidence that God shows no partiality (Acts 10:34–35)</li>
</ul>
<div><br></div>
<div><b><h2>3) Where He Appears in the Bible</h2></b></div>
<div><b>Primary narrative locations</b><br></div>
<ul>
<li><b>Acts — 10:1–48</b> — Cornelius’ vision, Peter’s visit, gospel preached, Spirit falls, baptism (Acts 10:1–48)</li>
<li><b>Acts — 11:1–18</b> — Peter defends Gentile inclusion by recounting Cornelius’ conversion event (Acts 11:1–18)</li>
</ul>
<div><b>Secondary references (themes echoed in Acts / NT)</b><br></div>
<ul>
<li><b>Acts — 15:7–11</b> — Peter later appeals to God giving the Spirit to Gentiles “just as” to Jews (echoing Acts 10–11) (Acts 15:7–11)</li>
<li><b>Ephesians — 2:11–22</b> — Theologically explains Jew–Gentile reconciliation in Christ (theme embodied in Acts 10–11) (Eph 2:13–18)</li>
<li><b>Romans — 3:29–30; 10:12–13</b> — God is God of Jews and Gentiles; same Lord for all who call on Him (Rom 3:29–30; 10:12–13)</li>
<li><b>Galatians — 3:8</b> — Gospel promise to the nations foreseen: “In you shall all the nations be blessed” (Gal 3:8; cf. Gen 12:3)</li>
</ul>
<div><br></div>
<div><b><h2>4) Vital Statistics</h2></b></div>
<ul>
<li><b>Historical setting (Scripture-grounded timeframe):</b> Early post-Pentecost church, after Peter’s broader ministry and before the Jerusalem Council’s formal debate (Acts 10–11 precedes Acts 15; exact year not stated) (Acts 10:1; 15:7–11).</li>
<li><b>Geography:</b><br></li>
<ul>
<li><b>Caesarea</b> (Cornelius’ base) (Acts 10:1, 24)</li>
<li><b>Joppa</b> (Peter’s location when summoned) (Acts 10:5–6, 23, 32)</li>
</ul>
<li><b>Family &amp relationships:</b><br></li>
<ul>
<li><b>Household</b> under his influence; they receive the word and Spirit together (Acts 10:2, 24, 44)</li>
<li><b>Friends/relatives gathered</b> to hear Peter (Acts 10:24, 27)</li>
<li><b>Peter</b> as apostolic witness and preacher of Christ to them (Acts 10:23–48)</li>
<li><b>Jewish believers (“circumcised believers”)</b> who accompany Peter and later hear his defense (Acts 10:45; 11:2)</li>
</ul>
<li><b>Roles/offices:</b> Roman <b>centurion</b>, of the “Italian cohort” (Acts 10:1). Spiritually, a <b>God-fearer</b> devoted to prayer and almsgiving (Acts 10:2).</li>
<li><b>Key turning points:</b><br></li>
<ul>
<li>Angelic visitation and command to summon Peter (Acts 10:3–6)</li>
<li>Humbling correction when he venerates Peter (Acts 10:25–26)</li>
<li>Hearing the gospel, receiving the Spirit, and baptism (Acts 10:43–48)</li>
<li>Peter’s testimony leads Jerusalem to glorify God for Gentile repentance (Acts 11:17–18)</li>
</ul>
</ul>

<div><b><h2>6) Strengths, Weaknesses, and Heart-Level Patterns</h2></b></div>
<div><b>Strengths (text-evidenced)</b></div>
<ul>
<li><b>Reverent devotion (“feared God”) with whole-household influence</b> (Acts 10:2).</li>
<li><b>Prayerful persistence</b> (Acts 10:2, 30–31).</li>
<li><b>Generosity toward God’s people</b> (Acts 10:2, 4).</li>
<li><b>Prompt obedience to God’s direction</b> (Acts 10:7–8, 33).</li>
<li><b>Teachability and hunger for God’s word</b>: assembled to hear “all” God commands (Acts 10:33).</li>
</ul>
<div><b>Weaknesses / blind spots (text-evidenced)</b></div>
<ul>
<li><b>Misplaced reverence toward a human messenger:</b> he falls at Peter’s feet; Peter must correct him (Acts 10:25–26). This suggests zeal that needs doctrinal alignment—worship belongs to God alone (Acts 10:26).</li>
</ul>
<div><b>Motives / fears / desires (careful inferences anchored to the text)</b></div>
<ul>
<li><b>Desire to know and obey God</b> is explicit in his posture: prayer (Acts 10:2, 30), and readiness to hear everything commanded (Acts 10:33).</li>
<li><b>Desire for God’s acceptance</b> may be inferred from his consistent devotion and seeking; however, Scripture emphasizes that even a devout God-fearer still needs the gospel message of Christ (Acts 10:36–43; 11:14).</li>
</ul>
<div><b>What changes (or doesn’t) over time?</b></div>
<ul>
<li>Cornelius is consistently <b>responsive</b>: devotion and prayer at the start (Acts 10:2–3) lead to obedience (Acts 10:7–8), humility under correction (Acts 10:25–26), and receptive faith evidenced by receiving the Spirit while hearing the word (Acts 10:44). The text presents <b>continuity of reverence</b>, with growth in clarity as he hears Christ proclaimed (Acts 10:36–43).</li>
</ul>
<div><br></div>
<div><b><h2>7) Victories and Challenges</h2></b></div>
<div><b>Major “wins” (with references)</b></div>
<ul>
<li><b>Obediently seeks apostolic instruction</b> rather than relying only on a private spiritual experience (Acts 10:5–6, 33).</li>
<li><b>Brings others to hear the gospel</b> (household and friends) (Acts 10:24, 27, 44).</li>
<li><b>Receives the Holy Spirit</b> as a Gentile—public confirmation of inclusion (Acts 10:44–46).</li>
<li><b>Baptized into Christ</b> (Acts 10:47–48).</li>
<li>His conversion becomes evidence used to persuade the Jerusalem church to glorify God for Gentile repentance (Acts 11:17–18).</li>
</ul>
<div><b>Major challenges (with references)</b></div>
<ul>
<li><b>Cultural and religious distance:</b> a Gentile officer inviting a Jewish apostle into his home is precisely what triggers controversy (Acts 10:28; 11:2–3).</li>
<li><b>Risk of spiritual confusion:</b> reverencing Peter (Acts 10:25) required correction (Acts 10:26).</li>
<li><b>Dependence on God’s orchestration:</b> Cornelius must wait for Peter and for God to open the door through Peter’s vision and Spirit’s instruction (Acts 10:19–23, 33).</li>
</ul>
<div><b>Cost and consequences themes:</b> Cornelius’ story shows that <b>faithfulness may require crossing social boundaries</b> and enduring misunderstanding in the believing community—yet God vindicates obedience by giving the Spirit and producing unity around the truth (Acts 10:44–48; 11:17–18).</div>

<div><b><h2>9) Theology and Big Themes</h2></b></div>
<ul>
<li><b>God’s sovereign initiative in salvation and mission:</b> God sends an angel to Cornelius (Acts 10:3–6), gives Peter a vision (Acts 10:9–16), and the Spirit commands Peter’s actions (Acts 10:19–20). The story is driven by God’s purpose, not human strategy (Acts 11:17–18).</li>
<li><b>Impartial grace and the widening of God’s people:</b> Peter’s declaration—God shows no partiality—interprets the event theologically (Acts 10:34–35). The Spirit’s falling on Gentiles is God’s own testimony (Acts 10:44–47).</li>
<li><b>The centrality of Christ and forgiveness through His name:</b> Cornelius’ household is not saved by generic theism; they hear about Jesus’ lordship, death, resurrection, and forgiveness through faith (Acts 10:36–43).</li>
<li><b>Holiness redefined around God’s declaring clean:</b> Peter’s vision confronts inherited categories and insists God is the one who makes clean (Acts 10:15, 28). The immediate application is people, not merely food laws (Acts 10:28).</li>
<li><b>Unity of the church under the Spirit’s witness:</b> The Jerusalem believers accept Gentile inclusion because the Spirit gave the same gift (Acts 11:15–18). The Spirit safeguards the gospel against ethnic gatekeeping (Acts 11:17).</li>
</ul>
<div><br></div>
<div><b><h2>10) How Cornelius Points Us to Christ</h2></b></div>
<ul>
<li><b>He highlights the necessity and sufficiency of Jesus for salvation.</b> Even a devout God-fearer must hear the message about Jesus—His death and resurrection—and receive forgiveness through His name (Acts 10:36–43; 11:14). Cornelius points away from moralism toward Christ-centered faith.</li>
<li><b>He illustrates the scope of Christ’s lordship and mission.</b> Peter proclaims “He is Lord of all” in the Cornelius episode (Acts 10:36), and Cornelius becomes living proof that Christ’s reign and saving mercy extend beyond Israel to the nations (Acts 10:44–48; 11:18).</li>
<li><b>He embodies the pattern of the gospel crossing boundaries by Spirit-empowered witness.</b> God sends Peter to proclaim Christ; then God Himself confirms inclusion by the Spirit (Acts 10:19–20, 44–46). This anticipates the apostolic mission trajectory: Jerusalem → Judea/Samaria → ends of the earth (Acts 1:8; fulfilled in pattern in Acts 10–11).</li>
</ul>
<div>(We should be cautious about calling Cornelius a “type” of Christ; Scripture instead uses him as a <b>case study</b> in gospel inclusion and Spirit authentication (Acts 10:34–35; 11:17–18).)</div>
<div><br></div>
<div><b><h2>11) Lessons and Applications (Scripture-tethered)</h2></b></div>
<ol>
<li><b>Religious devotion is not the same as saving faith—everyone needs the gospel of Jesus.</b> (Acts 10:2; 10:36–43; 11:14)</li>
<li><b>God hears prayer and sees generosity, yet still directs seekers to Christ’s message.</b> (Acts 10:3–6, 31; 10:43)</li>
<li><b>Prompt obedience matters—Cornelius acts immediately on God’s instruction.</b> (Acts 10:7–8)</li>
<li><b>Use your influence to gather others under God’s word.</b> (Acts 10:24, 33, 44)</li>
<li><b>Do not elevate human messengers—honor God alone.</b> (Acts 10:25–26)</li>
<li><b>God’s people must not resist whom God receives.</b> (Acts 10:34–35, 47; 11:17)</li>
<li><b>The Spirit’s gift dismantles prideful boundary lines in the church.</b> (Acts 10:44–46; 11:15–18)</li>
<li><b>The gospel creates a new, shared identity that transcends ethnicity and status.</b> (Acts 10:28, 34–35; Eph 2:13–18)</li>
<li><b>When criticized, answer with patient, detailed testimony to God’s work and God’s word.</b> (Acts 11:4–17)</li>
<li><b>Repentance leading to life is God’s grant, not human achievement.</b> (Acts 11:18)</li>
</ol>
<div><b>Warnings to heed</b><br></div>
<ul>
<li>Don’t confuse spiritual interest or moral seriousness with regeneration and faith in Christ (Acts 10:2; 11:14).</li>
<li>Don’t “withstand God” by adding barriers He has not required (Acts 11:17).</li>
</ul>
<div><b>Examples to imitate</b><br></div>
<ul>
<li>Cornelius’ earnest seeking, readiness to obey, and eagerness to hear all God commands (Acts 10:2, 8, 33).</li>
<li>Peter’s submission to the Spirit and willingness to cross uncomfortable boundaries (Acts 10:20, 28).</li>
</ul>
<div>Applications for:</div>
<ul>
<li><b>Personal holiness:</b> devotion expressed in prayer and generosity (Acts 10:2).</li>
<li><b>Relationships:</b> welcoming those you might otherwise avoid (Acts 10:28).</li>
<li><b>Prayer:</b> expect God to direct your steps through His word and providence (Acts 10:3–6).</li>
<li><b>Decision-making:</b> obey promptly when God’s will is clear (Acts 10:7–8).</li>
<li><b>Leadership:</b> use influence to bring others to Christ, not to yourself (Acts 10:24–26).</li>
</ul>

<div><b><h2>12) Discussion and Reflection Questions (10–20)</h2></b></div>
<div><b>Personal reflection</b><br></div>
<ol>
<li>What does Cornelius’ description teach about sincere devotion—and its limits apart from Christ? (Acts 10:2; 11:14)</li>
<li>Where do you see prompt obedience in Cornelius’ actions, and what holds you back from similar responsiveness? (Acts 10:7–8)</li>
<li>How do you respond when God corrects misplaced reverence or misunderstanding, as Cornelius is corrected? (Acts 10:25–26)</li>
</ol>
<div><b>Small group discussion</b>4. Why does God send Cornelius to Peter instead of having the angel deliver the full gospel message? (Acts 10:5–6; 10:36–43; 11:14) 5. What exactly convinces the Jerusalem believers, and what does that teach about unity and evidence? (Acts 11:15–18) 6. How should churches handle “boundary” questions today in light of Peter’s conclusion, “Who was I that I could stand in God’s way?” (Acts 11:17)</div>
<div><b>Deep study (text observation)</b>7. List everything Acts 10 says about Cornelius before Peter arrives. What is emphasized? (Acts 10:1–8) 8. Trace how Peter interprets his own vision: what does he conclude it means? (Acts 10:28; cf. 10:15) 9. What are the main components of Peter’s gospel proclamation in Acts 10:36–43? 10. What happens first—Spirit or baptism—and why does Luke emphasize this order here? (Acts 10:44–48) 11. Compare Acts 10:44–46 with Acts 2:1–4. What parallels does Peter later highlight? (Acts 11:15–16) 12. What does Acts 10:34–35 mean—and what does it not mean—based on the surrounding context? (Acts 10:34–43)</div>

Give us courage to cross sinful barriers and welcome those You welcome, so that we do not stand in Your way (Acts 10:28, 47; 11:17). Pour out Your Spirit to magnify Christ among all peoples, and grant repentance that leads to life (Acts 10:44–46; 11:18). In Jesus’ name, amen.</div>
