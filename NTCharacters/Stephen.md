<div><h1>Stephen</h1></div>
<div><br></div>
<div><b><h1>Stephen — Bible Character Study (Acts)</h1></b></div>
<div><b><h2>1) Story Summary (Chronological)</h2></b></div>
<div>Stephen enters the biblical narrative in the early Jerusalem church, when a practical crisis threatens unity: Hellenist (Greek-speaking) widows are being overlooked in the daily distribution of food. The apostles lead the church to appoint seven qualified men to oversee this ministry, so the apostles can devote themselves to prayer and the ministry of the word. Stephen is listed first among the seven and is described as “a man full of faith and of the Holy Spirit.” (Acts 6:1–6)</div>
<div>Stephen’s ministry quickly becomes public and contested. He is described as “full of grace and power,” doing “great wonders and signs among the people.” Opposition arises from members of the “synagogue of the Freedmen” and others; they dispute with him but cannot withstand his wisdom and the Spirit by whom he speaks. They then produce false witnesses, accusing him of speaking against Moses, God, the law, and the temple, claiming Jesus will destroy the temple and change Moses’ customs. Before the council, Stephen’s face is said to be “like the face of an angel.” (Acts 6:8–15)</div>
<div>In response to the high priest’s question, Stephen delivers the longest speech in Acts (Acts 7). He rehearses Israel’s history—from Abraham, to Joseph, to Moses, to the wilderness, to the tabernacle and temple—highlighting a repeated pattern: God’s people resist God’s chosen deliverers and reject God’s word. He climaxes by charging the council with being “stiff-necked,” uncircumcised in heart and ears, resisting the Holy Spirit, persecuting the prophets, betraying and murdering “the Righteous One,” and failing to keep the law they received. (Acts 7:1–53)</div>
<div>The council reacts with rage. Stephen, “full of the Holy Spirit,” gazes into heaven and sees the glory of God and Jesus standing at God’s right hand. He testifies to this vision; they drag him out of the city and stone him. As he dies, Stephen prays, “Lord Jesus, receive my spirit,” and, “Lord, do not hold this sin against them.” A young man named Saul is present, approving of his execution. Stephen “fell asleep,” becoming the first recorded Christian martyr in Acts and a catalytic moment for persecution and the mission’s spread. (Acts 7:54–60; Acts 8:1–4)</div>
<div><b>Where his story begins and ends:</b> Stephen’s narrative begins in <b>Acts 6:1</b> and ends with his death in <b>Acts 8:1a</b> (with immediate aftermath in Acts 8:1–4).</div>
<div><br></div>
<div><b><h2>2) What He’s Known For</h2></b></div>
<div>Stephen is most known for being the <b>first Christian martyr recorded in Scripture</b>, for his <b>Spirit-filled witness</b>, and for his <b>biblical-theological defense</b> that exposes Israel’s recurring resistance to God’s revelation—especially the temptation to idolize the temple while resisting the God who cannot be contained by it. (Acts 7:48–50)</div>
<div><b>Headlines</b><br></div>
<ul>
<li>Chosen as one of the Seven to protect unity and serve the needy (Acts 6:1–6)</li>
<li>Full of faith, the Holy Spirit, grace, and power (Acts 6:5, 8)</li>
<li>Unanswerable wisdom and Spirit-empowered speech (Acts 6:10)</li>
<li>Boldly indicts hardened resistance to God’s messengers (Acts 7:51–53)</li>
<li>Sees the exalted Christ standing at God’s right hand (Acts 7:55–56)</li>
<li>Dies praying to Jesus and forgiving enemies (Acts 7:59–60)</li>
<li>His martyrdom sparks wider persecution and mission expansion (Acts 8:1–4)</li>
</ul>
<div><br></div>
<div><b><h2>3) Where He Appears in the Bible</h2></b></div>
<div><b><h2>Primary narrative locations</h2></b></div>
<ul>
<li><b>Acts 6:1–7</b> — Appointed among the Seven in response to the widows’ neglect</li>
<li><b>Acts 6:8–15</b> — Signs, disputes, false accusations, arrest before the council</li>
<li><b>Acts 7:1–53</b> — Stephen’s speech: Israel’s history and the pattern of resistance</li>
<li><b>Acts 7:54–60</b> — Vision of Christ; stoning; prayers at death</li>
<li><b>Acts 8:1a</b> — Saul approves Stephen’s execution</li>
</ul>
<div><b><h2>Secondary references / impact narrative</h2></b></div>
<ul>
<li><b>Acts 8:1–4</b> — Persecution after Stephen; believers scattered preaching the word</li>
<li><b>Acts 11:19</b> — Scattering “because of the persecution that arose over Stephen” leads to wider gospel spread</li>
<li><b>Acts 22:20</b> — Paul recounts being present/approving at Stephen’s death (“the blood of Stephen your witness”)</li>
</ul>
<div><i>(Stephen is not mentioned in epistles by name beyond Acts’ later recollections, but the themes of faithful witness and suffering are echoed widely—see section 8.)</i><br></div>
<div><br></div>
<div><b><h2>4) Vital Statistics</h2></b></div>
<div><b>Historical setting (biblical timeframe)</b><br></div>
<ul>
<li>Early Jerusalem church, shortly after Pentecost and rapid growth (Acts 2:41; Acts 4:4; Acts 6:7). Exact year is not stated; place it in the earliest phase of Acts.</li>
</ul>
<div><b>Geography</b><br></div>
<ul>
<li><b>Jerusalem</b> (church life, temple precinct disputes implied, council trial) (Acts 6–7)</li>
<li>Executed <b>outside the city</b> (Acts 7:58)</li>
</ul>
<div><b>Family and relationships</b><br></div>
<ul>
<li><b>Not given</b>: parents, spouse, children are not mentioned. (Be careful not to speculate.)</li>
<li>Key relationships:</li>
<ul>
<li><b>The apostles</b> (appointed through their leadership) (Acts 6:2–6)</li>
<li><b>The Seven</b>: Philip, Prochorus, Nicanor, Timon, Parmenas, Nicolaus (Acts 6:5)</li>
<li><b>Opponents</b> from the synagogue(s) (Acts 6:9)</li>
<li><b>The Sanhedrin/council</b> and the <b>high priest</b> (Acts 6:12–7:1)</li>
<li><b>Saul</b> (present, approving) (Acts 7:58; Acts 8:1; Acts 22:20)</li>
</ul>
</ul>
<div><b>Roles/offices</b><br></div>
<ul>
<li>One of the <b>Seven</b> appointed for service/administration to guard church unity and care (Acts 6:1–6)</li>
<li>Functions as a <b>witness/evangelist-apologist</b> through signs and public testimony (Acts 6:8–10; Acts 7)</li>
<li><b>Martyr</b> (“witness” in deed and word) (Acts 7:59–60; cf. Acts 22:20)</li>
</ul>
<div><b>Key turning points</b><br></div>
<ul>
<li>Selected among the Seven (Acts 6:5–6)</li>
<li>Public disputation and arrest (Acts 6:9–12)</li>
<li>Landmark speech reframing temple/history around God’s presence and people’s resistance (Acts 7:1–53)</li>
<li>Vision of the exalted Christ and martyrdom (Acts 7:55–60)</li>
<li>Persecution/scattering that advances mission (Acts 8:1–4; Acts 11:19)</li>
</ul>

<div><b><h2>6) Strengths, Weaknesses, and Heart-Level Patterns</h2></b></div>
<div><b><h2>Strengths (with textual evidence)</h2></b></div>
<ul>
<li><b>Spirit-filled integrity and faith</b>: chosen as “full of faith and of the Holy Spirit.” (Acts 6:5)</li>
<li><b>Grace-and-power ministry</b>: “full of grace and power,” doing wonders and signs. (Acts 6:8)</li>
<li><b>Courageous truth-telling</b>: addresses the highest authorities with penetrating fidelity to Scripture (Acts 7:51–53).</li>
<li><b>Scriptural mastery and redemptive-historical clarity</b>: narrates and interprets Israel’s story with theological purpose (Acts 7:2–50).</li>
<li><b>Christ-centered endurance under suffering</b>: entrusts his spirit to the Lord Jesus and forgives. (Acts 7:59–60)</li>
<li><b>Love for enemies</b>: explicit prayer, “do not hold this sin against them.” (Acts 7:60; cf. Luke 23:34 as a resonant parallel)</li>
</ul>
<div><b><h2>Weaknesses / sins / blind spots</h2></b></div>
<ul>
<li><b>None explicitly recorded.</b> Scripture does not attribute moral failure to Stephen in Acts 6–7. We should not invent one. His sharp rebuke is forceful, but it is presented as Spirit-driven prophetic indictment, not sinful rage. (Acts 7:51–53; Acts 6:10)</li>
</ul>
<div><b><h2>Motives / fears / desires (careful inference, anchored to text)</h2></b></div>
<ul>
<li><b>Desire for God’s glory and truth</b>: he testifies to “the glory of God, and Jesus” even when it costs his life (Acts 7:55–56).</li>
<li><b>Desire for the church’s good and unity</b>: he accepts a service role to protect neglected widows (Acts 6:1–6).</li>
<li><b>Fear of man appears absent</b>: the narrative emphasizes boldness and Spirit fullness under threat (Acts 6:10–12; Acts 7:54–60).</li>
</ul>
<div><b><h2>What changes (or doesn’t) over time?</h2></b></div>
<ul>
<li><b>Consistent fullness and faithfulness.</b> From appointment (Acts 6:5) through disputation (Acts 6:10) to death (Acts 7:55), Stephen is consistently described and portrayed as Spirit-filled, wise, courageous, and Christ-focused. The “temperature” rises (service → public controversy → trial → martyrdom), but the character pattern remains steady.</li>
</ul>
<div><br></div>
<div><b><h2>7) Victories and Challenges</h2></b></div>
<div><b><h2>Major “wins” (faithful outcomes)</h2></b></div>
<ul>
<li><b>Protecting church unity and justice</b> through wise delegation and Spirit-qualified leadership (Acts 6:1–7).</li>
<li><b>Powerful public witness</b> validated by signs and unanswerable wisdom (Acts 6:8–10).</li>
<li><b>Theologically decisive testimony</b>: reframes the temple/law controversy under God’s transcendent presence and Israel’s history of resistance (Acts 7:48–50).</li>
<li><b>Faithful martyrdom</b>: entrusting himself to Jesus and forgiving enemies (Acts 7:59–60).</li>
<li><b>Missional ripple effect</b>: persecution leads to scattering and proclamation beyond Jerusalem (Acts 8:1–4; Acts 11:19).</li>
</ul>
<div><b><h2>Major challenges (costly pressures)</h2></b></div>
<ul>
<li><b>Institutional opposition</b> from religious communities (Acts 6:9).</li>
<li><b>False accusation and legal-religious prosecution</b> (Acts 6:11–14).</li>
<li><b>Violent death</b> by stoning (Acts 7:58–60).</li>
<li><b>Seeing the church suffer</b>: widespread persecution immediately follows (Acts 8:1–3).</li>
</ul>
<div><b>Cost of faithfulness:</b> Stephen’s fidelity to Jesus and Scripture leads directly to death (Acts 7:54–60), and indirectly to suffering for the broader church (Acts 8:1–3)—yet God uses that suffering to spread the word (Acts 8:4; Acts 11:19).</div>

<div><b><h2>9) Theology and Big Themes</h2></b></div>
<ul>
<li><b>God’s sovereignty in redemptive history:</b> Stephen shows God initiating covenant promises (Abraham), preserving through suffering (Joseph), and raising deliverers (Moses) despite human rejection. (Acts 7:2–36)</li>
<li><b>God’s presence not confined to sacred geography:</b> God appears in Mesopotamia (Acts 7:2), works in Egypt (Acts 7:9–10), speaks in Midian (Acts 7:30–34), and cannot be contained by the temple (Acts 7:48–50).</li>
<li><b>Human sin as persistent resistance to revelation:</b> The repeated motif is rejecting God’s chosen servants and resisting the Spirit. (Acts 7:35–39, 51)</li>
<li><b>True covenant fidelity is heart-level, not merely institutional:</b> “uncircumcised in heart and ears” critiques mere external religion. (Acts 7:51; cf. Deut 10:16; Jer 4:4 as thematic echoes)</li>
<li><b>Christology in Acts:</b> Jesus is the exalted “Son of Man”/Messiah at God’s right hand, vindicated even when authorities condemn his witnesses. (Acts 7:55–56)</li>
<li><b>Suffering and mission:</b> Persecution does not defeat the church; it disperses witness outward. (Acts 8:1–4; Acts 11:19)</li>
</ul>
<div><br></div>
<div><b><h2>10) How Stephen Points Us to Christ</h2></b></div>
<div><b><h2>A. Suffering-witness pattern (Christlike death)</h2></b></div>
<div>Stephen’s death mirrors Jesus’ own passion in notable ways:</div>
<ul>
<li>Entrusting spirit at death: Stephen, “Lord Jesus, receive my spirit” (Acts 7:59) resonates with Jesus’ “Father, into your hands I commit my spirit” (Luke 23:46).</li>
<li>Forgiving enemies: Stephen prays, “do not hold this sin against them” (Acts 7:60), echoing Jesus’ prayer for his executioners (Luke 23:34). This is not typology in the “office” sense, but a <b>discipleship conformity</b> to Christ in suffering. (cf. Luke 9:23–24)</li>
</ul>
<div><b><h2>B. Vindication of the rejected Righteous One</h2></b></div>
<div>Stephen explicitly identifies Israel’s pattern of rejecting deliverers and climaxes with “betrayed and murdered” “the Righteous One.” (Acts 7:52) The narrative then confirms Jesus’ vindication by Stephen’s vision of the exalted Christ standing at God’s right hand (Acts 7:55–56), aligning with enthronement texts (Ps 110:1) and the “Son of Man” kingdom vision (Dan 7:13–14).</div>
<div><b><h2>C. Temple fulfillment trajectory</h2></b></div>
<div>Stephen’s temple argument—God not confined to “houses made by hands” (Acts 7:48–50)—pushes toward the NT reality that God’s dwelling is ultimately centered in Christ and his people by the Spirit (cf. John 2:19–21; Eph 2:19–22). Stephen’s conflict foreshadows the gospel’s expansion beyond Jerusalem’s temple-centered horizon (Acts 8:1–4; Acts 11:19).</div>

<div><b><h2>11) Lessons and Applications (Scripture-tethered)</h2></b></div>
<ol>
<li><b>Guard unity by addressing practical injustice quickly and wisely.</b> (Acts 6:1–7)</li>
<li><b>Select leaders by spiritual maturity, not mere competence.</b> (Acts 6:3, 5)</li>
<li><b>Service roles can be platforms for powerful witness.</b> (Acts 6:5–10)</li>
<li><b>Expect opposition when Christ is proclaimed clearly.</b> (Acts 6:9–12; 2 Tim 3:12 theme)</li>
<li><b>Don’t be surprised by misrepresentation; answer with Scripture and clarity.</b> (Acts 6:11–14; Acts 7:1–53)</li>
<li><b>Know the Bible as one story pointing to God’s purposes and Christ.</b> (Acts 7:2–50)</li>
<li><b>Beware temple-idolatry—treating religious forms as substitutes for obedience.</b> (Acts 7:48–51)</li>
<li><b>The greatest danger is resisting the Holy Spirit while maintaining religious respectability.</b> (Acts 7:51)</li>
<li><b>Christ is present and reigning even when his witnesses suffer.</b> (Acts 7:55–56; Rom 8:34 theme)</li>
<li><b>Faithfulness may cost you dearly; God can use that cost missionally.</b> (Acts 7:58–60; Acts 8:1–4; Acts 11:19)</li>
<li><b>Forgiveness is a Spirit-empowered act, not denial of evil.</b> (Acts 7:60)</li>
<li><b>Your witness may shape future gospel history in ways you won’t see.</b> (Acts 7:58; Acts 22:20; Acts 9:1–6 in broader context)</li>
</ol>
<div><b><h2>Warnings to heed</h2></b></div>
<ul>
<li>Hardness to God’s word can hide behind religious zeal. (Acts 7:51–53)</li>
<li>Institutions can persecute truth when they feel threatened. (Acts 6:12–14; Acts 7:54)</li>
</ul>
<div><b><h2>Examples to imitate</h2></b></div>
<ul>
<li>Spirit-filled character (Acts 6:5)</li>
<li>Biblical clarity (Acts 7)</li>
<li>Courageous witness (Acts 6:10; Acts 7:56)</li>
<li>Enemy-love in suffering (Acts 7:60)</li>
</ul>
<div><b>Application arenas</b><br></div>
<ul>
<li><b>Personal holiness:</b> cultivate Spirit-filled integrity (Acts 6:3–5)</li>
<li><b>Relationships/church life:</b> pursue justice and unity (Acts 6:1–7)</li>
<li><b>Prayer:</b> entrust yourself to the Lord in crisis (Acts 7:59)</li>
<li><b>Suffering:</b> look to the exalted Christ (Acts 7:55–56)</li>
<li><b>Leadership:</b> prioritize word, prayer, and wise delegation (Acts 6:2–4)</li>
</ul>

</ol>

<li>What is the central argument of Stephen’s speech—what pattern is he exposing? (Acts 7:35–39, 51–53)</li>
<li>How can a church pursue justice for overlooked people without creating division? (Acts 6:1–7)</li>

</ol>

<li>Why does Stephen emphasize God’s work outside the land/temple (Mesopotamia, Egypt, Midian)? (Acts 7:2, 9–10, 30–34)</li>
<li>What is the significance of “the Righteous One” title in Stephen’s conclusion? (Acts 7:52)</li>

</ol>

<li>How does Stephen’s example challenge both harshness (anger) and cowardice (silence)? (Acts 7:51–53, 60)</li>
</ol>
