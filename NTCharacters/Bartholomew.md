<div><b><h1>Bartholomew</h1></b></div>
<div><b><h1><br></h1></b></div>
<div><b><h1>Bartholomew — Bible Character Study</h1></b></div>
<div><b>Name note (important):</b> “Bartholomew” appears in the NT only in lists of the Twelve (Matthew 10:3; Mark 3:18; Luke 6:14; Acts 1:13). <b>Scripture never explicitly tells us his personal backstory, calling scene, or later ministry.</b>A long-standing (but <b>not explicitly stated</b>) proposal is that <b>Bartholomew = Nathanael</b> (John 1:45–51; 21:2). I will treat that identification as <b>plausible but uncertain</b>, and I will clearly label where I’m leaning on Nathanael texts.</div>
<div><br></div>
<div><b><h2>1) Story Summary (Chronological)</h2></b></div>
<div><b>Where his story begins:</b> Bartholomew enters the biblical record as one of the <b>Twelve apostles</b> chosen by Jesus (Matthew 10:2–4; Mark 3:13–19; Luke 6:12–16).</div>
<ol>
<li><b>Chosen as one of the Twelve.</b> Jesus, after prayer, appoints twelve “whom he also named apostles,” and Bartholomew is listed among them (Luke 6:12–16; Mark 3:13–19).</li>
<li><b>Commissioned for mission with the Twelve.</b> In Matthew’s account of Jesus sending the Twelve, Bartholomew is included in the named list (Matthew 10:1–4). (He would therefore be among those authorized to preach and heal in that mission, though the text does not single him out individually: Matthew 10:1, 5–8.)</li>
<li><b>Present with the apostolic band at the end of Jesus’ earthly ministry and after the resurrection/ascension (as part of “the apostles”).</b> After the ascension, the apostles gather in Jerusalem and Bartholomew is named among them (Acts 1:13).</li>
</ol>
<div><b>Where his story ends (in Scripture):</b> The last explicit mention of Bartholomew by name is in the upper-room list after the ascension (Acts 1:13). After that, Acts continues to speak of “the apostles” without naming him again (e.g., Acts 2:1–4; 4:33; 5:12).</div>
<div><b>If (uncertain) Bartholomew is Nathanael:</b> his narrative “face” becomes clearer—first encountered when <b>Philip brings Nathanael to Jesus</b> (John 1:45–51), and later named among disciples present after the resurrection (John 21:2).</div>
<div><br></div>
<div><b><h2>2) What He’s Known For</h2></b></div>
<div>Because Scripture gives <b>few individualized details</b>, Bartholomew is known primarily for his <b>office and inclusion</b> among Christ’s chosen witnesses.</div>
<div><b>Headline descriptors (Scripture-anchored):</b><br></div>
<ul>
<li><b>One of the Twelve apostles</b> appointed by Jesus (Matthew 10:2–4; Mark 3:13–19; Luke 6:12–16).</li>
<li><b>A commissioned representative</b> in the apostolic mission (Matthew 10:1–4).</li>
<li><b>A persevering member of the apostolic community</b> after the ascension, devoted to prayer with the others (Acts 1:13–14).</li>
<li><b>Frequently paired with Philip</b> in apostolic lists (Matthew 10:3; Mark 3:18; Luke 6:14; Acts 1:13).</li>
<li><b>Possibly Nathanael</b> (uncertain): if so, associated with candid skepticism turned to confession about Jesus (John 1:46–49).</li>
</ul>
<div><br></div>
<div><b><h2>3) Where He Appears in the Bible</h2></b></div>
<div><b><h2>Primary narrative locations (where he is explicitly named)</h2></b></div>
<ul>
<li><b>Matthew — 10:2–4</b> — Listed among the Twelve when Jesus commissions them.</li>
<li><b>Mark — 3:13–19</b> — Listed among the Twelve appointed on the mountain.</li>
<li><b>Luke — 6:12–16</b> — Listed among the Twelve chosen after Jesus’ night of prayer.</li>
<li><b>Acts — 1:13</b> — Listed among the apostles gathered in Jerusalem after the ascension.</li>
</ul>
<div><b><h2>Secondary references (group references that include him as part of “the apostles”)</h2></b></div>
<ul>
<li><b>Acts — 1:14</b> — The apostles devote themselves to prayer (Bartholomew included by proximity to Acts 1:13).</li>
<li><b>Acts — 2:1–4; 2:42–43</b> — The apostolic community’s early life; wonders/signs done “through the apostles.”</li>
<li><b>Acts — 4:33; 5:12</b> — Apostolic witness and signs (again, as a member of the apostolic body).</li>
</ul>
<div><b><h2>If (uncertain) Bartholomew = Nathanael (texts name Nathanael, not “Bartholomew”)</h2></b></div>
<ul>
<li><b>John — 1:45–51</b> — Philip brings Nathanael; Jesus commends him and Nathanael confesses Jesus.</li>
<li><b>John — 21:2</b> — Nathanael is named among disciples present after the resurrection.</li>
</ul>
<div><br></div>
<div><b><h2>4) Vital Statistics</h2></b></div>
<div><b>Historical setting (NT era):</b><br></div>
<ul>
<li><b>Timeframe:</b> Jesus’ public ministry through the early post-ascension period (roughly the Gospel timeline into Acts 1; Luke 6:12–16; Acts 1:13).</li>
<li><b>Political/cultural backdrop:</b> Roman rule in Judea/Galilee during Jesus’ ministry (implied throughout the Gospels; explicit in broader narratives like Luke 3:1–2).</li>
</ul>
<div><b>Geography (strong associations):</b><br></div>
<ul>
<li><b>Jerusalem</b> — present with the apostles after the ascension (Acts 1:12–14).</li>
<li><b>If (uncertain) Nathanael:</b> <b>Cana in Galilee</b> (John 21:2 identifies “Nathanael of Cana in Galilee”).</li>
</ul>
<div><b>Family and relationships (explicit in Scripture):</b><br></div>
<ul>
<li><b>No explicit family details</b> are given for Bartholomew.</li>
<li><b>Close association in lists:</b> often adjacent to <b>Philip</b> (Matthew 10:3; Mark 3:18; Luke 6:14; Acts 1:13).</li>
<li><b>If (uncertain) Nathanael:</b> connected relationally to <b>Philip</b> (John 1:45–46).</li>
</ul>
<div><b>Roles/offices:</b><br></div>
<ul>
<li><b>Disciple / Apostle (one of the Twelve)</b> (Matthew 10:2–4; Luke 6:13–16; Acts 1:13).</li>
</ul>
<div><b>Key turning points (as Scripture allows):</b><br></div>
<ul>
<li><b>Appointment as one of the Twelve</b> (Mark 3:13–19; Luke 6:12–16).</li>
<li><b>Participation in the apostolic mission</b> (Matthew 10:1–8 with the named list in 10:2–4).</li>
<li><b>Persevering in prayer and unity after the ascension</b> (Acts 1:13–14).</li>
<li><b>If (uncertain) Nathanael:</b> movement from doubt to confession (John 1:46–49).</li>
</ul>

<div><b><h2>6) Strengths, Weaknesses, and Heart-Level Patterns</h2></b></div>
<div><b><h2>Strengths (text-based)</h2></b></div>
<div>Because Bartholomew is not individualized outside lists, these are either <b>(a)</b> corporate apostolic traits he shares by virtue of membership, or <b>(b)</b> Nathanael traits if the identification is accepted.</div>
<div><b>A. As an apostle among the Twelve</b><br></div>
<ul>
<li><b>Chosen after Jesus’ deliberate prayerful decision</b> (Luke 6:12–13) — suggests Jesus’ intentionality in selecting him for witness and mission.</li>
<li><b>Perseverance and unity in prayer</b> with the apostolic band after the ascension (Acts 1:13–14).</li>
</ul>
<div><b>B. If (uncertain) Bartholomew = Nathanael</b><br></div>
<ul>
<li><b>Integrity / guilelessness:</b> Jesus calls Nathanael “an Israelite indeed, in whom there is no deceit” (John 1:47).</li>
<li><b>Responsive faith:</b> Nathanael moves to a high confession: “Rabbi, you are the Son of God! You are the King of Israel!” (John 1:49).</li>
<li><b>Teachability:</b> He comes to “see” Jesus for himself at Philip’s invitation (John 1:46–47).</li>
</ul>
<div><b><h2>Weaknesses / blind spots (text-based and careful)</h2></b></div>
<div><b>A. Bartholomew explicitly:</b> Scripture records <b>no specific failure</b> by Bartholomew individually.</div>
<div><b>B. If (uncertain) Nathanael</b><br></div>
<ul>
<li><b>Initial prejudice/skepticism:</b> “Can anything good come out of Nazareth?” (John 1:46). The text shows a reflexive dismissal before meeting Jesus.</li>
</ul>
<div><b><h2>Motives / fears / desires (inferred cautiously)</h2></b></div>
<div><b>Minimal explicit data</b> means we infer lightly.</div>
<ul>
<li>His <b>continued presence</b> with the apostles in Acts 1 suggests a desire to remain faithful to Jesus’ calling and promise (Acts 1:13–14; cf. 1:4–5).</li>
<li>If Nathanael, his quick confession after Jesus’ disclosure suggests a heart drawn to truth and to the promised King (John 1:49).</li>
</ul>
<div><b><h2>What changes (or doesn’t) over time?</h2></b></div>
<ul>
<li><b>What doesn’t change (explicit):</b> His identification as a member of the Twelve persists from the Gospel lists into the post-ascension community (Matthew 10:3; Acts 1:13).</li>
<li><b>What changes (uncertain, if Nathanael):</b> Nathanael’s posture shifts from skepticism to worshipful confession after encountering Jesus’ knowledge and authority (John 1:46–49).</li>
</ul>
<div><br></div>
<div><b><h2>7) Victories and Challenges</h2></b></div>
<div><b><h2>Major “wins” (with references)</h2></b></div>
<ul>
<li><b>Included among the Twelve</b>—a foundational role in redemptive history as an appointed witness (Mark 3:13–19; Luke 6:12–16).</li>
<li><b>Participation in Jesus’ mission</b> through delegated authority (Matthew 10:1–4).</li>
<li><b>Faithful continuity after the ascension</b>—present, praying, waiting with the apostles (Acts 1:13–14).</li>
<li><b>(Uncertain) Confession of Jesus’ identity</b> (John 1:49).</li>
</ul>
<div><b><h2>Major challenges (with references)</h2></b></div>
<ul>
<li><b>The cost of discipleship is real for the Twelve</b> (general, but applies to all apostles): Jesus warns of hatred, persecution, and suffering tied to his name (e.g., Matthew 10:16–22). Bartholomew is in the group being commissioned and warned (Matthew 10:1–4, 16–22).</li>
<li><b>(Uncertain) Overcoming bias/skepticism</b> at the outset (John 1:46).</li>
</ul>
<div><b>Cost of faithfulness (text-grounded):</b> Even if Bartholomew is not individually spotlighted, Jesus frames apostolic mission as costly and contested (Matthew 10:16–39).</div>

<div><b><h2>9) Theology and Big Themes</h2></b></div>
<div><b><h2>God’s character revealed</h2></b></div>
<ul>
<li><b>Sovereign wisdom in choosing witnesses:</b> Jesus chooses the Twelve after prayer (Luke 6:12–13). The emphasis is on divine initiative, not human résumé.</li>
<li><b>Faithfulness to fulfill promise:</b> The apostles wait in prayer for what Jesus promised (Acts 1:4–5, 13–14), highlighting God’s reliability.</li>
</ul>
<div><b><h2>Discipleship, mission, and witness</h2></b></div>
<ul>
<li><b>Apostolic mission is authorized by Christ:</b> Jesus gives authority and sends (Matthew 10:1, 5–8).</li>
<li><b>The church is shaped by apostolic witness:</b> “the apostles’ teaching” is foundational in the early church (Acts 2:42).</li>
</ul>
<div><b><h2>Human nature (and grace) in discipleship</h2></b></div>
<ul>
<li><b>Disciples learn by following:</b> Bartholomew’s main “theme” is not individual heroics but faithful inclusion—showing that God uses many servants whose stories are not fully told.</li>
<li><b>(Uncertain) The grace that turns skepticism into confession:</b> If Nathanael is Bartholomew, John 1:46–49 shows Jesus meeting honest doubt with personal knowledge and revelation.</li>
</ul>
<div><br></div>
<div><b><h2>10) How He Points Us to Christ</h2></b></div>
<div><b><h2>Patterns that highlight Jesus (biblically grounded)</h2></b></div>
<ul>
<li><b>Christ the Caller and Sender:</b> Bartholomew’s significance rests in Jesus’ authority to appoint and commission apostles (Mark 3:13–15; Matthew 10:1).</li>
<li><b>Christ the Promise-Keeper:</b> Bartholomew’s presence in Acts 1 frames the transition to Pentecost—Jesus’ promised Spirit and power for witness (Acts 1:4–8, 13–14).</li>
<li><b>(Uncertain) Christ the Knower of hearts:</b> In John 1, Jesus’ insight into Nathanael leads to confession; this spotlights Jesus’ divine knowledge and messianic identity (John 1:47–49).</li>
</ul>
<div><b><h2>Gospel connections</h2></b></div>
<ul>
<li>The apostles’ role is to bear witness to Jesus’ resurrection and lordship (Acts 1:8; 4:33). Bartholomew’s very inclusion points beyond himself to the risen Christ.</li>
</ul>
<div><br></div>
<div><b><h2>11) Lessons and Applications (8–15, tied to Scripture)</h2></b></div>
<ol>
<li><b>Your significance can be real even if your story is quiet.</b> Bartholomew is named, chosen, and present—even without many recorded exploits (Luke 6:12–16; Acts 1:13).</li>
<li><b>Jesus calls intentionally, not randomly.</b> The Twelve are chosen after prayer and purpose (Luke 6:12–13).</li>
<li><b>Faithfulness includes showing up and praying with God’s people.</b> The apostles devote themselves to prayer together (Acts 1:14).</li>
<li><b>Mission is Christ-authorized, not self-assigned.</b> Jesus gives authority and sends (Matthew 10:1, 5–8).</li>
<li><b>Expect opposition when you bear Christ’s name.</b> Jesus warns the Twelve of persecution and endurance (Matthew 10:16–22).</li>
<li><b>God often works through teams, not solo stars.</b> Bartholomew is consistently presented in community (Matthew 10:2–4; Acts 1:13–14).</li>
<li><b>If (uncertain) Nathanael: bring honest questions to Jesus, not away from him.</b> Nathanael voices doubt and then meets Christ personally (John 1:46–49).</li>
<li><b>If (uncertain) Nathanael: let Jesus’ knowledge of you deepen your worship.</b> “I saw you…” leads to confession (John 1:48–49).</li>
<li><b>The church is nourished by apostolic teaching centered on Jesus.</b> Early believers devote themselves to it (Acts 2:42).</li>
<li><b>Waiting on God is active: prayerful, united, obedient.</b> Acts 1 models waiting as worshipful readiness (Acts 1:4–5, 14).</li>
</ol>
<div><b>Warnings to heed</b><br></div>
<ul>
<li>Don’t underestimate the cost of discipleship (Matthew 10:16–39).</li>
<li>Don’t let bias harden into unbelief (John 1:46–47, if Nathanael).</li>
</ul>
<div><b>Examples to imitate</b><br></div>
<ul>
<li>Steadfast presence with the praying church (Acts 1:13–14).</li>
<li>Honest approach to Christ that ends in confession (John 1:46–49, if Nathanael).</li>
</ul>
