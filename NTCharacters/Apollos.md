<div><h1>Apollos</h1></div>
<div><br></div>
<div><b><h1>Apollos — Bible Character Study</h1></b></div>
<div><b><h2>1) Story Summary (chronological)</h2></b></div>
<div>Apollos enters the biblical narrative during Paul’s wider missionary activity in the mid–first century. He is introduced as a Jewish man from Alexandria who arrives in <b>Ephesus</b> (in Asia Minor). Luke describes him as <b>“an eloquent man”</b> and <b>“competent in the Scriptures,”</b> and as one who had been <b>“instructed in the way of the Lord”</b> and taught about Jesus <b>accurately</b>, yet with an important limitation: <b>he knew only the baptism of John</b> (Acts 18:24–25).</div>
<div>In Ephesus, Apollos speaks boldly in the synagogue. <b>Priscilla and Aquila</b> hear him, take him aside, and <b>explain “the way of God more accurately”</b> (Acts 18:26). After this strengthening and clarification, Apollos desires to go to <b>Achaia</b>; the Ephesian believers encourage him and write to the disciples there to welcome him (Acts 18:27).</div>
<div>In <b>Achaia (notably Corinth)</b>, Apollos becomes a powerful help to the church: he <b>“greatly helped those who through grace had believed,”</b> and he <b>“powerfully refuted the Jews in public, showing by the Scriptures that the Christ was Jesus”</b> (Acts 18:27–28). His ministry is fruitful enough that, later, the Corinthian church splits into factions, with some claiming allegiance to him (“I follow Apollos”)—a division Paul rebukes sharply (1 Cor 1:10–12; 3:3–7; 4:6).</div>
<div>Paul consistently refuses to treat Apollos as a rival. Instead, he frames both men as <b>servants</b> assigned different tasks in one field: <b>Paul “planted,” Apollos “watered,” but God gave the growth</b> (1 Cor 3:5–7). Near the end of 1 Corinthians, Paul notes that he <b>urged Apollos to visit Corinth</b>, but Apollos <b>was not willing at that time</b>—and would come when he had opportunity (1 Cor 16:12). Later, Paul instructs Titus to <b>“speed Zenas the lawyer and Apollos on their way”</b>, implying Apollos is again in active gospel service (Titus 3:13).</div>
<div><b>Where his story begins:</b> Acts 18:24<b>Where it ends (last explicit mention):</b> Titus 3:13 (with later references being non-scriptural speculation)</div>
<div><br></div>
<div><b><h2>2) What He’s Known For</h2></b></div>
<div>Apollos is most known as a <b>gifted, Scripture-saturated teacher and apologist</b> whose ministry helped strengthen the early churches—especially in Corinth—while also becoming (unintentionally) a <b>flashpoint</b> for the Corinthians’ sinful factionalism.</div>
<div><b>Headline descriptors (3–7):</b><br></div>
<ul>
<li><b>Alexandrian Jew, eloquent and learned</b> (Acts 18:24)</li>
<li><b>“Mighty in the Scriptures”</b> (Acts 18:24)</li>
<li><b>Zealous teacher who spoke and taught accurately about Jesus</b> (Acts 18:25)</li>
<li><b>Teachable—received correction from Priscilla and Aquila</b> (Acts 18:26)</li>
<li><b>Powerful public defender of the gospel from the Old Testament</b> (Acts 18:28)</li>
<li><b>Key figure in Corinthian church life—misused by factions</b> (1 Cor 1:12; 3:4–7)</li>
<li><b>Model for “servant-ministry” under God’s sovereignty</b> (1 Cor 3:5–9)</li>
</ul>
<div><br></div>
<div><b><h2>3) Where He Appears in the Bible</h2></b></div>
<div><b>Primary narrative locations (main story)</b><br></div>
<ul>
<li><b>Acts — 18:24–28</b> — Introduced in Ephesus; corrected by Priscilla/Aquila; ministers in Achaia with power from Scripture.</li>
</ul>
<div><b>Secondary references (later mentions, teachings, implications)</b><br></div>
<ul>
<li><b>1 Corinthians — 1:10–13; 3:4–9; 4:6</b> — Corinthians form parties around leaders; Paul uses Apollos (and himself) as examples to expose pride and re-center the church on Christ.</li>
<li><b>1 Corinthians — 16:12</b> — Paul urges Apollos to return to Corinth; Apollos declines for the moment.</li>
<li><b>Titus — 3:13</b> — Titus is told to help Apollos on his journey (ongoing ministry).</li>
</ul>
<div><br></div>
<div><b><h2>4) Vital Statistics</h2></b></div>
<div><b>Historical setting (as Scripture indicates)</b><br></div>
<ul>
<li><b>Early church expansion after Pentecost</b>, during Paul’s missionary era (Acts 18 context; cf. Acts 13–20 broadly). Specific dates aren’t given, but it’s within the first-century apostolic mission.</li>
</ul>
<div><b>Geography (places associated)</b><br></div>
<ul>
<li><b>Alexandria (Egypt)</b> — his origin (Acts 18:24)</li>
<li><b>Ephesus (Asia Minor)</b> — where he teaches and is further instructed (Acts 18:24–26)</li>
<li><b>Achaia / Corinth (Greece)</b> — where his ministry greatly helps believers and becomes a reference point in Paul’s letters (Acts 18:27–28; 1 Cor 1–4)</li>
</ul>
<div><b>Family and relationships</b><br></div>
<ul>
<li><b>Priscilla and Aquila</b> — mentors/correctors who explain “the way of God more accurately” (Acts 18:26)</li>
<li><b>Paul</b> — fellow laborer; Paul refuses rivalry and teaches unity (1 Cor 3:5–9; 16:12)</li>
<li><b>Corinthian believers</b> — benefited by his ministry, yet some misuse his name for division (Acts 18:27; 1 Cor 1:12)</li>
<li><b>Zenas and Titus (implied ministry network)</b> — Apollos is partnered in travel/ministry logistics (Titus 3:13)</li>
</ul>
<div><b>Roles/offices</b><br></div>
<ul>
<li><b>Teacher / preacher / apologist-evangelist</b> (Acts 18:25, 28)</li>
<li>Described as a <b>minister/servant</b> in Paul’s framework (1 Cor 3:5)</li>
</ul>
<div><b>Key turning points</b><br></div>
<ul>
<li><b>Receives doctrinal clarification</b> from Priscilla and Aquila (Acts 18:26)</li>
<li><b>Transitions to Achaia</b> to strengthen believers and publicly argue from Scripture (Acts 18:27–28)</li>
<li><b>Becomes a test-case for church unity vs. celebrity factionalism</b> (1 Cor 1:12; 3:4–7)</li>
</ul>

<div><b><h2>6) Strengths, Weaknesses, and Heart-Level Patterns</h2></b></div>
<div><b>Strengths (with textual grounding)</b></div>
<ul>
<li><b>Deep scriptural competence</b>: “competent in the Scriptures… mighty in the Scriptures” (Acts 18:24).</li>
<li><b>Zeal joined to truth-seeking</b>: “fervent in spirit” (Acts 18:25).</li>
<li><b>Courage and clarity in public teaching</b>: “began to speak boldly in the synagogue” (Acts 18:26).</li>
<li><b>Teachable humility</b>: he receives further explanation from Priscilla and Aquila (Acts 18:26).</li>
<li><b>Gospel persuasion from Scripture</b>: “showing by the Scriptures that the Christ was Jesus” (Acts 18:28).</li>
<li><b>Church-building encouragement</b>: “greatly helped those who through grace had believed” (Acts 18:27).</li>
</ul>
<div><b>Weaknesses / blind spots (careful, text-anchored)</b></div>
<ul>
<li><b>Incomplete understanding at first</b>: “he knew only the baptism of John” (Acts 18:25). The text does not spell out every detail of what he lacked, but it clearly marks a deficiency that required correction (Acts 18:26).</li>
<li><b>Association with factionalism (not necessarily his personal sin, but a danger around him)</b>: some Corinthians say, “I follow Apollos” (1 Cor 1:12; 3:4). Paul’s rebuke targets the Corinthians’ pride and division (1 Cor 1:10–13), not an accusation that Apollos sought a following—yet it exposes the <i>real-world hazard</i> of gifted ministry.</li>
</ul>
<div><b>Motives, fears, desires (inferences kept close to the text)</b></div>
<ul>
<li><b>Desire to proclaim Christ faithfully</b>: he teaches about Jesus and proves from Scripture Jesus is the Christ (Acts 18:25, 28).</li>
<li><b>Desire to strengthen believers</b>: he helps those who had believed (Acts 18:27).</li>
<li><b>Prudent restraint</b> (possible inference): his refusal to go to Corinth at that time (1 Cor 16:12) suggests careful judgment about timing/circumstances, though the text does not specify his reasons.</li>
</ul>
<div><b>What changes (or doesn’t) over time?</b></div>
<ul>
<li><b>What changes:</b> He moves from <i>accurate but incomplete</i> understanding (Acts 18:25) to a more fully instructed ministry that powerfully strengthens churches (Acts 18:26–28).</li>
<li><b>What doesn’t change:</b> His ministry remains <b>Word-centered</b>, <b>Christ-focused</b>, and <b>publicly reasoned from Scripture</b> (Acts 18:24, 28), and he continues in cooperative gospel labor (1 Cor 3:5–9; Titus 3:13).</li>
</ul>
<div><br></div>
<div><b><h2>7) Victories and Challenges</h2></b></div>
<div><b>Major “wins” (with references)</b></div>
<ul>
<li><b>Effective scriptural proclamation of Jesus as Messiah</b> (Acts 18:28).</li>
<li><b>Strengthening believers as an instrument of grace</b> (Acts 18:27).</li>
<li><b>Model of receiving correction without collapse</b>—he is refined rather than defensive (Acts 18:26–28).</li>
<li><b>Healthy partnership in ministry without rivalry</b>—Paul honors him as a fellow servant (1 Cor 3:5–9).</li>
</ul>
<div><b>Major challenges (with references)</b></div>
<ul>
<li><b>Doctrinal incompleteness early on</b>—needed more accurate instruction (Acts 18:25–26).</li>
<li><b>Being used as a badge of party-spirit</b>—his name becomes a banner for division in Corinth (1 Cor 1:12; 3:4).</li>
<li><b>Complex pastoral dynamics</b>—returning to Corinth may have been sensitive; at least, Paul urges it and Apollos declines for the moment (1 Cor 16:12).</li>
</ul>
<div><b>Cost of faithfulness / consequences of compromise (highlight)</b><br></div>
<ul>
<li>Faithful, gifted ministry can still be <b>misused by immature hearers</b> (1 Cor 1:10–13). The “cost” is that a teacher’s name can become entangled in others’ pride—requiring continual re-centering on Christ and humility in the church (1 Cor 3:5–7).</li>
</ul>

<div><b><h2>9) Theology and Big Themes</h2></b></div>
<ul>
<li><b>The sufficiency and Christ-centeredness of Scripture:</b> Apollos’ apologetic is explicitly “by the Scriptures” and aimed at proving Jesus as Messiah (Acts 18:28). This echoes Jesus’ own method of Scripture-fulfillment teaching (Luke 24:27).</li>
<li><b>Doctrinal accuracy matters—zeal must be shaped by fuller truth:</b> Apollos is zealous and accurate in what he knows, but still needs further instruction (Acts 18:25–26). The church lovingly strengthens him rather than either condemning him or leaving him under-taught.</li>
<li><b>God’s sovereignty in growth versus human instruments:</b> Paul’s theology of ministry explicitly uses Apollos as an example: servants labor, but <b>God gives growth</b> (1 Cor 3:5–7). This guards the church from idolatry of leaders and despair when leaders change.</li>
<li><b>Unity of the church under the lordship of Christ:</b> The Corinthians’ sloganizing (“I follow Apollos”) is confronted with the question, “Is Christ divided?” (1 Cor 1:12–13). Apollos’ life becomes a lens for the church’s need to treasure Christ above personalities.</li>
<li><b>The value of wise, private correction in discipleship:</b> Priscilla and Aquila correct Apollos not through public shaming but by taking him aside and explaining more accurately (Acts 18:26)—a model of courageous, humble discipleship.</li>
</ul>
<div><br></div>
<div><b><h2>10) How Apollos Points Us to Christ</h2></b></div>
<div>Apollos is not an “office” figure like king/priest in a typological sense, so typology should be restrained. But he points us to Christ through <b>patterns of gospel ministry</b> anchored in Scripture:</div>
<ul>
<li><b>Christ is the center and goal of biblical interpretation:</b> Apollos demonstrates that faithful teaching aims at showing <b>“the Christ was Jesus”</b> (Acts 18:28). This aligns with Jesus’ claim that the Scriptures testify about Him (cf. Luke 24:27, 44–47).</li>
<li><b>Christ alone is Lord; ministers are servants:</b> Paul’s treatment of Apollos directs attention away from the messenger to the Master—<b>“servants through whom you believed”</b> (1 Cor 3:5). This protects Christ’s unique glory and headship over the church (cf. 1 Cor 1:12–13).</li>
<li><b>Christ builds His church by grace through means:</b> Apollos “greatly helped those who through grace had believed” (Acts 18:27). The grammar holds together grace as the cause and ministry as the instrument—consistent with the gospel pattern that God works through ordained means while remaining the source of life (1 Cor 3:6–7).</li>
</ul>

<div><b><h2>11) Lessons and Applications (8–15, Scripture-tied)</h2></b></div>
<ol>
<li><b>Cultivate Scripture-mastery for the sake of Christ, not self-display</b> (Acts 18:24, 28).</li>
<li><b>Zeal is good, but zeal must keep growing in accuracy</b> (Acts 18:25–26).</li>
<li><b>Receive correction as a gift, not an assault</b> (Acts 18:26).</li>
<li><b>Healthy churches commend and send gifted people rather than competing for them</b> (Acts 18:27).</li>
<li><b>Public persuasion should be anchored in Scripture, not personality</b> (Acts 18:28).</li>
<li><b>Refuse party-spirit: don’t turn teachers into tribes</b> (1 Cor 1:10–13; 3:4).</li>
<li><b>Remember the true math of ministry: servants labor, God gives growth</b> (1 Cor 3:5–7).</li>
<li><b>You can be a blessing and still be misunderstood or misused—stay Christ-centered</b> (1 Cor 1:12–13).</li>
<li><b>Timing and prudence matter in ministry decisions</b> (1 Cor 16:12).</li>
<li><b>Support gospel workers materially and logistically</b> (Titus 3:13).</li>
<li><b>Work toward unity by “not going beyond what is written”</b>—letting Scripture set boundaries on pride and speculation (1 Cor 4:6).</li>
<li><b>Church maturity shows in gratitude for various gifts without idolizing any</b> (1 Cor 3:5–9).</li>
</ol>
<div><b>Warnings to heed</b><br></div>
<ul>
<li><b>Leader-idolatry and comparison</b> (1 Cor 1:12–13; 3:4).</li>
<li><b>Pride that weaponizes good gifts for division</b> (1 Cor 4:6).</li>
</ul>
<div><b>Examples to imitate</b><br></div>
<ul>
<li><b>Teachable humility</b> (Acts 18:26).</li>
<li><b>Scripture-based evangelistic courage</b> (Acts 18:28).</li>
<li><b>God-centered view of fruitfulness</b> (1 Cor 3:6–7).</li>
</ul>
<div>Applications to key areas:</div>
<ul>
<li><b>Personal holiness:</b> receive correction; refuse pride (Acts 18:26; 1 Cor 4:6).</li>
<li><b>Relationships:</b> correct privately and patiently; welcome coworkers (Acts 18:26–27).</li>
<li><b>Prayer:</b> ask God for growth, since He alone gives it (1 Cor 3:6–7).</li>
<li><b>Suffering / conflict:</b> endure being misunderstood without retaliating; re-center on Christ (1 Cor 1:12–13).</li>
<li><b>Decision-making / leadership:</b> pursue prudence and timing, not people-pleasing (1 Cor 16:12).</li>
</ul>

<div><b><h2>12) Discussion and Reflection Questions (10–20)</h2></b></div>
<div><b>Personal reflection</b><br></div>
<ol>
<li>Where are you “fervent in spirit” (Acts 18:25) but still in need of deeper accuracy (Acts 18:26)?</li>
<li>How do you respond when someone “explains more accurately” what you’re missing (Acts 18:26)?</li>
<li>Do you measure “success” in ministry by visibility, or by faithfulness to Scripture and Christ (Acts 18:28; 1 Cor 3:6–7)?</li>
<li>Are there leaders you subtly treat as identity markers (“I follow ___”)? What would repentance look like (1 Cor 1:12–13)?</li>
</ol>
<div><b>Small group discussion</b>5) Why might Priscilla and Aquila’s method of correction be wise (Acts 18:26)? When is private correction appropriate? 6) What does Acts 18:27 suggest about discerning, commending, and sending gifted people? 7) How does 1 Corinthians 3:5–7 reshape the way a church should talk about pastors/teachers? 8) How can a church enjoy different preaching gifts without forming factions (1 Cor 1:10–13; 3:4)?</div>
<div><b>Deep study / observation</b>9) In Acts 18:24–28, list every description of Apollos. What do you learn about competence, character, and mission? 10) What exactly does Luke emphasize about Apollos’ message and method (Acts 18:25, 28)? 11) Compare Apollos’ “knowing only the baptism of John” (Acts 18:25) with the disciples in Acts 19:1–7. What similarities/differences do you observe? 12) In 1 Corinthians 1–4, what is Paul’s main diagnosis of Corinth’s problem, and how does he use Apollos’ name to address it (1 Cor 1:10–13; 3:3–7; 4:6)? 13) What does 1 Cor 16:12 reveal (and not reveal) about Apollos? What should we avoid assuming? 14) What does Titus 3:13 teach about the “support roles” behind ministry?</div>

<div><b><h2>13) Prayer Response</h2></b></div>
<div>Lord God, Thank You for raising up servants like Apollos—gifted in Your Word and eager to proclaim that Jesus is the Christ (Acts 18:28). Keep us fervent in spirit, yet humble enough to receive clearer instruction when we are incomplete (Acts 18:25–26). Guard our hearts and our churches from pride and party-spirit; forgive us when we exalt human instruments and divide the body of Christ (1 Cor 1:12–13). Teach us to labor faithfully while resting in Your sovereignty, remembering that You alone give the growth (1 Cor 3:6–7). Make our speech and our lives Scripture-shaped, Christ-centered, and full of grace, for the building up of Your people. In Jesus’ name, amen.</div>
