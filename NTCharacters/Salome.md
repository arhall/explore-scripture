<div><b><h1>Salome</h1></b></div>
<div><b><h1><br></h1></b></div>
<div><b><h1>Salome — Bible Character Study (NT)</h1></b></div>
<div><b><h2>First: which “Salome”?</h2></b></div>
<div>In the New Testament, <b>the only woman explicitly named “Salome” is a follower of Jesus</b> who appears in the passion and resurrection narratives (Mark 15:40; 16:1).</div>
<div>A <b>different, famous “Salome”</b> is the girl who danced for Herod Antipas and whose request led to John the Baptist’s execution—but <b>Scripture never gives her name</b> (Matt 14:6–11; Mark 6:22–28). Later historical tradition (outside Scripture) supplies the name “Salome,” but <b>we will not treat that as certain biblical data</b>.</div>
<div>This study focuses on <b>Salome the disciple</b> named in Mark.</div>
<div><br></div>
<div><b><h2>1) Story Summary (chronological)</h2></b></div>
<div>Salome appears near the end of Jesus’ earthly ministry as one of the <b>women disciples</b> who followed and served Jesus, first in <b>Galilee</b> and then in <b>Jerusalem</b> during the crucifixion week (Mark 15:40–41). When many fled, she is among the faithful who remain near the cross (Mark 15:40). After Jesus’ burial, she joins other women to bring spices to anoint Jesus’ body, arriving at the tomb early on the first day of the week (Mark 16:1–2). Mark’s narrative then turns to the angelic announcement of the resurrection and the commissioning to tell the disciples (Mark 16:5–7).</div>
<div><b>Where her story begins and ends in Scripture:</b><br></div>
<ul>
<li><b>Begins (explicitly named):</b> at the crucifixion scene (Mark 15:40–41).</li>
<li><b>Ends (explicitly named):</b> at the tomb with spices (Mark 16:1). (Anything beyond that is implied participation with the community of disciples, but not directly stated.)</li>
</ul>
<div><br></div>
<div><b><h2>2) What She’s Known For</h2></b></div>
<div>Salome is most known for being a <b>faithful woman disciple</b> present at Jesus’ death and among those who came to honor him after burial.</div>
<div><b>Headlines</b><br></div>
<ul>
<li><b>Woman disciple who followed Jesus from Galilee</b> (Mark 15:41)</li>
<li><b>Present at the crucifixion as a witness</b> (Mark 15:40)</li>
<li><b>Part of the group that came with burial spices</b> (Mark 16:1)</li>
<li><b>Associated with service/support of Jesus’ ministry</b> (Mark 15:41)</li>
<li><b>Example of steadfastness when others withdrew</b> (Mark 15:40–41)</li>
</ul>
<div><br></div>
<div><b><h2>3) Where She Appears in the Bible</h2></b></div>
<div><b><h2>Primary narrative locations</h2></b></div>
<ul>
<li><b>Mark — 15:40–41</b> — Named among women who watched the crucifixion; followed and served Jesus from Galilee.</li>
<li><b>Mark — 16:1</b> — Named among women who bought spices to anoint Jesus after the Sabbath.</li>
</ul>
<div><b><h2>Possible but uncertain identification</h2></b></div>
<ul>
<li><b>Matthew — 20:20–23; 27:55–56</b> — “The mother of the sons of Zebedee” appears (requesting honor; present near crucifixion). Some identify her with Salome by harmonizing lists, but <b>Matthew does not name her Salome</b>, so this remains <b>uncertain</b>.</li>
<li><b>John — 19:25</b> — Women at the cross are listed differently; <b>no Salome is named</b>. Any equation with Salome is <b>not explicit</b>.</li>
</ul>
<div><br></div>
<div><b><h2>4) Vital Statistics</h2></b></div>
<ul>
<li><b>Historical setting:</b> Late Second Temple period under Roman rule; Jesus’ ministry and passion week (1st century AD context implied by the Gospels; Mark 15–16).</li>
<li><b>Geography:</b><br></li>
<ul>
<li><b>Galilee</b> — region where she followed and served Jesus (Mark 15:41).</li>
<li><b>Jerusalem</b> — crucifixion and tomb narratives (Mark 15:40; 16:1).</li>
</ul>
<li><b>Family/relationships (explicit):</b><br></li>
<ul>
<li>Closely associated with <b>Mary Magdalene</b> and <b>Mary the mother of James the younger and of Joses</b> in Mark’s passion-resurrection scenes (Mark 15:40; 16:1).</li>
</ul>
<li><b>Family/relationships (uncertain):</b><br></li>
<ul>
<li>Possible identification with <b>the mother of James and John (sons of Zebedee)</b> (Matt 20:20; 27:56) is <b>not stated in Scripture</b> and should be held loosely.</li>
</ul>
<li><b>Roles/offices:</b><br></li>
<ul>
<li><b>Disciple/follower</b> (Mark 15:41).</li>
<li><b>Servant/supporter</b> of Jesus’ ministry (Mark 15:41).</li>
<li><b>Witness</b> to crucifixion and the empty-tomb events (Mark 15:40; 16:1–6).</li>
</ul>
<li><b>Key turning points (as Scripture shows them):</b><br></li>
<ul>
<li>Remaining present at the crucifixion (Mark 15:40).</li>
<li>Choosing costly devotion (spices; early visit) after Jesus’ death (Mark 16:1–2).</li>
</ul>
</ul>

<div><b><h2>6) Strengths, Weaknesses, and Heart-Level Patterns</h2></b></div>
<div><b><h2>Strengths (evidenced in the text)</h2></b></div>
<ul>
<li><b>Steadfast presence under pressure:</b> She is named among the women who “were looking on” at the crucifixion (Mark 15:40).</li>
<li><b>Service-oriented discipleship:</b> She is included among those who “followed him and ministered to him” (Mark 15:41).</li>
<li><b>Costly devotion:</b> Participates in preparing to anoint Jesus with spices (Mark 16:1).</li>
</ul>
<div><b><h2>Weaknesses / sins / blind spots (what we can and can’t say)</h2></b></div>
<div>Mark does <b>not</b> record personal failures, speech, or moral compromise for Salome. So we should <b>not invent</b> sins or psychological motives beyond the textual signals.</div>
<div>What we <i>can</i> observe is shared human limitation visible in the wider scene:</div>
<ul>
<li>The women come to anoint a dead body, which can reflect <b>limited expectation</b> of resurrection (Mark 16:1–3). That’s an inference from their actions, but it’s also consistent with the disciples’ general slowness to grasp resurrection predictions (cf. Mark 9:31–32; 10:33–34—Jesus foretells; they struggle to understand).</li>
</ul>
<div><b><h2>Motives / fears / desires (careful inference anchored to the text)</h2></b></div>
<ul>
<li><b>Desire to honor Jesus</b> despite death and danger (Mark 16:1).</li>
<li><b>Loyalty and love</b> expressed by staying near the cross (Mark 15:40) and continuing service (Mark 15:41).</li>
<li><b>Possible fear mixed with courage:</b> The resurrection scene in Mark emphasizes fear and astonishment among the women (Mark 16:8). While Salome is part of that group (Mark 16:1), Mark 16:8 describes the women’s reaction generally.</li>
</ul>
<div><b><h2>What changes (or doesn’t) over time?</h2></b></div>
<ul>
<li><b>Consistent pattern:</b> from Galilee through Jerusalem, Salome’s portrayal is <b>steady faithfulness expressed through service and presence</b> (Mark 15:41 → 15:40 → 16:1).</li>
<li><b>No “arc” of transformation is narrated</b> (unlike Peter, Paul, etc.). Her role is mainly <b>witness/support</b> in the climactic events of the gospel.</li>
</ul>
<div><br></div>
<div><b><h2>7) Victories and Challenges</h2></b></div>
<div><b><h2>Major “wins” (faithful influence)</h2></b></div>
<ul>
<li><b>Faithfulness at the cross:</b> She is named as present and watching (Mark 15:40).</li>
<li><b>Continued devotion after death:</b> She participates in honoring Jesus’ body (Mark 16:1).</li>
<li><b>Proximity to resurrection revelation:</b> She is among the first group at the tomb to encounter the announcement that Jesus is risen (Mark 16:1–6).</li>
</ul>
<div><b><h2>Major challenges (cost and consequence)</h2></b></div>
<ul>
<li><b>The cost of association with a crucified Messiah:</b> Remaining near the crucifixion is socially and spiritually costly (Mark 15:40–41; cf. the broader flight of disciples, Mark 14:50).</li>
<li><b>Grief and the darkness of Holy Saturday:</b> Buying spices assumes death has won (Mark 16:1).</li>
<li><b>Fear and shock at God’s surprising work:</b> Mark highlights the women’s trembling/astonishment at the end of the scene (Mark 16:8).</li>
</ul>
<div><b>Cost of faithfulness:</b> She serves and follows in the shadows, not from positions of public power, and stays when it is dangerous and painful (Mark 15:40–41).</div>
<div><br></div>
<div><b><h2>8) Essential Verses and Key Cross-References (organized by themes)</h2></b></div>
<div><b><h2>A. Faithful discipleship and service</h2></b></div>
<ul>
<li><b>Mark 15:41</b> — “followed him and ministered to him” (Salome included).</li>
<li><b>Mark 10:45</b> — The Son of Man came “to serve,” shaping the model of discipleship as service.</li>
<li><b>Luke 8:1–3</b> — Women who supported Jesus from their means (theme-parallel to Mark 15:41).</li>
</ul>
<div><b><h2>B. Witness at the cross</h2></b></div>
<ul>
<li><b>Mark 15:40</b> — Salome named among women who watched.</li>
<li><b>John 19:25–27</b> — Faithful presence of women near the cross (no Salome named, but same theme).</li>
<li><b>Mark 8:34–35</b> — Costly discipleship: taking up the cross.</li>
</ul>
<div><b><h2>C. Love that honors Christ beyond death</h2></b></div>
<ul>
<li><b>Mark 16:1</b> — Salome among those who brought spices.</li>
<li><b>Ecclesiastes 3:1,4</b> — A time to mourn (wisdom framing for grief and love).</li>
<li><b>Romans 12:1</b> — Devotion expressed bodily, practically.</li>
</ul>
<div><b><h2>D. Resurrection dawn and holy surprise</h2></b></div>
<ul>
<li><b>Mark 16:1–6</b> — From spices to announcement: “He has risen.”</li>
<li><b>Mark 9:31; 10:33–34</b> — Jesus foretells death and resurrection (background for the “surprise”).</li>
<li><b>1 Corinthians 15:3–4</b> — Core gospel: death, burial, resurrection.</li>
</ul>
<div><b><h2>E. The often-overlooked faithfulness of “ordinary” saints</h2></b></div>
<ul>
<li><b>Mark 15:40–41</b> — Named discipleship among women.</li>
<li><b>Hebrews 6:10</b> — God remembers love shown in service.</li>
<li><b>Matthew 25:40</b> — Christ receives service done to his people as service to him.</li>
</ul>

<div><b><h2>9) Theology and Big Themes</h2></b></div>
<div><b><h2>God’s character revealed</h2></b></div>
<ul>
<li><b>Faithfulness in salvation:</b> God brings redemption through the cross even when followers only see defeat (Mark 15:37–39; 16:1–6).</li>
<li><b>Gentle honor toward humble disciples:</b> Mark names and remembers those who served (Mark 15:40–41), showing God’s regard for “hidden” faithfulness.</li>
</ul>
<div><b><h2>Kingdom discipleship</h2></b></div>
<ul>
<li><b>Discipleship includes service, not status</b> (Mark 15:41; cf. Mark 10:42–45).</li>
<li><b>Persevering presence:</b> True allegiance is tested at the cross (Mark 15:40; cf. Mark 14:50).</li>
</ul>
<div><b><h2>Human nature: grief, limited understanding, and grace</h2></b></div>
<ul>
<li>The spices (Mark 16:1) show sincere devotion mingled with human limitation—yet God meets disciples in their weakness with revelation and commission (Mark 16:6–7).</li>
</ul>
<div><b><h2>Witness and proclamation</h2></b></div>
<ul>
<li>The women at the tomb are positioned as early witnesses to resurrection truth (Mark 16:1–7), underscoring that God often entrusts world-shaking news to unlikely messengers.</li>
</ul>
<div><br></div>
<div><b><h2>10) How Salome Points Us to Christ</h2></b></div>
<div><b><h2>Pattern: faithful presence at the cross</h2></b></div>
<div>Salome’s life (as far as Scripture presents it) <b>points away from herself and toward Jesus’ saving work</b>—she is there to witness and honor <i>him</i> (Mark 15:40–41; 16:1). Her story is a spotlight that falls on Christ crucified and risen (Mark 15:37–39; 16:6).</div>
<div><b><h2>Gospel connection: love that clings to Jesus when hope seems dead</h2></b></div>
<div>Bringing spices (Mark 16:1) embodies a kind of discipleship that says, <i>“Even if I cannot yet see the victory, I will not stop loving him.”</i> Then the resurrection announcement declares that Christ’s victory does not rest on the disciple’s strength but on <b>God’s power</b> (Mark 16:6; cf. Rom 4:24–25).</div>
<div><b><h2>The “remnant” motif fulfilled in the new covenant community</h2></b></div>
<div>At the cross and tomb, the faithful few remain (Mark 15:40–41; 16:1). This echoes the biblical pattern that God preserves witnesses to his saving acts—now centered on the climactic act: Christ’s death and resurrection (1 Cor 15:3–4).</div>
<div><br></div>
<div><b><h2>11) Lessons and Applications (Scripture-tethered)</h2></b></div>
<div><b><h2>Examples to imitate</h2></b></div>
<ol>
<li><b>Follow Jesus with practical service</b> (Mark 15:41; Mark 10:45).</li>
<li><b>Stay near Christ in suffering</b>—don’t disappear when discipleship costs (Mark 15:40; Mark 8:34).</li>
<li><b>Honor Christ when outcomes look bleak</b> (Mark 16:1; Rom 12:1).</li>
<li><b>Let love for Jesus outlast emotional clarity</b>—faithfulness is not the same as feeling strong (Mark 16:1–3).</li>
<li><b>Accept God’s surprising reversals</b>—resurrection often shatters our expectations (Mark 16:4–6).</li>
<li><b>Be content with “supporting roles” in the kingdom</b>—God records what the world overlooks (Mark 15:40–41; Heb 6:10).</li>
<li><b>Practice embodied devotion</b>—discipleship touches time, money, risk, and presence (Mark 16:1–2).</li>
<li><b>Treasure the gospel events as central</b>—cross and resurrection are not abstract doctrines but history you live from (Mark 15–16; 1 Cor 15:3–4).</li>
</ol>
<div><b><h2>Warnings to heed</h2></b></div>
<ol>
<li><b>Do not confuse proximity to Jesus with safety or ease</b>—the cross confronts every illusion of comfortable discipleship (Mark 15:40; 8:34).</li>
<li><b>Do not assume God’s work is limited to the powerful</b>—God spotlights faithful servants and witnesses (Mark 15:40–41).</li>
<li><b>Do not let fear have the final word</b>—Mark notes trembling/astonishment at the tomb scene (Mark 16:8); the gospel calls for witness (Mark 16:7).</li>
</ol>
<div><b><h2>Application areas</h2></b></div>
<ul>
<li><b>Personal holiness:</b> choose costly obedience in private (Mark 15:41).</li>
<li><b>Relationships:</b> be the kind of friend who stays near suffering (Mark 15:40).</li>
<li><b>Prayer:</b> bring your grief and confusion to God; he meets disciples at the tomb (Mark 16:1–6).</li>
<li><b>Decision-making:</b> when you don’t know what tomorrow holds, do the next faithful thing (Mark 16:1–2).</li>
<li><b>Leadership:</b> honor and protect the “serving class” of disciples; they often carry the ministry’s weight (Mark 15:41).</li>
</ul>

<div><b><h2>12) Discussion and Reflection Questions (10–20)</h2></b></div>
<div><b><h2>Personal reflection</h2></b></div>
<ol>
<li>What does Mark emphasize by naming Salome and the other women (Mark 15:40–41)?</li>
<li>Where are you tempted to “follow from a distance” rather than remain present (Mark 15:40)?</li>
<li>What would “ministering to Jesus” look like in your current season (Mark 15:41; cf. Matt 25:40)?</li>
<li>What “spices” (costly acts of devotion) might God be calling you to offer (Mark 16:1)?</li>
</ol>
<div><b><h2>Small group discussion</h2></b></div>
<ol>
<li>How does Salome’s quiet faithfulness challenge common ideas of spiritual influence (Mark 15:41)?</li>
<li>Why do you think Mark highlights the women’s presence when many disciples fled (Mark 14:50; 15:40–41)?</li>
<li>What helps a community stay faithful in seasons of fear and loss (Mark 16:8; Heb 10:23–25)?</li>
<li>How can the church better honor unseen service (Mark 15:41; Heb 6:10)?</li>
</ol>
<div><b><h2>Deep study (text-observation)</h2></b></div>
<ol>
<li>Compare Mark 15:40–41 with Luke 8:1–3: what similarities appear in how women disciples are described?</li>
<li>In Mark 16:1–3, what do the women assume, and what problem do they anticipate?</li>
<li>How does Mark 16:6 (“He has risen”) reinterpret the women’s mission in Mark 16:1?</li>
<li>How does Mark’s depiction of fear/astonishment (Mark 16:8) function literarily and pastorally?</li>
<li>If Salome is (uncertainly) identified with the mother of Zebedee’s sons (Matt 20:20–23), how would that reshape your reading—and what safeguards keep you from overstating the case?</li>
</ol>
<div><b><h2>Christ-centered synthesis</h2></b></div>
<ol>
<li>What do Salome’s actions teach about responding to Christ when you do not yet understand God’s plan (Mark 16:1–6)?</li>
<li>How does the resurrection announcement transform ordinary faithfulness into commissioned witness (Mark 16:7)?</li>
</ol>
