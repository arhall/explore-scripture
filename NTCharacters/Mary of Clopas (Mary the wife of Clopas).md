<div><b><h1>Mary of Clopas (Mary the wife of Clopas)</h1></b></div>
<div><b><h1><br></h1></b></div>
<div><b><h1>Mary of Clopas (Mary the wife of Clopas) — Bible Character Study</h1></b></div>
<div><b>Name note:</b> “Mary of Clopas” appears explicitly in <b>John 19:25</b>. Many scholars connect her with “Mary the mother of James and Joses/Joseph” in the Synoptics (<b>Matthew 27:56; Mark 15:40; Mark 15:47; Mark 16:1; Luke 24:10</b>), but the text does not <i>explicitly</i> equate them—so we’ll mark those links as <b>probable, not certain</b>.</div>
<div><br></div>
<div><b><h2>1) Story Summary (chronological)</h2></b></div>
<div>Mary of Clopas is one of the women who remained close to Jesus during His suffering, death, burial, and (very likely) the first Easter morning witness circle.</div>
<ol>
<li><b>At the cross:</b> John names her among the women near Jesus during the crucifixion (<b>John 19:25</b>). This places her in the small, faithful remnant that stayed when many disciples scattered (contrast <b>Mark 14:50</b>).</li>
<li><b>At the burial (probable link):</b> The Synoptic Gospels repeatedly mention a “Mary” who watched Jesus’ burial and returned with spices—identified as “Mary the mother of Joses/Joseph” (<b>Mark 15:47</b>) and later among the women coming to the tomb (<b>Mark 16:1</b>). Many reasonably infer this is the same Mary John calls “of Clopas” (<b>John 19:25</b>), but John does not say so directly.</li>
<li><b>At the empty tomb / resurrection message (probable link):</b> Luke lists “Mary the mother of James” among the women who reported the resurrection to the apostles (<b>Luke 24:10</b>). If this is the same person as Mary of Clopas, then she belongs to the first witnesses commissioned to report the risen Christ (cf. <b>Matthew 28:7–10</b>).</li>
</ol>
<div><b>Where her story begins/ends in Scripture:</b><br></div>
<ul>
<li>Begins (named): <b>John 19:25</b><br></li>
<li>Ends (probable echoes via linked “Mary” identifiers): <b>Luke 24:10</b> (and parallels)</li>
</ul>
<div><br></div>
<div><b><h2>2) What She’s Known For</h2></b></div>
<div>Mary of Clopas is most known for <b>steadfast, costly presence</b> with Jesus at the cross—faithfulness that does not flee when suffering intensifies.</div>
<div><b>Headlines:</b><br></div>
<ul>
<li><b>Faithful witness at the crucifixion</b> (<b>John 19:25</b>)</li>
<li><b>Part of the women disciples who “followed” and “served” Jesus</b> (theme: <b>Mark 15:40–41</b>)</li>
<li><b>Courage under public danger</b> (standing near a condemned man; <b>John 19:25</b>)</li>
<li><b>(Probable) witness of burial and empty tomb</b> (<b>Mark 15:47; Mark 16:1; Luke 24:10</b>)</li>
<li><b>Quiet, non-prominent, but pivotal</b>—present when redemption is accomplished (<b>John 19:30; 19:25</b>)</li>
</ul>
<div><br></div>
<div><b><h2>3) Where She Appears in the Bible</h2></b></div>
<div><b><h2>Primary narrative locations</h2></b></div>
<ul>
<li><b>John — 19:25</b> — Named among the women near the cross during Jesus’ crucifixion.</li>
</ul>
<div><b><h2>Probable secondary parallels (uncertain identification, but commonly linked)</h2></b></div>
<ul>
<li><b>Matthew — 27:55–56</b> — “Mary the mother of James and Joseph” among women who followed Jesus from Galilee and ministered to Him.</li>
<li><b>Mark — 15:40–41</b> — “Mary the mother of James the younger and of Joses” among women who followed and served Jesus.</li>
<li><b>Mark — 15:47</b> — “Mary the mother of Joses” sees where Jesus is laid.</li>
<li><b>Mark — 16:1</b> — “Mary the mother of James” brings spices to the tomb with Mary Magdalene and Salome.</li>
<li><b>Luke — 24:10</b> — “Mary the mother of James” among the women who tell the apostles about the resurrection.</li>
</ul>
<div><b>Why “probable”?</b> John’s descriptor is relational (“of Clopas”), while the Synoptics often identify by children (“mother of James/Joses”). These can fit the same person, but Scripture doesn’t formally equate them.</div>
<div><br></div>
<div><b><h2>4) Vital Statistics</h2></b></div>
<div><b>Historical setting:</b><br></div>
<ul>
<li>Late Second Temple Judaism under Roman rule; the crucifixion narrative occurs during Passover week (<b>John 18–19; Mark 14–16</b>).</li>
</ul>
<div><b>Geography:</b><br></div>
<ul>
<li><b>Jerusalem</b> (crucifixion and burial vicinity: <b>John 19:17–20, 41–42</b>)</li>
<li>If she is among the Galilean women, then her discipleship may have roots in <b>Galilee</b> (<b>Mark 15:40–41</b>).</li>
</ul>
<div><b>Family and relationships:</b><br></div>
<ul>
<li><b>Clopas/Clophas</b> — identified as connected to her name (“Mary of Clopas,” often read “wife of Clopas”) (<b>John 19:25</b>).</li>
<li><b>Children (probable):</b> “James” and “Joses/Joseph” if she is the same “Mary” in the Synoptics (<b>Mark 15:40; Matthew 27:56</b>).</li>
<li><b>Associates:</b> Mary Magdalene and other women disciples (<b>John 19:25; Mark 15:40–41; Luke 24:10</b>).</li>
</ul>
<div><b>Roles/offices:</b><br></div>
<ul>
<li>Not an “office” holder described (no formal title like prophet/judge), but functionally a <b>disciple/witness</b> (theme: those who follow and serve Jesus, <b>Mark 15:40–41</b>).</li>
</ul>
<div><b>Key turning points:</b><br></div>
<ul>
<li><b>Remaining near Jesus at the cross</b> (<b>John 19:25</b>)</li>
<li><b>(Probable) Participation in burial watching and resurrection discovery/reporting</b> (<b>Mark 15:47; Mark 16:1; Luke 24:10</b>)</li>
</ul>

<div><b><h2>6) Strengths, Weaknesses, and Heart-Level Patterns</h2></b></div>
<div><b><h2>Strengths (text-grounded)</h2></b></div>
<ul>
<li><b>Courageous fidelity in suffering:</b> She stands near the crucified Christ (<b>John 19:25</b>).</li>
<li><b>Steadfast service (probable):</b> Women “followed” and “ministered” to Jesus (<b>Mark 15:40–41</b>).</li>
<li><b>Persevering love (probable):</b> Returns to the tomb with spices (<b>Mark 16:1</b>).</li>
</ul>
<div><b><h2>Weaknesses / sins / blind spots</h2></b></div>
<ul>
<li>Scripture records <b>no explicit moral failure</b> or rebuke directed to Mary of Clopas.</li>
<li>If she is the same “Mary” who comes with spices, she may have come <b>expecting death to be final</b> (implied by bringing spices, <b>Mark 16:1</b>), but the text does not condemn her; it highlights the disciples’ slowness to believe more broadly (cf. <b>Luke 24:25</b>).</li>
</ul>
<div><b><h2>Motives / fears / desires (careful inference)</h2></b></div>
<ul>
<li><b>Desire:</b> to remain with Jesus and honor Him even in humiliation (<b>John 19:25; Mark 16:1</b>).</li>
<li><b>Possible fear:</b> real social/political danger (standing near an executed “King of the Jews,” <b>John 19:19–20</b>), yet she remains present (<b>John 19:25</b>).</li>
</ul>
<div><b><h2>What changes (or doesn’t) over time?</h2></b></div>
<ul>
<li>The consistent pattern (as far as we can see) is <b>presence and perseverance</b>: near the cross (<b>John 19:25</b>), near the tomb (<b>Mark 15:47</b>), and at the tomb again (<b>Mark 16:1</b>). Her discipleship appears steady rather than dramatic.</li>
</ul>
<div><br></div>
<div><b><h2>7) Victories and Challenges</h2></b></div>
<div><b><h2>Major “wins” (obedience, influence, faithfulness)</h2></b></div>
<ul>
<li><b>Faithful witness at the cross</b> (<b>John 19:25</b>) — she does not abandon Jesus in His lowest earthly hour.</li>
<li><b>(Probable) First-wave resurrection witness/reporting</b> (<b>Luke 24:10</b>) — participation in the earliest testimony chain.</li>
</ul>
<div><b><h2>Major challenges (suffering, opposition, doubt, cost)</h2></b></div>
<ul>
<li><b>The cost of public association with a condemned Messiah</b> (<b>John 19:25; 19:19–20</b>).</li>
<li><b>Grief and apparent defeat</b> at crucifixion and burial (<b>John 19:30; Mark 15:47</b>).</li>
<li><b>(Probable) Confusion and fear at the tomb</b> (women’s fear is emphasized in some accounts; <b>Mark 16:8</b> in the shorter ending tradition).</li>
</ul>
<div><br></div>
<div><b><h2>8) Essential Verses and Key Cross-References (organized by themes)</h2></b></div>
<div><b><h2>A) Faithful presence at the cross</h2></b></div>
<ul>
<li><b>John 19:25</b> — Mary of Clopas named near the cross.</li>
<li><b>Mark 15:40–41</b> — women watching from a distance; they followed and served Jesus.</li>
<li><b>Matthew 27:55–56</b> — women who followed from Galilee; includes “Mary the mother of James and Joseph.”</li>
</ul>
<div><b><h2>B) Burial and continuity of witness (probable)</h2></b></div>
<ul>
<li><b>Mark 15:47</b> — Mary sees where Jesus is laid.</li>
<li><b>Matthew 27:61</b> — Mary Magdalene and “the other Mary” sit opposite the tomb.</li>
</ul>
<div><b><h2>C) Devotion and resurrection witness (probable)</h2></b></div>
<ul>
<li><b>Mark 16:1</b> — Mary the mother of James brings spices.</li>
<li><b>Luke 24:10</b> — Mary the mother of James among those who report to the apostles.</li>
<li><b>Matthew 28:7–10</b> — women commissioned to tell the disciples; Jesus meets them.</li>
</ul>
<div><b><h2>D) Theological framing: costly discipleship and true kinship</h2></b></div>
<ul>
<li><b>Luke 8:1–3</b> — women who supported Jesus’ ministry (theme resonance).</li>
<li><b>Luke 23:49</b> — acquaintances and women who followed stand at a distance watching.</li>
<li><b>Mark 8:34–35</b> — take up the cross; losing life to save it (embodied at the crucifixion scene).</li>
<li><b>1 Corinthians 15:3–8</b> — resurrection testimony tradition (not naming her, but the broader framework of witnesses).</li>
</ul>

<div><br></div>
<div><b><h2>9) Theology and Big Themes</h2></b></div>
<ul>
<li><b>God sustains a faithful remnant at the darkest hour:</b> Even as leaders mock and disciples scatter, God preserves witnesses at the cross (<b>John 19:25; Mark 14:50</b>).</li>
<li><b>Discipleship expressed as presence, service, and perseverance:</b> The women’s “following” and “ministering” underscores that true discipleship is not only public preaching but faithful attachment to Christ in suffering (<b>Mark 15:40–41</b>).</li>
<li><b>The cross as the decisive center:</b> Her named placement at the crucifixion anchors her story in the atoning work of Christ (<b>John 19:25, 30</b>).</li>
<li><b>Witness and testimony:</b> The resurrection message is carried, in part, by women whose testimony was culturally discounted—highlighting God’s upside-down ways (<b>Luke 24:10–11</b>).</li>
<li><b>Human weakness and God’s mercy:</b> The burial spices (if linked) suggest limited expectation, yet God meets His people with revelation and commission (<b>Mark 16:1–7</b>).</li>
</ul>
<div><br></div>
<div><b><h2>10) How She Points Us to Christ</h2></b></div>
<ul>
<li><b>Gospel proximity:</b> Mary of Clopas points us not to herself but to <b>Christ crucified</b>—she is literally positioned in the narrative to keep our eyes fixed on Jesus’ saving work (<b>John 19:25–30</b>).</li>
<li><b>Pattern of faithful discipleship under the cross:</b> Her presence embodies Jesus’ call to cross-bearing discipleship (<b>Mark 8:34–35</b>) and anticipates the church’s witness that suffering does not negate faithfulness.</li>
<li><b>Resurrection witness theme (probable):</b> If she is among the women at the tomb and the messengers to the apostles (<b>Mark 16:1; Luke 24:10</b>), her life underlines that the risen Christ <b>creates witnesses</b> and sends them with good news (<b>Matthew 28:7–10</b>).</li>
<li><b>No independent typology required:</b> Scripture does not present her as a type (in the technical sense), but as a <b>historical witness</b> whose placement magnifies the Person and work of Jesus.</li>
</ul>
<div><br></div>
<div><b><h2>11) Lessons and Applications (tied to Scripture)</h2></b></div>
<ol>
<li><b>Faithfulness is often quiet presence, not spotlight leadership</b> (<b>John 19:25</b>).</li>
<li><b>Love for Christ persists when outcomes look like defeat</b> (<b>Mark 16:1</b>).</li>
<li><b>God’s kingdom advances through unlikely witnesses</b> (<b>Luke 24:10–11</b>).</li>
<li><b>Christian courage can be as simple—and costly—as being seen with Jesus</b> (<b>John 19:19–20, 25</b>).</li>
<li><b>Service is a real mark of discipleship</b> (<b>Mark 15:40–41</b>).</li>
<li><b>Grief doesn’t disqualify you from usefulness; God meets mourners with revelation</b> (<b>Matthew 28:5–7</b>).</li>
<li><b>Stay near the cross: spiritual clarity is found where Christ’s work is central</b> (<b>John 19:25–30</b>).</li>
<li><b>The church should honor the “hidden” faithful</b>—Scripture records names like hers for our instruction (<b>John 19:25; Romans 16:1–4</b> as a broader pattern of honoring coworkers).</li>
<li><b>Don’t underestimate the importance of continuity and eyewitness detail</b> (<b>Mark 15:47</b>)—God grounds faith in real events.</li>
<li><b>When fear and confusion hit, obey the message you’ve received</b> (women told “go, tell,” <b>Matthew 28:7–10</b>).</li>
</ol>
<div><b>Warnings to heed</b><br></div>
<ul>
<li>Don’t assume proximity to religious activity equals steadfastness; many fled (<b>Mark 14:50</b>), while some stayed (<b>John 19:25</b>).</li>
<li>Don’t dismiss “small” acts of devotion—God weaves them into redemptive history (<b>Mark 16:1</b>).</li>
</ul>
<div><b>Examples to imitate</b><br></div>
<ul>
<li>Presence with Christ in suffering (<b>John 19:25</b>)</li>
<li>Ongoing service and support (theme: <b>Mark 15:40–41</b>)</li>
<li>Willingness to carry and share hard news and then good news (<b>Luke 24:10</b>)</li>
</ul>
<div>Applications to key areas:</div>
<ul>
<li><b>Personal holiness:</b> remain near Christ when temptation is to self-protect (<b>John 19:25</b>).</li>
<li><b>Relationships:</b> faithful companionship in suffering is love’s maturity (<b>John 19:25</b>).</li>
<li><b>Prayer:</b> bring grief honestly; expect God’s gospel comfort (<b>Matthew 28:5–7</b>).</li>
<li><b>Suffering:</b> your “staying” may be your most Christlike act (<b>Mark 8:34–35; John 19:25</b>).</li>
<li><b>Decision-making:</b> choose allegiance to Jesus over reputation (<b>John 19:19–20, 25</b>).</li>
<li><b>Leadership:</b> influence is not always formal office; witness is leadership (<b>Luke 24:10</b>).</li>
</ul>

<div><b><h2>12) Discussion and Reflection Questions (10–20)</h2></b></div>
<div><b><h2>Personal reflection</h2></b></div>
<ol>
<li>What does it cost Mary to stand near the cross (<b>John 19:25</b>)? What does it cost us to be publicly identified with Jesus?</li>
<li>Where do you tend to “flee” when discipleship becomes costly (contrast <b>Mark 14:50</b>)?</li>
<li>If Mary is also among the women with spices (<b>Mark 16:1</b>), what does her devotion teach you about loving Christ when you don’t understand God’s plan?</li>
</ol>
<div><b><h2>Small group discussion</h2></b></div>
<ol>
<li>Why do the Gospels repeatedly name and remember these women witnesses (<b>Mark 15:40–41; John 19:25</b>)?</li>
<li>What do you learn about God’s ways from the apostles initially doubting the women’s report (<b>Luke 24:10–11</b>)?</li>
<li>How can churches better honor the “Mary of Clopas” types—steady, quiet servants?</li>
</ol>
<div><b><h2>Deep study (text observation)</h2></b></div>
<ol>
<li>Compare John’s “near the cross” scene (<b>John 19:25</b>) with Mark’s “watching from a distance” (<b>Mark 15:40</b>). What might each evangelist emphasize?</li>
<li>Trace the repeated identifiers: “Mary of Clopas” (<b>John 19:25</b>), “mother of James and Joses” (<b>Mark 15:40</b>), “mother of James” (<b>Luke 24:10</b>). What is certain, and what is inferential?</li>
<li>What role does eyewitness continuity play in Mark’s burial account (<b>Mark 15:47</b>) and the empty tomb narrative (<b>Mark 16:1–6</b>)?</li>
<li>How does <b>Mark 8:34–35</b> illuminate the cost implicit in <b>John 19:25</b>?</li>
</ol>
<div><b><h2>Application</h2></b></div>
<ol>
<li>Who in your life needs your faithful presence more than your advice right now?</li>
<li>What simple act of service could be your “staying near the cross” this week?</li>
<li>Where have you dismissed someone’s testimony because of bias (echo <b>Luke 24:11</b>)?</li>
<li>How does the cross reshape your definition of “success” in discipleship (<b>John 19:30</b>)?</li>
<li>What would it look like for your small group to practice “burial spices” devotion—loving acts when hope feels thin (<b>Mark 16:1</b>)?</li>
</ol>
