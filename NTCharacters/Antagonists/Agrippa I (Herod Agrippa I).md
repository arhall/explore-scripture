<div><b><h1>Agrippa I (Herod Agrippa I)</h1></b></div>
<div><b><h1><br></h1></b></div>
<div><b><h1>Agrippa I (Herod Agrippa I) — Bible Character Study</h1></b></div>
<div><b><h2>1) Story Summary (chronological)</h2></b></div>
<div>Agrippa I appears in Scripture as <b>“Herod the king”</b> who persecutes the Jerusalem church during the early years of Acts. His story begins when he <b>lays violent hands on believers</b>, executing <b>James the brother of John</b> and then imprisoning <b>Peter</b> to win public approval (Acts 12:1–4). While Peter is held under heavy guard, the church prays earnestly, and an angel delivers Peter from prison in a way that exposes the limits of Herod’s power (Acts 12:5–11).</div>
<div>After Peter’s escape, Herod investigates, <b>orders the guards executed</b>, and leaves Judea for Caesarea (Acts 12:18–19). There he becomes embroiled in a political/economic conflict with <b>Tyre and Sidon</b>; they seek peace through intermediaries, including <b>Blastus</b>, Herod’s chamberlain (Acts 12:20). At a public address, the crowd acclaims Herod with divine honors—“the voice of a god”—and <b>because he does not give God the glory</b>, the Lord strikes him down and he dies (Acts 12:21–23). The narrative ends with a sharp contrast: <b>“But the word of God increased and multiplied”</b> (Acts 12:24).</div>
<div><b>Important clarity:</b> The “Agrippa” who appears later with Paul is <b>Agrippa II</b> (Acts 25–26), a different ruler.</div>
<div><b>Where his story begins and ends in the Bible:</b> <b>Begins</b> Acts 12:1; <b>ends</b> Acts 12:23 (with a theological epilogue in Acts 12:24).</div>
<div><br></div>
<div><b><h2>2) What He’s Known For</h2></b></div>
<div>Agrippa I is most known in Scripture for <b>persecuting the church</b> and for a public, prideful moment in which he <b>accepts divine praise</b> and is judged by God.</div>
<div><b>Headline descriptors (3–7):</b><br></div>
<ul>
<li><b>Royal persecutor</b> of the Jerusalem church (Acts 12:1–3)</li>
<li><b>Executioner of James</b> the apostle (Acts 12:2)</li>
<li><b>Imprisoner of Peter</b>, seeking a public trial/execution (Acts 12:3–4)</li>
<li><b>Politically driven</b> ruler courting popular approval (Acts 12:3)</li>
<li><b>Recipient of blasphemous acclaim</b> who fails to glorify God (Acts 12:22–23)</li>
<li><b>Suddenly judged</b> by the Lord in the height of public honor (Acts 12:23)</li>
</ul>
<div><br></div>
<div><b><h2>3) Where He Appears in the Bible</h2></b></div>
<div><b><h2>Primary narrative locations</h2></b></div>
<ul>
<li><b>Acts — 12:1–23</b> — Persecutes believers; kills James; imprisons Peter; later receives divine honors and is struck down.</li>
<li><b>Acts — 12:24</b> — Narrative contrast: God’s word advances despite his opposition.</li>
</ul>
<div><b><h2>Secondary references (contextual / related figures)</h2></b></div>
<ul>
<li><b>Acts — 25:13–26:32</b> — <b>Agrippa II</b>, not Agrippa I, hears Paul’s defense (helps distinguish identities).</li>
<li><b>Matthew/Mark/Luke/John — various</b> — Other “Herods” appear (e.g., Herod the Great; Herod Antipas), underscoring the need to identify which Herod is in view (cf. Acts 12:1).</li>
</ul>
<div><br></div>
<div><b><h2>4) Vital Statistics</h2></b></div>
<ul>
<li><b>Historical setting (as Scripture indicates):</b> Early church era after Jesus’ ascension; apostolic mission expanding; persecution from both religious and political authorities (Acts 12:1; cf. Acts 1–11).</li>
<ul>
<li><b>Extra-biblical note (labelled uncertain from Scripture alone):</b> Many date Agrippa I’s death to the early 40s AD, but Acts does not provide a calendar year (Acts 12:23).</li>
</ul>
<li><b>Geography (strongly associated):</b><br></li>
<ul>
<li><b>Jerusalem/Judea</b> — persecution, imprisonment, public pressure during feast days (Acts 12:1–4).</li>
<li><b>Caesarea</b> — seat of power where he relocates and dies (Acts 12:19–23).</li>
<li><b>Tyre and Sidon</b> — political/economic dispute resolved through diplomacy (Acts 12:20).</li>
</ul>
<li><b>Family and relationships (explicit in Scripture):</b><br></li>
<ul>
<li><b>Allies / beneficiaries:</b> those who praise him publicly (Acts 12:22).</li>
<li><b>Intermediary:</b> <b>Blastus</b>, his chamberlain (Acts 12:20).</li>
<li><b>Opponents / targets:</b> the Jerusalem church, <b>James</b>, <b>Peter</b> (Acts 12:1–4).</li>
<li><b>Soldiers/guards:</b> instruments of his power, later punished (Acts 12:4, 18–19).</li>
</ul>
<li><b>Roles/offices:</b> <b>King/ruler</b> (“Herod the king”) wielding civil authority and coercive force (Acts 12:1, 4, 19–21).</li>
<li><b>Key turning points:</b><br></li>
<ul>
<li>Initiates persecution and kills James (Acts 12:1–2).</li>
<li>Imprisons Peter intending public spectacle (Acts 12:3–4).</li>
<li>Peter’s divine deliverance undermines Herod’s control (Acts 12:5–11).</li>
<li>Public acclaim + failure to glorify God leads to judgment (Acts 12:21–23).</li>
</ul>
</ul>

<div><b><h2>6) Strengths, Weaknesses, and Heart-Level Patterns</h2></b></div>
<div><b><h2>Strengths (evidenced, carefully)</h2></b></div>
<div>Scripture gives <b>few “virtues”</b> to commend; what it does show are capacities that function as <i>natural strengths</i> (not spiritual maturity):</div>
<ul>
<li><b>Decisive exercise of authority</b> (for evil ends): he acts quickly and effectively to arrest and execute (Acts 12:2–4).</li>
<li><b>Political instinct for public approval:</b> he recognizes what “pleases the Jews” and capitalizes on it (Acts 12:3).</li>
<li><b>Public presence / oratory setting:</b> he appears in royal apparel and addresses people publicly (Acts 12:21).</li>
</ul>
<div><b><h2>Weaknesses / sins / blind spots (clear in text)</h2></b></div>
<ul>
<li><b>Violence against God’s people:</b> “laid violent hands” on the church (Acts 12:1).</li>
<li><b>Bloodguilt / injustice:</b> execution of James (Acts 12:2).</li>
<li><b>Fear of man / craving approval:</b> he escalates persecution because it “pleased the Jews” (Acts 12:3).</li>
<li><b>Presumption of control over God’s work:</b> he intends a public display with Peter (Acts 12:4), yet God overturns it (Acts 12:6–11).</li>
<li><b>Harsh retaliation:</b> executes the guards after Peter’s escape (Acts 12:19).</li>
<li><b>Pride and sacrilege:</b> accepts divine acclaim and fails to glorify God (Acts 12:22–23).</li>
</ul>
<div><b><h2>Motives / fears / desires (careful inferences anchored to text)</h2></b></div>
<ul>
<li><b>Desire for popular legitimacy:</b> inferred from the explicit note that James’s death “pleased the Jews,” prompting further action (Acts 12:3).</li>
<li><b>Desire for public spectacle and control:</b> inferred from waiting to bring Peter out “after the Passover” (likely for maximum visibility) (Acts 12:4).</li>
<li><b>Desire for honor bordering on divinity:</b> inferred from receiving the crowd’s “voice of a god” without correction (Acts 12:22–23).</li>
</ul>
<div><b><h2>What changes (or doesn’t) over time?</h2></b></div>
<ul>
<li><b>What doesn’t change:</b> He consistently operates by <b>human power + public approval</b>, not humility before God (Acts 12:3–4, 21–23).</li>
<li><b>What changes:</b> His apparent control is progressively <b>exposed as fragile</b>—first by Peter’s deliverance (Acts 12:6–11), then by his own sudden judgment (Acts 12:23).</li>
</ul>
<div><br></div>
<div><b><h2>7) Victories and Challenges</h2></b></div>
<div><b><h2>Major “wins” (as the narrative reports his actions—often evil)</h2></b></div>
<ul>
<li><b>Persecuting the church effectively (temporarily):</b> arrests and kills James; arrests Peter (Acts 12:1–4).</li>
<li><b>Political leverage over Tyre/Sidon:</b> they seek peace because their region depends on his favor (Acts 12:20).</li>
</ul>
<div>These are not moral victories; Acts frames them as opposition to God’s people and subject to God’s overruling (Acts 12:5, 24).</div>
<div><b><h2>Major challenges (and consequences)</h2></b></div>
<ul>
<li><b>God answers the church’s prayers:</b> Peter’s escape shows Herod’s authority is not ultimate (Acts 12:5–11).</li>
<li><b>Public humiliation of thwarted plans:</b> “no little disturbance” follows (Acts 12:18).</li>
<li><b>Moral crisis at the height of acclaim:</b> he fails the test of glory, receiving worship-like praise (Acts 12:22–23).</li>
<li><b>Divine judgment:</b> struck down for not giving God glory (Acts 12:23).</li>
<li><b>Ultimate defeat:</b> the word of God increases (Acts 12:24).</li>
</ul>
<div><b>Cost of compromise / consequences:</b> His pursuit of approval and glory culminates in death and serves as a warning that God will not share His glory with rulers who exalt themselves (Acts 12:23).</div>

<div><b><h2>9) Theology and Big Themes</h2></b></div>
<ul>
<li><b>God’s sovereignty over rulers:</b> A king can imprison, but he cannot imprison the Lord’s purposes (Acts 12:4–11; cf. Psalm 2:1–4).</li>
<li><b>The church as a praying community:</b> The narrative highlights corporate, earnest prayer as central to endurance and deliverance (Acts 12:5).</li>
<li><b>Suffering in mission is real and mysterious:</b> James dies (Acts 12:2) while Peter is delivered (Acts 12:7–11). Acts does not explain “why” in the moment; it shows God is wise and present in both outcomes.</li>
<li><b>God’s jealousy for His glory:</b> Accepting divine honors is not a small error; it is an assault on God’s uniqueness (Acts 12:22–23; cf. Isaiah 42:8).</li>
<li><b>Judgment as a testimony:</b> Herod’s downfall functions as a public sign that God vindicates His name and protects His redemptive plan (Acts 12:23–24).</li>
<li><b>Kingdom conflict:</b> Herod’s “kingdom” clashes with Christ’s kingdom; Acts repeatedly shows Christ’s reign advancing through witnesses, not swords (Acts 12:24; cf. Acts 1:8).</li>
</ul>
<div><br></div>
<div><b><h2>10) How He Points Us to Christ</h2></b></div>
<div>Agrippa I points to Christ mostly by <b>contrast</b>, highlighting what true kingship is—and isn’t.</div>
<ul>
<li><b>False king vs. true King:</b> Agrippa uses power to crush witnesses; Jesus uses power to save and to establish a people who bear witness (Acts 12:1–4; cf. Luke 19:10).</li>
<li><b>Seeking glory vs. giving glory:</b> Agrippa receives worship-like praise and is judged (Acts 12:22–23). Jesus receives rightful worship and shares the Father’s glory without robbery (John 17:5; Philippians 2:9–11).</li>
<li><b>Persecuting the body vs. shepherding the flock:</b> Agrippa scatters through violence (Acts 12:1–2). Christ gathers and guards His flock, even through death (John 10:11–15, 27–29).</li>
<li><b>The unstoppable mission of Christ:</b> Herod’s power ends in death, while the gospel grows (Acts 12:23–24). This echoes Jesus’ promise that the church will endure because He builds it (Matthew 16:18).</li>
</ul>
<div><br></div>
<div><b><h2>11) Lessons and Applications (8–15, Scripture-tethered)</h2></b></div>
<ol>
<li><b>Political power is real, but never ultimate.</b> God can break chains in a moment (Acts 12:7).</li>
<li><b>Earnest prayer is not peripheral—it is frontline obedience.</b> The church prays “earnestly” (Acts 12:5).</li>
<li><b>God’s providence may look different for different saints.</b> James is martyred (Acts 12:2); Peter is rescued (Acts 12:7–11).</li>
<li><b>The fear of man produces injustice.</b> “It pleased the Jews,” so he proceeds (Acts 12:3).</li>
<li><b>Don’t confuse applause with approval.</b> Crowds can call a man “a god” and be profoundly wrong (Acts 12:22).</li>
<li><b>God will not share His glory.</b> Refusing to give God glory invites judgment (Acts 12:23; cf. Isaiah 42:8).</li>
<li><b>When human plans oppose God’s mission, they eventually collapse.</b> Herod’s plot fails; the word multiplies (Acts 12:4, 24).</li>
<li><b>The church should expect opposition and keep witnessing anyway.</b> Persecution is normal, not strange (Acts 12:1; cf. John 15:20).</li>
<li><b>Beware retaliatory harshness when your control is threatened.</b> Herod executes guards after Peter’s escape (Acts 12:19).</li>
<li><b>God can turn a persecutor’s story into a sermon.</b> Herod’s death becomes a narrative warning and magnifies God’s reign (Acts 12:23–24).</li>
</ol>
<div><b><h2>Warnings to heed</h2></b></div>
<ul>
<li><b>Craving approval</b> (Acts 12:3)</li>
<li><b>Accepting glory that belongs to God</b> (Acts 12:22–23)</li>
<li><b>Using authority to harm God’s people</b> (Acts 12:1–2)</li>
</ul>
<div><b><h2>Examples to imitate (in this narrative)</h2></b></div>
<div>Agrippa is not a model of godliness here; instead, imitate:</div>
<ul>
<li><b>The praying church</b> (Acts 12:5)</li>
<li><b>Peter’s simple obedience in deliverance</b> (Acts 12:7–11)</li>
</ul>

<div><b><h2>12) Discussion and Reflection Questions (10–20)</h2></b></div>
<div><b><h2>Personal reflection</h2></b></div>
<ol>
<li>Where do I see “it pleased the people” shaping my decisions (Acts 12:3)?</li>
<li>What kinds of applause am I tempted to accept that properly belongs to God (Acts 12:22–23)?</li>
<li>Do I treat prayer as essential, like the church in Acts 12:5, or as optional?</li>
<li>How do I respond when my plans are blocked—humility, or retaliation like Acts 12:19?</li>
</ol>
<div><b><h2>Small group discussion</h2></b></div>
<ol>
<li>Why might Acts place James’s death and Peter’s rescue in the same chapter (Acts 12:2, 7–11)?</li>
<li>What does this passage teach about God’s sovereignty and human responsibility?</li>
<li>How should Christians think about political power and persecution today (Acts 12:1–4)?</li>
<li>What do we learn about leadership by contrasting Agrippa and Jesus?</li>
</ol>
<div><b><h2>Deep study (text observation)</h2></b></div>
<ol>
<li>What details emphasize the strength of Herod’s security (Acts 12:4, 6)? Why include them?</li>
<li>What verbs describe the church’s posture in Acts 12:5?</li>
<li>Trace the movement of locations (Jerusalem → Caesarea). How does setting shape the narrative (Acts 12:1, 19–23)?</li>
<li>What is the theological “but” in Acts 12:24 doing in the story?</li>
<li>What exactly is Herod judged for in Acts 12:23 (note the stated reason)?</li>
<li>How does this passage define true “success” (compare Acts 12:3–4 with Acts 12:24)?</li>
</ol>

<div><b><h2>13) Prayer Response</h2></b></div>
<div>Lord God, You alone are King and You will not give Your glory to another (Isaiah 42:8). Forgive us for fearing people, craving approval, and chasing applause like Herod (Acts 12:3, 22). Make us a praying people—earnest, united, and dependent on You (Acts 12:5). Help us trust Your wise providence whether You deliver us as You delivered Peter or call us to suffer as James suffered (Acts 12:2, 7–11). Keep us humble, faithful, and bold in witness, and cause Your word to increase and multiply in our lives and churches (Acts 12:24). Through Jesus Christ our true King, amen.</div>
