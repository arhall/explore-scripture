<div><b><h1>Agrippa II (Herod Agrippa II)</h1></b></div>
<div><b><h1><br></h1></b></div>
<div><b><h1>Agrippa II — Bible Character Study (Herod Agrippa II)</h1></b></div>
<div><b><h2>1) Story Summary (Chronological)</h2></b></div>
<div>Herod Agrippa II enters the biblical narrative late in Acts, during Paul’s custody at Caesarea after Paul’s arrest in Jerusalem. Agrippa II, accompanied by his sister Bernice, visits the new Roman governor Porcius Festus. Festus is troubled by Paul’s case—especially because Paul has appealed to Caesar and Festus must send an explanatory report to Rome—so he welcomes Agrippa’s interest and invites him to hear Paul. (Acts 25:13–27)</div>
<div>Agrippa II presides (with Festus) over a formal hearing where Paul is permitted to speak in his defense. Paul recounts his Pharisaic background, the hope of Israel, and Christ’s resurrection, then narrates his conversion and commission to preach repentance and faith. (Acts 26:1–23) Paul addresses Agrippa directly, assuming Agrippa’s familiarity with Jewish customs and the prophets. (Acts 26:2–7, 26:27)</div>
<div>Agrippa’s most remembered line is his response to Paul’s appeal—often rendered, “In a short time would you persuade me to be a Christian?” (translation varies). (Acts 26:28) After the hearing, Agrippa and Festus privately conclude Paul has done nothing deserving death or imprisonment—and Agrippa notes Paul could have been released if he had not appealed to Caesar. (Acts 26:30–32) That is the end of Agrippa II’s appearance in Scripture.</div>
<div><b>Where his story begins/ends in the Bible:</b>Begins at his arrival in Caesarea (Acts 25:13). Ends after the verdict conversation with Festus (Acts 26:32).</div>
<div><b>Note:</b> Agrippa II is distinct from <b>Herod Agrippa I</b> (the king of Acts 12:1–23). Agrippa II appears in Acts 25–26.</div>
<div><br></div>
<div><b><h2>2) What They’re Known For</h2></b></div>
<div>Agrippa II is most known as the Herodian “king” who heard Paul’s defense and reacted with near-persuasion but no recorded repentance. (Acts 26:28)</div>
<div><b>Headline descriptors</b><br></div>
<ul>
<li>Herodian ruler called “king” in Acts (Acts 25:13, 26:27)</li>
<li>Public listener to Paul’s testimony and gospel defense (Acts 26:1–23)</li>
<li>“Expert” (as Paul assumes) in Jewish customs/controversies (Acts 26:2–3)</li>
<li>Personally confronted with the prophets and the resurrection claim (Acts 26:6–8, 26:27)</li>
<li>Nearly persuaded / evasively responsive (Acts 26:28)</li>
<li>Declares Paul not guilty of capital crime (Acts 26:31)</li>
<li>Illustrates how political power can coexist with spiritual indecision (Acts 26:28, 26:32)</li>
</ul>
<div><br></div>
<div><b><h2>3) Where They Appear in the Bible</h2></b></div>
<div><b><h2>Primary narrative locations</h2></b></div>
<ul>
<li><b>Acts — 25:13–27</b> — Agrippa and Bernice visit Festus; Festus explains Paul’s case and Paul’s appeal to Caesar.</li>
<li><b>Acts — 26:1–32</b> — Agrippa hears Paul; reacts; later declares Paul undeserving of death/imprisonment.</li>
</ul>
<div><b><h2>Secondary references (family connections adjacent in Acts)</h2></b></div>
<ul>
<li><b>Acts — 12:1–23</b> — Herod Agrippa I (likely Agrippa II’s father) persecutes the church and dies under judgment (Agrippa II is not named here, but the dynasty context matters).</li>
<li><b>Acts — 24:24</b> — Drusilla (a Herodian woman, identified as Felix’s wife) hears Paul with Felix; Scripture does not explicitly name her relationship here, though she is part of the Herodian web around Paul’s trials.</li>
</ul>
<div><br></div>
<div><b><h2>4) Vital Statistics</h2></b></div>
<div><b>Historical setting (as Scripture indicates):</b><br></div>
<ul>
<li>Occurs during Paul’s custody under Roman administration at Caesarea, involving the governor Festus and a Herodian king. (Acts 24:27–25:13) Scripture does not give exact dates.</li>
</ul>
<div><b>Geography (strongly associated):</b><br></div>
<ul>
<li><b>Caesarea</b> — where Agrippa arrives, hears Paul, and confers with Festus. (Acts 25:13, 25:23, 26:30)</li>
</ul>
<div><b>Family and relationships (explicit in Scripture):</b><br></div>
<ul>
<li><b>Sister:</b> <b>Bernice</b> accompanies him. (Acts 25:13, 25:23)</li>
<li><b>Father (very likely):</b> <b>Herod Agrippa I</b> is the prominent earlier “Herod” titled “king” in Acts. Scripture doesn’t explicitly say “father,” so treat as historical background rather than a textual assertion. (Acts 12:1)</li>
</ul>
<div><b>Allies / associates:</b><br></div>
<ul>
<li><b>Porcius Festus</b> (Roman governor) hosts him and seeks counsel. (Acts 25:13–22)</li>
<li>Military tribunes/“principal men” present at the hearing. (Acts 25:23)</li>
</ul>
<div><b>Opponents / tensions:</b><br></div>
<ul>
<li>Jewish leadership in Jerusalem presses accusations against Paul, which Festus reports to Agrippa. (Acts 25:2–3, 25:15)</li>
</ul>
<div><b>Roles/offices:</b><br></div>
<ul>
<li>Called <b>“king”</b> (Greek: basileus) in the narrative. (Acts 25:13, 26:27)</li>
<li>Functions as a politically significant regional ruler whose opinion assists Roman administration. (Acts 25:22–27)</li>
</ul>
<div><b>Key turning points (in his biblical arc):</b><br></div>
<ul>
<li>Chooses to <b>hear Paul</b> personally. (Acts 25:22)</li>
<li>Is <b>directly challenged</b> about belief in the prophets and the gospel claim. (Acts 26:27–29)</li>
<li>Gives a verdict-level assessment: Paul is not guilty of death or imprisonment. (Acts 26:31–32)</li>
</ul>

<div><b><h2>6) Strengths, Weaknesses, and Heart-Level Patterns</h2></b></div>
<div><b><h2>Strengths (evidenced in the text)</h2></b></div>
<ul>
<li><b>Willing to listen carefully to a disputed case</b>: Agrippa asks to hear Paul and then listens through a lengthy defense. (Acts 25:22; Acts 26:1–23)</li>
<li><b>Informed engagement with Jewish questions (as Paul assumes)</b>: Paul explicitly says Agrippa is knowledgeable in “all the customs and controversies of the Jews.” (Acts 26:3)</li>
<li><b>Able to render a sober judgment about innocence</b>: He agrees Paul has done nothing deserving death or imprisonment. (Acts 26:31–32)</li>
</ul>
<div><b><h2>Weaknesses / blind spots (with textual anchors)</h2></b></div>
<ul>
<li><b>Spiritual evasion under conviction</b>: Agrippa is directly asked about belief in the prophets (Acts 26:27) and responds with an ambiguous, distancing line rather than confession or repentance. (Acts 26:28)</li>
<li><b>Comfort with religious talk without decisive commitment</b>: He can host a hearing, discuss prophets, and recognize innocence (Acts 26:3, 26:31), yet no response of faith is recorded (contrast Paul’s explicit call to repentance, Acts 26:19–20).</li>
</ul>
<div><b><h2>Motives / fears / desires (careful inferences anchored to the scene)</h2></b></div>
<ul>
<li><b>Desire for political prudence and social safety:</b> The setting is public, with Festus, commanders, and prominent men (Acts 25:23). In such a context, open identification with Paul’s gospel could be costly; Agrippa’s measured response (Acts 26:28) plausibly reflects caution. (Inference anchored to Acts 25:23; 26:28)</li>
<li><b>Curiosity without surrender:</b> “I myself would like to hear the man” (Acts 25:22) suggests interest; yet the narrative records no conversion. (Acts 25:22; 26:28)</li>
</ul>
<div><b><h2>What changes (or doesn’t) over time?</h2></b></div>
<div>Scripture gives only a single focused episode, so we cannot trace a long development arc. In this episode, Agrippa moves from <b>interest</b> (Acts 25:22) to <b>exposure and accountability</b> (Acts 26:27) to <b>non-committal response</b> (Acts 26:28) while still affirming Paul’s legal innocence. (Acts 26:31–32) The consistent pattern is: <b>he can evaluate, even appreciate, yet not yield</b>.</div>
<div><br></div>
<div><b><h2>7) Victories and Challenges</h2></b></div>
<div><b><h2>Major “wins” (influence/decisions with references)</h2></b></div>
<ul>
<li><b>Provides Paul a platform before elites</b> (even if unintentionally): his desire to hear Paul results in a major public testimony. (Acts 25:22–23; 26:1)</li>
<li><b>Affirms Paul’s innocence</b>: a significant moral/legal conclusion in the narrative. (Acts 26:31–32)</li>
</ul>
<div><b><h2>Major challenges (temptations/failures/opposition)</h2></b></div>
<ul>
<li><b>Confronted with the prophets and resurrection, yet stops short</b>: Paul presses him directly (Acts 26:27); Agrippa’s response deflects rather than confesses. (Acts 26:28)</li>
<li><b>Political constraints and the machinery of power</b>: Even after recognizing innocence, the situation proceeds toward Caesar because of Paul’s appeal. (Acts 26:32)</li>
</ul>
<div><b>Cost of faithfulness / consequences of compromise (as the passage frames it):</b><br></div>
<ul>
<li>Paul embodies costly faithfulness—chains and trials—while still testifying boldly. (Acts 26:29; cf. Acts 25:23)</li>
<li>Agrippa embodies the opposite danger: proximity to truth without conversion. (Acts 26:27–28)</li>
</ul>

<div><b><h2>9) Theology and Big Themes</h2></b></div>
<div><b><h2>God’s character revealed</h2></b></div>
<ul>
<li><b>Sovereign providence in witness:</b> God places Paul before rulers, turning legal entanglement into mission. (Acts 25:23; 26:1; cf. Acts 9:15)</li>
<li><b>Patient clarity:</b> The gospel is articulated plainly—resurrection, repentance, forgiveness—so rulers are not left in ignorance. (Acts 26:18–20, 26:22–23)</li>
</ul>
<div><b><h2>Kingdom, mission, and Scripture fulfillment</h2></b></div>
<ul>
<li><b>Continuity with Israel’s hope:</b> Paul frames the gospel as the fulfillment of the promises and prophetic hope, especially in resurrection. (Acts 26:6–8, 26:22–23)</li>
<li><b>Mission to Jews and Gentiles:</b> Paul’s commission explicitly includes opening eyes and bringing “light,” matching Luke-Acts’ outward movement. (Acts 26:18, 26:23)</li>
</ul>
<div><b><h2>Human nature: conviction vs. conversion</h2></b></div>
<ul>
<li><b>Exposure without surrender:</b> Agrippa’s story is a case study in the difference between being informed, intrigued, even stirred—and actually repenting and believing. (Acts 26:3, 26:27–28)</li>
</ul>
<div><br></div>
<div><b><h2>10) How Agrippa II Points Us to Christ</h2></b></div>
<div>Agrippa II is not a “type of Christ” in the positive sense; rather, he functions as a <b>mirror and contrast</b> that highlights Christ and the gospel.</div>
<ul>
<li><b>Christ’s lordship confronts earthly power:</b> A human “king” sits in judgment while Paul proclaims the risen King’s authority and commission. (Acts 26:13–18; cf. 1 Timothy 6:15)</li>
<li><b>The resurrection is the dividing line:</b> Paul centers everything on the risen Christ and Israel’s hope; Agrippa must decide whether that hope is credible. (Acts 26:6–8, 26:22–23)</li>
<li><b>Gospel clarity before the nations’ elites:</b> This hearing embodies Jesus’ promise that witnesses will stand before rulers for testimony. (Acts 25:23; 26:1; cf. Matthew 10:18)</li>
<li><b>The tragedy of “almost”:</b> Agrippa shows that proximity to truth is not salvation; the gospel demands repentance and faith. (Acts 26:18–20, 26:28) This pushes us toward the necessity of genuine response to Christ (Acts 17:30–31).</li>
</ul>
<div><br></div>
<div><b><h2>11) Lessons and Applications (8–15)</h2></b></div>
<ol>
<li><b>Curiosity about Christianity is not the same as conversion.</b> (Acts 25:22; 26:28)</li>
<li><b>Spiritual knowledge increases accountability.</b> Agrippa is treated as one who understands Jewish matters and prophets. (Acts 26:2–3, 26:27)</li>
<li><b>The resurrection is not a side issue; it is central.</b> Paul makes it the hinge of hope and credibility. (Acts 26:6–8, 26:22–23)</li>
<li><b>Public pressure can harden indecision.</b> The hearing is crowded with dignitaries and “pomp.” (Acts 25:23; inference applied to Acts 26:28)</li>
<li><b>God can turn political systems into pulpits.</b> Paul’s chained testimony reaches the powerful. (Acts 26:29; cf. Acts 25:23)</li>
<li><b>Repentance must show up in life.</b> Paul calls for deeds “in keeping with repentance.” (Acts 26:20)</li>
<li><b>You can affirm what’s right legally and still be wrong spiritually.</b> Agrippa affirms Paul’s innocence yet is not shown embracing Paul’s gospel. (Acts 26:31, 26:28)</li>
<li><b>Do not gamble with “almost.”</b> Agrippa’s near-persuasion warns against delay. (Acts 26:28)</li>
<li><b>Aim to persuade with humility and love.</b> Paul’s desire is that all become as he is—except chains. (Acts 26:29)</li>
<li><b>When you can’t see a clear “charge,” something is off.</b> Festus’ confusion highlights the difference between religious dispute and criminal guilt. (Acts 25:18–19, 25:26–27)</li>
<li><b>God’s mission includes the halls of power.</b> Christ said Paul would bear His name “before…kings.” (Acts 9:15; Acts 26:1–2)</li>
<li><b>Decision-making: don’t hide behind ambiguity.</b> Agrippa is pressed on prophets; evasive answers are spiritually perilous. (Acts 26:27–28)</li>
</ol>
<div><b>Warnings to heed</b><br></div>
<ul>
<li>“Almost persuaded” is still unpersuaded. (Acts 26:28)</li>
<li>Being informed is not being transformed. (Acts 26:3)</li>
</ul>
<div><b>Examples to imitate</b><br></div>
<ul>
<li>Patient listening and fair assessment of evidence (at least legally). (Acts 26:31)</li>
<li>Creating space for truthful speech (Agrippa allows Paul to speak). (Acts 26:1)</li>
</ul>

<div><b><h2>12) Discussion and Reflection Questions (10–20)</h2></b></div>
<div><b><h2>Personal reflection</h2></b></div>
<ol>
<li>Where do you see yourself in Agrippa: curious, informed, cautious, or evasive? (Acts 25:22; 26:28)</li>
<li>What “costs” might make you hesitate to openly follow Christ? (Acts 25:23; cf. Acts 26:28)</li>
<li>How do you respond when Scripture presses you toward a decision? (Acts 26:27–28)</li>
<li>Do you treat the resurrection as essential or optional? Why? (Acts 26:6–8)</li>
</ol>
<div><b><h2>Small group discussion</h2></b></div>
<ol>
<li>Why does Paul aim his appeal at the prophets with Agrippa? (Acts 26:27)</li>
<li>What’s the difference between being “not far” and being in the kingdom? (Acts 26:28; cf. Mark 12:34)</li>
<li>How can Christians speak persuasively without being manipulative—like Paul in Acts 26? (Acts 26:22–29)</li>
<li>What does Paul’s “except for these chains” teach about evangelism and suffering? (Acts 26:29)</li>
</ol>
<div><b><h2>Deep study (text observation)</h2></b></div>
<ol>
<li>List every way Paul ties his story to Israel’s hope in Acts 26. What phrases does he repeat? (Acts 26:6–8, 26:22–23)</li>
<li>What words describe the hearing’s atmosphere, and how might that shape responses? (Acts 25:23)</li>
<li>What exactly does Festus say he’s confused about, and what does that reveal about Christian claims? (Acts 25:18–20, 25:26–27)</li>
<li>How does Paul describe the content of his preaching after conversion? (Acts 26:19–20)</li>
<li>What reasons does Paul give that his message is not novel? (Acts 26:22–23)</li>
<li>What do Agrippa and Festus conclude legally—and what do they not conclude spiritually? (Acts 26:31–32, 26:28)</li>
</ol>
<div><b><h2>Application to leadership and decision-making</h2></b></div>
<ol>
<li>How should leaders handle cases where they don’t understand the religious issues involved? (Acts 25:26–27)</li>
<li>What’s a wise way to use influence to protect the innocent? (Acts 26:31–32)</li>
<li>Where might “procedure” become an excuse for avoiding moral clarity? (Acts 26:32)</li>
</ol>
