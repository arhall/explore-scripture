<div><h1>Mathew</h1></div>
<div><br></div>
<div><b><h1>Matthew (also called Levi) — Bible Character Study</h1></b></div>
<div><b>Note on name/spelling:</b> You wrote <b>“Mathew.”</b> I’m treating this as <b>Matthew</b> (Greek: <i>Matthaios</i>), the disciple also called <b>Levi</b> in the call narrative (Mark 2:14; Luke 5:27). Where Scripture is not explicit (e.g., later-life details), I’ll label it <b>uncertain</b>.</div>
<div><br></div>
<div><b><h2>1) Story Summary (chronological)</h2></b></div>
<div>Matthew enters the biblical story as a <b>tax collector</b> in Galilee. Jesus calls him directly: “Follow me.” Matthew rises and follows immediately (Matt 9:9). Mark and Luke tell the same episode but call him <b>Levi</b>, seated at the tax booth (Mark 2:14; Luke 5:27–28). Soon after, Matthew/Levi hosts a <b>banquet</b> for Jesus, surrounded by “tax collectors and sinners,” provoking Pharisaic complaint; Jesus answers that he came to call sinners and cites God’s desire for mercy (Matt 9:10–13; Mark 2:15–17; Luke 5:29–32).</div>
<div>Matthew is then numbered among <b>the Twelve</b>—listed in the apostolic catalogs (Matt 10:2–4; Mark 3:16–19; Luke 6:13–16; Acts 1:13). Matthew records Jesus’ commissioning of the Twelve for mission in Matthew’s Gospel (Matt 10:5–42) and later the <b>Great Commission</b> to make disciples of all nations (Matt 28:18–20). After Jesus’ ascension, Matthew is present with the apostles in Jerusalem, devoted to prayer as they await the Spirit (Acts 1:12–14). Scripture does not narrate Matthew’s later ministry or death; those details are <b>uncertain</b> beyond the Bible.</div>
<div><b>Where his story begins in Scripture:</b> the call from the tax booth (Matt 9:9; Mark 2:14; Luke 5:27).<b>Where it ends in Scripture:</b> listed among the apostles after the ascension (Acts 1:13), with no further individual narrative.</div>
<div><br></div>
<div><b><h2>2) What He’s Known For</h2></b></div>
<div>Matthew is most known for being a <b>tax collector</b> called by Jesus into apostleship—an emblem of grace that reaches the socially despised—and for his place among <b>the Twelve</b> (Matt 9:9–13; Matt 10:2–4).</div>
<div><b>Headlines</b><br></div>
<ul>
<li><b>Tax collector turned disciple</b> (Matt 9:9; Luke 5:27–28)</li>
<li><b>Host of Jesus’ “table fellowship” with sinners</b> (Matt 9:10–13; Luke 5:29–32)</li>
<li><b>Named among the Twelve apostles</b> (Matt 10:2–4; Acts 1:13)</li>
<li><b>Living example of Jesus’ mission to call sinners</b> (Matt 9:12–13)</li>
<li><b>Linked to the Great Commission tradition</b> (Matt 28:18–20)</li>
<li><b>Associated (by church tradition) with authorship of “Matthew”</b> (<i>uncertain</i>—the text itself is formally anonymous; the canonical title is ancient but not a self-identification)</li>
</ul>
<div><br></div>
<div><b><h2>3) Where He Appears in the Bible</h2></b></div>
<div><b><h2>Primary narrative locations</h2></b></div>
<ul>
<li><b>Matthew — 9:9–13</b> — Jesus calls Matthew; meal with “tax collectors and sinners”; “I desire mercy” (Matt 9:9–13)</li>
<li><b>Matthew — 10:2–4</b> — Listed among the Twelve (Matt 10:2–4)</li>
<li><b>Mark — 2:14–17</b> — Same call scene; named <b>Levi</b>; meal; Jesus calls sinners (Mark 2:14–17)</li>
<li><b>Luke — 5:27–32</b> — Same call scene; named <b>Levi</b>; “left everything”; banquet (Luke 5:27–32)</li>
</ul>
<div><b><h2>Secondary references (lists/summaries)</h2></b></div>
<ul>
<li><b>Mark — 3:16–19</b> — Listed among the Twelve (Mark 3:16–19)</li>
<li><b>Luke — 6:13–16</b> — Listed among the Twelve (Luke 6:13–16)</li>
<li><b>Acts — 1:13–14</b> — Listed among apostles in Jerusalem after ascension; gathered in prayer (Acts 1:13–14)</li>
</ul>
<div><br></div>
<div><b><h2>4) Vital Statistics</h2></b></div>
<ul>
<li><b>Historical setting:</b> Early 1st century, Roman rule in Judea/Galilee; Jesus’ public ministry and the early apostolic community (implicit across the Gospels; Acts 1).</li>
<li><b>Geography strongly associated:</b><br></li>
<ul>
<li><b>Galilee</b> (tax booth context; call narrative occurs in Jesus’ Galilean ministry—Matt 9:9; Mark 2:14; Luke 5:27)</li>
<li><b>Jerusalem</b> (post-ascension gathering—Acts 1:12–14)</li>
</ul>
<li><b>Family/relationships:</b><br></li>
<ul>
<li><b>“Son of Alphaeus”</b> is attached to Levi in Mark’s call account (Mark 2:14). Scripture does not explicitly state Matthew’s wider family life.</li>
<li><b>Close relationships:</b> Jesus (Matt 9:9), fellow apostles (Matt 10:2–4; Acts 1:13).</li>
</ul>
<li><b>Roles/offices:</b><br></li>
<ul>
<li><b>Tax collector</b> (Matt 9:9)</li>
<li><b>Disciple/Apostle (one of the Twelve)</b> (Matt 10:2–4; Acts 1:13)</li>
</ul>
<li><b>Key turning points:</b><br></li>
<ul>
<li><b>Call:</b> “Follow me” → immediate response (Matt 9:9; Luke 5:28)</li>
<li><b>Public identification with Jesus:</b> hosting Jesus among the marginalized (Matt 9:10–13)</li>
<li><b>Commission into mission:</b> named among the Twelve (Matt 10:2–4) and included in the apostolic community awaiting the Spirit (Acts 1:13–14)</li>
</ul>
</ul>

<div><b><h2>6) Strengths, Weaknesses, and Heart-Level Patterns</h2></b></div>
<div><b><h2>Strengths (with textual evidence)</h2></b></div>
<ul>
<li><b>Responsive obedience:</b> He rises and follows immediately when called (Matt 9:9; Luke 5:28).</li>
<li><b>Bold identification with Jesus:</b> He hosts Jesus at a meal that draws criticism (Matt 9:10–11; Luke 5:29–30).</li>
<li><b>Missional hospitality:</b> He leverages his relationships to bring “tax collectors and others” near Jesus (Luke 5:29).</li>
<li><b>Persevering fellowship and prayer:</b> Continues with the apostolic community after the ascension (Acts 1:13–14).</li>
</ul>
<div><b><h2>Weaknesses / sins / blind spots (careful, text-anchored)</h2></b></div>
<ul>
<li><b>Prior life bound to a compromised system:</b> Tax collectors were commonly associated with exploitation and collaboration; Scripture doesn’t narrate Matthew’s specific abuses, but his occupation places him among those publicly regarded as “sinners” (Matt 9:10–13; Luke 5:30).</li>
<li><b>Potential social and spiritual baggage:</b> The Pharisaic criticism implies Matthew’s old identity carried stigma that had to be surrendered and reinterpreted under Jesus’ call (Matt 9:11–13).</li>
</ul>
<div><b><h2>Motives / fears / desires (inferred cautiously)</h2></b></div>
<ul>
<li><b>Desire for belonging and mercy:</b> Hosting a banquet suggests an impulse to gather people around Jesus and to step into a new identity publicly (Luke 5:29).</li>
<li><b>Courage over reputation:</b> Following Jesus meant embracing shame from religious elites and likely loss of income/security (implied by leaving the booth; Luke 5:28; compare the critique in Luke 5:30).</li>
</ul>
<div><b><h2>What changes (or doesn’t) over time?</h2></b></div>
<ul>
<li><b>Changes:</b> From an identity defined by the tax booth to one defined by following Jesus (Matt 9:9). From social isolation/stigma to inclusion in the apostolic band (Matt 10:2–4).</li>
<li><b>Continuity:</b> Matthew remains “Matthew the tax collector” even in the apostle list (Matt 10:3), suggesting the past is not erased but redeemed—his story becomes a testimony of mercy.</li>
</ul>
<div><br></div>
<div><b><h2>7) Victories and Challenges</h2></b></div>
<div><b><h2>Major wins (faithfulness/influence)</h2></b></div>
<ul>
<li><b>Immediate discipleship response:</b> leaving to follow Jesus (Matt 9:9; Luke 5:28).</li>
<li><b>Public witness through table fellowship:</b> bringing sinners into contact with Jesus (Matt 9:10–13).</li>
<li><b>Apostolic calling affirmed:</b> counted among the Twelve (Matt 10:2–4).</li>
<li><b>Perseverance after ascension:</b> joined in unified prayer awaiting God’s promise (Acts 1:13–14).</li>
</ul>
<div><b><h2>Major challenges (cost/opposition)</h2></b></div>
<ul>
<li><b>Social contempt and religious opposition:</b> Pharisees question Jesus’ association with Matthew’s circle (Matt 9:11; Luke 5:30).</li>
<li><b>Cost of leaving a lucrative position:</b> Luke emphasizes he “left everything” (Luke 5:28).</li>
<li><b>Future suffering implied for apostles:</b> Jesus warns of persecution for his messengers (Matt 10:16–22), which would include Matthew as one of the sent ones (Matt 10:2–4).</li>
</ul>

</ul>

<ul>

<li><b>James 2:13</b> — mercy triumphs over judgment (theme resonance).</li>
</ul>
<div><br></div>
<div><b><h2>9) Theology and Big Themes in Matthew’s Life</h2></b></div>
<ul>
<li><b>God’s mercy toward the morally and socially “unclean.”</b> Jesus’ call of a tax collector shows divine mercy moving outward to the despised (Matt 9:9–13).</li>
<li><b>The purpose of Christ’s coming:</b> not affirming self-righteousness, but calling sinners to repentance (Matt 9:13; Luke 5:32).</li>
<li><b>Kingdom mission creates a new community:</b> Jesus forms a people around himself that includes the unlikely (Matt 10:2–4).</li>
<li><b>True righteousness is compatible with compassion:</b> Jesus rebukes a religion that guards status but neglects mercy (Matt 9:11–13; Hos 6:6).</li>
<li><b>The cost and authority of discipleship:</b> Matthew leaves his vocation and later stands among those sent under Jesus’ authority (Luke 5:28; Matt 28:18–20).</li>
<li><b>Waiting, prayer, and dependence:</b> after ascension, the apostles persist in prayer, emphasizing God’s initiative and empowerment (Acts 1:13–14).</li>
</ul>
<div><br></div>
<div><b><h2>10) How Matthew Points Us to Christ</h2></b></div>
<ul>
<li><b>Christ as the Seeker of sinners:</b> Matthew’s call embodies Jesus’ stated mission—physician for the sick, caller of sinners (Matt 9:12–13).</li>
<li><b>Christ fulfills God’s mercy-priority:</b> Jesus grounds his actions in Scripture (“I desire mercy…”), showing continuity with God’s revealed character (Matt 9:13; Hos 6:6).</li>
<li><b>Apostolic calling anticipates gospel proclamation:</b> Matthew’s inclusion among the Twelve previews Christ’s plan to spread the gospel through ordinary, transformed people (Matt 10:2–4; Matt 28:18–20; Acts 1:8).</li>
<li><b>Gospel pattern of repentance and restored identity:</b> “Matthew the tax collector” becomes “Matthew the apostle,” illustrating the grace that redefines a person without denying their past (Matt 10:3; cf. 1 Tim 1:12–16 for the same pattern in Paul).</li>
</ul>
<div><br></div>
<div><b><h2>11) Lessons and Applications (Scripture-tethered)</h2></b></div>
<ol>
<li><b>Jesus calls people from compromised pasts into holy futures</b> (Matt 9:9–13).</li>
<li><b>Obedience is often immediate, not incremental</b>—Matthew rises and follows (Matt 9:9; Luke 5:28).</li>
<li><b>Hospitality can be evangelism</b>—a table can become a mission field (Luke 5:29–32).</li>
<li><b>Expect criticism when mercy disrupts religious respectability</b> (Matt 9:11–13).</li>
<li><b>Don’t confuse sacrifice with righteousness</b>—God demands mercy-shaped obedience (Matt 9:13; Hos 6:6).</li>
<li><b>Your old identity can become a redeemed testimony</b> (“Matthew the tax collector,” Matt 10:3).</li>
<li><b>Following Jesus may cost security and income</b> (Luke 5:28).</li>
<li><b>Discipleship is communal</b>—Matthew is formed among the Twelve (Matt 10:2–4).</li>
<li><b>Mission includes suffering</b>—Jesus warns his sent ones (Matt 10:16–22).</li>
<li><b>Prayerful waiting is part of faithfulness</b>—Matthew persists with the apostles (Acts 1:13–14).</li>
<li><b>Jesus’ authority drives the church’s mission</b> (Matt 28:18–20).</li>
<li><b>Repentance is the appropriate response to mercy</b> (Luke 5:32).</li>
</ol>
<div><b>Warnings to heed</b><br></div>
<ul>
<li>The danger of <b>self-righteous distance</b> from “sinners” (Matt 9:11–13; Luke 18:9–14).</li>
<li>A religion that prizes <b>performance over compassion</b> (Hos 6:6; Matt 12:7).</li>
</ul>
<div><b>Examples to imitate</b><br></div>
<ul>
<li>Prompt obedience (Matt 9:9).</li>
<li>Courageous public alignment with Jesus (Luke 5:29).</li>
<li>Persevering prayer with the church (Acts 1:14).</li>
</ul>

<li>What “tax booth” (identity, security, sin pattern) might Jesus be calling you to leave? (Luke 5:28)</li>
<li>How do you respond when Jesus’ call threatens your stability or reputation? (Matt 9:9–11)</li>
<li>Do you believe Jesus genuinely calls sinners—<i>including you</i>—to himself? (Matt 9:13)</li>
<li>Where might hospitality become a concrete next step of witness? (Luke 5:29)</li>
<li>Are there people you avoid because they feel “unclean” or inconvenient? (Matt 9:10–13)</li>
</ol>
<div><b><h2>Small group discussion</h2></b></div>
<ol>
<li>Why do you think Jesus intentionally chose a tax collector as an apostle? (Matt 10:2–4)</li>
<li>How does Jesus’ “physician” metaphor reshape the church’s posture toward outsiders? (Matt 9:12)</li>
<li>What does “I desire mercy, and not sacrifice” confront in your community? (Matt 9:13; Hos 6:6)</li>
<li>How should Christians handle criticism that comes from practicing mercy? (Matt 9:11–13)</li>
</ol>
<div><b><h2>Deep study (text observation)</h2></b></div>
<ol>
<li>Compare Matt 9:9–13 with Mark 2:14–17 and Luke 5:27–32. What details does each author emphasize?</li>
<li>In Luke 5:28, what might “left everything” imply in context?</li>
<li>In Matt 10, what expectations does Jesus set for apostolic mission and suffering? (Matt 10:16–22)</li>
<li>In Acts 1:13–14, what practices mark the apostles between ascension and Pentecost?</li>
<li>How does Hosea 6:6 function in Jesus’ argument in Matthew? (Matt 9:13)</li>
</ol>
<div><b><h2>Christ-centered synthesis</h2></b></div>
<ol>
<li>How does Matthew’s calling scene illustrate the gospel message of grace leading to repentance? (Matt 9:13; Luke 5:32)</li>
<li>What does Matthew’s story teach about how Jesus forms leaders? (Matt 10:2–4)</li>
<li>How does the Great Commission connect to the kind of people Jesus calls? (Matt 28:18–20; Matt 9:9–13)</li>
</ol>
