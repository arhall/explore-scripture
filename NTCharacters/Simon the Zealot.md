<div><b><h1>Simon the Zealot </h1></b></div>
<div><b><h1><br></h1></b></div>
<div><b><h1>Simon the Zealot — Bible Character Study</h1></b></div>
<div><b>Identity note:</b> Simon the Zealot is <b>not</b> Simon Peter. Simon the Zealot is one of “the twelve apostles” listed in the Gospels and Acts (Matthew 10:2–4; Mark 3:16–19; Luke 6:13–16; Acts 1:13).</div>
<div><br></div>
<div><b><h2>1) Story Summary</h2></b></div>
<div><b>Where his story begins:</b> Simon first appears when Jesus <b>chooses and names the Twelve</b> and the apostolic lists record him as one of them (Matthew 10:1–4; Mark 3:13–19; Luke 6:12–16).</div>
<div><b>Chronological overview (as Scripture gives it):</b><br></div>
<ul>
<li><b>Chosen by Jesus as an apostle</b> after Jesus prays and then appoints twelve (Luke 6:12–16; Mark 3:13–19).</li>
<li><b>Named in the apostolic lists</b> as “Simon the Zealot” (Luke 6:15; Acts 1:13) and as “Simon the Cananaean/Cananaite” in Matthew and Mark (Matthew 10:4; Mark 3:18).</li>
<li><b>Remains with the apostolic band after the resurrection/ascension</b>, gathering in Jerusalem for prayer as they wait for the promised Spirit (Acts 1:13–14).</li>
</ul>
<div><b>Where his story ends (in Scripture):</b> His last explicit mention is in the <b>post-ascension list</b> of apostles in Jerusalem (Acts 1:13). After that, the New Testament does not clearly record his individual actions.</div>
<div><b>What Scripture does </b><b><i>not</i></b><b> give:</b> No recorded sayings, miracles, or individual episodes are directly attributed to Simon the Zealot by name.</div>
<div><br></div>
<div><b><h2>2) What He’s Known For</h2></b></div>
<div>Simon is most known for <b>his identifying descriptor</b>: <i>“the Zealot”</i> (Luke 6:15; Acts 1:13).</div>
<div><b>Headline descriptors (3–7):</b><br></div>
<ul>
<li><b>Chosen apostle of Jesus</b> (Luke 6:13–16)</li>
<li><b>“The Zealot” — marked by zeal</b> (Luke 6:15; Acts 1:13)</li>
<li><b>Listed among the Twelve across multiple NT books</b> (Matthew 10:4; Mark 3:18; Luke 6:15; Acts 1:13)</li>
<li><b>Part of the praying, waiting church in Jerusalem</b> (Acts 1:13–14)</li>
<li><b>A picture of Jesus forming a unified band from diverse backgrounds</b> (compare Matthew 10:4 with Matthew 9:9)</li>
</ul>
<div><br></div>
<div><b><h2>3) Where He Appears in the Bible</h2></b></div>
<div><b>Primary narrative locations (core story setting):</b><br></div>
<ul>
<li><b>Matthew — 10:2–4</b> — Named in the list of the Twelve; called “Simon the Cananaean” (Matthew 10:4).</li>
<li><b>Mark — 3:13–19</b> — Jesus appoints the Twelve; Simon listed; called “Simon the Cananaean” (Mark 3:18).</li>
<li><b>Luke — 6:12–16</b> — Jesus prays and chooses twelve; Simon listed as “Simon who was called the Zealot” (Luke 6:15).</li>
<li><b>Acts — 1:13–14</b> — Apostles gathered in Jerusalem after the ascension; Simon listed as “Simon the Zealot” and persevering in prayer (Acts 1:13–14).</li>
</ul>
<div><b>Secondary references (themes echoed though not naming him directly):</b><br></div>
<ul>
<li><b>Matthew — 28:16–20</b> — The (remaining) apostles commissioned to disciple the nations (Matthew 28:16–20). (Simon is not named here, but the commission is given to the apostolic band.)</li>
<li><b>Acts — 1:8</b> — Jesus promises power for worldwide witness (Acts 1:8). (Again, spoken to the apostles as a group.)</li>
</ul>
<div><br></div>
<div><b><h2>4) Vital Statistics</h2></b></div>
<ul>
<li><b>Historical setting:</b> Jesus’ public ministry through post-resurrection days leading up to Pentecost (Luke 6:12–16; Acts 1:13–14).</li>
<li><b>Geography associated (by context, not by explicit biography):</b><br></li>
<ul>
<li>Jesus’ ministry sphere: <b>Galilee/Judea</b> (general Gospel context; Luke 6:12–16).</li>
<li>Post-ascension: <b>Jerusalem</b> (Acts 1:12–14).</li>
</ul>
<li><b>Family and relationships:</b><br></li>
<ul>
<li><b>Parents, spouse, children:</b> not stated in Scripture.</li>
<li><b>Allies:</b> the Twelve / apostolic community (Luke 6:13–16; Acts 1:13–14).</li>
<li><b>Enemies:</b> not stated for him personally.</li>
</ul>
<li><b>Roles/offices:</b> <b>Disciple / Apostle</b> (Luke 6:13–16).</li>
<li><b>Key turning points (explicit in the text):</b><br></li>
<ul>
<li><b>Appointment as one of the Twelve</b> (Luke 6:13–16).</li>
<li><b>Persevering in united prayer while waiting for the Spirit</b> (Acts 1:13–14).</li>
</ul>
</ul>

<div><b><h2>6) Strengths, Weaknesses, and Heart-Level Patterns</h2></b></div>
<div><b><h2>Strengths (text-grounded)</h2></b></div>
<ul>
<li><b>Called and included by Christ</b> — Whatever his prior identity, Jesus intentionally appoints him among the Twelve (Luke 6:13–16).</li>
<li><b>Persevering unity and prayer</b> — Simon is with the apostles “with one accord” devoting themselves to prayer (Acts 1:13–14).</li>
</ul>
<div><b><h2>Weaknesses / sins / blind spots (what Scripture actually shows)</h2></b></div>
<ul>
<li><b>Scripture does not record any personal failure or rebuke</b> specific to Simon the Zealot by name.</li>
<li>The only descriptor we have (“Zealot”) invites caution: zeal can be godly or misdirected, but <b>the text does not narrate his inner life</b> (Luke 6:15; Acts 1:13). Any claim beyond that should be labeled as inference.</li>
</ul>
<div><b><h2>Motives / fears / desires (careful inference, anchored to text)</h2></b></div>
<ul>
<li><b>Possible inference:</b> The label “Zealot” likely indicates he was known for zeal—either temperamentally (“zealous”) or by association with zealous commitments (Luke 6:15; Acts 1:13).</li>
<ul>
<li>What we <i>can</i> say from Scripture: Jesus takes a man known for zeal and places him in a community shaped by Jesus’ teaching, mission, and prayer (Luke 6:13–16; Acts 1:13–14).</li>
</ul>
<li><b>Possible inference:</b> His presence alongside a former tax collector (Matthew) in the same apostolic list is a quiet testimony to Jesus creating unity across social/political divides (Matthew 10:4; Matthew 9:9).</li>
</ul>
<div><b><h2>What changes (or doesn’t) over time?</h2></b></div>
<ul>
<li><b>What we can observe:</b> He moves (at least by the time Acts opens) as part of a unified, praying apostolic community rather than as a standalone figure (Acts 1:13–14).</li>
<li><b>What we can’t track:</b> Scripture gives no “before/after” narrative scene for Simon the Zealot specifically; his transformation is suggested by <b>belonging to Jesus’ band</b>, but not individually narrated (Luke 6:13–16).</li>
</ul>
<div><br></div>
<div><b><h2>7) Victories and Challenges</h2></b></div>
<div><b><h2>Major “wins” (with references)</h2></b></div>
<ul>
<li><b>Faithful inclusion among the Twelve</b> — being chosen and sent as an apostle is itself a mark of grace and calling (Luke 6:13–16; Mark 3:13–19).</li>
<li><b>Persevering with the church in prayer</b> — present in Jerusalem, united, praying, waiting (Acts 1:13–14).</li>
</ul>
<div><b><h2>Major challenges (with references)</h2></b></div>
<ul>
<li><b>The cost of discipleship for any apostle</b> — While not unique to Simon by name, the Twelve are called into costly allegiance to Jesus (Luke 6:13–16; cf. the broader call to deny self and follow Christ, e.g., Luke 9:23).</li>
<li><b>Potential reordering of “zeal”</b> — If “the Zealot” indicates strong prior commitments, discipleship would require zeal to be submitted to Christ’s kingdom purposes (Acts 1:8; Acts 1:13–14). <i>(Inference, but consistent with the kingdom reorientation Jesus demands of all disciples.)</i><br></li>
</ul>
<div><br></div>
<div><b><h2>8) Essential Verses and Key Cross-References (Grouped by Themes)</h2></b></div>
<div>Because Simon is mostly mentioned in lists, “essential verses” necessarily include (1) the places he is named, and (2) key apostolic themes addressed to the apostolic band.</div>
<div><b><h2>A) Calling and Apostolic Identity</h2></b></div>
<ul>
<li>Luke 6:12–16 — Jesus prays, chooses twelve; Simon “called the Zealot.”</li>
<li>Mark 3:13–19 — Jesus appoints the Twelve; Simon listed.</li>
<li>Matthew 10:2–4 — The Twelve named; Simon “the Cananaean.”</li>
<li>Acts 1:13 — Apostles listed in Jerusalem; Simon “the Zealot.”</li>
</ul>
<div><b>Cross-references (apostolic calling and purpose):</b><br></div>
<ul>
<li>John 15:16 — “You did not choose me, but I chose you…” (to the disciples as a group).</li>
<li>Ephesians 2:19–20 — Church built on the foundation of apostles and prophets.</li>
</ul>
<div><b><h2>B) Unity, Prayer, and Waiting on God</h2></b></div>
<ul>
<li>Acts 1:13–14 — “With one accord…devoting themselves to prayer.”</li>
</ul>
<div><b>Cross-references:</b><br></div>
<ul>
<li>John 17:20–23 — Jesus prays for unity among his people.</li>
<li>Philippians 4:6–7 — Prayer replacing anxious striving.</li>
</ul>
<div><b><h2>C) Kingdom Mission Redirecting Human Zeal</h2></b></div>
<ul>
<li>Acts 1:8 — Power to be witnesses “to the end of the earth.”</li>
<li>Matthew 28:18–20 — Commission to disciple the nations (to the apostolic band).</li>
</ul>
<div><b>Cross-references:</b><br></div>
<ul>
<li>Romans 10:2–4 — Example of zeal needing true knowledge and Christ-centered righteousness.</li>
<li>John 18:36 — Jesus’ kingdom “not of this world” (reframing political expectations).</li>
</ul>
<div><b><h2>D) Jesus Calls People from Varied Backgrounds</h2></b></div>
<ul>
<li>Matthew 10:4 (Simon listed) + Matthew 9:9 (Matthew’s call) — Jesus forms one band from very different life stories.</li>
</ul>
<div><b>Cross-references:</b><br></div>
<ul>
<li>Galatians 3:28 — Unity in Christ across dividing lines.</li>
<li>Colossians 3:11–14 — New humanity in Christ marked by love.</li>
</ul>
<div><br></div>
<div><b><h2>9) Theology and Big Themes</h2></b></div>
<ul>
<li><b>Jesus’ sovereign calling and formation of leaders</b> — Simon’s significance is grounded in Jesus’ initiative: Jesus prays, chooses, appoints (Luke 6:12–16; Mark 3:13–19).</li>
<li><b>Kingdom reorientation</b> — Whatever “zeal” meant for Simon, the apostolic mission is global witness empowered by the Spirit, not merely local or political aims (Acts 1:8; Acts 1:13–14).</li>
<li><b>Unity in Christ</b> — The apostolic band is a community “with one accord,” suggesting that allegiance to Jesus can overcome deep differences (Acts 1:14; compare the diverse callings reflected in the lists, Matthew 10:2–4).</li>
<li><b>Ordinary faithfulness and hiddenness</b> — Simon’s lack of individual spotlight underscores how God often uses people whose faithfulness is real but not narrated in detail (Acts 1:13–14).</li>
</ul>
<div><br></div>
<div><b><h2>10) How Simon the Zealot Points Us to Christ</h2></b></div>
<ul>
<li><b>Christ gathers and transforms zeal</b> — Jesus does not merely recruit personalities; he <b>redirects hearts</b> into his kingdom mission (Luke 6:13–16; Acts 1:8).</li>
<li><b>Christ creates a reconciled community</b> — The same Jesus who calls Simon “the Zealot” also calls Matthew the tax collector (Matthew 10:4; Matthew 9:9), forming one apostolic family. This anticipates the gospel’s reconciling power (Ephesians 2:14–16).</li>
<li><b>Christ’s kingdom is not a partisan project</b> — Jesus’ kingship reframes expectations of power and victory (John 18:36). The apostles wait in prayer for the Spirit’s power rather than seizing control by human force (Acts 1:13–14; Acts 1:8).</li>
</ul>
<div><br></div>
<div><b><h2>11) Lessons and Applications (8–15)</h2></b></div>
<ol>
<li><b>Jesus calls whom he wills</b>—and your story’s center becomes his call, not your résumé (Luke 6:13–16).</li>
<li><b>Zeal must be sanctified</b>: passion is powerful, but it must be governed by Christ’s mission (Acts 1:8).</li>
<li><b>Faithfulness can be real even when hidden</b>—Simon’s quiet presence in Scripture still matters (Acts 1:13–14).</li>
<li><b>Prayer is the posture of waiting obedience</b> (Acts 1:14).</li>
<li><b>Christian unity is not natural—it’s Christ-made</b> (Acts 1:14; John 17:21).</li>
<li><b>Don’t absolutize earthly causes</b>: Jesus’ kingdom reorders priorities and methods (John 18:36; Acts 1:8).</li>
<li><b>God binds together unlikely people</b> in one mission (Matthew 10:2–4; Matthew 9:9).</li>
<li><b>The Spirit empowers witness</b> more than personality or intensity does (Acts 1:8).</li>
<li><b>Labels don’t have the final word</b>—Jesus renames identity around himself (Luke 6:15; cf. Luke 6:13).</li>
<li><b>Stay with the church</b> in the in-between seasons; unity and prayer are not optional extras (Acts 1:13–14).</li>
</ol>
<div><b>Warnings to heed</b><br></div>
<ul>
<li>Zeal without Christ’s truth and aims can misfire (Romans 10:2–4).</li>
<li>Replacing Spirit-powered mission with fleshly striving (Acts 1:8 vs. mere human strength).</li>
</ul>
<div><b>Examples to imitate</b><br></div>
<ul>
<li>Staying “with one accord” in prayer and obedience (Acts 1:14).</li>
</ul>
<div><br></div>
<div><b><h2>12) Discussion and Reflection Questions (10–20)</h2></b></div>
<div><b><h2>Personal reflection</h2></b></div>
<ol>
<li>What “labels” or defining descriptors do you carry, and how does Christ reframe them (Luke 6:15–16)?</li>
<li>Where do you need your zeal redirected toward Spirit-empowered witness (Acts 1:8)?</li>
<li>Are you more comfortable acting quickly or waiting prayerfully—and what does Acts 1:14 challenge in you?</li>
<li>What does it look like for you to be faithful even when no one notices (Acts 1:13–14)?</li>
</ol>
<div><b><h2>Small group discussion</h2></b></div>
<ol>
<li>Why do you think Scripture includes apostles like Simon with almost no narrated details (Luke 6:13–16)?</li>
<li>How does Jesus forming the Twelve speak to unity across social divisions (Matthew 10:2–4)?</li>
<li>What practices help a group stay “with one accord” (Acts 1:14)?</li>
<li>How do we distinguish kingdom mission from political or personal agendas (Acts 1:8; John 18:36)?</li>
</ol>
<div><b><h2>Deep study (text-observation)</h2></b></div>
<ol>
<li>What do Luke and Acts emphasize by using the phrase “who was called the Zealot” (Luke 6:15; Acts 1:13)?</li>
<li>Compare Matthew 10:4 / Mark 3:18 (“Cananaean”) with Luke 6:15 / Acts 1:13 (“Zealot”). What might the different descriptors suggest, and what must we avoid assuming?</li>
<li>In Acts 1:13–14, what verbs describe the community’s posture, and what does that imply about spiritual preparation?</li>
<li>What does Acts 1:8 teach about the source, scope, and goal of Christian witness?</li>
</ol>
<div><b><h2>Application and leadership</h2></b></div>
<ol>
<li>Where has “zeal” become harshness, impatience, or control in your leadership?</li>
<li>How can your group practice prayerful waiting without sliding into passivity (Acts 1:14)?</li>
<li>What would it mean for your church/community to welcome people with very different backgrounds into one mission (Matthew 10:2–4)?</li>
</ol>
<div><br></div>
<div><b><h2>13) Prayer Response</h2></b></div>
<div>Lord Jesus, you are the One who chooses and calls your servants (Luke 6:13–16). Thank you that you gather people with strong passions and varied pasts and make them one in your kingdom (Acts 1:14). Sanctify our zeal—where our desires are tangled with pride, anger, or earthly hopes, redirect us to your purposes and your ways (Acts 1:8; John 18:36). Teach us to wait in prayer with one accord, trusting your timing and seeking your power (Acts 1:14). Make us faithful witnesses to the ends of the earth, for your glory. Amen.</div>
