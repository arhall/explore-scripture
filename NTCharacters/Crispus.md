<div><h1>Crispus</h1></div>
<div><br></div>
<div><b><h1>Crispus — Bible Character Study</h1></b></div>
<div><b><h2>1) Story Summary (chronological)</h2></b></div>
<div>Crispus enters the biblical narrative in <b>Corinth</b> during Paul’s ministry there on the <b>second missionary journey</b> (Acts 18:1–11). Paul reasons in the <b>synagogue</b> (Acts 18:4), faces rising opposition (Acts 18:5–6), and then shifts his base of teaching next door to the synagogue (Acts 18:7). In that setting, <b>Crispus, “the ruler of the synagogue,” believes in the Lord together with his whole household</b>, and “many of the Corinthians hearing Paul believed and were baptized” (Acts 18:8).</div>
<div>Later, when Paul writes to the Corinthian church, he references Crispus again: Paul thanks God that he baptized only a few in Corinth—<b>specifically naming “Crispus and Gaius”</b>—to undercut party-spirit and identity-building around human leaders (1 Cor 1:14–17). That mention is the <b>last explicit reference</b> to Crispus in Scripture (1 Cor 1:14).</div>
<div><b>Where his story begins and ends:</b><br></div>
<ul>
<li>Begins: <b>Corinth, as synagogue ruler who believes</b> (Acts 18:8).</li>
<li>Ends: <b>Named in Paul’s letter as one Paul baptized</b> (1 Cor 1:14).</li>
</ul>
<div><b><h2>2) What He’s Known For</h2></b></div>
<div>Crispus is most known for being a <b>Jewish synagogue leader in Corinth who believed in Jesus</b>, with household-wide belief, and for being one of the few Corinthians Paul personally baptized (Acts 18:8; 1 Cor 1:14).</div>
<div><b>Headline descriptors</b><br></div>
<ul>
<li><b>Ruler of the Corinthian synagogue</b> (Acts 18:8)</li>
<li><b>Believed in the Lord</b> (Acts 18:8)</li>
<li><b>Household faith</b> (Acts 18:8)</li>
<li><b>A named Corinthian convert connected to Paul’s ministry</b> (Acts 18:8)</li>
<li><b>One of the few Paul personally baptized in Corinth</b> (1 Cor 1:14)</li>
<li><b>A quiet witness that the gospel penetrated established religious leadership</b> (Acts 18:8; cf. Acts 18:4–6)</li>
</ul>
<div><b><h2>3) Where He Appears in the Bible</h2></b></div>
<div><b><h2>Primary narrative locations</h2></b></div>
<ul>
<li><b>Acts — 18:1–11 (esp. 18:8)</b> — Paul’s ministry in Corinth; Crispus believes with his household (Acts 18:8).</li>
</ul>
<div><b><h2>Secondary references</h2></b></div>
<ul>
<li><b>1 Corinthians — 1:10–17 (esp. 1:14)</b> — Paul names Crispus as one he baptized, in a section addressing divisions (1 Cor 1:14).</li>
</ul>
<div><b><h2>4) Vital Statistics</h2></b></div>
<ul>
<li><b>Historical setting (approximate):</b> Paul’s Corinthian ministry under Roman administration in Achaia (Acts 18:12). (Dating is <b>approximate</b> based on Paul’s journeys and the narrative sequence of Acts; Scripture itself does not give a calendar year.)</li>
<li><b>Geography:</b> <b>Corinth</b> in <b>Achaia</b>; synagogue context; immediate proximity to “the house of Titius Justus…next door to the synagogue” (Acts 18:7–8, 12).</li>
<li><b>Family/relationships:</b><br></li>
<ul>
<li><b>Household</b>: explicitly mentioned as believing with him (Acts 18:8).</li>
<li><b>Key ministry relationships</b>: connected to Paul’s Corinthian work (Acts 18:1–8) and later referenced in Paul’s letter (1 Cor 1:14).</li>
<li><b>Associates in the Corinth narrative</b>: Aquila and Priscilla (Acts 18:2–3), Silas and Timothy (Acts 18:5), Titius Justus (Acts 18:7), and later Sosthenes (Acts 18:17).</li>
</ul>
<li><b>Roles/offices:</b><br></li>
<ul>
<li><b>“Ruler of the synagogue”</b> (Acts 18:8).</li>
<li><b>Believer / disciple</b> (Acts 18:8) and likely a member of the Corinthian church (implied by Paul’s later mention, 1 Cor 1:14).</li>
</ul>
<li><b>Key turning points:</b><br></li>
<ul>
<li><b>Conversion:</b> believes in the Lord (Acts 18:8).</li>
<li><b>Household identification with Christ:</b> his “whole household” believes (Acts 18:8).</li>
<li><b>Baptism connected with Paul:</b> named as one Paul baptized (1 Cor 1:14).</li>
</ul>
</ul>

<div><b><span style="font-size: 16px">6) Strengths, Weaknesses, and Heart-Level Patterns</span></b></div>
<div><b><h2>Strengths (with textual grounding)</h2></b></div>
<ul>
<li><b>Responsive faith to the gospel despite high social/religious status.</b> As synagogue ruler, Crispus’s belief is explicitly stated and stands out amid opposition (Acts 18:6–8).</li>
<li><b>Household leadership toward faith.</b> Scripture highlights that his “whole household” believed (Acts 18:8), suggesting his faith had immediate relational impact within his sphere of responsibility (Acts 18:8).</li>
<li><b>Public identification with Christ (implied).</b> In a context of synagogue conflict and social pressure, he is openly named as believing (Acts 18:6–8).</li>
</ul>
<div><b><h2>Weaknesses / sins / blind spots</h2></b></div>
<ul>
<li>Scripture <b>does not record</b> specific failures or sins for Crispus. We should not invent them. The narrative simply highlights his conversion and later mention (Acts 18:8; 1 Cor 1:14).</li>
</ul>
<div><b><h2>Motives / fears / desires (careful inference anchored to the text)</h2></b></div>
<ul>
<li><b>Desire for truth and readiness to believe:</b> The text emphasizes belief “in the Lord” (Acts 18:8). While the narrative does not describe his inner thoughts, his action aligns with a willingness to submit to God’s revelation in Christ (Acts 18:8; cf. Rom 1:16).</li>
<li><b>Potential social cost:</b> The immediate context includes sharp opposition in the synagogue setting (Acts 18:6). Crispus’s conversion likely carried relational consequences, though Acts does not narrate details (Acts 18:6–8).</li>
</ul>
<div><b><h2>What changes (or doesn’t) over time?</h2></b></div>
<ul>
<li><b>Change:</b> From synagogue ruler to believer identified with the Corinthian church (Acts 18:8; 1 Cor 1:14).</li>
<li><b>Silence afterward:</b> Scripture does not track his later ministry, leadership, or trials; his “arc” is intentionally brief (Acts 18:8; 1 Cor 1:14).</li>
</ul>
<div><b><h2>7) Victories and Challenges</h2></b></div>
<div><b><h2>Major “wins” (with references)</h2></b></div>
<ul>
<li><b>Conversion of a religious leader and his household:</b> Crispus believes “with his entire household” (Acts 18:8).</li>
<li><b>Participation in the early Corinthian revival:</b> His conversion is set alongside “many of the Corinthians” believing and being baptized (Acts 18:8).</li>
<li><b>A witness to gospel power among Jews and Greeks:</b> Paul’s Corinthian ministry embodies the pattern “to the Jew first and also to the Greek” (Rom 1:16), and Crispus is a concrete example of Jewish synagogue fruit (Acts 18:4, 8).</li>
</ul>
<div><b><h2>Major challenges (with references)</h2></b></div>
<ul>
<li><b>Hostile environment and synagogue conflict:</b> Crispus’s belief is narrated in the same unit where Paul faces rejection and must pivot away from synagogue ministry (Acts 18:5–7).</li>
<li><b>A church vulnerable to factionalism afterward:</b> Paul’s later letter shows Corinth’s temptation to rally around personalities (“I follow Paul…”)—the very context in which Crispus is mentioned (1 Cor 1:12–15).</li>
</ul>
<div><b>Cost of faithfulness / consequences of compromise (as the text frames it):</b>Acts spotlights the <b>costly friction</b> surrounding the gospel in Corinth (Acts 18:6, 12–17). In Corinthians, Paul shows the <b>spiritual cost</b> of identity politics inside the church and insists that Christ—not the baptizer—must be central (1 Cor 1:12–17).</div>
<div><b><h2>8) Essential Verses and Key Cross-References (organized by themes)</h2></b></div>
<div><b><h2>Theme A: Conversion and household faith</h2></b></div>
<ul>
<li><b>Acts 18:8</b> — Crispus (synagogue ruler) believes with his whole household.</li>
<li><b>Acts 16:31–34</b> — Household faith/baptism pattern in Acts (jailer).</li>
<li><b>Josh 24:15</b> — “As for me and my house…” (a background pattern of household-oriented covenant allegiance).</li>
</ul>
<div><b><h2>Theme B: Gospel advance amid opposition</h2></b></div>
<ul>
<li><b>Acts 18:4–8</b> — Synagogue reasoning, opposition, pivot, and Crispus’s conversion.</li>
<li><b>Acts 18:9–11</b> — The Lord’s encouragement to Paul to keep speaking in Corinth.</li>
<li><b>Rom 1:16</b> — Gospel power “to the Jew first and also to the Greek,” echoing what happens in Corinth (Acts 18:4, 8).</li>
</ul>
<div><b><h2>Theme C: Baptism, unity, and Christ-centered identity</h2></b></div>
<ul>
<li><b>1 Cor 1:10–13</b> — Divisions and party slogans in Corinth.</li>
<li><b>1 Cor 1:14</b> — Crispus named as one Paul baptized.</li>
<li><b>1 Cor 1:15–17</b> — Paul’s logic: baptism must not be used to create factions; Christ’s cross is central.</li>
<li><b>Eph 4:4–6</b> — “One body…one Lord, one faith, one baptism.”</li>
</ul>
<div><b><h2>Theme D: The synagogue-to-church transition</h2></b></div>
<ul>
<li><b>Acts 18:7</b> — Ministry base next door to synagogue (Titius Justus).</li>
<li><b>Acts 18:17</b> — Sosthenes appears in the Corinth conflict narrative (related Corinth synagogue context).</li>
<li><b>John 9:22; 12:42–43</b> — Social pressures surrounding synagogue exclusion (a broader NT motif).</li>
</ul>
<div><b><h2>9) Theology and Big Themes</h2></b></div>
<ul>
<li><b>God’s sovereignty in gospel advance:</b> Despite opposition, the Lord strengthens Paul to continue speaking in Corinth (Acts 18:9–10), and conversions follow—Crispus included (Acts 18:8–10).</li>
<li><b>Grace reaching the “insider” and the “outsider”:</b> Crispus’s position as synagogue ruler highlights that the gospel confronts and fulfills religious expectation, calling even respected leaders to faith in Jesus as Lord (Acts 18:8; cf. Acts 18:4).</li>
<li><b>Household discipleship and covenantal echoes:</b> Acts repeatedly notes households believing together (Acts 16:31–34; 18:8), underscoring the social spread of faith and the responsibility of leadership within families (Acts 18:8).</li>
<li><b>The cross-centered church vs. personality-driven religion:</b> Crispus appears in Paul’s argument against factions. Paul refuses to let baptisms become badges of loyalty to human leaders; the church must be defined by Christ crucified (1 Cor 1:12–17).</li>
<li><b>Human nature in Corinth:</b> even real converts can drift into pride, comparison, and party spirit—requiring apostolic correction (1 Cor 1:10–13).</li>
</ul>
<div><b><h2>10) How Crispus Points Us to Christ</h2></b></div>
<ul>
<li><b>A “ruler” who submits to the true King:</b> Crispus is a “ruler of the synagogue” who “believed in the Lord” (Acts 18:8). His story quietly dramatizes the rightful transfer of allegiance from religious status to Jesus’ lordship (Acts 18:8; cf. Phil 2:9–11).</li>
<li><b>Christ gathers a unified people, not factions:</b> Crispus is named not to elevate him, but to help the church renounce identity built on personalities and return to the <b>cross</b> as the center (1 Cor 1:14–17). In that sense, Crispus’s mention serves the gospel: <i>Christ is not divided</i> (1 Cor 1:13).</li>
<li><b>Gospel power for unlikely converts:</b> Crispus embodies the truth that salvation is not restricted by social standing or office—Christ calls and saves through the preached word (Acts 18:8; cf. Rom 1:16).</li>
</ul>
<div><b><h2>11) Lessons and Applications (tied to Scripture)</h2></b></div>
<ol>
<li><b>No one is “too established” to need conversion.</b> Even a synagogue ruler must “believe in the Lord” (Acts 18:8).</li>
<li><b>Faith should reshape our closest relationships.</b> Crispus’s household is named as believing with him (Acts 18:8).</li>
<li><b>Expect real resistance where the gospel is preached.</b> Opposition precedes and surrounds Crispus’s conversion (Acts 18:5–8).</li>
<li><b>God sustains his messengers—and his mission advances.</b> The Lord encourages Paul to keep speaking in Corinth (Acts 18:9–10).</li>
<li><b>Don’t build Christian identity around human leaders.</b> Corinth’s slogans are rebuked (1 Cor 1:12–13).</li>
<li><b>Baptism is precious—but must never become a status marker.</b> Paul mentions baptizing Crispus to <i>undercut</i> factional pride (1 Cor 1:14–15).</li>
<li><b>The church’s center is Christ crucified, not who baptized whom.</b> Paul’s priority is the gospel of the cross (1 Cor 1:17).</li>
<li><b>The gospel can transform public, visible leaders—and that matters.</b> Crispus’s conversion is publicly notable in the narrative (Acts 18:8).</li>
<li><b>Household leadership can be used for spiritual good.</b> Crispus’s “whole household” believing suggests influence stewarded toward faith (Acts 18:8).</li>
<li><b>Unity is a gospel issue, not a “secondary” concern.</b> Paul treats divisions as serious and doctrinally inconsistent with Christ (1 Cor 1:10–13; Eph 4:4–6).</li>
</ol>
<div><b>Warnings to heed</b><br></div>
<ul>
<li>Beware <b>party spirit</b> and celebrity Christianity (1 Cor 1:12–13).</li>
<li>Beware turning good gifts (like baptism) into tools for pride (1 Cor 1:14–15).</li>
</ul>
<div><b>Examples to imitate</b><br></div>
<ul>
<li>Crispus’s <b>prompt belief</b> in the Lord amid conflict (Acts 18:6–8).</li>
<li>Paul’s refusal to let ministry details distract from the <b>cross</b> (1 Cor 1:17).</li>
</ul>
<div><b><h2>12) Discussion and Reflection Questions (10–20)</h2></b></div>
<ol>
<li>What stands out about Crispus being a <b>synagogue ruler</b> who believed (Acts 18:8)?</li>
<li>Why do you think Acts highlights that Crispus believed <b>with his whole household</b> (Acts 18:8)?</li>
<li>What does the sequence in Acts 18:5–8 teach about gospel fruit coming <b>through</b> opposition, not only after it?</li>
<li>How might Crispus’s conversion have affected synagogue dynamics in Corinth (Acts 18:4–8)? (Stick to what the text suggests.)</li>
<li>What does Paul’s mention of Crispus in 1 Corinthians 1:14 reveal about the <b>problems</b> in Corinth (1 Cor 1:10–15)?</li>
<li>How can modern Christians unintentionally repeat Corinth’s “I follow Paul…” slogans (1 Cor 1:12)?</li>
<li>Why does Paul connect unity so closely to the question, “Is Christ divided?” (1 Cor 1:13)?</li>
<li>What’s the difference between valuing a minister and <b>boasting</b> in a minister (1 Cor 1:12–13)?</li>
<li>How does Eph 4:4–6 reinforce Paul’s unity argument in 1 Corinthians 1?</li>
<li>What does Acts 18:9–10 teach about God’s encouragement in ministry when people oppose the message?</li>
<li>If you lead a household (or influence one), what practices help you steward that influence toward faith (Acts 18:8)?</li>
<li>What are healthy ways to honor baptism without turning it into an identity badge (1 Cor 1:14–17)?</li>
<li>In your context, what would it look like to make the <b>cross</b> central in your conversations and conflicts (1 Cor 1:17)?</li>
<li>Where do you see the temptation to measure spiritual legitimacy by “who you know” or “who discipled you” (1 Cor 1:12–15)?</li>
<li>What details does Scripture <i>not</i> tell us about Crispus—and how does that shape responsible application (Acts 18:8; 1 Cor 1:14)?</li>
</ol>
<div><b><h2>13) Prayer Response</h2></b></div>

Thank you for saving sinners from every station— even leaders like Crispus—by calling them to believe in you (Acts 18:8). Grant our households faith and obedience, and make our homes places where your name is honored (Acts 18:8). Guard us from pride, factions, and celebrity-driven religion; unite your church in one Lord, one faith, one baptism (1 Cor 1:10–13; Eph 4:4–6). Give us courage to confess you when the gospel is opposed, and keep us centered on Christ crucified. Amen.</div>
