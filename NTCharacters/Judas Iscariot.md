<div><b><h1>Judas Iscariot</h1></b></div>
<div><b><h1><br></h1></b></div>
<div><b><h1>Judas Iscariot — Bible Character Study</h1></b></div>
<div><b><h2>1) Story Summary</h2></b></div>
<div>Judas Iscariot appears in the Gospels as one of “the twelve” chosen by Jesus, entrusted with the group’s funds, and ultimately the betrayer who delivers Jesus into the hands of His enemies.</div>
<div><b>Where his story begins:</b> Judas first enters the narrative when he is named among the Twelve apostles (Matthew 10:1–4; Mark 3:16–19; Luke 6:12–16). John later identifies him as “Judas (son of Simon) Iscariot” (John 6:71).</div>
<div><b>Key movements (chronological):</b><br></div>
<ul>
<li><b>Chosen as an apostle</b> among the Twelve (Matthew 10:1–4; Mark 3:16–19; Luke 6:12–16).</li>
<li><b>Included in the apostolic circle</b> that preached and ministered in Jesus’ name (the Twelve are sent out; Judas is included in the list—Matthew 10:1–8; Mark 6:7–13; Luke 9:1–6).</li>
<li><b>Hardened unbelief beneath proximity</b>: Jesus speaks of one disciple as “a devil” and notes Judas’s unbelief (John 6:64, 70–71).</li>
<li><b>A pattern of hypocrisy and covetousness</b>: Judas keeps the moneybag and is described as a thief (John 12:4–6).</li>
<li><b>Betrayal</b>: he conspires with the chief priests, then identifies Jesus with a kiss (Matthew 26:14–16, 47–50; Mark 14:10–11, 43–46; Luke 22:3–6, 47–48; John 18:2–5).</li>
<li><b>Aftermath</b>: Judas feels remorse, returns the money, and dies by suicide (Matthew 27:3–5). Acts describes his death in a way that is commonly read alongside Matthew and notes his replacement among the Twelve (Acts 1:15–26).</li>
</ul>
<div><b>Where his story ends:</b> Judas’s narrative concludes with his death (Matthew 27:3–10; Acts 1:15–20) and the appointment of Matthias to take his “office” (Acts 1:20–26).</div>
<div><br></div>
<div><b><h2>2) What He’s Known For</h2></b></div>
<div>Judas Iscariot is most known for <b>betraying Jesus</b>, despite close access to Him as a chosen apostle.</div>
<div><b>Headline descriptors</b><br></div>
<ul>
<li><b>One of the Twelve</b> personally appointed by Jesus (Matthew 10:1–4).</li>
<li><b>The betrayer</b> who hands Jesus over (Matthew 26:14–16).</li>
<li><b>A hypocrite near holy things</b>—trusted with funds, yet a thief (John 12:4–6).</li>
<li><b>An unbeliever in the inner circle</b> (John 6:64, 70–71).</li>
<li><b>An instrument of Satanic temptation/influence</b> (Luke 22:3; John 13:2, 27).</li>
<li><b>A tragic example of remorse without repentance</b> (Matthew 27:3–5; cf. 2 Corinthians 7:10 for the category).</li>
</ul>
<div><br></div>
<div><b><h2>3) Where He Appears in the Bible</h2></b></div>
<div><b><h2>Primary narrative locations</h2></b></div>
<ul>
<li><b>Matthew — 10:1–4; 26:14–16; 26:20–25; 26:47–50; 27:3–10</b> — chosen as apostle; bargains to betray; exposed at supper; betrays with a kiss; remorse and death; money and “field.”</li>
<li><b>Mark — 3:16–19; 14:10–11; 14:18–21; 14:43–46</b> — chosen; plots betrayal; foretold at supper; betrayal scene.</li>
<li><b>Luke — 6:12–16; 22:3–6; 22:21–23; 22:47–48</b> — chosen; Satan enters Judas; betrayal foretold; betrayal with a kiss.</li>
<li><b>John — 6:64, 70–71; 12:4–6; 13:2, 21–30; 18:2–5</b> — Judas’s unbelief; theft; Satanic influence; departure into the night; leads arrest party.</li>
</ul>
<div><b><h2>Secondary references / later mentions</h2></b></div>
<ul>
<li><b>Acts — 1:15–26 (esp. 1:16–20)</b> — Judas’s end, the “field,” OT citations, and his replacement.</li>
<li><b>Psalm — 41:9</b> — close friend betrays (echoed in the passion context; cf. John 13:18).</li>
<li><b>Psalm — 69:25; 109:8</b> — applied in Acts to Judas’s vacancy (Acts 1:20).</li>
<li><b>Zechariah — 11:12–13</b> — “thirty pieces of silver” imagery often associated with Judas’s payment and the “potter” theme (cf. Matthew 27:9–10; note discussion under “uncertainties”).</li>
</ul>
<div><br></div>
<div><b><h2>4) Vital Statistics</h2></b></div>
<ul>
<li><b>Historical setting:</b> Late Second Temple Judaism under Roman rule; Jesus’ ministry culminating at Passover in Jerusalem (Pilate appears in the passion narratives: e.g., Matthew 27:11; Luke 23:1; John 18:28–19:16).</li>
<li><b>Geography strongly associated:</b><br></li>
<ul>
<li><b>Jerusalem</b> (betrayal plot and passion week events—Matthew 26–27; John 18).</li>
<li><b>Gethsemane / Mount of Olives area</b> (betrayal—Matthew 26:36, 47; Luke 22:39, 47).</li>
<li><b>His epithet “Iscariot”</b> is <i>uncertain</i> in meaning: commonly explained as “man of Kerioth” (a Judean town; cf. Joshua 15:25 lists a Kerioth), but Scripture itself does not explicitly define it. So we keep the label <b>uncertain</b>.</li>
</ul>
<li><b>Family / relationships:</b><br></li>
<ul>
<li><b>Father:</b> “Simon Iscariot” (John 6:71; John 13:2, 26).</li>
<li><b>Associations:</b> the Twelve (Matthew 10:1–4); chief priests and officers involved in the arrest (Matthew 26:14–16; John 18:3).</li>
</ul>
<li><b>Roles/offices:</b> <b>disciple/apostle</b> (Matthew 10:1–4); <b>keeper of the moneybag</b> (John 12:6; implied group expectations in John 13:29).</li>
<li><b>Key turning points:</b><br></li>
<ul>
<li><b>Chosen</b> as apostle (Matthew 10:1–4).</li>
<li><b>Exposed unbelief</b> despite discipleship proximity (John 6:64, 70–71).</li>
<li><b>Entrusted with finances</b> yet indulging sin (John 12:4–6).</li>
<li><b>Agreement to betray</b> for money (Matthew 26:14–16; Luke 22:3–6).</li>
<li><b>Final betrayal and arrest</b> (Matthew 26:47–50; John 18:2–5).</li>
<li><b>Remorse and death</b> (Matthew 27:3–5; Acts 1:18–19).</li>
</ul>
</ul>

<div><b><h2>6) Strengths, Weaknesses, and Heart-Level Patterns</h2></b></div>
<div><b><h2>Strengths (as evidenced in the text)</h2></b></div>
<div>These are limited, because Scripture emphasizes his failure; still, a few <b>real privileges/entrustments</b> appear:</div>
<ul>
<li><b>Chosen and commissioned</b> as one of the Twelve (Matthew 10:1–4).</li>
<li><b>Entrusted with responsibility</b> (the moneybag), implying a measure of outward trust from the group (John 12:6; John 13:29).</li>
</ul>
<div>Note: The Gospels show the Twelve preaching and casting out demons when sent (Mark 6:7–13; Luke 9:1–6). Since Judas is included among the Twelve (Mark 3:19), it is <i>reasonable</i> (though not individually itemized) that he participated outwardly in this mission.</div>
<div><b><h2>Weaknesses / sins / blind spots (explicit in Scripture)</h2></b></div>
<ul>
<li><b>Unbelief beneath discipleship</b> (John 6:64; John 6:70–71).</li>
<li><b>Greed and theft</b>: “a thief… used to help himself” (John 12:4–6).</li>
<li><b>Hypocrisy / false concern for the poor</b> masking self-interest (John 12:4–6).</li>
<li><b>Betrayal for money</b> (Matthew 26:14–16).</li>
<li><b>Betrayal with a kiss</b>—using a sign of affection as deception (Luke 22:47–48).</li>
<li><b>Spiritual vulnerability and complicity</b>: “Satan entered into Judas” (Luke 22:3; John 13:27), alongside Judas’s own willing action (Luke 22:4–6).</li>
</ul>
<div><b><h2>Motives / fears / desires (careful inferences anchored to the text)</h2></b></div>
<ul>
<li><b>Desire for money / material gain</b> is the clearest motive explicitly connected to him (Matthew 26:15; John 12:6).</li>
<li><b>Desire to conceal sin</b> (hypocrisy) is suggested by his performative “concern for the poor” while stealing (John 12:4–6).</li>
<li><b>Possible fear of loss of advantage</b> (inference): As Jesus’ path turns toward suffering (e.g., Mark 8:31–33; John 12:23–27), Judas may have perceived a collapsing “kingdom payoff.” Scripture doesn’t state this directly, so it should remain a cautious possibility, not a claim.</li>
</ul>
<div><b><h2>What changes (or doesn’t) over time?</h2></b></div>
<ul>
<li><b>What doesn’t change:</b> Judas remains close in proximity yet unmoved in saving faith (John 6:64, 70–71). His pattern of greed is not a single moment but an ongoing practice (John 12:6).</li>
<li><b>What changes:</b> The narrative shows an intensification—from hidden theft (John 12:6) → negotiated betrayal (Matthew 26:14–16) → completed betrayal (Matthew 26:47–50) → despairing end (Matthew 27:3–5). John and Luke underline a darkening spiritual trajectory (Luke 22:3; John 13:27).</li>
</ul>
<div><br></div>
<div><b><h2>7) Victories and Challenges</h2></b></div>
<div><b><h2>Major “wins” (limited, but real privileges)</h2></b></div>
<ul>
<li><b>Included in Jesus’ apostolic band and mission</b> (Matthew 10:1–8; Mark 6:7–13).</li>
<li><b>Entrusted with shared resources</b> (John 12:6).</li>
</ul>
<div><b><h2>Major challenges (and failures) with consequences</h2></b></div>
<ul>
<li><b>Love of money</b> leading to betrayal (Matthew 26:14–16; John 12:6; cf. 1 Timothy 6:9–10 as a thematic echo).</li>
<li><b>Satanic assault embraced rather than resisted</b> (Luke 22:3–6; John 13:27).</li>
<li><b>Betraying innocent blood</b> acknowledged even by Judas afterward (Matthew 27:4).</li>
<li><b>Consequences of compromise:</b> Judas’s betrayal contributes to Jesus’ arrest (Matthew 26:47–50), and Judas’s end is death and loss of office (Matthew 27:5; Acts 1:20).</li>
</ul>

<div><b><h2>8) Essential Verses and Key Cross-References (Organized by Themes)</h2></b></div>
<div><b><h2>Identity and calling (proximity to Jesus)</h2></b></div>

<li>Matthew 10:1–4 — Judas named among the Twelve.</li>
<li>Mark 3:16–19 — Judas listed as the one “who betrayed him.”</li>

</ul>

<li>John 6:64 — Jesus knows who does not believe and who will betray.</li>

<li>John 13:18 — “He who ate my bread has lifted his heel against me” (echoing Psalm 41:9).</li>
</ul>

<li>John 13:2 — betrayal put into Judas’s heart.</li>
<li>John 13:27–30 — Satan enters; Judas goes out “and it was night.”</li>
</ul>
<div><b><h2>Betrayal and arrest</h2></b></div>
<ul>
<li>Matthew 26:14–16 — agreement for thirty pieces of silver.</li>
<li>Luke 22:47–48 — betrayal with a kiss (and Jesus’ rebuke).</li>
<li>John 18:2–5 — Judas knows the place; leads arresting group.</li>
</ul>
<div><b><h2>Aftermath: remorse, death, replacement</h2></b></div>
<ul>

<li>Acts 1:16–20 — Judas’s end, the “field,” Scripture applied to his vacancy.</li>

</ul>
<div><b><h2>Cross-references that interpret/echo themes</h2></b></div>
<ul>
<li><b>Betrayed friend pattern:</b> Psalm 41:9 (quoted/echoed in John 13:18).</li>
<li><b>Vacant office/judgment language:</b> Psalm 69:25; Psalm 109:8 (applied in Acts 1:20).</li>
<li><b>Betrayal price imagery:</b> Zechariah 11:12–13 (connected in Matthew 27:9–10; see “uncertainties” below).</li>
<li><b>Love of money warning:</b> 1 Timothy 6:9–10.</li>
<li><b>Godly repentance vs worldly grief:</b> 2 Corinthians 7:10 (useful lens alongside Matthew 27:3–5).</li>
<li><b>A close OT parallel:</b> Ahithophel betrays David and hangs himself (2 Samuel 15–17; esp. 2 Samuel 17:23).</li>
</ul>

<div><b><h2>9) Theology and Big Themes</h2></b></div>
<div><b><h2>God’s character revealed</h2></b></div>
<ul>
<li><b>Sovereignty without moral excuse:</b> Jesus foretells betrayal; Scripture is fulfilled; yet Judas is genuinely culpable (John 13:18–27; Matthew 26:24).</li>
<li><b>Justice and truth:</b> Judas calls it “innocent blood” and is not treated as a victim of fate but as a responsible actor (Matthew 27:4).</li>
<li><b>Patience and offered warnings:</b> Jesus warns repeatedly at the supper and even addresses Judas in the moment of betrayal (Matthew 26:20–25; Luke 22:47–48).</li>
</ul>
<div><b><h2>Discipleship, apostasy, and hypocrisy</h2></b></div>
<ul>
<li><b>Nearness to Jesus is not the same as knowing Jesus.</b> Judas hears teaching, sees miracles, shares ministry, yet remains unbelieving (John 6:64, 70–71).</li>
<li><b>Hidden sin hardens into open treachery.</b> The trajectory from theft (John 12:6) to betrayal (Matthew 26:14–16) is a sober pattern.</li>
</ul>
<div><b><h2>Spiritual warfare and human responsibility</h2></b></div>
<ul>
<li>Judas is portrayed as acted upon (“Satan entered”) and acting voluntarily (“he went… and conferred… how he might betray”) (Luke 22:3–6; John 13:27). Scripture holds both together without contradiction.</li>
</ul>
<div><b><h2>Repentance vs remorse</h2></b></div>
<ul>
<li>Matthew records genuine anguish and confession of wrongdoing (“I have sinned…”) yet Judas’s response terminates in despair and self-destruction rather than turning to God (Matthew 27:3–5). This stands as a living case study beside the biblical distinction between <b>worldly grief</b> and <b>godly grief</b> (2 Corinthians 7:10).</li>
</ul>
<div><b><h2>The church’s continuity and Christ’s mission</h2></b></div>
<ul>
<li>Acts shows the early church treating Judas’s vacancy seriously, seeking God’s direction to fill it (Acts 1:15–26). Jesus’ redemptive mission is not derailed; it advances.</li>
</ul>
<div><br></div>
<div><b><h2>10) How Judas Points Us to Christ</h2></b></div>
<div><b><h2>1) The betrayed righteous one (suffering pattern)</h2></b></div>
<div>Judas’s betrayal intensifies the portrait of Jesus as the righteous sufferer: betrayed by a close companion (Psalm 41:9; John 13:18), handed over unjustly (Matthew 26:47–50), yet submitting to accomplish salvation (John 18:11; cf. Isaiah 53’s broader suffering-servant theme).</div>
<div><b><h2>2) Davidic typology (betrayed king)</h2></b></div>
<div>Like David, Jesus is betrayed by someone close. The Ahithophel parallel is striking: a trusted associate betrays the anointed king and ends by hanging (2 Samuel 15–17; 17:23). This pattern heightens Jesus as the greater David who endures treachery and still triumphs.</div>
<div><b><h2>3) Sovereign salvation through human sin (without excusing sin)</h2></b></div>
<div>The passion narratives show Scripture being fulfilled (John 13:18; Acts 1:16–20), while Judas remains morally accountable (Matthew 26:24; Matthew 27:4). The cross reveals a God who is so sovereign that even betrayal becomes the path by which atonement is accomplished—yet so holy that betrayers are not declared innocent.</div>
<div><b><h2>4) Gospel contrast: Peter and Judas</h2></b></div>
<div>Both fail grievously (Peter denies; Judas betrays). Yet Peter weeps and is later restored (Luke 22:61–62; John 21:15–19), while Judas’s remorse collapses into despair (Matthew 27:3–5). The contrast magnifies Christ’s restoring grace and the necessity of true repentance.</div>

<div><b><h2>11) Lessons and Applications</h2></b></div>
<div><b><h2>Warnings to heed (with Scripture anchors)</h2></b></div>
<ol>
<li><b>Proximity to Jesus can coexist with unbelief</b>—don’t confuse church nearness with new birth (John 6:64, 70–71).</li>
<li><b>Small secret sins grow</b>—unchecked theft becomes betrayal (John 12:6 → Matthew 26:14–16).</li>
<li><b>Love of money is spiritually lethal</b> (John 12:6; Matthew 26:15; 1 Timothy 6:9–10).</li>
<li><b>Hypocrisy uses righteous language to mask selfish aims</b> (John 12:4–6).</li>
<li><b>Satan targets disciples; vigilance matters</b> (Luke 22:3–6; cf. Luke 22:31–32 for the wider context of satanic sifting).</li>
<li><b>Remorse is not repentance</b>—pain over sin is not the same as turning to God (Matthew 27:3–5; 2 Corinthians 7:10).</li>
<li><b>Betrayal can look affectionate</b>—a “kiss” can be spiritual camouflage (Luke 22:47–48).</li>
</ol>
<div><b><h2>Examples to imitate (limited, but instructive by contrast)</h2></b></div>
<ol>
<li><b>Receive Jesus’ warnings as mercy</b>: Christ’s exposure of sin is meant to call for repentance (Matthew 26:20–25; John 13:21–26).</li>
<li><b>Bring guilt to God, not to self-destruction</b>: Judas brings money back to priests; Scripture never shows him seeking the Lord (Matthew 27:3–5). The application is to flee <i>to</i> God with sin (cf. Psalm 51 as the biblical posture).</li>
</ol>
<div><b><h2>Application to key life areas</h2></b></div>
<ul>
<li><b>Personal holiness:</b> kill secret sin early (John 12:6).</li>
<li><b>Relationships:</b> beware performing affection while cultivating betrayal in the heart (Luke 22:47–48).</li>
<li><b>Decision-making:</b> don’t trade eternal realities for temporary gain (Matthew 26:15).</li>
<li><b>Leadership/ministry:</b> gifts and positions don’t replace character and faith (Matthew 10:1–8; John 6:64).</li>
<li><b>Suffering and temptation:</b> spiritual warfare is real; resist the devil by clinging to Christ (Luke 22:3–6; cf. James 4:7 as a thematic echo).</li>
</ul>

<div><b><h2>12) Discussion and Reflection Questions (10–20)</h2></b></div>
<div><b><h2>Personal reflection</h2></b></div>
<ol>
<li>In what ways can “nearness” to Christian activity mask unbelief (John 6:64, 70–71)?</li>
<li>What “small” compromises have you normalized that could harden into bigger sins (John 12:6)?</li>
<li>How do you discern the difference between remorse and repentance in your own life (Matthew 27:3–5; 2 Corinthians 7:10)?</li>
<li>Where does money or comfort exert disproportionate influence on your decisions (Matthew 26:15; 1 Timothy 6:9–10)?</li>
</ol>
<div><b><h2>Small group discussion</h2></b></div>
<ol>
<li>Why do you think the disciples trusted Judas with the moneybag, and what does that teach about how easily hypocrisy can hide (John 12:6; John 13:29)?</li>
<li>How do the Gospels present both Judas’s responsibility and Satan’s involvement (Luke 22:3–6; John 13:27)?</li>
<li>What do you learn about Jesus’ patience and truthfulness in His handling of Judas at the Last Supper (John 13:21–26; Matthew 26:20–25)?</li>
</ol>
<div><b><h2>Deep study (text-observation)</h2></b></div>
<ol>
<li>Compare how each Gospel narrates the betrayal (Matthew 26; Mark 14; Luke 22; John 13, 18). What details does each emphasize?</li>
<li>Trace the “knowledge” theme in John: Jesus knows who will betray Him (John 6:64; John 13:11). How does this shape your view of sovereignty?</li>
<li>In Matthew 27:3–10 and Acts 1:16–20, what is said about Judas’s death and the “field”? What is explicit, and what requires careful inference?</li>
<li>How does Psalm 41:9 function when quoted/echoed in John 13:18?</li>
<li>Read 2 Samuel 17:23 (Ahithophel) alongside Matthew 27:5 (Judas). What similarities stand out, and how might this illuminate “greater David” typology?</li>
</ol>
<div><b><h2>Application / pastoral wisdom</h2></b></div>
<ol>
<li>What accountability structures help expose hidden sin before it metastasizes (John 12:6)?</li>
<li>If someone is overwhelmed by guilt, what would it look like to bring sin to Christ rather than spiraling into despair (contrast Matthew 27:3–5 with the gospel invitations, e.g., Matthew 11:28)?</li>
<li>How should Christians handle positions of trust in ministry with sobriety and safeguards (John 12:6)?</li>
</ol>

<div><b><h2>Notes on uncertainties (handled carefully)</h2></b></div>
<ul>
<li><b>Meaning of “Iscariot”:</b> Scripture does not define it; common proposals exist, but they should be held loosely.</li>
<li><b>Death accounts (Matthew 27:5 vs Acts 1:18):</b> Matthew reports Judas “hanged himself.” Acts describes a gruesome fall. Many readers harmonize these (e.g., hanging followed by a fall), but Scripture itself doesn’t narrate the mechanism in one continuous account—so treat harmonizations as <b>possible</b>, not certain (Matthew 27:5; Acts 1:18).</li>
<li><b>Matthew’s citation “Jeremiah” in Matthew 27:9–10:</b> The wording echoes themes from Zechariah 11:12–13 and also Jeremiah’s “potter/field” imagery (e.g., Jeremiah 19; 32). How exactly Matthew is citing/combining is debated; the safest approach is to affirm Matthew’s point: Judas’s payment and the field purchase are presented as fulfilling Scripture (Matthew 27:9–10).</li>
</ul>
