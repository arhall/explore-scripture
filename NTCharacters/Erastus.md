<div><b><h1>Erastus</h1></b></div>
<div><b><h1><br></h1></b></div>
<div><b><h1>Erastus — Bible Character Study</h1></b></div>
<div><b><h2>1) Story Summary (Chronological)</h2></b></div>
<div>Erastus appears briefly but meaningfully in the orbit of the apostle Paul as a trusted coworker who helps advance the mission through practical service and travel. His story begins (as far as Scripture records) during Paul’s extended ministry in Ephesus, when Paul “sent into Macedonia two of his helpers, Timothy and Erastus” while Paul himself stayed in Asia a while longer (Acts 19:22). This places Erastus among Paul’s deployable, reliable team members during a strategically important phase of the Gentile mission (Acts 19:10, 19:21–22).</div>
<div>Later, Erastus is named in the closing greetings of Romans: “Erastus, the city treasurer, and our brother Quartus greet you” (Rom 16:23). The immediate context suggests Paul is sending greetings from the church where he is writing, alongside Gaius (Rom 16:23; cf. Rom 16:1–2). Erastus is therefore presented as both a believer (“brother”) and a person with an identifiable civic role (“city treasurer,” Rom 16:23).</div>
<div>Finally, near the end of Paul’s last letter (as commonly received), Paul notes: “Erastus remained at Corinth” (2 Tim 4:20). This is the last canonical mention of Erastus, and it shows him connected to Corinth at that time.</div>
<div><b>Where his story begins and ends in the Bible:</b><br></div>
<ul>
<li><b>Begins:</b> Acts 19:22 (named as one of Paul’s helpers sent to Macedonia)</li>
<li><b>Ends:</b> 2 Timothy 4:20 (noted as remaining at Corinth)</li>
</ul>
<div><b>A key interpretive caution:</b> “Erastus” was a common Greco-Roman name. Scripture does not explicitly state that the Erastus of Acts 19:22, Romans 16:23, and 2 Timothy 4:20 is the same man. Many readers harmonize them as one coworker of Paul, but that identification should be held with appropriate humility since the text does not directly confirm it (Acts 19:22; Rom 16:23; 2 Tim 4:20).</div>
<div><br></div>
<div><b><h2>2) What He’s Known For</h2></b></div>
<div>Erastus is most known for being:</div>
<ul>
<li><b>A trusted helper in Paul’s mission team</b> (Acts 19:22)</li>
<li><b>A believer connected to Corinth</b> (Rom 16:23; 2 Tim 4:20)</li>
<li><b>Possibly a civic official (“city treasurer”) who remained publicly identified by his role</b> (Rom 16:23)</li>
</ul>
<div><b>Headline descriptors (3–7):</b><br></div>
<ul>
<li>Paul’s <b>trusted emissary / helper</b> (Acts 19:22)</li>
<li>A <b>mission logistics</b> coworker (Acts 19:22)</li>
<li>A <b>recognized brother in the church</b> (Rom 16:23)</li>
<li>A man with a <b>public civic role</b> (Rom 16:23)</li>
<li>Connected to <b>Corinth</b> in Paul’s network (Rom 16:23; 2 Tim 4:20)</li>
<li>A reminder that the gospel reached people across <b>social and vocational strata</b> (Rom 16:23)</li>
</ul>
<div><br></div>
<div><b><h2>3) Where He Appears in the Bible</h2></b></div>
<div><b><h2>Primary narrative locations</h2></b></div>
<ul>
<li><b>Acts — 19:22</b> — Paul sends Timothy and Erastus to Macedonia as helpers during the Ephesian phase of the mission.</li>
</ul>
<div><b><h2>Secondary references (later mentions / greetings)</h2></b></div>
<ul>
<li><b>Romans — 16:23</b> — Erastus is with the church sending greetings; identified as “the city treasurer.”</li>
<li><b>2 Timothy — 4:20</b> — Paul reports that “Erastus remained at Corinth.”</li>
</ul>
<div><br></div>
<div><b><h2>4) Vital Statistics</h2></b></div>
<div><b>Historical setting (as Scripture indicates):</b><br></div>
<ul>
<li>Mid–first century AD, during Paul’s missionary activity and correspondence (Acts 19:10, 19:21–22; Rom 16:23; 2 Tim 4:20).</li>
</ul>
<div><b>Geography strongly associated:</b><br></div>
<ul>
<li><b>Ephesus / Asia</b> (context of Acts 19:22)</li>
<li><b>Macedonia</b> (destination when he is sent with Timothy, Acts 19:22)</li>
<li><b>Corinth</b> (explicitly connected, Rom 16:23; 2 Tim 4:20)</li>
</ul>
<div><b>Family and relationships (explicit in Scripture):</b><br></div>
<ul>
<li><b>Paul</b> — served as one of Paul’s “helpers” (Acts 19:22).</li>
<li><b>Timothy</b> — partnered in the assignment to Macedonia (Acts 19:22).</li>
<li><b>Gaius / Quartus and the local church</b> — part of the group sending greetings (Rom 16:23).</li>
</ul>
<div><b>Roles/offices:</b><br></div>
<ul>
<li><b>“Helper” / coworker in mission</b> (Acts 19:22).</li>
<li><b>“City treasurer”</b> (Rom 16:23). <i>(The Greek term can denote a city financial administrator; Scripture gives the title without further detail.)</i><br></li>
</ul>
<div><b>Key turning points (call/crisis/covenant moments):</b><br></div>
<ul>
<li><b>Deployment from Ephesus to Macedonia</b> — entrusted with mission movement and coordination (Acts 19:22).</li>
<li><b>Public identity alongside Christian fellowship</b> — named both by civic office and as part of Christian greetings (Rom 16:23).</li>
<li><b>Remaining at Corinth</b> — a location note that hints at ongoing responsibility or assignment (2 Tim 4:20).</li>
</ul>

<div><b><h2>6) Strengths, Weaknesses, and Heart-Level Patterns</h2></b></div>
<div><b><h2>Strengths (evidenced in passages)</h2></b></div>
<ul>
<li><b>Reliability and trustworthiness in ministry</b> — Paul can “send” him with Timothy as a helper in a strategic moment (Acts 19:21–22).</li>
<li><b>Service-minded partnership</b> — he is explicitly a “helper,” not the headline preacher; his name surfaces in logistical movement (Acts 19:22).</li>
<li><b>Integration of faith and public vocation</b> — if Rom 16:23 refers to the same man, he is known both in civic terms and as a Christian brother among the saints (Rom 16:23).</li>
</ul>
<div><b><h2>Weaknesses / sins / blind spots (what the text shows)</h2></b></div>
<ul>
<li><b>None are recorded explicitly.</b> Scripture neither praises nor rebukes Erastus for specific moral failure. We should not invent one. (Compare how Scripture <i>does</i> note failures when relevant, e.g., 2 Tim 4:10 for Demas—Erastus is not treated that way.)</li>
</ul>
<div><b><h2>Motives / fears / desires (careful inferences, anchored to text)</h2></b></div>
<ul>
<li><b>Likely desire to serve the mission faithfully</b> — inferred from being entrusted with a mission assignment (Acts 19:22).</li>
<li><b>Likely willingness to be identified with Christians publicly</b> — implied by being named in church greetings (Rom 16:23). These are reasonable inferences because the text frames him as a coworker and greeter; still, Scripture does not directly narrate his internal life.</li>
</ul>
<div><b><h2>What changes (or doesn’t) over time?</h2></b></div>
<ul>
<li><b>Continuity of association with Paul’s network</b> seems to persist across references: he is a helper (Acts 19:22), part of greeting-senders (Rom 16:23), and later still a known coworker located at Corinth (2 Tim 4:20).</li>
<li><b>His public role (if consistent across mentions)</b> suggests the gospel can shape a person’s life without necessarily removing them from ordinary employment or civic responsibility (Rom 16:23; cf. 1 Cor 7:20–24 for the broader principle).</li>
</ul>
<div><br></div>
<div><b><h2>7) Victories and Challenges</h2></b></div>
<div><b><h2>Major “wins” (with references)</h2></b></div>
<ul>
<li><b>Trusted deployment for mission expansion</b> — being sent by Paul indicates proven usefulness and dependability (Acts 19:22).</li>
<li><b>Recognized brother within the church’s fellowship</b> — participates in the network of mutual greetings and unity across congregations (Rom 16:23; cf. Rom 16:1–16).</li>
<li><b>Stability of presence in a key city (Corinth)</b> — “remained at Corinth” may imply ongoing service or responsibility there (2 Tim 4:20).</li>
</ul>
<div><b><h2>Major challenges (with references)</h2></b></div>
<ul>
<li><b>Serving in a context of opposition and upheaval</b> — Acts 19’s setting includes spiritual conflict and civic unrest (Acts 19:23–41). Even if Erastus’s specific hardships are not described, the mission environment he served in was contested (Acts 19:8–10, 19:23–27).</li>
<li><b>The cost and complexity of travel-based ministry</b> — being sent to Macedonia reflects the demands placed on coworkers in Paul’s itinerant mission (Acts 19:22; cf. 2 Cor 11:23–28 for the broader missionary context).</li>
<li><b>Operating as a believer with public responsibilities</b> — if he truly held a civic financial office, faithfulness would involve navigating public trust, integrity, and reputation (Rom 16:23; cf. Titus 2:9–10; 1 Pet 2:12).</li>
</ul>

<div><b><h2>9) Theology and Big Themes</h2></b></div>
<div><b><h2>God’s character revealed</h2></b></div>
<ul>
<li><b>God advances his mission through ordinary means and ordinary people</b> — not only apostles, but also helpers and administrators (Acts 19:22; Rom 16:23).</li>
<li><b>God builds a trans-local church</b> — congregations knit together through shared gospel identity and mutual care (Rom 16:23; cf. Rom 1:8–12).</li>
</ul>
<div><b><h2>Kingdom, discipleship, and mission</h2></b></div>
<ul>
<li><b>Team-based discipleship and mission strategy</b> — Paul sends coworkers, demonstrating that the mission is larger than one personality (Acts 19:21–22).</li>
<li><b>The gospel penetrates civic and social structures</b> — a “city treasurer” is named among believers, hinting that Christ’s lordship reaches public life (Rom 16:23; cf. 1 Pet 2:12).</li>
</ul>
<div><b><h2>Human nature: calling, faithfulness, and integrity</h2></b></div>
<ul>
<li><b>Faithfulness often looks like steady service</b> — “helper,” “greeter,” “remained”—small phrases that imply dependable presence (Acts 19:22; 2 Tim 4:20).</li>
<li><b>Holiness in vocation</b> — Scripture regularly connects Christian witness to integrity in daily work and public reputation (Rom 16:23; Titus 2:9–10; Col 3:23–24).</li>
</ul>
<div><br></div>
<div><b><h2>10) How Erastus Points Us to Christ</h2></b></div>
<div>Erastus is not a typological “office” like king or prophet, so typology should be modest. Still, his life participates in patterns that ultimately center on Jesus:</div>
<ul>
<li><b>The “sent one” pattern</b> — Erastus is sent in mission (Acts 19:22). This echoes the larger New Testament rhythm: the Father sends the Son (John 20:21), and the risen Christ sends his people (Matt 28:18–20). Erastus’s sending is one small instance of the church living out Christ’s commission.</li>
<li><b>Servant-partnership under the true Head</b> — Erastus serves as a helper rather than seeking prominence (Acts 19:22). This coheres with Christ’s teaching that greatness is expressed in service (Mark 10:42–45).</li>
<li><b>Gospel witness in ordinary callings</b> — if Erastus is indeed the “city treasurer,” his public identity alongside Christian fellowship illustrates that Jesus claims lordship over all of life—not by retreat from society but by faithful presence within it (Rom 16:23; Col 3:17).</li>
</ul>
<div><br></div>
<div><b><h2>11) Lessons and Applications (Tied to Scripture)</h2></b></div>
<ol>
<li><b>God uses “helpers,” not only leaders.</b> (Acts 19:22; cf. 1 Cor 12:12–27)</li>
<li><b>Be dependable enough to be sent.</b> (Acts 19:22; cf. Luke 16:10)</li>
<li><b>Mission advances through teams and delegated trust.</b> (Acts 19:21–22; cf. 2 Tim 2:2)</li>
<li><b>Your job title can sit under your gospel identity.</b> (Rom 16:23; cf. Col 3:23–24)</li>
<li><b>Public responsibility calls for visible integrity.</b> (Rom 16:23; cf. 1 Pet 2:12; Titus 2:9–10)</li>
<li><b>Small acts—travel, messages, presence—can be strategic obedience.</b> (Acts 19:22; cf. Rom 15:25–28)</li>
<li><b>Stay faithful in a place when needed, even when others move on.</b> (2 Tim 4:20; cf. 1 Cor 7:20–24)</li>
<li><b>Christian fellowship spans distance; cultivate it intentionally.</b> (Rom 16:23; cf. Rom 1:8–12)</li>
<li><b>Don’t assume visibility equals significance in the kingdom.</b> (Acts 19:22; cf. Mark 10:43–45)</li>
<li><b>Serve Christ in your vocation without splitting “sacred” and “secular.”</b> (Rom 16:23; Col 3:17)</li>
</ol>
<div><b>Warnings to heed (text-grounded):</b><br></div>
<ul>
<li>Don’t despise quiet roles in the body (Acts 19:22; 1 Cor 12:21–26).</li>
<li>Don’t compartmentalize holiness away from public life (Rom 16:23; 1 Pet 2:12).</li>
</ul>
<div><b>Examples to imitate:</b><br></div>
<ul>
<li>Willingness to be sent and to serve (Acts 19:22).</li>
<li>Willingness to be publicly identified with the church (Rom 16:23).</li>
</ul>

<div><b><h2>12) Discussion and Reflection Questions (10–20)</h2></b></div>
<div><b><h2>Personal reflection</h2></b></div>
<ol>
<li>What does it take to become the kind of person others can “send” with confidence (Acts 19:22)?</li>
<li>Where are you tempted to undervalue “helper” roles in ministry (Acts 19:22)?</li>
<li>If someone introduced you like Paul introduces Erastus (“city treasurer… brother”), what identities would be named first in your life (Rom 16:23)?</li>
<li>What pressures come with public responsibility, and how does Scripture call you to live before outsiders (Rom 16:23; 1 Pet 2:12)?</li>
<li>What might it look like for you to “remain” faithfully in a place or assignment God has given you (2 Tim 4:20)?</li>
</ol>
<div><b><h2>Small group discussion</h2></b></div>
<ol>
<li>Why does Paul include so many personal names and greetings in his letters (Rom 16:1–23)? What does that teach about the church?</li>
<li>How do teams and delegated tasks strengthen mission work compared to a single charismatic leader (Acts 19:21–22)?</li>
<li>How should Christians think about vocations connected to money, finance, or public trust (Rom 16:23; Luke 16:10)?</li>
</ol>
<div><b><h2>Deep study (text observation)</h2></b></div>
<ol>
<li>In Acts 19:21–22, what is Paul’s plan, and what does the sending of Timothy and Erastus accomplish?</li>
<li>In Romans 16, what do the greetings reveal about the social diversity of the early church (Rom 16:1–23)?</li>
<li>In 2 Timothy 4:9–22, what is Paul experiencing, and how do the location notes (including Erastus) contribute to the letter’s realism and urgency (2 Tim 4:20)?</li>
<li>What details are <i>not</i> given about Erastus, and how does that shape responsible interpretation? (Acts 19:22; Rom 16:23; 2 Tim 4:20)</li>
</ol>
<div><b><h2>Synthesis</h2></b></div>
<ol>
<li>What consistent portrait emerges if these references describe one man? What changes if they describe multiple men with the same name?</li>
<li>How does Erastus’s brief appearance challenge modern assumptions about “significant” ministry?</li>
<li>What practices help you integrate Christian identity with career identity (Col 3:23–24; Rom 16:23)?</li>
</ol>
