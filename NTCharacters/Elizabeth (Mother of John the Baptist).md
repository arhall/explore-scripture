<div><b><h1><br></h1></b></div>
<div><b><h1>Elizabeth (Mother of John the Baptist) — Bible Character Study</h1></b></div>
<div><b>Name note:</b> “Elizabeth” in the New Testament refers to the wife of <b>Zechariah</b> and mother of <b>John the Baptist</b> (Luke 1). There is an Old Testament figure <b>Elisheba</b> (Aaron’s wife, Exod 6:23) whose name is sometimes rendered similarly; this study focuses on <b>Luke’s Elizabeth</b>.</div>
<div><br></div>
<div><b><h2>1) Story Summary (Chronological)</h2></b></div>
<div>Elizabeth’s story is told almost entirely in the opening chapter of Luke. She is introduced as the wife of Zechariah, a priest, living “in the days of Herod, king of Judea” (Luke 1:5). Though both Elizabeth and Zechariah are described as “righteous before God” and walking blamelessly in God’s commandments, they carry a long grief: Elizabeth is barren, and both are advanced in years (Luke 1:6–7).</div>
<div>While Zechariah serves in the temple, an angel announces that their prayers have been heard and Elizabeth will bear a son who must be named <b>John</b> (Luke 1:11–13). After Zechariah returns home, Elizabeth conceives (Luke 1:23–24). She interprets this mercy as God removing her reproach among the people (Luke 1:25).</div>
<div>In Elizabeth’s sixth month, Mary (her relative) visits. When Mary greets her, Elizabeth is filled with the Holy Spirit, and the child in her womb leaps (Luke 1:39–41). Elizabeth blesses Mary, calls her “the mother of my Lord,” and commends Mary’s faith in God’s promise (Luke 1:42–45). Mary stays with Elizabeth about three months (Luke 1:56).</div>
<div>When Elizabeth gives birth, neighbors and relatives rejoice (Luke 1:57–58). At the naming/circumcision, Elizabeth insists the child is to be called <b>John</b>, even against family expectations (Luke 1:59–60). Zechariah confirms this by writing “His name is John,” and immediately his speech returns, leading to awe and widespread talk about the child’s future (Luke 1:63–66). Luke then shifts to Zechariah’s prophecy and summarizes John’s later growth (Luke 1:67–80).</div>
<div><b>Where her story begins and ends:</b> It begins at <b>Luke 1:5</b> and her direct narrative presence largely concludes after the naming of John (<b>Luke 1:57–66</b>), with the chapter continuing through Zechariah’s prophecy and John’s growth (<b>Luke 1:67–80</b>).</div>
<div><br></div>
<div><b><h2>2) What She’s Known For</h2></b></div>
<div>Elizabeth is most known as the <b>miraculously provided mother of John the Baptist</b> and as a <b>Spirit-filled witness</b> who recognizes Jesus’ identity while He is still in Mary’s womb.</div>
<div><b>Headline descriptors (3–7):</b><br></div>
<ul>
<li><b>Righteous, faithful Israelite</b> in a dark political era (Luke 1:5–6)</li>
<li><b>Barren yet not abandoned</b>—recipient of God’s merciful intervention (Luke 1:7, 24–25)</li>
<li><b>Spirit-filled speaker of blessing</b> (Luke 1:41–45)</li>
<li><b>First in Luke to call Jesus “my Lord”</b> through Mary (“mother of my Lord”) (Luke 1:43)</li>
<li><b>Humble honesty about reproach</b> and God’s removal of shame (Luke 1:25)</li>
<li><b>Courage to obey God’s word over social pressure</b> in naming John (Luke 1:59–63)</li>
<li><b>A catalyst for communal awe and renewed expectation</b> (Luke 1:65–66)</li>
</ul>
<div><br></div>
<div><b><h2>3) Where She Appears in the Bible</h2></b></div>
<div><b><h2>Primary narrative locations</h2></b></div>
<ul>
<li><b>Luke — 1:5–7</b> — Introduced with Zechariah; righteous, barren, advanced in years</li>
<li><b>Luke — 1:23–25</b> — Conceives; interprets God’s mercy in removing reproach</li>
<li><b>Luke — 1:39–45</b> — Mary visits; Elizabeth filled with the Spirit; blesses Mary; “mother of my Lord”</li>
<li><b>Luke — 1:57–66</b> — Birth and naming of John; insists on the name “John”; community awe</li>
</ul>
<div><b><h2>Secondary references (themes echoed; Elizabeth not the main actor)</h2></b></div>
<ul>
<li><b>Luke — 1:13–17</b> — Angelic promise about her son’s role (spoken to Zechariah)</li>
<li><b>Luke — 1:67–80</b> — Zechariah’s prophecy frames John’s mission; John grows (Elizabeth implied as mother, Luke 1:80)</li>
</ul>
<div><br></div>
<div><b><h2>4) Vital Statistics</h2></b></div>
<div><b>Historical setting (as Scripture indicates):</b><br></div>
<ul>
<li>“<b>In the days of Herod, king of Judea</b>” (Luke 1:5) — late Second Temple period, with priestly service functioning in Jerusalem (Luke 1:8–9).</li>
</ul>
<div><b>Geography (places strongly associated):</b><br></div>
<ul>
<li><b>Hill country of Judah</b> — Mary travels there to visit Elizabeth (Luke 1:39).</li>
<li><b>Jerusalem/Temple</b> (associated via Zechariah’s priestly service) (Luke 1:8–10).</li>
</ul>
<div><b>Family and relationships:</b><br></div>
<ul>
<li><b>Husband:</b> <b>Zechariah</b>, a priest “of the division of Abijah” (Luke 1:5, 8–9).</li>
<li><b>Lineage:</b> Elizabeth is “<b>of the daughters of Aaron</b>” (Luke 1:5) — priestly descent.</li>
<li><b>Child:</b> <b>John</b> (John the Baptist) (Luke 1:13, 57–60).</li>
<li><b>Relative:</b> <b>Mary</b> is her “relative” (Luke 1:36) (exact relationship unspecified).</li>
</ul>
<div><b>Roles/offices:</b><br></div>
<ul>
<li><b>Priestly household matriarch</b> (Luke 1:5)</li>
<li><b>Mother of a prophet/forerunner</b> (Luke 1:13–17)</li>
<li><b>Spirit-filled witness/blesser</b> (Luke 1:41–45) (not titled “prophetess,” but her speech is explicitly Spirit-prompted)</li>
</ul>
<div><b>Key turning points (call/crisis/covenant moments):</b><br></div>
<ul>
<li>Long barrenness and social reproach (Luke 1:7, 25)</li>
<li>Conception as divine mercy (Luke 1:24–25)</li>
<li>Spirit-filled recognition of Jesus and blessing of Mary (Luke 1:41–45)</li>
<li>Public obedience in naming John (Luke 1:59–63), triggering communal fear/awe (Luke 1:65–66)</li>
</ul>

<div><b><h2>6) Strengths, Weaknesses, and Heart-Level Patterns</h2></b></div>
<div><b><h2>Strengths (evidenced in the text)</h2></b></div>
<ul>
<li><b>Genuine righteousness and devotion</b> (shared with her husband): “righteous before God… walking blamelessly” (Luke 1:6).</li>
<li><b>God-centered interpretation of life</b>: she traces her changed condition to the Lord’s action (Luke 1:25).</li>
<li><b>Spirit-filled discernment and speech</b>: “filled with the Holy Spirit” and speaks blessing and truth about Mary and Jesus (Luke 1:41–45).</li>
<li><b>Courageous obedience amid social pressure</b>: she stands firm on the God-given name “John” (Luke 1:59–60).</li>
</ul>
<div><b><h2>Weaknesses / sins / blind spots (what Scripture actually shows)</h2></b></div>
<ul>
<li><b>No explicit sin of Elizabeth is recorded</b> in Luke 1. The text consistently presents her as faithful (Luke 1:6, 25, 41–45, 60).</li>
<li><b>Possible vulnerability:</b> She speaks of “reproach” (Luke 1:25), showing the emotional and social weight she bore. Any conclusions about bitterness or unbelief would go beyond what the text states.</li>
</ul>
<div><b><h2>Motives / fears / desires (careful, text-anchored inferences)</h2></b></div>
<ul>
<li><b>Desire for God’s vindication and mercy</b> is explicit in her praise: God has “looked on” her and “taken away” reproach (Luke 1:25).</li>
<li><b>Desire to honor God’s word</b> is shown in the naming moment (Luke 1:60).</li>
<li><b>Awe-filled joy and faith</b> appear in her blessing of Mary and emphasis on believing God’s fulfillment (Luke 1:42–45).</li>
</ul>
<div><b><h2>What changes (or doesn’t) over time?</h2></b></div>
<ul>
<li><b>Her circumstances change dramatically</b> (barrenness → conception → birth) (Luke 1:7, 24, 57).</li>
<li><b>Her posture stays consistent:</b> righteousness, Godward interpretation, and obedience remain steady (Luke 1:6, 25, 41–45, 60).</li>
</ul>
<div><br></div>
<div><b><h2>7) Victories and Challenges</h2></b></div>
<div><b><h2>Major “wins” (obedience, faith, influence)</h2></b></div>
<ul>
<li><b>Receives God’s mercy and bears John</b> (Luke 1:24–25, 57).</li>
<li><b>Speaks Spirit-filled blessing and Christ-centered truth</b> (Luke 1:41–45).</li>
<li><b>Models obedience over custom</b> by insisting on the revealed name (Luke 1:59–60).</li>
<li><b>Her household becomes a sign</b> that provokes reverent fear and expectation (Luke 1:65–66).</li>
</ul>
<div><b><h2>Major challenges (suffering, waiting, opposition)</h2></b></div>
<ul>
<li><b>Long-term barrenness and age-related impossibility</b> (Luke 1:7).</li>
<li><b>Social reproach/shame</b> associated with childlessness in her context (Luke 1:25).</li>
<li><b>Community/family pressure</b> around naming conventions (Luke 1:59–61).</li>
</ul>
<div><b>Cost of faithfulness:</b> Her obedience is not merely private; it requires resisting public expectations (Luke 1:60–61).</div>

<div><b><h2>9) Theology and Big Themes</h2></b></div>
<ul>
<li><b>God’s sovereignty in salvation history:</b> God initiates and orchestrates the forerunner’s birth “in the days of Herod” (Luke 1:5), showing His purposes advance even under oppressive regimes.</li>
<li><b>Mercy and reversal:</b> God “looks on” the lowly and removes reproach (Luke 1:25), a theme that harmonizes with Mary’s later praise of God exalting the humble (Luke 1:48–52).</li>
<li><b>The Holy Spirit’s role in revelation:</b> Elizabeth’s Christ-centered confession is explicitly tied to being “filled with the Holy Spirit” (Luke 1:41–43).</li>
<li><b>Promise → fulfillment:</b> Elizabeth highlights the blessedness of believing God’s word will be fulfilled (Luke 1:45), reinforcing Luke’s emphasis on reliable fulfillment (Luke 1:1–4, 45).</li>
<li><b>Preparation for the Lord:</b> Elizabeth’s life is bound to God’s preparation of John, who will “make ready for the Lord a people prepared” (Luke 1:17).</li>
</ul>
<div><br></div>
<div><b><h2>10) How Elizabeth Points Us to Christ</h2></b></div>
<ul>
<li><b>She witnesses to Christ’s lordship early:</b> Elizabeth calls Mary “the mother of <b>my Lord</b>” (Luke 1:43), recognizing Jesus’ identity before His birth and before His public ministry.</li>
<li><b>Her son is the forerunner of Christ:</b> John is appointed to go “before him… to make ready for the Lord a people prepared” (Luke 1:17). Elizabeth’s motherhood serves the coming of the King.</li>
<li><b>Incarnation joy and recognition:</b> The unborn John’s leap “for joy” at Mary’s greeting (Luke 1:44) underscores that Jesus is the promised Savior whose presence brings Spirit-born joy even before visible power or throne.</li>
<li><b>Gospel connection:</b> Elizabeth’s story teaches that salvation history advances by <b>grace</b>, not human ability—God brings life where there is barrenness and hope where there is shame (Luke 1:7, 24–25), anticipating the larger gospel pattern of God raising the dead and calling into existence what is not (cf. Rom 4:17).</li>
</ul>
<div><br></div>
<div><b><h2>11) Lessons and Applications (Scripture-tied)</h2></b></div>
<ol>
<li><b>Righteous people may still endure long sorrow</b>—faithfulness does not exempt from suffering (Luke 1:6–7).</li>
<li><b>God sees hidden reproach and acts in mercy</b> (Luke 1:25).</li>
<li><b>Delay is not denial</b>—God’s timing can be late by human expectation but perfect in His plan (Luke 1:7, 24).</li>
<li><b>The Spirit enables true confession of Christ</b> (Luke 1:41–43).</li>
<li><b>Bless others with Scripture-shaped words</b>—Elizabeth’s blessing is God-centered, promise-centered, and faith-honoring (Luke 1:42–45).</li>
<li><b>Honor faith that clings to God’s promises</b> (Luke 1:45).</li>
<li><b>Obey God’s revealed will over social pressure</b> (Luke 1:59–61).</li>
<li><b>God turns private mercy into public testimony</b> (Luke 1:65–66).</li>
<li><b>Hospitality can become a holy intersection</b>—Mary’s visit leads to encouragement and Spirit-filled joy (Luke 1:39–45, 56).</li>
<li><b>God often prepares the way quietly before He acts publicly</b>—John’s significance is recognized in infancy (Luke 1:66, 80).</li>
</ol>
<div><b><h2>Warnings to heed</h2></b></div>
<ul>
<li>Don’t measure God’s favor only by immediate outcomes (Luke 1:6–7).</li>
<li>Don’t let community expectations override God’s word (Luke 1:59–61).</li>
</ul>
<div><b><h2>Examples to imitate</h2></b></div>
<ul>
<li>Godward interpretation of suffering and deliverance (Luke 1:25).</li>
<li>Spirit-shaped encouragement of other believers (Luke 1:42–45).</li>
<li>Courageous obedience in ordinary, family-level decisions (Luke 1:60).</li>
</ul>
<div><b>Application areas</b><br></div>
<ul>
<li><b>Personal holiness:</b> faithful walking over the long haul (Luke 1:6).</li>
<li><b>Relationships:</b> blessing others’ faith; honoring God’s work in them (Luke 1:42–45).</li>
<li><b>Prayer &amp waiting:</b> persevering through years of unanswered longing (Luke 1:7, 13).</li>
<li><b>Suffering:</b> naming grief honestly (“reproach”) while trusting God’s mercy (Luke 1:25).</li>
<li><b>Leadership/influence:</b> using words to magnify Christ and strengthen faith (Luke 1:43–45).</li>
</ul>
