<div><b><h1>John (son of Zebedee)</h1></b></div>
<div><b><h1><br></h1></b></div>
<div><b><h1>John (son of Zebedee) — Bible Character Study</h1></b></div>
<div><b>Important identifiers:</b> This study concerns <b>John the apostle</b>, brother of <b>James (son of Zebedee)</b> (Matt 4:21–22; Mark 1:19–20). He is <b>not</b> John the Baptist (Matt 3:1), nor John Mark (Acts 12:12), nor “John” in other contexts unless the text clearly indicates it.</div>
<div><br></div>
<div><b><h2>1) Story Summary (chronological)</h2></b></div>
<div>John enters the biblical story as a <b>Galilean fisherman</b> working with his brother <b>James</b> and their father <b>Zebedee</b>. Jesus calls them while they are mending nets, and they <b>immediately leave</b> their boat and father to follow Him (Matt 4:21–22; Mark 1:19–20). John becomes one of the Twelve (Matt 10:2; Mark 3:17; Luke 6:14).</div>
<div>During Jesus’ ministry, John belongs to Jesus’ <b>inner circle</b> with Peter and James—present at pivotal moments such as <b>Jairus’s daughter’s raising</b> (Mark 5:37–42), the <b>Transfiguration</b> (Matt 17:1–2; Mark 9:2–4), and Jesus’ agony in <b>Gethsemane</b> (Mark 14:33–42). Early on, John’s zeal can take harsh forms: he wants to <b>forbid</b> an outsider casting out demons in Jesus’ name (Mark 9:38–41), and he and James ask to call down <b>fire</b> on a Samaritan village (Luke 9:51–56). Jesus rebukes their spirit and redirects them (Luke 9:55–56).</div>
<div>John is also marked by proximity to Jesus. At the Last Supper, a disciple described as <b>“the one whom Jesus loved”</b> reclines close to Jesus and asks about the betrayer (John 13:23–26). <b>The Gospel never names this disciple</b>, and many identify him with John son of Zebedee, but <b>that identification is an inference</b>, not explicit in the text.</div>
<div>At Jesus’ crucifixion, John is the named disciple who stands near the cross with several women, and Jesus entrusts <b>His mother</b> to him: “Behold, your mother,” and “from that hour the disciple took her to his own home” (John 19:25–27). After the resurrection, John runs to the tomb with Peter; he believes upon seeing the grave cloths (John 20:2–8). Later, at the Sea of Tiberias, he is the first to recognize the risen Jesus: “It is the Lord!” (John 21:7).</div>
<div>In Acts, John appears as a leading apostolic witness in Jerusalem alongside Peter. Through them, Jesus heals a lame man at the temple (Acts 3:1–10), and they preach Christ’s resurrection and are arrested and questioned (Acts 4:1–22). The church prays, and they continue speaking the word with boldness (Acts 4:23–31). John is then sent with Peter to Samaria to confirm the gospel’s advance and to pray for believers to receive the Holy Spirit (Acts 8:14–17).</div>
<div>John’s story <b>as a named narrative figure</b> continues in the NT epistles primarily as a significant leader: Paul later refers to “James and Cephas and John” as those “reputed to be pillars” who gave the right hand of fellowship (Gal 2:9). Beyond that, Scripture gives fewer biographical details.</div>
<div>Finally, <b>Revelation</b> is written by someone named <b>John</b> (Rev 1:1, 1:4, 1:9; 22:8). Whether that John is <i>the same</i> John son of Zebedee is <b>debated</b>; Scripture itself does not explicitly identify him as “son of Zebedee,” so we should label that as uncertain (Rev 1:9).</div>
<div><b>Where his story begins:</b> his call from fishing to discipleship (Matt 4:21–22).<b>Where it ends (in Scripture):</b> John appears as an apostolic leader in Acts (Acts 8:14–17) and is referenced as a “pillar” (Gal 2:9). Revelation’s author “John” may be him, but that identification is uncertain (Rev 1:9).</div>
<div><br></div>
<div><b><h2>2) What He’s Known For</h2></b></div>
<div>John is most known as one of Jesus’ <b>closest apostles</b>, an early <b>church leader</b>, and a key <b>witness to Christ</b>—with a notable transformation from sharp zeal to mature, Christ-shaped love (Luke 9:54–56; Acts 4:13, 4:19–20).</div>
<div><b>Headline descriptors (3–7):</b><br></div>
<ul>
<li><b>Called from the nets</b>—left everything to follow Jesus (Matt 4:21–22).</li>
<li><b>“Son of Thunder”</b> (with James)—zealous temperament redirected by Christ (Mark 3:17; Luke 9:54–56).</li>
<li><b>Inner-circle eyewitness</b>—present at major revelations of Jesus’ glory (Mark 5:37; Matt 17:1–2; Mark 14:33).</li>
<li><b>Faithful at the cross</b>—entrusted with Mary’s care (John 19:25–27).</li>
<li><b>Bold public witness</b>—preached under pressure with Peter (Acts 4:13, 4:19–20).</li>
<li><b>Bridge-builder in mission</b>—prayed for Samaritans to receive the Spirit (Acts 8:14–17).</li>
<li><b>Associated with “truth and love” teaching</b> in Johannine letters (e.g., 1 John 1:1–3; 1 John 4:7–12; 2 John 1–6). <i>(Authorship attribution is traditional; Scripture does not explicitly name the author inside 1–3 John, though the content is canonical.)</i><br></li>
</ul>
<div><br></div>
<div><b><h2>3) Where He Appears in the Bible</h2></b></div>
<div><b><h2>Primary narrative locations</h2></b></div>
<ul>
<li><b>Matthew</b> — Matt 4:21–22; 10:2; 17:1–2; 20:20–28; 26:37 — Called; listed among Twelve; transfiguration; ambition corrected; Gethsemane.</li>
<li><b>Mark</b> — Mark 1:19–20; 3:17; 5:37; 9:2–4; 9:38–41; 10:35–45; 14:33 — Called; “Boanerges”; inner circle; sectarian zeal corrected; seats request corrected; Gethsemane.</li>
<li><b>Luke</b> — Luke 5:10; 6:14; 8:51; 9:49–56; 22:8 — Called (with partners); listed; inner circle; fire-from-heaven impulse rebuked; prepares Passover.</li>
<li><b>John</b> — John 13:23–26; 19:25–27; 20:2–8; 21:7, 21:20–24 — “beloved disciple” (unnamed); at cross; at empty tomb; recognizes Jesus; testimony reference.</li>
<li><b>Acts</b> — Acts 3:1–10; 4:1–22; 8:14–17 — Temple miracle and preaching; trial and boldness; Samaria mission.</li>
</ul>
<div><b><h2>Secondary references (later mentions / summaries)</h2></b></div>
<ul>
<li><b>Galatians</b> — Gal 2:9 — “John” named among reputed “pillars” who affirm Gentile mission.</li>
<li><b>Revelation</b> — Rev 1:1, 1:4, 1:9; 22:8 — “John” as recipient/writer; identity with son of Zebedee is <b>uncertain</b> in Scripture.</li>
</ul>
<div><br></div>
<div><b><h2>4) Vital Statistics</h2></b></div>
<ul>
<li><b>Historical setting:</b> First-century Judaism under Roman rule; Jesus’ ministry and the early church era (implicit across the Gospels; Acts 1–8).</li>
<li><b>Geography:</b><br></li>
<ul>
<li><b>Galilee</b> (calling context; fishing life) (Matt 4:18–22).</li>
<li><b>Jerusalem / temple</b> (apostolic witness and conflict) (Acts 3:1–2; 4:1–3).</li>
<li><b>Samaria</b> (mission extension) (Acts 8:14–17).</li>
<li><b>Patmos</b> (if Revelation’s John is the same person; text only says “I, John… on the island called Patmos”) (Rev 1:9).</li>
</ul>
<li><b>Family &amp relationships:</b><br></li>
<ul>
<li>Father: <b>Zebedee</b> (Matt 4:21; Mark 1:19–20).</li>
<li>Brother: <b>James (son of Zebedee)</b> (Matt 4:21; Mark 3:17).</li>
<li>Business partners with Simon and Andrew (Luke 5:10).</li>
<li>Close ministry relationships: <b>Peter</b> (Acts 3:1; 4:13), and (earlier) <b>James</b> (Mark 5:37).</li>
<li>Mary (Jesus’ mother) entrusted to his care (John 19:26–27).</li>
<li>Mother’s identity (often inferred as “the mother of the sons of Zebedee,” Matt 20:20; possibly “Salome,” Mark 15:40) is <b>not explicitly stated</b> as one person with a single name across all texts; treat with caution.</li>
</ul>
<li><b>Roles/offices:</b> Disciple/apostle (Matt 10:2), eyewitness (Acts 4:20), early church leader (Acts 8:14), “pillar” (Gal 2:9).</li>
<li><b>Key turning points:</b><br></li>
<ul>
<li>Call to follow Jesus (Matt 4:21–22).</li>
<li>Rebukes that reshape zeal (Luke 9:54–56; Mark 9:38–41).</li>
<li>Witness of Jesus’ glory (Matt 17:1–2).</li>
<li>Faithfulness at the cross (John 19:25–27).</li>
<li>Boldness in persecution (Acts 4:13, 4:19–20).</li>
<li>Mission expansion to Samaria (Acts 8:14–17).</li>
</ul>
</ul>

<div><b><h2>6) Strengths, Weaknesses, and Heart-Level Patterns</h2></b></div>
<div><b><h2>Strengths (evidenced in passages)</h2></b></div>
<ul>
<li><b>Responsive obedience / decisiveness:</b> leaves family livelihood immediately (Matt 4:21–22).</li>
<li><b>Willingness to be near Jesus in crucial hours:</b> present in inner-circle moments (Mark 5:37; Matt 17:1–2; Mark 14:33).</li>
<li><b>Faithful presence at the cross:</b> remains when others flee; receives Mary as responsibility (John 19:25–27).</li>
<li><b>Spiritual discernment:</b> recognizes Jesus at the Sea (John 21:7).</li>
<li><b>Courage under pressure:</b> seen as bold despite being “uneducated, common men” (Acts 4:13) and refuses silence (Acts 4:19–20).</li>
<li><b>Mission-minded unity:</b> supports Samaritans’ inclusion and Spirit reception (Acts 8:14–17).</li>
</ul>
<div><b><h2>Weaknesses / sins / blind spots (with brief context)</h2></b></div>
<ul>
<li><b>Sectarian / possessive instinct:</b> tries to stop an outsider ministering in Jesus’ name (Mark 9:38–39).</li>
<li><b>Vindictive zeal:</b> wants judgment-fire on Samaritans; Jesus rebukes (Luke 9:54–56).</li>
<li><b>Ambition for status:</b> seeks seats of honor; Jesus teaches servanthood and the cross (Mark 10:35–45).</li>
</ul>
<div><b><h2>Motives, fears, desires (careful inferences anchored to text)</h2></b></div>
<ul>
<li><b>Desire for honor and closeness to power:</b> implied by the request for right/left hand in glory (Mark 10:35–37).</li>
<li><b>Desire to protect Jesus’ movement and “our group”:</b> implied by forbidding the outsider (Mark 9:38).</li>
<li><b>Desire for justice and zeal for Messiah’s dignity:</b> implied by calling down fire when rejected (Luke 9:53–55), though Jesus shows their approach is misplaced.</li>
</ul>
<div><b><h2>What changes (or doesn’t) over time?</h2></b></div>
<ul>
<li><b>What changes:</b> John’s early “thunder” impulses (Luke 9:54–56) are progressively redirected into <b>Spirit-empowered witness and leadership</b> marked by boldness and unity (Acts 4:13–20; 8:14–17).</li>
<li><b>What doesn’t change:</b> He remains a man of <b>strong convictions</b>—first expressed as misguided zeal, later as steadfast testimony to Christ in the face of opposition (Acts 4:19–20).</li>
</ul>
<div><br></div>
<div><b><h2>7) Victories and Challenges</h2></b></div>
<div><b><h2>Major “wins” (obedience, influence, faithfulness)</h2></b></div>
<ul>
<li><b>Immediate discipleship response:</b> leaves boat and father (Matt 4:21–22).</li>
<li><b>Witness of Christ’s glory and authority:</b> Jairus’s daughter; Transfiguration (Mark 5:37–42; Matt 17:1–2).</li>
<li><b>Faithful stewardship:</b> cares for Mary at Jesus’ request (John 19:26–27).</li>
<li><b>Resurrection faith and testimony:</b> believes at the empty tomb (John 20:8).</li>
<li><b>Bold gospel proclamation under threat:</b> refuses to stop speaking (Acts 4:19–20).</li>
<li><b>Promotes unity in mission expansion:</b> supports Samaritans’ inclusion (Acts 8:14–17).</li>
</ul>
<div><b><h2>Major challenges (temptations, failures, suffering, opposition)</h2></b></div>
<ul>
<li><b>Misguided zeal and harshness:</b> fire-from-heaven impulse (Luke 9:54–56).</li>
<li><b>Pride/ambition:</b> quest for prominence in the kingdom (Mark 10:35–45).</li>
<li><b>Persecution and legal intimidation:</b> arrest and interrogation by authorities (Acts 4:1–22).</li>
<li><b>Cost of faithfulness:</b> public hostility and restrictions; risk for continuing witness (Acts 4:18–20).</li>
<li><b>(If Revelation’s John is him—uncertain):</b> suffering/exile on Patmos “on account of the word of God and the testimony of Jesus” (Rev 1:9).</li>
</ul>
<div><b>Cost highlighted:</b> In Acts, John’s faithfulness is not abstract—he faces real institutional power and chooses obedience to God over man (Acts 4:19–20).</div>

<div><b><h2>9) Theology and Big Themes</h2></b></div>
<div><b><h2>God’s character revealed</h2></b></div>
<ul>
<li><b>Jesus’ sovereign authority</b> over death and disease (Mark 5:41–42; Acts 3:6–8).</li>
<li><b>God’s patience in discipling flawed people</b>—Jesus rebukes and reshapes John (Luke 9:55–56; Mark 10:42–45).</li>
<li><b>God’s mission mercy</b>—the gospel moves beyond Jewish boundaries to Samaritans (Acts 8:14–17).</li>
</ul>
<div><b><h2>Discipleship: from ambition to servanthood</h2></b></div>
<div>John’s desire for status (Mark 10:35–37) becomes a stage for Jesus’ theology of greatness: leadership is cruciform—service, not domination (Mark 10:42–45). John’s later courage in Acts reflects a man increasingly shaped by that vision (Acts 4:19–20).</div>
<div><b><h2>Witness: seeing Christ and speaking Christ</h2></b></div>
<div>John’s life is framed by <b>eyewitness proximity</b> (Mark 5:37; John 19:25–27; John 20:8) and the apostolic vocation to <b>testify publicly</b> (Acts 4:20). The “we saw/heard” theme is central to apostolic authority (cf. Acts 1:21–22; 1 John 1:1–3).</div>
<div><b><h2>Kingdom expansion and church unity</h2></b></div>
<div>John participates in a crucial theological moment: Samaritan inclusion and Spirit-confirmation, safeguarding the oneness of the church under one gospel (Acts 8:14–17; cf. Acts 1:8).</div>
<div><b><h2>Human nature: zeal without knowledge vs. Spirit-formed love</h2></b></div>
<div>John’s “thunder” moments show how sincere zeal can misfire (Luke 9:54–56). The Spirit then produces boldness and (in Johannine teaching) love rooted in God’s own nature and Christ’s saving work (Acts 4:31; 1 John 4:10–12).</div>
<div><br></div>
<div><b><h2>10) How John Points Us to Christ</h2></b></div>
<div><b><h2>1) Apostolic witness to the true Christ</h2></b></div>
<div>John’s nearness to Jesus’ glory (Matt 17:1–2), suffering (John 19:25–27), and resurrection (John 20:8) highlights Christianity’s historical, embodied claim: the gospel rests on God’s acted revelation in Christ, testified by witnesses (Acts 4:20; 1 John 1:1–3).</div>
<div><b><h2>2) The transformation Christ accomplishes in disciples</h2></b></div>
<div>John’s arc—harsh zeal (Luke 9:54–56) to bold gospel witness and unity-building mission (Acts 4:19–20; 8:14–17)—exemplifies Christ’s sanctifying power. Jesus does not merely recruit leaders; He <b>remakes</b> them (Mark 10:42–45).</div>
<div><b><h2>3) Servant-kingdom logic (anti-triumphalism)</h2></b></div>
<div>John’s ambition (Mark 10:35–37) becomes a teaching moment about Jesus’ own mission: “the Son of Man came not to be served but to serve, and to give his life as a ransom for many” (Mark 10:45). John’s story therefore points beyond John to <b>Christ’s ransom-giving</b> as the center of greatness.</div>
<div><b><h2>4) Gospel pattern: mercy over judgment</h2></b></div>
<div>When John wants judgment on rejecters (Luke 9:54), Jesus embodies the gospel’s priority: not immediate destruction, but saving mission (Luke 9:55–56; cf. Luke 19:10). John’s correction points to Christ as the One who bears judgment to extend mercy (Isa 53:5–6; Mark 10:45).</div>

<div><b><h2>11) Lessons and Applications (tied to Scripture)</h2></b></div>
<ol>
<li><b>Immediate obedience honors Christ’s call.</b> (Matt 4:21–22)</li>
<li><b>Zeal must be governed by Jesus’ mission, not your temper.</b> (Luke 9:54–56)</li>
<li><b>God can use imperfect disciples without endorsing their blind spots.</b> (Mark 9:38–41)</li>
<li><b>Greatness in Christ’s kingdom is measured by service and sacrifice.</b> (Mark 10:42–45)</li>
<li><b>Stay near the cross—discipleship includes costly faithfulness.</b> (John 19:25–27)</li>
<li><b>True faith faces the empty tomb honestly and believes God’s act.</b> (John 20:8)</li>
<li><b>Discernment grows with intimacy: learn to say, “It is the Lord.”</b> (John 21:7)</li>
<li><b>Boldness is Spirit-enabled, not personality-driven.</b> (Acts 4:13, 4:31)</li>
<li><b>When pressured to be silent, obey God rather than man.</b> (Acts 4:19–20)</li>
<li><b>Christ’s mission breaks barriers; pursue unity across divides.</b> (Acts 8:14–17)</li>
<li><b>Stewardship can be personal and practical, not only “ministry platform.”</b> (John 19:26–27)</li>
<li><b>Let God redirect your intensity into faithful testimony.</b> (Luke 9:55–56; Acts 4:20)</li>
</ol>
<div><b>Warnings to heed</b><br></div>
<ul>
<li>Don’t confuse <b>being “right”</b> or “protecting the group” with faithfulness to Jesus (Mark 9:38–40).</li>
<li>Don’t baptize personal outrage as holy judgment (Luke 9:54–56).</li>
<li>Don’t seek influence without embracing the cross-shaped path (Mark 10:42–45).</li>
</ul>
<div><b>Examples to imitate</b><br></div>
<ul>
<li>Swift obedience (Matt 4:22).</li>
<li>Staying near suffering saints and suffering Savior (John 19:25–27).</li>
<li>Courage and clarity under pressure (Acts 4:19–20).</li>
<li>Commitment to gospel unity (Acts 8:14–17).</li>
</ul>

</ol>

<li>How does Jesus’ teaching on greatness confront modern leadership models (Mark 10:42–45)?</li>
<li>What pressures tempt Christians to silence today, and what does Acts 4:19–20 require of us?</li>
<li>What does John’s participation in Samaria teach about unity and the Spirit’s work (Acts 8:14–17)?</li>

<ol>
<li>Compare Mark 9:38–41 and Luke 9:54–56: what do these episodes reveal about John’s instincts and Jesus’ priorities?</li>
<li>In John 19:25–27, what does Jesus’ action teach about family, community, and discipleship obligations?</li>
<li>In John 20:2–8, what exactly does John “see,” and how does the text describe his believing?</li>
<li>In Acts 4:13, what surprises the authorities, and what explanation does the passage imply?</li>
<li>Trace how Acts 3–4 keeps redirecting attention from the miracle to Jesus (Acts 3:12–16; 4:10–12). What does that teach about ministry “success”?</li>
<li>In Acts 8:14–17, why might God have providentially involved Peter and John in Samaria at this moment?</li>
</ol>
