<div><b><h1>Paul (Saul of Tarsus) — Bible Character Study</h1></b></div>
<div><b><h2>1) Story Summary (chronological)</h2></b></div>
<div>Paul first appears as <b>Saul</b>, a zealous Pharisee involved in the persecution of the early church. He guards the garments of those who stone Stephen (Acts 7:58) and becomes a leading aggressor against believers, ravaging the church and imprisoning Christians (Acts 8:1–3).</div>
<div>On the road to Damascus—armed with authority to arrest disciples—Saul is confronted by the risen Jesus (“I am Jesus, whom you are persecuting”), blinded, and led into the city (Acts 9:1–9). Through Ananias, Saul receives sight, is filled with the Holy Spirit, and is baptized (Acts 9:10–18). He immediately begins proclaiming Jesus as the Son of God (Acts 9:20–22). After early opposition and escape (Acts 9:23–25), Saul spends time in Jerusalem and is introduced to the apostles through Barnabas (Acts 9:26–28), but opposition drives him away to Tarsus (Acts 9:29–30).</div>
<div>From Antioch, Barnabas brings Saul into ministry; for a year they teach the church there (Acts 11:25–26). The Holy Spirit then sets apart Barnabas and Saul for mission (Acts 13:1–3). Paul’s life unfolds largely through <b>Acts 13–28</b>: missionary journeys, church planting, pastoral strengthening of believers, theological conflict over the law and Gentile inclusion, repeated persecution, and ultimately imprisonment that becomes a platform for gospel witness “to the end of the earth” (Acts 1:8; Acts 28:30–31).</div>
<div>Paul’s story, as narrated in Scripture, <b>begins</b> at Stephen’s martyrdom and the persecution of the church (Acts 7:58; Acts 8:1–3) and <b>ends</b> with Paul under house arrest in Rome, welcoming visitors and proclaiming the kingdom of God and Christ “with all boldness and without hindrance” (Acts 28:30–31). (What happened after Acts 28 is <b>not</b> recorded in Scripture.)</div>
<div><br></div>
<div><b><h2>2) What He’s Known For</h2></b></div>
<div>Paul is most known as the <b>apostle to the Gentiles</b>, a former persecutor transformed by sovereign grace into a premier missionary-theologian and church planter (Acts 9:15; Rom 11:13; Gal 1:15–16).</div>
<div><b>Headlines</b><br></div>
<ul>
<li><b>Former persecutor turned preacher</b> by Christ’s direct intervention (Acts 9:1–22; 1 Tim 1:12–16)</li>
<li><b>Apostle to the Gentiles</b> by divine appointment (Acts 9:15; Rom 15:15–21)</li>
<li><b>Church planter and missionary</b> across the Mediterranean world (Acts 13–21)</li>
<li><b>Suffering servant of Christ</b> who embraces affliction for the gospel (Acts 9:16; 2 Cor 11:23–28)</li>
<li><b>Theologian of grace and justification by faith</b> (Rom 3:21–28; Gal 2:15–21)</li>
<li><b>Pastoral builder</b> who labors for maturity in Christ (Col 1:28–29; 1 Thess 2:7–12)</li>
<li><b>Prolific letter-writer</b> whose epistles shape the church’s doctrine and life (e.g., Rom 1:1; 1 Cor 1:1; Gal 1:1)</li>
</ul>
<div><br></div>
<div><b><h2>3) Where Paul Appears in the Bible</h2></b></div>
<div><b><h2>Primary narrative locations (main story)</h2></b></div>
<ul>
<li><b>Acts — 7:58; 8:1–3</b> — Saul appears as a persecutor connected to Stephen’s death and the scattered church</li>
<li><b>Acts — 9:1–31</b> — Damascus road encounter, conversion, early preaching, early opposition</li>
<li><b>Acts — 11:25–30; 12:25</b> — Antioch ministry begins; relief visit</li>
<li><b>Acts — 13:1–14:28</b> — First missionary journey (Cyprus, Pisidian Antioch, Iconium, Lystra, Derbe)</li>
<li><b>Acts — 15:1–35</b> — Jerusalem Council: Gentiles, circumcision, and gospel clarity</li>
<li><b>Acts — 15:36–18:22</b> — Second journey (Macedonia/Greece), Philippi–Thessalonica–Berea–Athens–Corinth</li>
<li><b>Acts — 18:23–21:16</b> — Third journey (Ephesus-centered), widespread teaching and church strengthening</li>
<li><b>Acts — 21:17–28:31</b> — Arrest, trials (Jewish and Roman), voyage, shipwreck, Rome imprisonment</li>
</ul>
<div><b><h2>Secondary references (letters + later mentions)</h2></b></div>
<div>(These are writings <b>from</b> Paul or references <b>to</b> him.)</div>
<ul>
<li><b>Romans — 1:1; 15:15–33</b> — Paul’s gospel, mission aims, planned travel</li>
<li><b>1 Corinthians — 1:1; 9:1–23; 15:1–11</b> — apostleship, mission flexibility, resurrection gospel</li>
<li><b>2 Corinthians — 1:1; 11:23–12:10</b> — suffering catalog; “thorn”; power in weakness</li>
<li><b>Galatians — 1:11–2:21</b> — calling, Jerusalem interactions, justification by faith</li>
<li><b>Ephesians — 1:1; 3:1–13</b> — stewardship of grace to Gentiles; mystery of Christ</li>
<li><b>Philippians — 1:12–26; 3:4–11</b> — imprisonment for gospel; loss for Christ</li>
<li><b>Colossians — 1:24–2:5; 4:7–18</b> — ministry for maturity; co-laborers named</li>
<li><b>1 Thessalonians — 1:1; 2:1–12</b> — pastoral ministry model; affection and exhortation</li>
<li><b>2 Thessalonians — 1:1; 2:13–17</b> — encouragement amid persecution; steadying doctrine</li>
<li><b>1 Timothy — 1:12–17; 4:6–16</b> — mercy to a former blasphemer; ministry charge</li>
<li><b>2 Timothy — 1:8–14; 4:6–18</b> — suffering, endurance; “poured out” near end of race</li>
<li><b>Titus — 1:1–5</b> — church order and sound doctrine</li>
<li><b>Philemon — 1–25</b> — gospel reconciliation (Onesimus)</li>
<li><b>2 Peter — 3:15–16</b> — Peter references “our beloved brother Paul” and his letters</li>
</ul>
<div>Note: <b>Hebrews</b> does <i>not</i> name Paul. Any claim that Paul authored Hebrews is a <b>traditional hypothesis</b>, not an explicit biblical statement.</div>
<div><br></div>
<div><b><h2>4) Vital Statistics</h2></b></div>
<ul>
<li><b>Historical setting:</b> Early Roman Empire; post-resurrection church expansion (Acts 1–28). Paul is active from the early years after Stephen (Acts 7–8) through the period of Roman imprisonment (Acts 28).</li>
<li><b>Geography strongly associated:</b><br></li>
<ul>
<li><b>Tarsus</b> (birth/citizenship context) (Acts 21:39; Acts 22:3)</li>
<li><b>Jerusalem</b> (training; persecution; arrests) (Acts 22:3–5; Acts 21:17–33)</li>
<li><b>Damascus</b> (conversion) (Acts 9:1–19)</li>
<li><b>Antioch</b> (launching base) (Acts 11:25–26; Acts 13:1–3)</li>
<li><b>Asia Minor &amp Aegean world</b> (Pisidian Antioch, Iconium, Lystra, Derbe, Troas, Philippi, Thessalonica, Berea, Athens, Corinth, Ephesus) (Acts 13–20)</li>
<li><b>Rome</b> (imprisonment; witness) (Acts 28:14–31)</li>
</ul>
<li><b>Family &amp relationships (explicit and prominent):</b><br></li>
<ul>
<li><b>Mentors/associations:</b> Gamaliel (training) (Acts 22:3)</li>
<li><b>Key partners:</b> Barnabas (Acts 11:25–26; Acts 13:1–3), Silas (Acts 15:40), Timothy (Acts 16:1–3), Luke (implied “we” sections; explicit in Col 4:14; Phlm 24), Titus (Gal 2:1–3; Titus 1:4)</li>
<li><b>Coworkers:</b> Priscilla &amp Aquila (Acts 18:1–3, 18–26; Rom 16:3–5), Epaphroditus (Phil 2:25), Tychicus (Eph 6:21–22; Col 4:7–8), Onesimus (Phlm 10–16)</li>
<li><b>Apostolic relationships:</b> Peter/Cephas (Gal 1:18; Gal 2:7–14), James (Gal 1:19; Acts 15:13), John (Gal 2:9)</li>
<li><b>Opponents:</b> hostile crowds and leaders (Acts 13:45–50; Acts 19:23–41; Acts 21:27–36), false teachers/Judaizers (Gal 1:6–9; Phil 3:2), Alexander (2 Tim 4:14–15)</li>
</ul>
<li><b>Roles/offices:</b><br></li>
<ul>
<li><b>Apostle of Jesus Christ</b> (Rom 1:1; 1 Cor 9:1–2)</li>
<li><b>Chosen instrument to the Gentiles</b> (Acts 9:15; Rom 11:13)</li>
<li><b>Evangelist/missionary &amp church planter</b> (Acts 13–14; Acts 18–20; Rom 15:20)</li>
<li><b>Teacher and theologian</b> (Acts 19:9–10; 2 Tim 1:11)</li>
<li><b>Pastor-shepherd through letters and visits</b> (1 Thess 2:7–12; 2 Cor 11:28–29)</li>
</ul>
<li><b>Key turning points:</b><br></li>
<ul>
<li>Conversion/commission (Acts 9:1–19; Gal 1:15–16)</li>
<li>First major mission sending from Antioch (Acts 13:1–3)</li>
<li>Jerusalem Council clarification of the gospel for Gentiles (Acts 15:1–35; Gal 2:1–10)</li>
<li>Macedonia call (expansion into Europe) (Acts 16:9–10)</li>
<li>Ephesus ministry and widespread regional impact (Acts 19:8–10)</li>
<li>Arrest in Jerusalem and witness through trials (Acts 21–26)</li>
<li>Voyage, shipwreck, arrival in Rome, bold witness under custody (Acts 27–28)</li>
</ul>
</ul>

<div><b><h2>6) Strengths, Weaknesses, and Heart-Level Patterns</h2></b></div>
<div><b><h2>Strengths (with textual evidence)</h2></b></div>
<ul>
<li><b>Courage and single-minded mission</b>: He counts his life of no value compared to finishing the ministry received from the Lord (Acts 20:24; cf. Phil 1:12–14).</li>
<li><b>Gospel clarity and doctrinal precision</b>: He defends justification by faith apart from works of the law (Rom 3:21–28; Gal 2:15–21).</li>
<li><b>Pastoral affection and sacrificial care</b>: He describes gentle, parental ministry—sharing not only the gospel but his own life (1 Thess 2:7–12; cf. 2 Cor 11:28–29).</li>
<li><b>Humility rooted in grace</b>: He calls himself “least of the apostles” and attributes labor to the grace of God (1 Cor 15:9–10; Eph 3:7–8).</li>
<li><b>Endurance in suffering</b>: He catalogs beatings, imprisonments, dangers, and hardship (2 Cor 11:23–28), yet persists in ministry (2 Tim 2:8–10).</li>
<li><b>Strategic missionary ambition</b>: He aims to preach where Christ has not been named (Rom 15:20–21).</li>
</ul>
<div><b><h2>Weaknesses / sins / blind spots (with context)</h2></b></div>
<ul>
<li><b>Violent opposition to Christ and the church (pre-conversion)</b>: Saul “ravaging the church” and imprisoning believers (Acts 8:3), later confessing he was “a blasphemer, persecutor, and insolent opponent” (1 Tim 1:13).</li>
<li><b>Relational rupture in leadership</b>: The “sharp disagreement” with Barnabas over John Mark (Acts 15:36–40) shows real conflict even among mature leaders.</li>
<li><b>Former confidence in flesh (pre-conversion)</b>: He had grounds for boasting in law-keeping and zeal, yet counts it loss for Christ (Phil 3:4–9). This reveals a deep heart-level shift from self-righteousness to Christ-righteousness.</li>
</ul>
<div><b><h2>Motives / fears / desires (careful inference anchored to text)</h2></b></div>
<ul>
<li><b>Desire to magnify Christ and finish the race</b>: “Christ will be honored in my body… to live is Christ” (Phil 1:20–21); “finish my course” (Acts 20:24).</li>
<li><b>Burden for the church’s stability</b>: “anxiety for all the churches” (2 Cor 11:28) and intense concern over false gospels (Gal 1:6–9).</li>
<li><b>Holy fear of disqualification</b>: He disciplines himself “lest… I myself should be disqualified” (1 Cor 9:27).</li>
<li><b>Longing for Israel’s salvation</b>: “great sorrow and unceasing anguish” for his kinsmen (Rom 9:1–3; Rom 10:1).</li>
</ul>
<div><b><h2>What changes (or doesn’t) over time?</h2></b></div>
<ul>
<li><b>Radical change:</b> From persecutor to apostle; from confidence in the flesh to boasting only in Christ (Acts 8:3 → Acts 9:20; Phil 3:4–9; Gal 6:14).</li>
<li><b>Continuity redirected:</b> His intense zeal remains, but it is transformed—no longer against Jesus, but for Jesus and his gospel (Phil 3:6; Acts 20:24).</li>
</ul>
<div><br></div>
<div><b><h2>7) Victories and Challenges</h2></b></div>
<div><b><h2>Major “wins” (obedience, leadership, influence)</h2></b></div>
<ul>
<li><b>Faithful obedience to Christ’s commission</b> to carry Jesus’ name to Gentiles and kings (Acts 9:15; Acts 26:19–20).</li>
<li><b>Gospel expansion through church planting</b> across regions (Acts 13–14; Acts 16–18; Acts 19).</li>
<li><b>Doctrinal preservation of the gospel of grace</b> at the Jerusalem Council (Acts 15:1–35; Gal 2:1–10).</li>
<li><b>Evangelistic fruit through suffering</b>: Philippian jailer converted amid imprisonment (Acts 16:25–34); Paul’s chains advance the gospel (Phil 1:12–14).</li>
<li><b>Formation of leaders and co-laborers</b> (Timothy, Titus, others) (Acts 16:1–3; 2 Tim 2:2; Titus 1:5).</li>
</ul>
<div><b><h2>Major challenges (temptations, failures, suffering, opposition)</h2></b></div>
<ul>
<li><b>Repeated persecution and physical suffering</b> (Acts 14:19; Acts 16:22–24; 2 Cor 11:23–28).</li>
<li><b>Internal church conflict and false teaching pressures</b> (Gal 1:6–9; 2 Cor 11:3–4).</li>
<li><b>“Thorn in the flesh”</b> and ongoing weakness, met by Christ’s sufficient grace (2 Cor 12:7–10).</li>
<li><b>Legal entanglements and unjust trials</b> after Jerusalem arrest (Acts 21–26).</li>
<li><b>Relational strain in ministry partnerships</b> (Barnabas/Mark episode) (Acts 15:36–40).</li>
</ul>
<div><b>Cost of faithfulness:</b> Paul explicitly frames suffering as part of his calling (Acts 9:16) and as a participation in Christ’s sufferings (Phil 3:10; cf. 2 Tim 1:8).</div>

<div><b><h2>9) Theology and Big Themes</h2></b></div>
<ul>
<li><b>Sovereign grace in salvation:</b> God intervenes decisively—calling the undeserving (Acts 9:1–16; Gal 1:15; 1 Tim 1:13–16).</li>
<li><b>Union with Christ:</b> Paul’s life is redefined as “in Christ” (Gal 2:20; Rom 8:1; 2 Cor 5:17).</li>
<li><b>Justification by faith:</b> Right standing comes through faith, not law-works (Rom 3:21–28; Gal 2:16).</li>
<li><b>New covenant people of God:</b> Jew and Gentile united in one body, one new humanity (Eph 2:11–22; Gal 3:28–29).</li>
<li><b>Mission to the nations:</b> The gospel fulfills the worldwide promise and prophetic trajectory (Acts 13:47; Rom 15:8–21; cf. Gen 12:3 as background).</li>
<li><b>Suffering as normal for ministry:</b> Affliction is not accidental but appointed and fruitful (Acts 9:16; Phil 1:12–14; 2 Tim 2:9–10).</li>
<li><b>Resurrection-centered hope:</b> Ethics and endurance flow from resurrection reality (1 Cor 15:12–58; Phil 3:10–11).</li>
<li><b>God’s glory as the end:</b> Theology culminates in worship (Rom 11:33–36).</li>
</ul>
<div><br></div>
<div><b><h2>10) How Paul Points Us to Christ</h2></b></div>
<ul>
<li><b>Christ’s sovereign mercy on enemies:</b> Paul’s conversion dramatizes Jesus’ authority to save rebels and make them servants (Acts 9:1–16; 1 Tim 1:15–16). Paul becomes a living “pattern” of Christ’s perfect patience (1 Tim 1:16).</li>
<li><b>Union with Christ in death and life:</b> Paul’s “crucified with Christ” confession centers identity, sanctification, and ministry on Jesus (Gal 2:20; cf. Rom 6:3–11).</li>
<li><b>A “suffering messenger” pattern:</b> Paul’s call explicitly includes suffering (Acts 9:16). His hardships display that gospel power is God’s, not the messenger’s (2 Cor 4:7–12; 2 Cor 12:9–10). This echoes the cruciform pattern of Christ’s strength through weakness (cf. 2 Cor 13:4).</li>
<li><b>Ministry of reconciliation grounded in the cross:</b> Paul’s mission is explicitly Christ-centered: God reconciles through Christ and entrusts the message to ambassadors (2 Cor 5:18–21).</li>
<li><b>Scripture fulfillment and resurrection proclamation:</b> Paul frames his preaching as consistent with “what the prophets and Moses said would come to pass,” including the Christ’s suffering and resurrection (Acts 26:22–23; cf. Isa 53; Ps 16:10 as apostolic logic elsewhere).</li>
</ul>
<div>Typology caution: Paul is not an “office” like king or priest that directly types Christ. The clearest biblical connection is <b>pattern</b> (conversion by grace), <b>union</b> (life in Christ), and <b>cruciform mission</b> (suffering service that magnifies Jesus).</div>

<div><b><h2>11) Lessons and Applications (tied to Scripture)</h2></b></div>
<ol>
<li><b>No one is beyond Christ’s reach</b>—even violent opponents (Acts 9:1–6; 1 Tim 1:15–16).</li>
<li><b>Calling flows from grace, not merit</b> (Gal 1:15–16; Eph 3:7–8).</li>
<li><b>Guard the gospel against additions</b> (Acts 15:1–11; Gal 1:6–9).</li>
<li><b>Hold doctrine and love together</b>—truth with pastoral tenderness (1 Thess 2:7–12; Titus 1:9).</li>
<li><b>Suffering can advance the gospel</b> rather than hinder it (Phil 1:12–14; 2 Tim 2:9–10).</li>
<li><b>Weakness is not disqualifying</b> when Christ’s power rests on you (2 Cor 12:9–10).</li>
<li><b>Mission requires cultural wisdom without compromising truth</b> (1 Cor 9:19–23; Acts 17:22–31).</li>
<li><b>Leadership includes vigilance</b>—wolves and drift are real (Acts 20:28–31).</li>
<li><b>Perseverance matters</b>—finish the race faithfully (Acts 20:24; 2 Tim 4:7–8).</li>
<li><b>Identity is “in Christ,” not in religious credentials</b> (Phil 3:4–9; Gal 6:14).</li>
<li><b>Discipleship multiplies</b>—entrust truth to faithful people (2 Tim 2:2).</li>
<li><b>Reconciliation is gospel-shaped</b>—treat people as brothers/sisters in Christ (Phlm 15–16; 2 Cor 5:18–19).</li>
</ol>
<div><b><h2>Warnings to heed</h2></b></div>
<ul>
<li><b>A different gospel is damnable</b> even if it sounds “religious” (Gal 1:8–9).</li>
<li><b>Confidence in the flesh deceives</b>—religious performance can mask unbelief (Phil 3:4–9).</li>
<li><b>Ministry conflict is real</b>; don’t romanticize leaders—pursue peace and wisdom (Acts 15:36–40; cf. Rom 12:18).</li>
</ul>
<div><b><h2>Examples to imitate</h2></b></div>
<ul>
<li>Gospel boldness under pressure (Acts 28:30–31; Phil 1:12–14)</li>
<li>Humble dependence on grace (1 Cor 15:9–10)</li>
<li>Pastoral labor for maturity (Col 1:28–29)</li>
</ul>

<div><b><h2>Personal reflection</h2></b></div>
<ol>
<li>Where do you most need to remember that salvation is <b>by grace</b>, not spiritual résumé (Phil 3:7–9; Eph 2:8–10)?</li>
<li>What “past identity” still tries to define you more than “in Christ” does (2 Cor 5:17; Gal 2:20)?</li>
<li>How do you respond when obedience increases suffering rather than comfort (Acts 9:16; Phil 1:29)?</li>
<li>What weakness in you could become a stage for Christ’s power (2 Cor 12:9–10)?</li>
<li>Where are you tempted to “add” to the gospel—subtly making acceptance depend on performance (Gal 1:6–9)?</li>
</ol>
<div><b><h2>Small group discussion</h2></b></div>
<ol>
<li>Why is Acts 15 such a turning point for the church’s unity and mission (Acts 15:1–11)?</li>
<li>What can we learn from Paul’s conflict with Barnabas about leadership, conscience, and continuing ministry (Acts 15:36–40)?</li>
<li>How does Paul balance contextual engagement with unyielding claims (Acts 17:22–31; 1 Cor 9:19–23)?</li>
<li>In what ways did Paul’s imprisonment expand gospel witness (Phil 1:12–14; Acts 28:30–31)?</li>
<li>What does Paul’s farewell in Acts 20 teach about shepherding, doctrine, and danger (Acts 20:28–31)?</li>
</ol>

<ol>

<li>Trace Paul’s conversion story across Acts 9, 22, and 26—what details are emphasized in each retelling, and why (Acts 22:3–21; Acts 26:12–23)?</li>
<li>Compare Romans 3:21–28 with Galatians 2:15–21—what shared logic and unique emphases do you see?</li>
<li>Read 2 Corinthians 11:23–12:10—how does Paul redefine “credentials” and “power”?</li>
<li>In Philippians 3:4–11, list what Paul loses and what he gains—how does this reshape Christian ambition?</li>
</ol>
