1) Story Summary (Chronological)

Jeremiah is a prophet called by the LORD in Judah during the final decades before Jerusalem’s fall to Babylon. His ministry begins in the 13th year of King Josiah (Jer 1:1–2) and spans the reigns of Judah’s last kings—Josiah, Jehoahaz, Jehoiakim, Jehoiachin, Zedekiah—and continues after Jerusalem is destroyed (Jer 1:2–3; 2 Kgs 23:31–24:20; 25:1–26).
From the start, Jeremiah is commissioned “to pluck up and to break down… to build and to plant” (Jer 1:10). He preaches repentance, exposes Judah’s covenant-breaking and idolatry (Jer 2–6), and warns that the temple cannot be used as a religious “charm” while the nation persists in injustice and false worship (Jer 7:1–15). His words provoke intense opposition—threats from his hometown (Jer 11:18–23), public trials (Jer 26:7–16), beatings and imprisonment (Jer 20:1–2), and repeated confinement during the siege (Jer 37–38).
Jeremiah’s ministry includes vivid sign-acts: a marred linen loincloth (Jer 13:1–11), a shattered jar (Jer 19:1–13), yokes symbolizing Babylon’s rule (Jer 27:1–11), and the purchase of a field during siege to proclaim future restoration (Jer 32:6–15). He dictates God’s words to Baruch, whose scroll is burned by King Jehoiakim—only for Jeremiah to dictate it again with “many similar words” added (Jer 36:1–32). He confronts false prophets, most notably Hananiah, whose message of quick deliverance is judged as deception (Jer 28:1–17).
In the darkest days, Jeremiah also becomes a prophet of hope: he sends a letter to the exiles urging faithful life in Babylon while awaiting God’s long-term restoration (Jer 29:1–14), and he announces the New Covenant—God’s law written on the heart, sins forgiven, and true knowledge of the LORD (Jer 31:31–34). After Jerusalem falls, Jeremiah remains with the remnant under Babylonian governance (Jer 40:1–6). When violence and fear lead the people to flee toward Egypt against God’s word, Jeremiah continues to warn them there (Jer 42–44). Scripture does not clearly record Jeremiah’s death; later traditions exist, but they are not explicit in the Bible.
Where his story begins: Jeremiah’s call and commission (Jer 1:1–10).Where his story ends (in Scripture): His post-fall ministry among the remnant and in Egypt (Jer 40–44), with Jerusalem’s fall narrated as the book’s historical capstone (Jer 52; cf. 2 Kgs 25).

2) What He’s Known For

Jeremiah is most known as the prophet who faithfully proclaimed God’s judgment and mercy on the eve of exile—often at great personal cost—while also delivering some of Scripture’s richest promises of restoration, especially the New Covenant (Jer 31:31–34).
Headline descriptors
  • Called from the womb for a hard prophetic mission (Jer 1:4–10).
  • “Prophet to the nations” declaring God’s sovereignty over kingdoms (Jer 1:5; 18:7–10).
  • Persecuted, rejected, and imprisoned, yet compelled to speak (Jer 20:7–9; 37–38).
  • Temple sermon preacher exposing false security (Jer 7:1–15).
  • Opponent of false prophecy, insisting on truth over optimism (Jer 23:16–22; 28).
  • Prophet of the New Covenant, heart transformation, and forgiveness (Jer 31:31–34).
  • Weeping intercessor, pouring out lament and grief before God (Jer 9:1; 14:7–9; 20:14–18).

3) Where He Appears in the Bible

Primary narrative locations

  • Jeremiah 1–44 — Jeremiah’s call, preaching, conflicts, and ministry through and after Jerusalem’s fall.
  • Jeremiah 46–51 — Oracles against the nations (Babylon, Egypt, etc.).
  • Jeremiah 52 — Historical appendix recounting Jerusalem’s fall and aftermath (cf. 2 Kgs 24–25).

Secondary references (later mentions, summaries, teachings)

  • 2 Kings 22–25 — Historical context for Judah’s last kings and the fall of Jerusalem; aligns with Jeremiah’s era (2 Kgs 23:31–25:26).
  • 2 Chronicles 35–36 — Jeremiah laments Josiah and is linked to Judah’s final collapse (2 Chr 35:25; 36:21–22).
  • Ezra 1:1–4 — Cyrus’s decree is connected to “the word of the LORD by the mouth of Jeremiah” (Ezra 1:1).
  • Daniel 9:2 — Daniel studies “the books,” specifically Jeremiah’s word about Jerusalem’s desolations (Dan 9:2; cf. Jer 25:11–12; 29:10).
  • Matthew 2:17–18 — Jeremiah’s “Rachel weeping” is cited in connection with Herod’s massacre (Matt 2:17–18; Jer 31:15).
  • Hebrews 8:8–12; 10:16–17 — The New Covenant promise is quoted as fulfilled in Christ (Heb 8:8–12; 10:16–17; Jer 31:31–34).

4) Vital Statistics

Historical setting (as Scripture indicates)
  • Begins in Josiah’s 13th year and runs to the exile era (Jer 1:2–3).
  • Ministers through Judah’s final kings and the Babylonian conquest (Jer 1:2–3; 2 Kgs 24–25; Jer 39–40).
Geography
  • Anathoth (in Benjamin) — Jeremiah’s hometown (Jer 1:1; 11:21).
  • Jerusalem — center of his preaching, trials, imprisonments, and siege narratives (Jer 7; 26; 37–39).
  • Ramah / Benjamin region — scenes surrounding deportations and remnant movements (Jer 40:1).
  • Egypt — Jeremiah’s later ministry among refugees (Jer 43–44).
Family and relationships
  • Father: Hilkiah (Jer 1:1). (Scripture does not explicitly identify him with Josiah’s high priest—avoid assuming.)
  • No wife/children: commanded not to marry as a sign of coming judgment (Jer 16:1–4).
  • Scribe/ally: Baruch son of Neriah (Jer 36:4; 45:1).
  • Protector: Ahikam son of Shaphan helps preserve Jeremiah’s life (Jer 26:24).
  • Rescuer: Ebed-melech the Ethiopian delivers Jeremiah from the cistern (Jer 38:7–13).
  • Adversaries: priests/prophets/officials (Jer 20:1–2; 26:7–11), false prophets like Hananiah (Jer 28), hostile leaders during siege (Jer 37–38), and even townsmen/family networks (Jer 11:18–23; 12:6).
Roles/offices
  • Prophet (Jer 1:5).
  • Watchman/Herald of covenant lawsuit against Judah’s unfaithfulness (Jer 2:1–13).
  • Intercessor—though at times forbidden to pray for the people due to hardened rebellion (Jer 7:16; 14:11).
Key turning points
  • Call and commissioning (Jer 1:4–10).
  • Temple sermon conflict (Jer 7:1–15; 26:1–16).
  • Personal crisis and “fire in bones” resolve (Jer 20:7–9).
  • Scroll burned and rewritten (Jer 36:1–32).
  • Clash with Hananiah over the yoke (Jer 28).
  • Hope enacted: purchase of a field during siege (Jer 32:6–15).
  • New Covenant proclamation (Jer 31:31–34).
  • Imprisonment/cistern and rescue (Jer 38:6–13).
  • Post-fall ministry and forced journey to Egypt (Jer 40–44).

6) Strengths, Weaknesses, and Heart-Level Patterns

Strengths (with passages)

  • Courage under pressure: He is commanded not to fear faces, and he stands firm before kings, priests, and crowds (Jer 1:17–19; 26:12–15; 38:14–18).
  • Faithful proclamation of God’s word: Even when silence would be easier, the word burns within him (Jer 20:8–9).
  • Compassion and grief for sin’s consequences: His tears match the seriousness of judgment (Jer 9:1; 13:17; 14:17).
  • Willingness to embody God’s message: Jeremiah’s life becomes a sign (no marriage, Jer 16:1–4; sign-acts, Jer 13; 19; 27; 32).
  • Theological clarity about true repentance and worship: He refuses superficial religion and demands covenant fidelity (Jer 7:5–11; 23:16–22).

Weaknesses / sins / blind spots (shown with careful context)

  • Fear and inadequacy at the outset: “I do not know how to speak… I am only a youth” (Jer 1:6). God corrects and strengthens him (Jer 1:7–9).
  • Deep discouragement and anguish: Jeremiah’s laments include sharp complaint and despair (Jer 15:10–18; 20:14–18). Scripture presents these transparently—lament is not celebrated as virtue, but it is brought honestly before God.
  • Longing for vindication against opponents: He prays for justice against persecutors (Jer 11:20; 18:19–23). This must be read in the context of covenant violence against him (Jer 11:18–19) and God’s righteous judgment; still, it reveals the strain and intensity of his emotional world.

Motives / fears / desires (careful inferences anchored to text)

  • Desire to be faithful to God’s voice even when it costs him reputation and safety (Jer 20:9; 26:12–15).
  • Fear of rejection and suffering, honestly acknowledged (Jer 1:6; 20:7–8).
  • Longing for a repentant people and a healed covenant relationship, seen in tears and intercession (Jer 14:7–9, 19–22).

What changes (or doesn’t) over time?

  • Doesn’t change: The core conflict remains the same—Jeremiah’s message contradicts the people’s preferred narrative (Jer 6:14; 8:11; 23:16–17), and opposition persists (Jer 26; 36; 37–38; 43).
  • Deepens over time: His inner life grows more exposed (Jer 15; 20), and his theology of hope becomes more articulated—especially restoration and the New Covenant (Jer 30–33). Judgment is not his final word; mercy is real, but it comes God’s way and in God’s time (Jer 29:10–14; 31:31–34; 32:36–44).

7) Victories and Challenges

Major “wins” (obedience, influence, faith, leadership)

  • He accepts a daunting calling and is strengthened by God’s presence (Jer 1:4–10, 17–19).
  • He speaks truth at the temple despite life-threatening backlash (Jer 7:1–15; 26:12–16).
  • God’s word preserved despite royal hostility: scroll burned, then rewritten and expanded (Jer 36:20–32).
  • He exposes false prophecy and upholds God’s long-range discipline and hope (Jer 28:1–17; 29:1–14).
  • He embodies hope in catastrophe: purchase of land during siege and prayers that anchor hope in God’s sovereignty (Jer 32:6–17, 26–44).
  • He delivers the New Covenant promise—a pinnacle of redemptive hope (Jer 31:31–34).
  • He remains with the remnant rather than pursuing comfort, continuing ministry after the fall (Jer 40:1–6).

Major challenges (suffering, opposition, doubt)

  • Threats from his own town and networks (Jer 11:18–23; 12:6).
  • Public shaming and imprisonment (Jer 20:1–2; 37:15–16).
  • Accusations of treason and repeated confinement during siege (Jer 37:11–14; 38:1–6).
  • Near-death in a cistern, rescued by an unlikely ally (Jer 38:6–13).
  • Emotional and spiritual anguish in sustained rejection (Jer 15:10–18; 20:14–18).
  • Post-fall defiance: the remnant asks for guidance, then rejects it and flees to Egypt (Jer 42:1–22; 43:1–7).
Cost of faithfulness: Jeremiah’s obedience brings isolation, loss of ordinary life (Jer 16:1–4), and repeated suffering (Jer 20; 37–38).Consequences of compromise: Judah’s refusal to hear God’s word ends in exile and devastation (Jer 25:8–11; 39:1–10), and the remnant’s fear-driven “escape plan” leads them into renewed idolatry warnings in Egypt (Jer 44:1–14).

9) Theology and Big Themes

God’s character revealed

  • Holiness and justice: God judges covenant-breaking, violence, and idolatry (Jer 7:5–15; 25:8–11).
  • Patience and persistence: God sends prophets “again and again,” yet the people refuse (cf. Jer 7:25–26).
  • Sovereignty over nations: God uproots and plants kingdoms by His word (Jer 1:10; 18:7–10; 27:5–7; 32:27).
  • Mercy and covenant love: Restoration is promised not because Judah deserves it, but because God is faithful to His purposes (Jer 31:3; 32:36–41).

Covenant, worship, exile, restoration

  • Covenant lawsuit: Jeremiah indicts Israel/Judah for breaking covenant, especially in idolatry and injustice (Jer 2:1–13; 7:9–11).
  • True worship: Temple presence is not protection if hearts remain rebellious (Jer 7:1–15).
  • Exile as covenant discipline: Babylon’s rise is framed as God’s instrument of judgment (Jer 25:8–11; 27:6).
  • Restoration beyond judgment: God promises return, rebuilding, and renewed relationship (Jer 30:18–22; 31:27–28).

Human nature: sin, grace, repentance, perseverance

  • The heart problem: The human heart is profoundly unreliable (Jer 17:9), hence the need for heart-level renewal (Jer 31:33; 24:7).
  • Repentance must be real: superficial “peace” and ritual cannot substitute for obedience (Jer 6:14; 7:9–11).
  • Persevering under rejection: Jeremiah models continuing to speak God’s truth when it is unwelcome (Jer 20:9; 26:12–15).

10) How Jeremiah Points Us to Christ

1) The suffering prophet pattern (careful typology)

Jeremiah is rejected by leaders, accused falsely, beaten, imprisoned, and nearly killed (Jer 20:1–2; 26:7–11; 37–38). This pattern anticipates the greater rejection of Jesus—the faithful prophet who suffers at the hands of His people’s leaders (cf. Matt 21:33–46; Luke 13:33–34). Jeremiah is not the Messiah, but his life prefigures the cost of speaking God’s truth in a hostile world.

2) The New Covenant fulfilled in Jesus

Jeremiah’s climactic promise is the New Covenant: God writes His law on hearts, forgives sin, and brings true knowledge of Himself (Jer 31:31–34). The New Testament explicitly applies this to Christ’s saving work (Heb 8:8–12; 10:16–17), and Jesus identifies His death as inaugurating the New Covenant (Luke 22:20).

3) The Righteous Branch and “The LORD is our righteousness”

Jeremiah foretells a Davidic king who will reign wisely and be called “The LORD is our righteousness” (Jer 23:5–6; 33:14–16). This points forward to the Messiah’s righteous rule and the gospel reality that righteousness is found in the LORD Himself—fulfilled in Christ (cf. 1 Cor 1:30).

4) Rachel weeping and gospel consolation

Jeremiah’s image of Rachel weeping (Jer 31:15) is cited in Matthew in connection with Herod’s slaughter (Matt 2:17–18). In Jeremiah, that weeping sits in a restoration context (Jer 31:16–17), and in Matthew it sits near the coming of the true King—sorrow meets the dawn of redemption.

11) Lessons and Applications

Examples to imitate (tied to Scripture)

  1. Let God define your calling, not your fears (Jer 1:6–9).
  2. Speak truth even when it costs you (Jer 26:12–15; 20:9).
  3. Refuse shallow religion—pursue obedient worship (Jer 7:5–11).
  4. Test “encouraging” messages by God’s word, not by vibes (Jer 23:16–22; 28:1–17).
  5. Practice faithful presence in hard places (Jer 29:4–7).
  6. Anchor hope in God’s sovereignty, not circumstances (Jer 32:17, 27).
  7. Bring grief to God honestly—lament is permitted, not prohibited (Jer 15:10–18; 20:14–18).
  8. Invest in obedience that looks foolish to the world (Jer 32:6–15).
  9. Keep God’s long view—His timing may be longer than yours (Jer 29:10–14).
  10. Cherish the New Covenant promise: heart change and forgiveness (Jer 31:31–34).

Warnings to heed

  1. Don’t treat spiritual symbols as immunity (Jer 7:1–15).
  2. Idolatry is often an exchange—leaving living water for broken cisterns (Jer 2:13).
  3. Fear-based decisions can become disobedience (Jer 42:19–22; 43:1–7).
  4. False prophecy thrives where people prefer comfort over repentance (Jer 6:14; 23:17).

Application areas

  • Personal holiness: repent with integrity, not performance (Jer 7:5–11; 24:7).
  • Relationships: beware betrayal and pressure; seek faithful companions (Jer 12:6; 26:24; 36:4).
  • Prayer: pray with honesty and reverence; accept God’s hard answers (Jer 14:7–9; 7:16).
  • Suffering: endurance doesn’t erase pain; it brings pain to God and keeps obeying (Jer 20:9; 15:15–21).
  • Decision-making: don’t ask God for guidance while planning to reject it (Jer 42:1–22).
  • Leadership: lead by truth, not crowds; resist “peace” slogans that ignore sin (Jer 23:16–22; 28).

12) Discussion and Reflection Questions

Personal reflection

  1. Where do you feel “too young / not enough” like Jeremiah (Jer 1:6), and what does God’s answer teach you (Jer 1:7–9)?
  2. What modern “broken cisterns” tempt you to look for life apart from God (Jer 2:13)?
  3. How do you respond when obedience costs reputation or comfort (Jer 26:12–15; 20:9)?
  4. What does Jeremiah’s lament teach you about bringing pain to God without abandoning God (Jer 15:10–21; 20:14–18)?
  5. In what ways might fear be steering your choices more than faith (Jer 42:19–22)?

Small group discussion

  1. Why is the temple sermon still relevant for religious communities today (Jer 7:1–11)?
  2. How do you distinguish true encouragement from false reassurance (Jer 23:16–22; 28:1–17)?
  3. What does it look like to “seek the welfare of the city” as believers in a hostile culture (Jer 29:4–7)?
  4. How can a community cultivate hope without denying judgment or consequences (Jer 30–33)?
  5. What practices help a group submit to God’s word when it contradicts their plans (Jer 42)?

Deep study (text observation)

  1. In Jer 1:4–10, list everything God does vs. what Jeremiah does. What does that teach about vocation?
  2. In Jer 7:5–11, identify the ethical behaviors God emphasizes. How are they linked to worship?
  3. In Jer 23:16–22, what marks false prophets, and what marks true prophetic standing “in the council of the LORD”?
  4. Read Jer 29:4–14 carefully: what commands are immediate, and what promises are time-bound (Jer 29:10)?
  5. In Jer 31:31–34, what are the New Covenant’s features (law, knowledge, forgiveness), and how do Hebrews 8 and 10 use them (Heb 8:8–12; 10:16–17)?
  6. In Jer 32:6–44, track the movement from sign-act to prayer to God’s answer. What logic does God use to ground hope?

13) Prayer Response

Forgive us for trusting in religious forms while resisting Your voice (Jer 7:8–11), and for drinking from broken cisterns that cannot hold life (Jer 2:13). Write Your law on our hearts, forgive our sins, and make us truly know You, as You promised in the New Covenant (Jer 31:33–34). Give us courage to speak truth with humility, to endure suffering without abandoning faith, and to hope in You when circumstances look hopeless (Jer 20:9; 32:17, 27). In Jesus’ name, Amen.
