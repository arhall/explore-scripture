1) Story Summary (chronological)

Jesus Christ is introduced as the eternal Word/Son who was “with God” and “was God,” through whom all things were made (John 1:1–3; Col 1:15–17). In the fullness of time, the Word became flesh (John 1:14), conceived by the Holy Spirit and born of the virgin Mary (Matt 1:18–25; Luke 1:26–35), born in Bethlehem in David’s line (Matt 1:1–17; Luke 2:1–20), raised in Nazareth (Matt 2:23; Luke 2:39–52).
At about thirty years of age (Luke 3:23), Jesus was baptized by John to “fulfill all righteousness,” and the Father publicly testified to Him as the beloved Son while the Spirit descended upon Him (Matt 3:13–17). Immediately He was tempted in the wilderness and overcame the devil by obedience to God’s word (Matt 4:1–11; Luke 4:1–13).
He proclaimed the arrival of God’s kingdom and called people to repent and believe (Mark 1:14–15), gathered disciples and appointed apostles (Mark 3:13–19), taught with authority (Matt 5–7; Mark 1:21–22), performed signs and wonders that displayed compassion and divine authority (Matt 8–9; Mark 4–5; John 2–11), confronted hypocrisy and hardened unbelief (Matt 23; John 8:39–47), and revealed Himself as the promised Messiah and Son (Matt 16:15–17; John 10:24–30). He was transfigured in glory as a preview of His majesty and as confirmation of His identity (Matt 17:1–8; 2 Pet 1:16–18).
In Jerusalem, Jesus instituted the Lord’s Supper as the sign of the new covenant in His blood (Luke 22:19–20; 1 Cor 11:23–26), prayed in Gethsemane in anguish while submitting to the Father’s will (Matt 26:36–46), was betrayed and unjustly tried (Matt 26:47–68; 27:1–26), crucified under Pontius Pilate (Matt 27:27–56; John 19:16–37), died as the righteous sufferer (1 Pet 3:18), was buried (Matt 27:57–66), and on the third day rose bodily (Matt 28:1–10; Luke 24:36–43; John 20:24–29). He appeared to many witnesses (1 Cor 15:3–8), commissioned His followers to disciple the nations (Matt 28:18–20; Acts 1:8), and ascended to the Father’s right hand (Acts 1:9–11), where He reigns and intercedes (Rom 8:34; Heb 7:25).
His story continues in the church’s witness empowered by the Spirit (Acts 2), and it concludes in Scripture with His promised return, judgment, and consummated kingdom—the Lamb enthroned and worshiped forever (Rev 5:9–14; 19:11–16; 21:1–5; 22:12–13).
Where His story begins and ends in the Bible
  • Begins (in the fullest canonical sense): preexistence and creation through Him (John 1:1–3), and the earliest gospel promise anticipating a deliverer (Gen 3:15; cf. Gal 4:4–5).
  • Ends: the risen, reigning Christ who returns to make all things new (Rev 22:12–21).

2) What He’s Known For

Jesus is most known as the Christ (Messiah), the Son of God, the Savior—the one who reveals the Father (John 14:9), announces and embodies the kingdom (Mark 1:14–15), gives His life as a ransom (Mark 10:45), rises from the dead (Matt 28:5–7), and reigns as Lord (Phil 2:9–11).
Headlines
  • The Incarnate Son / Word made flesh (John 1:14; Gal 4:4)
  • The Promised Messiah / Christ (Matt 16:16; Luke 24:25–27)
  • The Suffering Servant who bears sin (Isa 53:4–6; 1 Pet 2:24)
  • The King of the Kingdom (Luke 1:32–33; Matt 28:18)
  • The Great High Priest and mediator (Heb 4:14–16; 9:11–15)
  • The Lamb of God who takes away sin (John 1:29; Rev 5:9)
  • The Risen Lord and coming Judge (Acts 17:31; Rev 19:11–16)

4) Vital Statistics

  • Historical setting: late Second Temple Judaism under Roman rule (Luke 2:1–2; 3:1–2); ministries across Galilee/Judea; crucifixion under Pontius Pilate (Matt 27:1–2).
  • Geography: Bethlehem (Matt 2:1), Nazareth (Matt 2:23), Galilee/Capernaum (Matt 4:13), Sea of Galilee (Mark 4:1), Samaria (John 4:4–26), Judea/Jerusalem/temple (Luke 19:45–48), Mount of Olives (Acts 1:12).
  • Family & relationships:
    • Mother: Mary (Luke 1:30–35; John 19:26–27)
    • Legal father/guardian: Joseph (Matt 1:19–25; Luke 2:51)
    • Brothers/sisters: named brothers include James, Joses/Joseph, Simon, Judas (Matt 13:55–56; Mark 6:3)
    • No earthly spouse or children stated in Scripture; the church is pictured as His bride (Eph 5:25–32; Rev 19:7).
    • Close followers: the Twelve (Mark 3:13–19), the inner circle (Peter/James/John: Mark 5:37; 9:2), Mary/Martha/Lazarus (John 11:1–5).
    • Opponents: some Pharisees/scribes, chief priests, elders (Mark 3:6; 11:18); Judas as betrayer (Luke 22:3–6).
  • Roles/offices:
    • Prophet: promised “like Moses” (Deut 18:15–19; Acts 3:22–23)
    • Priest: great high priest (Heb 4:14–16; 7:23–28)
    • King: Davidic king (Luke 1:32–33; Rev 19:16)
    • Servant/Suffering Servant: (Isa 52:13–53:12; Mark 10:45)
    • Shepherd: good shepherd (John 10:11–18)
    • Judge: appointed judge of the world (Acts 17:31; John 5:22–29)
  • Key turning points:
    • Baptism and Father’s testimony (Matt 3:16–17)
    • Wilderness temptation (Matt 4:1–11)
    • Peter’s confession & Jesus’ passion predictions (Matt 16:16–21)
    • Transfiguration (Matt 17:1–8)
    • Triumphal entry and temple confrontation (Matt 21:1–17)
    • Last Supper / New covenant (Luke 22:19–20)
    • Gethsemane submission (Matt 26:39)
    • Crucifixion (Mark 15:24–39)
    • Resurrection and commission (Matt 28:5–20)
    • Ascension and exaltation (Acts 1:9–11; Phil 2:9–11)

6) Strengths, Weaknesses, and Heart-Level Patterns

Strengths (with text evidence)

  • Perfect obedience and submission to the Father: “not as I will, but as you will” (Matt 26:39); “I always do the things that are pleasing to him” (John 8:29).
  • Compassionate mercy toward the weak/outcast: moved with compassion (Matt 9:36; Mark 1:41); welcomes sinners and eats with them (Luke 5:29–32; 15:1–2).
  • Courage and truthfulness under pressure: speaks openly of His identity and mission even when it provokes hostility (John 8:58–59; 18:37).
  • Prayerful dependence: frequent solitary prayer (Mark 1:35; Luke 5:16); high-priestly prayer (John 17).
  • Wisdom and authority in teaching: astonished crowds (Matt 7:28–29); answers traps with divine wisdom (Matt 22:15–46).
  • Humility and servant leadership: washes disciples’ feet (John 13:3–15); “came not to be served but to serve” (Mark 10:45).

Weaknesses / sins / blind spots (what Scripture shows)

  • No sins are attributed to Jesus in Scripture. He is explicitly described as without sin: “tempted as we are, yet without sin” (Heb 4:15); “he committed no sin” (1 Pet 2:22); “in him there is no sin” (1 John 3:5).
  • True human limitations (not moral failures): hunger (Matt 4:2), fatigue (John 4:6), grief (John 11:35), deep anguish (Matt 26:38), thirst (John 19:28). These display real humanity (Heb 2:14–18), not sin.

Motives, desires, and (careful) inner life in the text

  • Chief desire: to do the Father’s will and finish His work (John 4:34; 6:38–40).
  • Love as driving force: “having loved his own… he loved them to the end” (John 13:1); “Greater love has no one than this…” (John 15:13).
  • Zeal for true worship: temple cleansing (John 2:13–17; cf. Ps 69:9).
  • Holy sorrow and troubled spirit in view of the cross: “My soul is very sorrowful” (Matt 26:38); “Now is my soul troubled” (John 12:27), yet moving forward in obedience.

What changes (or doesn’t) over time?

  • His mission clarifies progressively to the disciples (Mark 8:27–33; Luke 24:25–27), but His character and obedience do not shift—He consistently does the Father’s will (John 8:29) and consistently moves toward “his hour” (John 2:4; 12:23; 13:1).
  • Opposition intensifies from skepticism to conspiracy to execution (Mark 3:6; John 11:47–53), while His resolve intensifies toward Jerusalem (Luke 9:51).

7) Victories and Challenges

Major “wins” (obedience, deliverance, influence)

  • Victory over Satan’s temptations (Matt 4:1–11).
  • Authority over demons, disease, nature, and death (Mark 1:34; 4:39–41; John 11:43–44).
  • Revealing the Father and truth (John 1:18; 14:6–9).
  • Making and shaping disciples (Mark 3:13–19; John 17:6–19).
  • Atoning death and resurrection (Rom 4:25; 1 Cor 15:3–4).
  • Exaltation and ongoing intercession (Eph 1:20–23; Heb 7:25).

Major challenges (temptations, suffering, opposition)

  • Temptation and testing (Matt 4:1–11; Heb 4:15).
  • Misunderstanding by family and disciples (Mark 3:21; 8:31–33; 9:32).
  • Hostility and plots to kill Him (John 5:16–18; 7:1; 11:53).
  • Betrayal and denial in His inner circle (Luke 22:3–6; 22:54–62).
  • Agony of Gethsemane and the cross (Matt 26:36–46; 27:46; Heb 12:2–3).
Cost of faithfulness: He “endured the cross” (Heb 12:2), was mocked and rejected (Isa 53:3; Matt 27:27–31), and yet obeyed unto death (Phil 2:8), securing salvation for His people (Matt 1:21; John 10:11, 15).

9) Theology and Big Themes

  • Revelation of God’s character: the Father is made known in the Son (John 1:18; 14:9); mercy to sinners (Luke 19:10), holiness and truth (John 8:46; 17:17), justice and judgment (John 5:22–29).
  • Kingdom of God: inaugurated in Jesus’ ministry (Mark 1:14–15), displayed in His authority and teaching (Matt 12:28), and consummated at His return (Rev 11:15; 19:11–16).
  • Covenant fulfillment: promises to Abraham and David converge in Him (Matt 1:1; Gal 3:16); new covenant established in His blood (Luke 22:20; Heb 9:15).
  • Atonement and salvation: Christ dies “for our sins” (1 Cor 15:3), bearing our sins (1 Pet 2:24), to reconcile us to God (Rom 5:10–11; Col 1:20–22).
  • Priesthood and access: He is the sympathetic high priest granting confidence to draw near (Heb 4:14–16).
  • Discipleship and mission: following Jesus involves cross-bearing obedience (Luke 9:23), Spirit-empowered witness to the nations (Acts 1:8).
  • Human nature and grace: the Gospels expose unbelief, hypocrisy, and need for new birth (John 3:3–8), while Christ provides grace, forgiveness, and transformation (Luke 7:48–50; Titus 2:11–14).

10) How Scripture Points to Christ (fulfilled patterns, offices, promises)

(Since Jesus is the Christ, this section traces how the Scriptures anticipate and interpret Him.)

Offices: Prophet, Priest, King

  • Prophet like Moses: Deut 18:15–19 fulfilled as applied to Jesus (Acts 3:22–23).
  • Priest and mediator: Melchizedek pattern and superior priesthood (Ps 110:4; Heb 7:11–28).
  • Davidic King: promised throne and eternal reign (2 Sam 7:12–16; Luke 1:32–33).

Key typological patterns (biblically grounded)

  • Last Adam / true man: Adam’s failure contrasted with Christ’s obedience (Rom 5:12–19; 1 Cor 15:21–22, 45).
  • Passover Lamb: deliverance through blood fulfilled in Christ (Exod 12; John 1:29; 1 Cor 5:7).
  • Exodus/true Israel: Jesus relives Israel’s story and succeeds (Matt 2:15; 4:1–11).
  • Suffering righteous one: Psalms and prophets converge in His passion (Ps 22; Isa 53; Luke 24:44–46).
  • Temple presence: Jesus as the locus of God’s presence (John 2:19–21) and the one who makes worship “in spirit and truth” (John 4:21–24).

Gospel connections (salvation, grace, kingdom)

  • Salvation comes through His name and work (John 3:16–18; Acts 4:12).
  • The kingdom is entered through repentance and new birth (Mark 1:15; John 3:3–8).
  • Grace produces a people zealous for good works (Titus 2:11–14), shaped into His likeness (Rom 8:29).

11) Lessons and Applications (tied to Scripture)

  1. Worship Jesus as Lord and God (John 20:28; Phil 2:9–11).
  2. Follow His pattern of humble service (John 13:14–15; Mark 10:45).
  3. Resist temptation with Scripture and obedience (Matt 4:4,7,10; Heb 4:15–16).
  4. Pray with dependence and submission (Mark 1:35; Matt 26:39).
  5. Love sacrificially, even at cost (John 13:1; 15:13; Eph 5:2).
  6. Pursue heart-level righteousness, not mere externalism (Matt 5:20–48; 23:25–28).
  7. Keep the cross central—it is the heart of the gospel (Mark 8:31; 1 Cor 15:3–4).
  8. Live in resurrection hope (1 Pet 1:3; 1 Cor 15:20–22).
  9. Make disciples in His authority (Matt 28:18–20).
  10. Suffer faithfully, looking to Jesus (Heb 12:2–3; 1 Pet 2:21).
  11. Draw near to God with confidence because of Christ’s priesthood (Heb 4:14–16).
  12. Guard against rejecting the light—unbelief has consequences (John 3:19–21; 8:24).
Warnings to heed
  • Hypocrisy and hardened religion (Matt 23:13–36).
  • Refusing Jesus’ words and identity (John 8:24; 12:48).
Examples to imitate
  • Compassion (Matt 9:36), truthfulness (John 18:37), steadfast obedience (John 8:29), and forgiving love (Luke 23:34; cf. Eph 4:32).
Applications for:
  • Personal holiness: abide and obey (John 15:4–10).
  • Relationships: forgive as forgiven (Matt 18:21–35; Eph 4:32).
  • Prayer: ask, seek, submit (Matt 6:9–13; 26:39).
  • Suffering: endure with eyes on Him (Heb 12:2–3).
  • Decision-making/leadership: serve, don’t domineer (Mark 10:42–45; John 13:3–15).

12) Discussion and Reflection Questions (10–20)

Personal reflection

  1. Where do you most need to “abide” in Christ this week (John 15:4–5)?
  2. What does Jesus’ submission in Gethsemane teach you about prayer under pressure (Matt 26:36–46)?
  3. How does the resurrection reshape your view of fear, loss, and death (1 Cor 15:20–22)?
  4. In what ways are you tempted to seek external righteousness rather than heart obedience (Matt 23:25–28)?

Small group discussion

  1. What do the Gospels emphasize about Jesus’ compassion, and how should that shape the church (Matt 9:36; Mark 1:41)?
  2. How do Jesus’ “I am” claims in John function as both revelation and division (John 8:24, 58; 10:30)?
  3. What does it mean that Jesus has “all authority” for our evangelism and disciple-making (Matt 28:18–20)?
  4. How do Hebrews’ descriptions of Jesus as high priest change how you approach confession and prayer (Heb 4:14–16)?

Deep study (text-observation)

  1. In Matthew 4:1–11, what categories of temptation are present, and how does Jesus answer each?
  2. In Mark 8:27–38, how are Jesus’ identity and the call to discipleship joined together?
  3. In John 6, what responses to Jesus do you observe, and what distinguishes true belief from grumbling (John 6:60–69)?
  4. In Isaiah 52:13–53:12, list what the servant does and what God does—how do NT writers apply this to Christ (1 Pet 2:24–25)?
  5. In Luke 24:13–35, what does Jesus teach about reading Scripture Christologically?
  6. In Revelation 5, why is the Lamb worthy, and how does worship connect to redemption and mission (Rev 5:9–10)?

Application synthesis

  1. What is one concrete way your life would change if you truly believed Jesus’ words in John 14:6–7?
  2. How can your community embody John 13:34–35 so that love becomes a visible apologetic?
