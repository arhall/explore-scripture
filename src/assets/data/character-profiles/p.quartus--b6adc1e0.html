Name: Quartus (Latin for “fourth”)Key text: Romans 16:23
Core limitation: Quartus appears only once in Scripture, so we must build the study primarily from that single verse and from the wider biblical theology of Christian fellowship and “brotherhood,” without inventing biography.

1) Story Summary

Quartus enters the biblical record in the closing greetings of Paul’s letter to the Romans. Alongside other believers present with Paul, Quartus sends greetings to the Roman church and is identified simply as “our brother.” (Romans 16:23)
That is the beginning and end of Quartus’s explicit story in Scripture. We are not told his background, conversion account, travel history, church role, or later ministry.

2) What He’s Known For

Quartus is known for one thing in the text: being recognized among Paul’s companions as a genuine Christian “brother” and participating in the ministry of encouragement through greetings to the saints. (Romans 16:23)
Headline descriptors (3–7):
  • “Our brother” — affirmed as a true member of Christ’s family (Romans 16:23)
  • A greeter/encourager — joins in strengthening the church through greetings (Romans 16:23; cf. Romans 16:16)
  • A believer connected to Paul’s circle — present among those sending greetings (Romans 16:23)
  • An “ordinary” saint with an “extraordinary” place in Scripture — briefly mentioned, yet included in God’s Word (Romans 16:23)
  • A picture of gospel unity — linked with others (Gaius, Erastus) in a shared fellowship (Romans 16:23)

3) Where He Appears in the Bible

Primary narrative location

  • Romans — 16:23 — Quartus sends greetings and is called “our brother.”

Secondary references (theme echoes; not naming Quartus)

These passages help interpret what it means to be a “brother,” to send greetings, and to share in gospel fellowship:
  • Romans — 16:16 — the churches practice holy greetings (a context for Romans 16:23).
  • 1 Corinthians — 16:19–20 — churches/saints send greetings; similar closing pattern.
  • Philippians — 4:21–22 — greetings from saints with Paul to another church.
  • Colossians — 4:7–18 — coworkers named in greetings; networked ministry.
  • 1 Thessalonians — 5:26 — “Greet all the brothers…” (church-wide practice).
  • Hebrews — 2:11–12 — Jesus is not ashamed to call believers “brothers.”
  • John — 13:34–35 — love marks disciples; greeting flows from love.
  • Ephesians — 2:19 — believers are members of God’s household.

4) Vital Statistics

Historical setting (as Scripture indicates)

  • Quartus is present at the time Paul writes Romans and participates in the greetings at the letter’s close (Romans 16:23).
  • Uncertain (not explicit in Scripture): Many place Paul’s writing of Romans during a season of travel and ministry recorded in Acts (cf. Acts 20:2–3). Scripture itself does not name the city in Romans 16, so treat any location as informed inference, not certainty.

Geography

  • Explicit: Quartus is with Paul when the letter to Rome is sent (Romans 16:23).
  • Uncertain: The precise city is not stated in the text of Romans.

Family and relationships

  • Explicit relationships: Quartus is connected to the group with Paul sending greetings (Romans 16:23).
    • Gaius (host), Erastus (city treasurer), Quartus (“our brother”) (Romans 16:23)
  • Not stated: parents, spouse, children.

Roles/offices

  • Explicit: “Brother” (a believer; member of the Christian family) (Romans 16:23).
  • Not stated: elder, deacon, evangelist, teacher, etc.

Key turning points

  • Explicit: Participation in sending greetings (Romans 16:23).
  • Not stated: conversion, call to ministry, crises, covenant moments.

6) Strengths, Weaknesses, and Heart-Level Patterns

Strengths (text-grounded)

  • Recognized as a true believer (“our brother”) — the church’s language of family implies shared faith in Christ and shared standing in grace (Romans 16:23; cf. Ephesians 2:19; Hebrews 2:11).
  • Participates in edifying communion — joining greetings is part of mutual encouragement and unity among churches (Romans 16:23; cf. Romans 16:16; 1 Thessalonians 5:26).

Weaknesses / sins / blind spots

  • Unknown. Scripture records none for Quartus. We should not project strengths or failures beyond what is written (cf. Deuteronomy 29:29 for the principle that some things remain hidden).

Motives / fears / desires (careful inference)

  • Most cautious inference: Quartus likely desired the Roman believers’ good, expressed through greeting and solidarity (Romans 16:23).
    • Anything more specific (e.g., his temperament, leadership gifts, social status) is not stated and should be treated as speculation.

What changes (or doesn’t) over time?

  • Scripture does not provide a development arc for Quartus.
  • What doesn’t change in the single snapshot: Quartus stands among the saints as a brother, participating in the shared life of the churches (Romans 16:23).

7) Victories and Challenges

Major “wins” (text-grounded)

  • Counted among the faithful and named in Scripture — even a brief mention honors the reality that the gospel advances through many ordinary saints (Romans 16:23; cf. 1 Corinthians 12:12–27).

Major challenges (text-grounded)

  • Not specified.
  • Contextual (general, not personal): Paul’s circle often faced hardship in ministry (2 Corinthians 11:23–28), but we cannot assign specific sufferings to Quartus without textual warrant.
Cost of faithfulness / consequences of compromise: not given for Quartus personally.

9) Theology and Big Themes

God’s character revealed

  • God values the whole body, not only the most visible members (1 Corinthians 12:22–27). Quartus’s brief mention underscores God’s care to record the names and fellowship of ordinary saints (Romans 16:23).
  • God creates a new family in Christ — believers are “brothers,” adopted children, members of God’s household (Romans 16:23; Romans 8:15–17; Ephesians 2:19).

Covenant / kingdom / discipleship / mission

  • The church as a translocal communion — greetings connect congregations separated by geography but united in Christ (Romans 16:16, 16:23; Philippians 4:21–22).
  • Partnership in gospel mission — even small contributions (like strengthening bonds and encouragement) serve the larger mission (cf. Philippians 1:3–5).

Human nature: grace, belonging, perseverance

  • Belonging answers isolation — in Christ, believers receive a new identity and family (Romans 8:15–17).
  • Perseverance often looks ordinary — showing up, staying connected, and encouraging the saints (Hebrews 6:10).

10) How Quartus Points Us to Christ

Quartus does not function as a major “type” (no office, no narrative arc), but he points to Christ through the family reality Christ creates:
  • Christ is the one who makes “brothers”: Jesus is not ashamed to call believers “brothers,” grounding Christian brotherhood in union with Him (Hebrews 2:11–12). Quartus’s title “our brother” (Romans 16:23) implicitly testifies to Christ’s redeeming work that forms a new people.
  • Gospel fruit: love and unity: The exchange of greetings reflects the love that marks disciples of Jesus (John 13:34–35) and the unity created by the Spirit (Ephesians 4:1–6).
  • Christ’s body includes many members: Quartus illustrates the “many-membered” body where every part matters (1 Corinthians 12:12–27). Christ is the Head who gives life and coherence (Colossians 1:18).

11) Lessons and Applications

Examples to imitate (with Scripture anchors)

  1. Embrace “ordinary faithfulness.” God records and values saints who serve without spotlight (Romans 16:23; Colossians 3:23–24).
  2. Practice meaningful Christian encouragement. Even greetings can strengthen real unity (Romans 16:16, 16:23; 1 Thessalonians 5:11, 5:26).
  3. Live as family, not as spiritual individuals. “Brother” is identity, not mere nicety (Romans 16:23; Ephesians 2:19).
  4. See the church as bigger than your local circle. The gospel binds distant believers into one communion (Philippians 4:21–22).
  5. Value unseen members of the body. The “less visible” are indispensable (1 Corinthians 12:22–27).
  6. Measure impact by faithfulness, not fame. God does not overlook love and labor (Hebrews 6:10).
  7. Let your speech build communion. Greetings can be holy expressions of peace and fellowship (Romans 16:16; Colossians 4:6).
  8. Honor other believers as siblings. Treat “brother/sister” as a lived reality (Romans 8:15–17; Hebrews 2:11).

Warnings to heed

  1. Don’t despise small acts of love. The kingdom often advances through quiet faithfulness (Matthew 10:42; Hebrews 6:10).
  2. Don’t neglect fellowship. A Christian identity of “brother” implies shared life and mutual care (Ephesians 2:19; Hebrews 10:24–25).

Application areas

  • Personal holiness: serve the Lord in whatever sphere you occupy (Colossians 3:23–24).
  • Relationships: cultivate genuine family-like bonds in the church (Ephesians 2:19).
  • Prayer: pray and connect across the broader body of Christ (Philippians 1:3–5).
  • Suffering: remember you are not alone; you belong to a household (Romans 8:15–17).
  • Leadership: honor and mobilize the “hidden” saints (1 Corinthians 12:22–27).

12) Discussion and Reflection Questions (10–20)

Personal reflection

  1. What does it mean to you to be called a “brother/sister” in Christ (Romans 16:23; Hebrews 2:11)?
  2. Where are you tempted to measure your value by visibility rather than faithfulness (Colossians 3:23–24)?
  3. What “small” acts of encouragement could you practice this week (1 Thessalonians 5:11, 5:26)?
  4. Do you feel like you belong in the church as family—or more like an attendee (Ephesians 2:19)?

Small group discussion

  1. Why do you think Paul includes so many names and greetings (Romans 16)?
  2. How do greetings and relational warmth guard unity in a church (Romans 16:16)?
  3. How can a group honor “indispensable” members who are not upfront leaders (1 Corinthians 12:22–27)?
  4. What habits build translocal (church-to-church) love and awareness (Philippians 4:21–22)?

Deep study (text-observation)

  1. In Romans 16:23, what does the cluster of names suggest about the communal nature of ministry?
  2. What might “our brother” communicate about shared identity and shared status before God?
  3. Compare Romans 16:23 with other greeting lists (Colossians 4:7–18; Philippians 4:21–22). What patterns repeat?
  4. How does Hebrews 2:11–12 deepen the meaning of Christian “brotherhood”?

Application / practice

  1. Who in your church is faithful but unnoticed, and how can you honor them biblically (1 Corinthians 12:24–26)?
  2. How can you make your communication more “holy” and peace-giving (Romans 16:16; Colossians 4:6)?
  3. What would it look like for your household to treat the church as extended family (Ephesians 2:19)?
