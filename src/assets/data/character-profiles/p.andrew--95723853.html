(Simon Peter’s brother)



1) Story Summary (Chronological)

Andrew enters the biblical story as a disciple of John the Baptist. When John points to Jesus as “the Lamb of God,” Andrew follows Jesus, spends time with Him, and quickly becomes an “introducer”—bringing his brother Simon to meet Jesus (John 1:35–42). Andrew and Peter are from Bethsaida (John 1:44) and work as fishermen (Matt 4:18; Mark 1:16). Jesus later calls them publicly to follow Him in a life-defining way: “Follow me, and I will make you fishers of men” (Matt 4:19; Mark 1:17). Andrew becomes one of the Twelve apostles (Matt 10:2; Mark 3:18; Luke 6:14).
In the Gospel narratives, Andrew often appears in moments where someone needs to be brought to Jesus or where a need is identified. At the feeding of the five thousand, Andrew notices a boy with loaves and fish—small resources that Jesus will multiply (John 6:8–9). Near the end of Jesus’ earthly ministry, when some Greeks want to see Jesus, they approach Philip, and Philip turns to Andrew; together they bring the request to Jesus (John 12:20–22). Andrew is also among the inner circle who ask Jesus about the timing and signs surrounding the temple’s destruction and the end (Mark 13:3–4).
After Jesus’ resurrection and ascension, Andrew is listed among the apostles who gather in Jerusalem, devoting themselves to prayer as they await the promised Spirit (Acts 1:13–14). Scripture does not narrate Andrew’s later missionary work or death; later church traditions speak of his ministry and martyrdom, but those details are not explicit in Scripture.
Where his story begins and ends in the Bible:
  • Begins: his first identification and calling around Jesus’ early ministry (John 1:35–42; Matt 4:18–20).
  • Ends: last explicit mention among the praying apostles after the ascension (Acts 1:13–14).

2) What He’s Known For

Andrew is most known in Scripture as a first-responder disciple—quick to follow Jesus and quick to bring others to Him.
Headline descriptors (3–7):
  • Early disciple who responds to John the Baptist’s witness (John 1:35–40)
  • “Bringer”: introduces Simon Peter to Jesus (John 1:41–42)
  • Called from fishing to apostleship (Matt 4:18–20)
  • Practical observer who identifies resources in need-moments (John 6:8–9)
  • Bridge-builder in access to Jesus (Greeks → Jesus) (John 12:20–22)
  • Participant in key private instruction (Olivet discourse question) (Mark 13:3–4)

3) Where He Appears in the Bible

Primary narrative locations (main story)

  • John — 1:35–42 — Leaves John the Baptist, follows Jesus, brings Simon to Jesus.
  • John — 1:43–44 — Identified as from Bethsaida (shared hometown with Philip and Peter).
  • Matthew — 4:18–20 — Called from fishing alongside Peter to follow Jesus.
  • Mark — 1:16–18 — Parallel call narrative: leaves nets to follow Jesus.
  • John — 6:5–9 — Notes the boy with loaves and fish before the feeding miracle.
  • Mark — 13:3–4 — With Peter/James/John asks Jesus about the future and the temple.
  • John — 12:20–22 — With Philip brings Greeks’ request to see Jesus.
  • Acts — 1:13–14 — Listed among apostles praying after the ascension.

Secondary references (lists/summaries)

  • Matthew — 10:2 — Included in the Twelve; paired with Peter in the list.
  • Mark — 3:18 — Included in the apostolic list.
  • Luke — 6:14 — Included in the apostolic list (Luke’s naming order differs).

4) Vital Statistics

  • Historical setting: Early 1st century AD, during the ministries of John the Baptist and Jesus, under Roman rule in Judea/Galilee (Gospel setting broadly; see John 1; Matt 4).
  • Geography (strong associations):
    • Bethsaida (hometown) (John 1:44)
    • Sea of Galilee / fishing context (implied by fishing vocation and call) (Matt 4:18; Mark 1:16)
    • Jerusalem (post-ascension gathering) (Acts 1:12–14)
  • Family and relationships:
    • Brother: Simon Peter (John 1:40–42; Matt 4:18)
    • Father (inference by shared patronymic): Peter is called “son of Jonah/John” (Matt 16:17; John 1:42). Scripture does not explicitly name Andrew’s father, but it is a reasonable inference they share the same father.
    • Close associates: Philip (John 1:44; 12:20–22); the inner-circle context includes Peter/James/John alongside Andrew in Mark 13:3–4.
  • Roles/offices: Disciple; apostle (one of the Twelve) (Matt 10:2; Acts 1:13).
  • Key turning points:
    • Turns from John the Baptist to follow Jesus (John 1:35–40)
    • Brings Peter to Jesus (John 1:41–42)
    • Leaves fishing life to follow Jesus (Matt 4:18–20)
    • Learns Jesus’ sufficiency when resources seem small (John 6:8–13)
    • Helps bring outsiders’ interest directly to Jesus (John 12:20–22)
    • Perseveres with the church-in-waiting after the ascension (Acts 1:13–14)

6) Strengths, Weaknesses, and Heart-Level Patterns

Strengths (with textual grounding)

  • Responsive faith: He follows when Jesus is identified and invited (John 1:37–39).
  • Evangelistic instinct (“bringer”): “He first found his own brother… and brought him to Jesus” (John 1:41–42).
  • Service-oriented practicality: He pays attention to concrete needs and available resources (John 6:8–9).
  • Willingness to facilitate access to Jesus: With Philip, he helps connect seekers to Christ (John 12:20–22).
  • Persevering disciple: He remains with the apostles in prayerful dependence after the ascension (Acts 1:13–14).

Weaknesses / blind spots (careful, text-anchored)

Scripture gives few explicit failures for Andrew. The clearest “limitations” appear indirectly:
  • Realism that can sound like limitation: “There is a boy here… but what are they for so many?” (John 6:9). Andrew sees the resource but also the impossibility—honest assessment that still needs Jesus’ power.

Motives / fears / desires (inferred carefully)

  • Desire to share good news: Andrew immediately seeks his brother after meeting Jesus (John 1:41). The text supports a heart that doesn’t hoard discovery.
  • Concern for practical needs: He speaks up about available food (John 6:8–9), suggesting attentiveness and responsibility.
  • Openness to outsiders seeking Jesus: He participates in bringing Greeks’ interest forward (John 12:20–22), suggesting a wideness-of-mercy instinct aligned with Jesus’ mission.

What changes (or doesn’t) over time?

  • Consistent pattern: Andrew repeatedly appears as a connector—Peter to Jesus (John 1:41–42), resources to Jesus (John 6:8–9), seekers to Jesus (John 12:20–22).
  • Growing context: early personal witness (John 1) expands into participation in Jesus’ broader mission and the post-ascension praying community (Acts 1:13–14). He remains present and faithful even when not prominent.

7) Victories and Challenges

Major “wins” (with references)

  • Introduces Peter to Jesus: A quiet act with enormous ripple effects (John 1:41–42).
  • Immediate obedience to follow: Leaves old life to become a fisher of men (Matt 4:18–20; Mark 1:16–18).
  • Faithful participation as an apostle: Named among the Twelve and later the praying apostles (Matt 10:2; Acts 1:13–14).
  • Helps facilitate Jesus’ provision: Brings forward the loaves and fish that Jesus multiplies (John 6:8–13).
  • Serves as a bridge for Gentile interest: Greeks seeking Jesus are brought through Andrew and Philip (John 12:20–22).

Major challenges (with references)

  • Cost of leaving livelihood and identity: The call requires abandoning nets/boat/work patterns (Matt 4:18–20; Mark 1:16–18).
  • Facing “too small” realities: He sees the limits of resources before Jesus acts (John 6:9).
  • Living through end-times discourse and uncertainty: He asks about “when” and “what sign,” entering weighty instruction meant to produce endurance (Mark 13:3–13).
  • Waiting after ascension: No immediate visible resolution—devotion to prayer while awaiting the Spirit (Acts 1:13–14).
Cost of faithfulness: Andrew’s faithfulness is not portrayed as public prominence but as steady, sometimes hidden service—following, bringing, waiting (John 1:41–42; Acts 1:13–14).

9) Theology and Big Themes

God’s character revealed

  • Christ’s worthiness to be followed: Andrew’s immediate response highlights Jesus as the true center once revealed (John 1:35–39).
  • Christ’s power to provide: Jesus turns scarcity into abundance (John 6:11–13).
  • Christ’s accessibility to seekers: Even Greeks (outsiders to Israel’s covenant community) are brought near in Jesus’ presence (John 12:20–22), anticipating the global mission.

Discipleship and mission

  • Calling reshapes vocation: Fishermen are redirected toward people (“fishers of men”) (Matt 4:19).
  • Witness spreads relationally: The gospel often moves along family/friend lines—Andrew to Peter (John 1:41–42).
  • Ordinary faithfulness matters: Andrew’s recurring role is not spotlight leadership but faithful mediation—bringing, noticing, serving, waiting (John 1; 6; 12; Acts 1).

Human nature: limitation and faith

  • Andrew voices the realistic gap: “what are they for so many?” (John 6:9). The text showcases a common disciple experience: honest awareness of limits that must be surrendered to Jesus’ competence.

10) How Andrew Points Us to Christ

Pattern: “Come and see” becomes “come to Jesus”

Andrew first follows Jesus at the invitation “Come and you will see” (John 1:39). Immediately, he becomes one who says, in effect, come meet Him—bringing Peter (John 1:41–42). This points to Christ as the true treasure and center: discipleship naturally overflows into testimony.

Gospel connection: Jesus receives small offerings and makes them fruitful

Andrew brings forward a small, insufficient-seeming provision (John 6:8–9). Jesus receives it, gives thanks, and multiplies it for the crowd (John 6:11–13). The pattern preaches the gospel logic: our insufficiency is not the final word; Christ’s sufficiency is (cf. 2 Cor 12:9).

Mission horizon: outsiders seeking Jesus

When Greeks want to see Jesus, Andrew participates in facilitating access (John 12:20–22). This anticipates the post-resurrection expansion of salvation blessing to the nations (Acts 1:8; Matt 28:18–20)—not by ethnic boundary markers, but by coming to Christ.
(Note: Scripture does not present Andrew as a direct “type” like an OT office-holder might be; the most grounded approach is to trace these gospel-shaped patterns explicitly rooted in his narrative scenes.)

11) Lessons and Applications (8–15, Scripture-tied)

  1. Follow the clearest light you have: Andrew moves when Jesus is identified (John 1:35–37).
  2. Don’t keep Christ to yourself: Andrew brings his brother to Jesus (John 1:41–42).
  3. The kingdom often advances through ordinary relationships: family witness matters (John 1:41–42).
  4. Obedience can be immediate and costly: leaving nets models decisive repentance and reorientation (Matt 4:19–20).
  5. Bring what you have, even if it feels small: Andrew offers the loaves/fish; Jesus supplies the rest (John 6:8–13).
  6. Name the need, but don’t enthrone the limitation: “but what are they for so many?” is honest—yet Jesus is the answer (John 6:9–11).
  7. Help seekers get to Jesus, even if you’re not the main speaker: Andrew with Philip is a model of facilitating access (John 12:20–22).
  8. Stay prayerful in waiting seasons: after the ascension, the disciples devote themselves to prayer (Acts 1:13–14).
  9. Ask Jesus hard questions and receive His instruction: Andrew asks about the future; disciples learn endurance (Mark 13:3–13).
  10. Faithfulness isn’t measured by prominence: Andrew’s quiet consistency is repeatedly honored by inclusion and presence (Matt 10:2; Acts 1:13).

Warnings to heed

  • Don’t let “too small” thinking choke obedience: Andrew’s realistic question is common; disciples must learn to act in dependence (John 6:9–11).
  • Don’t confuse nearness to Jesus with full understanding: the disciples ask “when” and “what sign,” and Jesus calls them to watchfulness and endurance (Mark 13:3–13).

Examples to imitate

  • Initiating witness: bringing Peter (John 1:41–42).
  • Practical service: noticing resources/needs (John 6:8–9).
  • Persevering prayer: waiting together (Acts 1:14).
Applications to specific areas:
  • Personal holiness: immediate obedience to Christ’s call (Matt 4:19–20).
  • Relationships: intentional, humble invitation to Jesus (John 1:41–42).
  • Prayer: persistent dependence in uncertain seasons (Acts 1:14).
  • Suffering/uncertainty: learn watchfulness and endurance from Jesus’ teaching (Mark 13).
  • Leadership: lead by connecting people to Christ, not to yourself (John 12:20–22).

12) Discussion and Reflection Questions (10–20)

Personal reflection

  1. Who is your “Peter”—someone close to you who needs a simple, direct introduction to Jesus? (John 1:41–42)
  2. What “nets” (identity, security, routine) make following Jesus feel costly right now? (Matt 4:18–20)
  3. Where do you tend to say, “What is that among so many?” and stop short of obedience? (John 6:9)
  4. What does it look like for you to “devote yourself to prayer” in a waiting season? (Acts 1:14)

Small group discussion

  1. Why do you think Scripture repeatedly shows Andrew as a connector rather than a spotlight preacher? What does that say about body-life? (John 1:41–42; 12:20–22)
  2. How can a group cultivate attentiveness to needs/resources without drifting into anxiety or control? (John 6:8–11)
  3. What are healthy ways to help “seekers” get to Jesus without making ourselves gatekeepers? (John 12:20–22)

Deep study (text observation)

  1. In John 1:35–42, what verbs describe Andrew’s movement (heard, followed, stayed, found, brought)? What sequence of discipleship is implied?
  2. Compare Matt 4:18–20 with John 1:35–42: how do these passages relate (initial following vs decisive vocational call)?
  3. In John 6:8–13, what is Andrew’s exact contribution, and what is Jesus’ contribution? How does the narrative highlight the difference?
  4. In Mark 13:3–13, what does Jesus emphasize in response to the disciples’ question—dates or endurance? Why?
  5. In Acts 1:13–14, what communal practices mark the disciples in transition, and what might that teach about preparation for mission?

Application synthesis

  1. What “bringing” step could you take this week (a conversation, an invitation, a prayerful introduction)? (John 1:41–42)
  2. What small resource can you offer to Jesus for His use—time, skills, hospitality, money—without waiting for it to feel “enough”? (John 6:8–11)
  3. How can your group make space for “Andrew” roles—people who quietly connect others to Christ?

13) Prayer Response (Scripture-saturated)

Lord Jesus, You are the Lamb of God and the One worth following (John 1:35–39). Make us like Andrew—quick to come to You and quick to bring others to You (John 1:41–42). Forgive us for shrinking back when what we have seems too small (John 6:9). Teach us to offer it to You in faith, trusting Your power to provide beyond our limits (John 6:11–13). In seasons of waiting, keep us devoted to prayer and united in hope (Acts 1:14). Use our ordinary relationships and quiet acts of service to help many see You and believe. Amen.
