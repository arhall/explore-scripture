Important note on names: The New Testament also includes a “Lazarus” in Jesus’ parable of the rich man and Lazarus (Luke 16:19–31). That is a parable character, and Scripture does not identify him as the same person as Lazarus of Bethany (John 11–12). In this study, “Lazarus” refers primarily to Lazarus of Bethany unless otherwise stated.

1) Story Summary

Lazarus enters the biblical narrative as a beloved friend of Jesus and the brother of Martha and Mary in Bethany (John 11:1–5). When Lazarus falls seriously ill, his sisters send word to Jesus, expecting His help (John 11:3). Jesus delays, explaining that the illness will serve God’s glory (John 11:4–6). During that delay, Lazarus dies (John 11:14).
Jesus then travels to Bethany near Jerusalem (John 11:17–18). Martha meets Him first, grieving yet confessing faith in God’s ability and in the future resurrection (John 11:21–27). Mary also comes weeping, and Jesus—deeply moved—weeps with them (John 11:32–35). At the tomb, Jesus commands the stone removed, prays to the Father, and calls Lazarus out; Lazarus comes out alive, still bound in grave cloths (John 11:38–44). Many believe, while others report to the authorities, and opposition intensifies (John 11:45–53).
Afterward, Lazarus appears again at a meal in Bethany where Mary anoints Jesus (John 12:1–8). Crowds come not only to see Jesus but also to see Lazarus—the living sign of Jesus’ life-giving power (John 12:9). The chief priests plot to kill Lazarus too, because many were believing in Jesus on account of him (John 12:10–11). Lazarus’s narrative effectively ends there, with his resurrected life functioning as public testimony that provokes both faith and hostility (John 12:9–11).
Where his story begins and ends:
  • Begins: John 11:1–3 (introduced as the brother of Mary and Martha, sick in Bethany)
  • Ends: John 12:10–11 (leaders plot to kill him; many believe because of him)

2) What He’s Known For

Lazarus is most known as the man Jesus raised from the dead, a climactic “sign” that displays Christ’s authority over death and foreshadows Jesus’ own resurrection (John 11:43–44; cf. John 12:9–11).
Headline descriptors
  • Beloved friend of Jesus (John 11:3, 11, 36)
  • Center of Jesus’ greatest public sign in John’s Gospel (John 11:43–44)
  • Living testimony that stirred belief (John 12:9–11)
  • A flashpoint for escalating opposition (John 11:45–53; 12:10–11)
  • Silent-but-powerful witness (not recorded speaking; the sign speaks through him—John 12:11)
  • A picture of life granted by Christ’s call (John 11:43–44)

3) Where He Appears in the Bible

Primary narrative locations

  • John — 11:1–44 — Lazarus is sick, dies, and is raised by Jesus.
  • John — 11:45–53 — The raising triggers belief and intensifies the leaders’ plot against Jesus.
  • John — 12:1–11 — Lazarus reclines at table; crowds come; leaders plot to kill Lazarus because many believe.

Secondary / thematic references

  • Luke — 16:19–31 — “Lazarus” in a parable (not identified with Bethany; treat as distinct unless Scripture says otherwise).
  • John — 11:25–26 — Jesus’ “I am the resurrection and the life” declaration occurs in Lazarus’s narrative context.
  • John — 5:25–29 — Jesus teaches His voice will raise the dead; Lazarus’s raising illustrates this claim.
  • John — 10:17–18 — Jesus’ authority over life/death frames the sign.
  • John — 20:30–31 — John’s purpose statement helps interpret Lazarus’s raising as a “sign” written to produce belief.

4) Vital Statistics

  • Historical setting: Late in Jesus’ earthly ministry, near Jerusalem, shortly before Passover and the crucifixion (John 11:18, 55; 12:1).
  • Geography: Bethany, near Jerusalem (about fifteen stadia away) (John 11:18); associated with gatherings at the home shared with Mary and Martha (John 11:1; 12:1–2).
  • Family & relationships:
    • Sisters: Martha and Mary (John 11:1–2, 5, 19).
    • Relationship with Jesus: described as loved and as friend (John 11:3, 5, 11).
    • Wider community: mourners and visitors from Jerusalem present (John 11:19, 31).
  • Roles/offices: No official office named; functions as a disciple/friend whose life becomes a public witness (John 11:11; 12:10–11).
  • Key turning points:
    • Illness and Jesus’ delay (John 11:3–6)
    • Death and burial (John 11:14–17)
    • Jesus’ declaration: “I am the resurrection and the life” (John 11:25–26)
    • Resurrection at Jesus’ command (John 11:43–44)
    • Becoming a catalyst for belief and opposition (John 11:45–53; 12:10–11)

6) Strengths, Weaknesses, and Heart-Level Patterns

Strengths (evidenced in the text)

  • Beloved in the community of faith and friendship with Jesus (John 11:3, 5, 11, 36).
  • His life becomes a powerful, unarguable testimony to Christ’s authority—many believe “on account of him” (John 12:10–11).
  • Receives life by responding to Christ’s call (Lazarus comes out when Jesus calls) (John 11:43–44).

Weaknesses / sins / blind spots

  • Scripture records no explicit moral failure or spoken doubt from Lazarus himself. He is notably silent in the narrative. We should not invent faults where the text is quiet.

Motives / fears / desires (careful inferences)

  • The sisters’ message (“he whom you love is ill”) suggests Lazarus is part of a relationship of trust and love with Jesus (John 11:3, 5).
  • Lazarus’s “role” is less about his inner psychology (not narrated) and more about what his story reveals about Jesus (John 11:4, 25–26; 12:9–11).

What changes (or doesn’t) over time?

  • Radical change: from death to life by the command of Christ (John 11:43–44).
  • Relational continuity: he remains in fellowship—reclining at table with Jesus afterward (John 12:1–2).
  • Public pressure increases: after resurrection, he becomes a target of hostility (John 12:10–11).

7) Victories and Challenges

Major “wins”

  • Raised from the dead by Jesus’ word (John 11:43–44).
  • Becomes an instrument through whom many believe in Jesus (John 12:9–11).
  • His story magnifies God’s glory in Christ (John 11:4, 40).

Major challenges

  • Suffering and death (John 11:14–17).
  • Family grief and communal mourning surrounding his death (John 11:19, 31–35).
  • Becoming a target for murder because of association with Jesus’ work (John 12:10–11).
Cost of faithfulness / consequences of hardened unbelief:The same miracle that births faith also provokes lethal opposition (John 11:45–53; 12:10–11). Lazarus’s resurrected life becomes a “sign” that forces a response.

  • John 11:21–27 — Martha’s confession and Jesus’ self-revelation.

9) Theology and Big Themes

  • Christology: Jesus as Life-giver. Lazarus’s raising is not merely compassion; it reveals Jesus’ identity and authority (John 11:25–26, 43–44).
  • Glory of God revealed in suffering and reversal. The narrative repeatedly interprets the event as aimed at God’s glory (John 11:4, 40).
  • The already/not-yet of resurrection hope. Martha affirms future resurrection (John 11:24), and Jesus brings a present sign that anticipates the final resurrection (John 11:25–26; cf. John 5:28–29).
  • The division caused by Jesus’ works. The sign produces both belief and hardened plotting (John 11:45–53; 12:10–11).
  • Compassion and incarnation. Jesus’ tears show His genuine engagement with human grief even while He intends to overturn death (John 11:33–35).
  • Substitution and mission trajectory. The raising of Lazarus accelerates the plot that leads to Jesus’ death (John 11:47–53), fitting John’s theme that Jesus gives life through His own sacrifice (cf. John 10:11, 17–18).

10) How Lazarus Points Us to Christ

  • As a “sign” that validates Jesus’ identity and mission. John frames miracles as signs meant to produce faith in Jesus as the Christ (John 20:30–31). Lazarus’s resurrection functions as a climactic sign (John 11:43–44; 12:9–11).
  • As a living illustration of Jesus’ claim: “I am the resurrection and the life” (John 11:25–26). Lazarus’s experience embodies that declaration (John 11:43–44).
  • Pattern that anticipates Jesus’ own death and resurrection. Lazarus is raised and becomes a catalyst for the leaders’ resolve to kill Jesus (John 11:45–53). In John’s narrative logic, life for others is bound up with Jesus’ path to the cross (John 11:50–52; cf. John 10:11).
  • Gospel connection: life comes by Christ’s effective call. Lazarus does not cooperate his way out of death; he lives because Jesus calls him (John 11:43–44), echoing the broader truth that the Son gives life by His voice (John 5:25).

11) Lessons and Applications

  1. Jesus’ delays are not indifference. His love is explicit even when He waits (John 11:5–6).
  2. Suffering can be a stage for God’s glory without minimizing pain. Jesus interprets the illness theologically (John 11:4) and still weeps (John 11:35).
  3. Bring honest grief to Jesus. Martha and Mary speak plainly (“Lord, if you had been here…”) (John 11:21, 32).
  4. Anchor hope in Jesus’ person, not only in future events. “I am the resurrection…” (John 11:25–26).
  5. Faith grows through revelation amid sorrow. “so that you may believe” (John 11:14–15).
  6. Jesus’ power is exercised through His word. “Lazarus, come out” (John 11:43).
  7. God often uses ordinary households for extraordinary testimony. A family in Bethany becomes the setting for major revelation (John 11:1–2; 12:1–2).
  8. A public witness to Christ can attract hostility. Lazarus becomes a target because many believe (John 12:10–11).
  9. Signs demand a response; neutrality is not stable. Some believe, others plot (John 11:45–53).
  10. New life leads into fellowship and worship. Lazarus is soon at table with Jesus; Mary’s devotion frames the aftermath (John 12:1–3).
Warnings to heed
  • Don’t interpret God’s timing as lack of love (John 11:5–6).
  • Beware hardening against clear evidence of Christ (John 11:47–53).
Examples to imitate
  • Bring grief to Jesus; confess truth about Him (John 11:21–27).
  • Let your life—quietly or loudly—point others to Christ (John 12:11).
Applications to specific areas:
  • Personal holiness: Live as someone Christ has “called out” of death into life (John 5:25; 11:43–44).
  • Relationships: Enter others’ grief with Christlike compassion (John 11:33–35).
  • Prayer: Trust the Father’s hearing and purposes (John 11:41–42).
  • Suffering: Hold together lament and hope (John 11:21–27, 35).
  • Leadership: Recognize that truth can polarize; respond with faith, not manipulation (John 11:45–53).

12) Discussion and Reflection Questions (10–20)

Personal reflection

  1. Where do you most struggle with Jesus’ timing, and how does John 11:5–6 challenge your assumptions?
  2. What do Martha’s words in John 11:21–22 reveal about faith that is grieving?
  3. How does Jesus’ weeping (John 11:35) shape your view of His compassion in your suffering?

Small group discussion

  1. Why does Jesus interpret Lazarus’s illness in terms of God’s glory (John 11:4, 40)? What does that mean—and what does it not mean?
  2. What’s the difference between believing in “a resurrection someday” (John 11:24) and trusting Jesus as resurrection and life now (John 11:25–26)?
  3. How do you see both mercy and authority in Jesus at the tomb (John 11:33–44)?
  4. Why do some people respond with faith while others respond with hostility (John 11:45–53)?

Deep study (text observation)

  1. Track the language of love/friendship in John 11:3, 5, 11, 36. What role does it play in interpreting the delay?
  2. What repeated misunderstandings appear in John 11, and how does Jesus clarify them (John 11:11–15, 23–26, 39–40)?
  3. What does Jesus’ prayer in John 11:41–42 teach about His relationship with the Father and the purpose of the sign?
  4. Compare John 5:25–29 with John 11:43–44. What claims are illustrated by Lazarus’s raising?
  5. Why do the leaders want Lazarus dead in John 12:10–11? What does that reveal about the nature of unbelief?
  6. How does Mary’s anointing (John 12:1–8) frame the Lazarus sign within Jesus’ approaching death?
  7. How does John 20:30–31 function as a “lens” for reading the Lazarus narrative?

13) Prayer Response

Lord Jesus, You are the resurrection and the life (John 11:25). Teach us to trust Your love even when You delay (John 11:5–6). Meet us in our grief, and form real faith in us as You did in Bethany (John 11:21–27, 35). By Your life-giving word, call us out of death and into obedience, so our lives would point others to You (John 5:25; 11:43–44; 12:11). Keep us steadfast when witness brings opposition (John 12:10–11), and fix our hope on the glory of God revealed in You (John 11:40). Amen.
