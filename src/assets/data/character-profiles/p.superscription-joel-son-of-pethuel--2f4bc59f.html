Note on identity: Several men named Joel appear in Scripture (e.g., Samuel’s son, civic leaders, genealogies). This study focuses on Joel the prophet, identified as “the son of Pethuel” and associated with the canonical Book of Joel (Joel 1:1). Where dates/details are debated, I’ll label them as uncertain and stick to what the text makes explicit.

1) Story Summary (Chronological)

Joel’s “story” is primarily his prophetic ministry, preserved in the Book of Joel. It begins with the prophetic word identifying him (Joel 1:1) and unfolds as a response to a devastating national crisis—described as a catastrophic locust invasion (and/or locust-like devastation) and severe drought that ruins grain, wine, and oil (Joel 1:4–12, 1:17–20). Joel interprets this calamity not merely as nature run amok but as a covenantally significant warning—a preview of “the day of the LORD” (Joel 1:15).
He calls every level of society to lamentation, fasting, and assembly: elders, inhabitants, priests, and ministers are summoned to cry out to the LORD (Joel 1:13–14; 2:15–17). The call is not only external ritual; it is heart-deep repentance: “Return to me with all your heart” (Joel 2:12–13). Joel highlights God’s character as the basis for repentance—gracious, merciful, slow to anger, abounding in steadfast love (Joel 2:13).
A major turning occurs when Joel announces God’s compassionate response: the LORD’s pity for his people (Joel 2:18) and promises of restoration—grain, wine, and oil; removal of reproach; and reversal of devastation (Joel 2:19–27). From there the horizon expands: Joel proclaims a future outpouring of God’s Spirit on all kinds of people—sons, daughters, old, young, servants—accompanied by cosmic signs tied to the coming day of the LORD (Joel 2:28–31). In that context comes a wide-open gospel-like invitation: “Everyone who calls on the name of the LORD shall be saved” (Joel 2:32).
The final movement turns outward to the nations: God will gather the nations for judgment in the “valley” of decision/judgment (Joel 3:2, 3:12–14). He denounces violence and exploitation (Joel 3:3–8) and names surrounding peoples as examples (Joel 3:4, 3:19). The book ends with a vision of the LORD dwelling in Zion, making his people secure, with creation renewed and a life-giving river image flowing from God’s presence (Joel 3:16–18, 3:20–21).
Where it begins and ends:
  • Begins: Joel 1:1 (Joel introduced; crisis described)
  • Ends: Joel 3:21 (final promise of God’s dwelling and vindication)

2) What Joel Is Known For

Joel is most known as a prophet who:
  • Interprets national disaster through the lens of the day of the LORD (Joel 1:15; 2:1–2).
  • Calls for communal repentance grounded in God’s merciful character (Joel 2:12–13).
  • Proclaims the future outpouring of the Spirit (Joel 2:28–29), later cited in the NT (Acts 2:16–21).
  • Announces salvation for all who call on the LORD (Joel 2:32), echoed in Romans (Rom 10:13).
  • Foretells judgment of the nations and the LORD’s reign from Zion (Joel 3:12–17).
Headline descriptors
  • Prophet of the Day of the LORD (Joel 1:15; 2:1, 2:11)
  • Herald of heart-level repentance (Joel 2:12–13)
  • Voice calling leaders to pray and intercede (Joel 2:17)
  • Proclaimer of Spirit outpouring (Joel 2:28–29)
  • Announcer of universal invitation to salvation (Joel 2:32)
  • Watchman of divine justice among nations (Joel 3:2, 3:12–14)

3) Where Joel Appears in the Bible

Primary narrative location (main story)

  • Joel — 1:1–3:21 — Joel’s prophetic message: crisis → repentance → restoration → Spirit outpouring → judgment and renewal.

Secondary references (later mentions / echoes)

  • Acts — 2:16–21 — Peter cites Joel 2:28–32 to interpret Pentecost and the Spirit’s coming.
  • Romans — 10:13 — Paul cites Joel 2:32 (“Everyone who calls…”), applying it within gospel proclamation.
  • Revelation — 9:1–11 (thematic echo) — locust imagery resonates with Joel’s locust/dread-day imagery (cf. Joel 2:1–11). (Echo/theme, not a named citation.)
  • Ezekiel — 47:1–12 (thematic echo) — life-giving river from God’s presence parallels Joel’s river/water imagery (Joel 3:18). (Echo/theme.)
  • Isaiah/Zephaniah/Amos — various “day of the LORD” texts — Joel stands in a wider prophetic chorus on judgment and salvation (e.g., Isa 13:6; Zeph 1:14–18; Amos 5:18–20). (Theme.)

Other “Joel” individuals (not the prophet)

  • 1 Samuel — 8:2 — Joel, Samuel’s firstborn son; judged as corrupt in leadership (1 Sam 8:2–3).
  • Nehemiah — 11:9 — Joel son of Zichri, a leader over Jerusalem’s inhabitants.
  • 1 Chronicles — various genealogies — multiple Joels appear in tribal records (e.g., 1 Chron 5:4; 6:33–34 depending on numbering/traditions).

4) Vital Statistics

  • Name: Joel (יוֹאֵל), commonly understood as “Yahweh is God” (theological weight fits the book’s emphasis on the LORD’s sovereignty).
  • Father: Pethuel (Joel 1:1). No other family details given.
  • Role/office: Prophet (Joel 1:1; functionally throughout the book).
  • Historical setting (uncertain): The book does not explicitly date Joel by naming a king (contrast Hosea 1:1; Isaiah 1:1).
    • What is explicit: He addresses Judah/Jerusalem/Zion, speaks of priests, the house of the LORD, and temple worship (Joel 1:9, 1:13–14; 2:17; 3:16–17).
    • Therefore: He ministered in a context where temple worship was functioning (at least in his prophetic frame).
  • Geography strongly associated: Judah, Jerusalem, Zion (Joel 2:1, 2:15, 2:32; 3:16–20). References to surrounding peoples (e.g., Philistia, Tyre/Sidon region; Egypt/Edom) appear in judgment oracles (Joel 3:4, 3:19).
  • Key turning points:
    • Interprets disaster as “day of the LORD” warning (Joel 1:15; 2:1–2).
    • Calls for national repentance and priestly intercession (Joel 2:12–17).
    • Announces divine pity and restoration (Joel 2:18–27).
    • Proclaims Spirit outpouring and salvation for all who call (Joel 2:28–32).
    • Declares judgment on oppressing nations and secure dwelling of the LORD in Zion (Joel 3:2, 3:16–21).

6) Strengths, Weaknesses, and Heart-Level Patterns

Strengths (evidenced in the text)

  • Theological clarity under crisis: Joel interprets disaster through God’s sovereignty and the day of the LORD (Joel 1:15; 2:11).
  • Pastoral urgency and courage: He sounds the alarm and calls for public repentance (Joel 2:1, 2:12, 2:15).
  • God-centered repentance: He roots the call to return in God’s revealed character (Joel 2:13).
  • Intercessory instinct: He directs priests to plead not only for relief but for God’s name among the nations (Joel 2:17).
  • Hope without denial: He holds together judgment-warning and restoration-promise (Joel 2:1–11; 2:18–27).

Weaknesses / sins / blind spots (what Scripture shows)

  • No personal moral failures are recorded for Joel in the book. The text presents him as a prophetic messenger; Scripture does not narrate his private life or missteps. (We should not invent any.)

Motives / fears / desires (careful inferences, anchored to the text)

  • Desire for covenant faithfulness: His relentless call to return suggests a burden for true repentance (Joel 2:12–13).
  • Concern for God’s honor: The prayer he puts on priests’ lips centers God’s reputation among nations (Joel 2:17).
  • Longing for God’s presence with his people: The book’s conclusion emphasizes the LORD dwelling in Zion (Joel 3:17, 3:21).

What changes (or doesn’t) over time?

  • The book’s movement shifts from devastation → repentance → restoration → global/judicial horizon, but Joel’s central emphasis does not change: the LORD is sovereign, the day of the LORD is real, repentance is necessary, and refuge is found in the LORD (Joel 1:15; 2:13; 2:32; 3:16).

7) Victories and Challenges

Major “wins” (faithfulness and impact)

  • Mobilizes a whole-community response—elders, priests, people—toward fasting and prayer (Joel 1:13–14; 2:15–17).
  • Proclaims a wide mercy grounded in God’s character (Joel 2:13–14).
  • Announces restoration and removal of shame (“my people shall never again be put to shame”) (Joel 2:26–27).
  • Becomes a key prophetic voice for NT fulfillment (Joel 2:28–32 → Acts 2:16–21; Joel 2:32 → Rom 10:13).

Major challenges (opposition, suffering, tension)

  • Ministering in national trauma: locust devastation, drought, and worship disruption (Joel 1:9–12, 1:16–20).
  • Calling for repentance when people may prefer explanations without moral weight (Joel 2:12–13).
  • Holding together terror and hope: The day of the LORD is both dreadful and a day of refuge—this tension is difficult to proclaim faithfully (Joel 2:11; 2:32; 3:16).
Cost dynamic: Joel’s message refuses cheap comfort; it demands repentance (Joel 2:12–17) while offering real hope (Joel 2:18–29). That combination often provokes resistance in every age.

9) Theology and Big Themes

God’s character revealed

  • Sovereign Judge: The LORD commands cosmic-scale events and judges nations (Joel 2:11; 3:12).
  • Merciful Covenant Lord: Repentance is possible because God is gracious and relents from disaster (Joel 2:13–14).
  • Present Refuge: God is not only a future judge but a present shelter for his people (Joel 3:16).

Covenant, worship, and communal holiness

  • The crisis disrupts offerings and priestly ministry (Joel 1:9, 1:13), pushing worship to the center of national life again (Joel 2:15–17). Joel reads calamity in covenant categories: sin, repentance, mercy, and restoration (Joel 2:12–27; compare Deut 28–30).

The day of the LORD

  • Joel intensifies the day-of-the-LORD theme: it is dreadful (Joel 2:11) yet paired with salvation for those who call (Joel 2:32). Judgment and mercy are not competing storylines; they are intertwined.

The Spirit and the widening of God’s people

  • The Spirit is poured out broadly—across age, gender, and social status (Joel 2:28–29). This anticipates the NT’s Spirit-empowered witness and the inclusion of “all flesh” (Acts 2:16–21).

Human nature: alarm → repentance → restoration

  • People need awakening (Joel 1:5), summons (Joel 2:1), and heart-rending return (Joel 2:12–13). God’s restoration aims at worship and knowing him, not just relief (Joel 2:26–27; 3:17).

10) How Joel Points Us to Christ

1) Promise-to-fulfillment connection (explicit OT↔NT)

  • Joel 2:28–32 is explicitly applied at Pentecost: Peter says, “This is what was uttered through the prophet Joel” (Acts 2:16–21). The Spirit’s coming through the risen Christ (Acts 2:32–33) matches Joel’s promised outpouring.
  • Joel 2:32 (“Everyone who calls on the name of the LORD shall be saved”) is applied to salvation in Christ (Rom 10:9–13). Paul’s logic connects calling on the LORD with confessing Jesus as Lord (Rom 10:9–13).

2) Office and pattern

  • Joel’s role as a prophet who calls God’s people back to God (Joel 2:12–13) participates in the prophetic pattern fulfilled perfectly in Jesus, the ultimate Word-bearer and climactic prophet (cf. Deut 18:15; Acts 3:22–23).

3) Day of the LORD and final judgment

  • Joel’s day-of-the-LORD theme sets categories that the NT develops around Christ’s return and final judgment (Joel 2:11; 3:14–16; cf. 2 Thess 1:7–10; Rev 19:11–16). Christ is both Savior for those who call and Judge of the nations (John 5:22–29).

4) Presence and new-creation imagery

  • Joel ends with God dwelling among his people and creation renewed (Joel 3:17–18). The NT culminates in God dwelling with his people in the new creation (Rev 21:3–4), with river imagery again (Rev 22:1–2; cf. Joel 3:18).

11) Lessons and Applications (8–15, tied to Scripture)

  1. Don’t waste crises—interpret them before God. Calamity should drive us to seek the LORD, not merely explanations (Joel 1:15; 1:19–20).
  2. Repentance is heart work, not performance. “Rend your hearts and not your garments” (Joel 2:13).
  3. God’s character is the engine of repentance. We return because he is gracious and merciful (Joel 2:13–14).
  4. True spiritual renewal is communal. Joel calls assemblies, not just private regret (Joel 1:14; 2:15–16).
  5. Spiritual leaders must model intercession. Priests are told to weep and plead for God’s people and God’s honor (Joel 2:17).
  6. God can restore what seems permanently lost. “I will restore… the years…” (Joel 2:25).
  7. Salvation is offered as wide as God’s invitation. “Everyone who calls…” (Joel 2:32; cf. Rom 10:13).
  8. The Spirit empowers ordinary people for God’s purposes. Sons/daughters, old/young, servants—no category is excluded (Joel 2:28–29; Acts 2:16–18).
  9. God cares about justice between peoples and nations. He judges exploitation and violence (Joel 3:2–8, 3:19).
  10. The final refuge is God himself, not circumstances. “The LORD is a refuge” (Joel 3:16), even when the day is terrifying (Joel 2:11).
  11. Worship belongs at the center of national and personal renewal. Joel’s calls revolve around the house of the LORD and prayer (Joel 1:9, 1:13–14; 2:17).

Warnings to heed

  • Don’t treat God’s warnings lightly (Joel 2:1–2, 2:11).
  • Don’t substitute religious activity for repentance (Joel 2:12–13).

Examples to imitate

  • Prompt, humble return to the LORD (Joel 2:12–13).
  • Intercessory leadership that pleads for mercy and God’s honor (Joel 2:17).

12) Discussion and Reflection Questions (10–20)

Personal reflection

  1. What “alarms” has God used to get your attention, and how have you responded (Joel 2:1)?
  2. Where do you most need “rend your heart” repentance instead of external fixes (Joel 2:13)?
  3. Which aspect of God’s character most helps you return to him today (Joel 2:13)?
  4. What would it look like for God to “restore the years” in your life—spiritually, not just circumstantially (Joel 2:25)?
  5. Do you live more as if the LORD is your refuge or as if stability is your refuge (Joel 3:16)?

Small group discussion

  1. Why do you think Joel emphasizes communal gatherings and leadership repentance (Joel 1:14; 2:15–17)?
  2. How do we hold together the dread of judgment and the openness of mercy without flattening either (Joel 2:11; 2:32)?
  3. What does Joel teach us about prayer that is concerned for God’s reputation (Joel 2:17)?

Deep study (observation/interpretation)

  1. Trace the repeated commands (“lament,” “fast,” “return,” “blow the trumpet”). What do they reveal about urgency (Joel 1:13–14; 2:1; 2:12; 2:15)?
  2. What is the function of the phrases “that you may know…” and “then you shall know…” (Joel 2:27; 3:17)?
  3. In Joel 2:28–32, list every group included. What does that inclusion imply about God’s mission?
  4. Compare Joel 2:32 with Romans 10:9–13. How does the NT apply Joel’s promise to Jesus?
  5. What sins of the nations are highlighted in Joel 3, and what does that teach about God’s justice (Joel 3:2–8, 3:19)?
  6. How does the book’s ending reshape the meaning of “restoration” (Joel 3:17–18, 3:21)?

13) Prayer Response (Scripture-shaped)

Lord GOD, you are gracious and merciful, slow to anger and abounding in steadfast love (Joel 2:13). We confess that we often treat your warnings lightly and cling to outward fixes instead of returning to you with all our hearts (Joel 2:12–13). Teach us to lament rightly, to pray humbly, and to seek your face together as your people (Joel 1:14; 2:15–17). Restore what sin and sorrow have devoured, and remove our shame by your mercy (Joel 2:25–27). Pour out your Spirit on us that we would live and speak as your witnesses (Joel 2:28–29; Acts 2:16–18). Make us a people who call on your name with faith, knowing you alone save (Joel 2:32; Rom 10:13). Be our refuge in the day of trouble, and dwell among us in holiness (Joel 3:16–17). Amen.
