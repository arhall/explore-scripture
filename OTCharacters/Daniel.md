<div><h1>Daniel</h1></div>
<div><br></div>
<div><b><h1>Daniel — Bible Character Study</h1></b></div>
<div><b><h2>1) Story Summary (Chronological)</h2></b></div>
<div>Daniel’s story begins with <b>his forced relocation from Judah to Babylon</b> during the first wave of exile. In the <b>third year of Jehoiakim king of Judah</b>, Nebuchadnezzar besieges Jerusalem, takes temple vessels, and brings select youths from Judah into Babylonian court training (Dan 1:1–7). Daniel (given the Babylonian name <b>Belteshazzar</b>, Dan 1:7) resolves <b>not to defile himself</b> with the royal food and drink, and God grants him favor, learning, and exceptional wisdom (Dan 1:8–20).</div>
<div>Daniel rises to prominence by God’s enablement to <b>reveal and interpret dreams</b>. When Nebuchadnezzar demands the dream and its interpretation, Daniel seeks mercy through prayer with his companions and God reveals the mystery; Daniel then confesses God’s sovereignty over kings and times (Dan 2:17–23) and interprets the dream of the statue and the coming kingdom (Dan 2:24–45). Daniel is promoted within Babylon’s administration (Dan 2:48–49).</div>
<div>Under Nebuchadnezzar, Daniel later interprets a second dream—of a great tree cut down—warning the king of divine judgment and calling him to repentance (Dan 4:19–27). The prophecy is fulfilled as Nebuchadnezzar is humbled until he acknowledges the Most High’s dominion (Dan 4:28–37).</div>
<div>In the reign of <b>Belshazzar</b>, Daniel is summoned to interpret the “writing on the wall,” announcing the end of Babylon’s rule and Belshazzar’s judgment (Dan 5:10–28). Babylon falls that night (Dan 5:30–31).</div>
<div>Under <b>Darius</b>, Daniel’s faithful prayer life becomes the pretext for a legal trap. Refusing to cease praying to the God of Israel, Daniel is thrown into the lions’ den, and God delivers him (Dan 6:10–23). Daniel continues to receive apocalyptic visions across the transition to Persian rule—visions of beasts and kingdoms, the “Son of Man,” angelic conflict, and the future of God’s people (Dan 7–12). Near the end of the book, Daniel is told to seal the words until the time of the end and is personally assured of final rest and resurrection hope: “you shall rest and shall stand… at the end of the days” (Dan 12:9–13).</div>
<div><b>Where his story begins:</b> Daniel 1:1–7<b>Where it ends:</b> Daniel 12:13 (with the final personal word to Daniel)</div>
<div><br></div>
<div><b><h2>2) What He’s Known For</h2></b></div>
<div>Daniel is most known for <b>faithful witness in exile</b>, <b>God-given wisdom</b>, <b>courageous prayer</b>, and <b>revelatory visions</b> about God’s kingdom and the course of empires.</div>
<div><b>Headline descriptors</b><br></div>
<ul>
<li><b>Exile who would not defile</b> (Dan 1:8)</li>
<li><b>Wise counselor and dream interpreter</b> (Dan 2:19–23; 4:19–27)</li>
<li><b>Unshakable in prayer under pressure</b> (Dan 6:10–11)</li>
<li><b>Delivered from the lions</b> (Dan 6:22–23)</li>
<li><b>Prophet of kingdoms and the “Son of Man”</b> (Dan 7:13–14)</li>
<li><b>Intercessor confessing national sin</b> (Dan 9:3–19)</li>
<li><b>Receiver of visions about the end and resurrection</b> (Dan 12:2–3)</li>
</ul>
<div><br></div>
<div><b><h2>3) Where He Appears in the Bible</h2></b></div>
<div><b><h2>Primary narrative locations</h2></b></div>
<ul>
<li><b>Daniel — Dan 1–12</b> — Daniel’s life in exile, court service, deliverance narratives, and apocalyptic visions.</li>
</ul>
<div><b><h2>Secondary references and later mentions (and notes on uncertainty where needed)</h2></b></div>
<ul>
<li><b>Ezekiel — Ezek 14:14, 14:20</b> — “Daniel” listed with Noah and Job as exemplary righteousness. <i>(Note: Some scholars debate whether Ezekiel’s “Daniel” is the same figure as the Daniel of the exile; Scripture’s text uses the name “Daniel,” but the identification beyond that is not explicit.)</i><br></li>
<li><b>Ezekiel — Ezek 28:3</b> — “Are you wiser than Daniel?” used proverbially for wisdom. <i>(Same identification uncertainty as above.)</i><br></li>
<li><b>Matthew — Matt 24:15</b> — Jesus references “the abomination of desolation spoken of by the prophet Daniel.”</li>
<li><b>Mark — Mark 13:14</b> — Parallel to Matthew’s reference to Daniel.</li>
<li><b>Hebrews — Heb 11:33</b> — Faith “stopped the mouths of lions” (echoing Dan 6).</li>
<li><b>Revelation — Rev 13; 17</b> — Imagery of beasts and kingdoms strongly echoes Daniel’s visions (esp. Dan 7), though Daniel is not named in these chapters.</li>
</ul>
<div><br></div>
<div><b><h2>4) Vital Statistics</h2></b></div>
<div><b><h2>Historical setting (as Scripture indicates)</h2></b></div>
<ul>
<li><b>Late kingdom of Judah → Babylonian exile → early Persian period</b> (Dan 1:1–2; 5:30–31; 6:1; 10:1).</li>
<li>Daniel’s recorded span includes <b>the reigns of Nebuchadnezzar, Belshazzar, Darius, and Cyrus</b> (Dan 1–6; 10:1).</li>
<li>Daniel is said to continue “until the first year of King Cyrus” (Dan 1:21), and later receives a vision in “the third year of Cyrus” (Dan 10:1). The “until” language naturally reads as <b>at least until</b> (not necessarily stopping there).</li>
</ul>
<div><b><h2>Geography (places strongly associated)</h2></b></div>
<ul>
<li><b>Jerusalem / Judah</b> (origin; exile begins from Jerusalem’s fall/tribute, Dan 1:1–2)</li>
<li><b>Babylon</b> (court training and service, Dan 1:4; 2:48; 4:30; 5:1)</li>
<li><b>Susa (Shushan) in Elam</b> (vision location, Dan 8:2)</li>
<li><b>River Ulai</b> (vision setting, Dan 8:2)</li>
<li><b>Tigris (Hiddekel)</b> (vision setting, Dan 10:4)</li>
</ul>
<div><b><h2>Family and relationships</h2></b></div>
<ul>
<li><b>Ethnicity/covenant identity:</b> “from Judah” (Dan 1:6), among the “people of Israel” (Dan 9:20).</li>
<li><b>Family status:</b> Taken from Judah’s elite/royal circles is implied by selection criteria (“of the royal family and of the nobility,” Dan 1:3), but Daniel’s specific parents are <b>not named</b>.</li>
<li><b>Spouse/children:</b> <b>Not mentioned</b>.</li>
<li><b>Key allies:</b> Hananiah, Mishael, Azariah (Shadrach, Meshach, Abednego) (Dan 1:6–7; 2:17–18).</li>
<li><b>Key earthly authorities:</b> Nebuchadnezzar (Dan 1–4), Belshazzar (Dan 5), Darius (Dan 6), Cyrus (Dan 10:1).</li>
<li><b>Key opponents:</b> jealous officials/satraps who scheme against him (Dan 6:4–9).</li>
</ul>
<div><b><h2>Roles/offices</h2></b></div>
<ul>
<li><b>Exilic court official / administrator</b> (Dan 2:48; 6:1–3)</li>
<li><b>Wise man / counselor</b> (Dan 2:12–14, 27–28)</li>
<li><b>Prophetic visionary / interpreter of revelation</b> (Dan 7:1–2; 8:1–2; 9:20–23; 10:1)</li>
</ul>
<div><b><h2>Key turning points</h2></b></div>
<ul>
<li><b>Resolve not to defile</b> (Dan 1:8)</li>
<li><b>Prayerful dependence for revelation</b> (Dan 2:17–23)</li>
<li><b>Public confession of God’s sovereignty before kings</b> (Dan 2:27–28; 4:34–35)</li>
<li><b>Costly fidelity in prayer leading to lions’ den</b> (Dan 6:10–23)</li>
<li><b>Intercessory confession for Israel and reception of “seventy weeks” vision</b> (Dan 9:3–27)</li>
<li><b>Overwhelming visions and strengthened by God’s messenger</b> (Dan 10:8–19; 12:8–13)</li>
</ul>

<div><b><h2>6) Strengths, Weaknesses, and Heart-Level Patterns</h2></b></div>
<div><b><h2>Strengths (with textual anchors)</h2></b></div>
<ul>
<li><b>Holiness with resolve</b>: “Daniel resolved that he would not defile himself” (Dan 1:8).</li>
<li><b>Wisdom and discernment from God</b>: God gave him “learning and skill… and… understanding in all visions and dreams” (Dan 1:17).</li>
<li><b>Prayerful dependence</b>: Daniel seeks mercy with his friends rather than relying on position or panic (Dan 2:17–18); he prays and then blesses God for revelation (Dan 2:19–23).</li>
<li><b>God-centered humility</b>: He refuses personal credit—“There is a God in heaven who reveals mysteries” (Dan 2:27–28).</li>
<li><b>Courageous truth-telling to power</b>: He speaks soberly to Nebuchadnezzar (Dan 4:19–27) and indicts Belshazzar’s pride and idolatry (Dan 5:22–23).</li>
<li><b>Consistency in devotion</b>: Daniel prays “as he had done previously,” even when it becomes illegal (Dan 6:10).</li>
<li><b>Intercessory repentance</b>: He confesses Israel’s sin as “we have sinned and done wrong” (Dan 9:5), appealing to God’s mercy and covenant name (Dan 9:18–19).</li>
</ul>
<div><b><h2>Weaknesses / sins / blind spots (what Scripture actually shows)</h2></b></div>
<div>Daniel is notable because <b>no specific moral scandal is recorded</b> about him in Daniel. Still, Scripture does show his <b>human limitations</b>:</div>
<ul>
<li><b>Overwhelmed by revelation</b>: He is “appalled” and “sick for some days” after the vision (Dan 8:27); he is left without strength and falls into deep sleep during a vision encounter (Dan 10:8–9). This is not sin, but creaturely frailty under divine disclosure.</li>
<li><b>Unresolved questions</b>: “I heard, but I did not understand” (Dan 12:8). Daniel’s humility includes accepting partial understanding.</li>
</ul>
<div><i>(Any stronger claims about personal sins, motives, or failures would go beyond what the text explicitly provides.)</i><br></div>
<div><b><h2>Motives / fears / desires (careful inferences anchored to text)</h2></b></div>
<ul>
<li><b>Desire for covenant faithfulness and purity</b>: implied by his refusal to be defiled (Dan 1:8).</li>
<li><b>Desire for God’s glory over self-advancement</b>: shown when he repeatedly credits God as revealer and ruler (Dan 2:20–23, 27–28; 4:34–35).</li>
<li><b>Burden for God’s people and God’s name</b>: in his prayer, Daniel pleads for Jerusalem “for your own sake” and “because your city and your people are called by your name” (Dan 9:17–19).</li>
<li><b>Fear of God outweighing fear of men</b>: demonstrated by continued prayer despite lethal consequences (Dan 6:10).</li>
</ul>
<div><b><h2>What changes (or doesn’t) over time?</h2></b></div>
<ul>
<li><b>Doesn’t change:</b> Daniel’s steady pattern of <b>principled holiness and prayer</b> from youth (Dan 1:8) to old age under new regimes (Dan 6:10; 9:3).</li>
<li><b>Develops/intensifies:</b> The book moves from <b>court narratives</b> (Dan 1–6) to <b>heavier apocalyptic revelation</b> (Dan 7–12), and Daniel’s role increasingly becomes <b>intercessor and receiver of mysteries</b> (Dan 9:20–23; 10:1–14).</li>
</ul>
<div><br></div>
<div><b><h2>7) Victories and Challenges</h2></b></div>
<div><b><h2>Major “wins” (obedience, influence, deliverance)</h2></b></div>
<ul>
<li><b>Faithful non-defilement and God-given favor</b> (Dan 1:8–16)</li>
<li><b>Excellence and witness through wisdom</b> (Dan 1:17–20)</li>
<li><b>Revelation through prayer; saving others from death</b> (Dan 2:17–24)</li>
<li><b>Publicly exalting God before kings</b> (Dan 2:27–28; 4:34–35; 6:26–27)</li>
<li><b>Deliverance from lions and vindication</b> (Dan 6:21–23)</li>
<li><b>Intercession grounded in God’s character and promises</b> (Dan 9:3–19)</li>
</ul>
<div><b><h2>Major challenges (pressure, suffering, opposition, cost)</h2></b></div>
<ul>
<li><b>Exile and cultural assimilation pressure</b> (Dan 1:3–7)</li>
<li><b>Death threats tied to impossible demands</b> (Dan 2:12–13)</li>
<li><b>Standing firm when prayer becomes illegal</b> (Dan 6:7–11)</li>
<li><b>Political jealousy and manipulation</b> (Dan 6:4–9)</li>
<li><b>Emotional and physical weight of visions</b> (Dan 7:28; 8:27; 10:8–19)</li>
<li><b>Living with partial understanding of God’s timetable</b> (Dan 12:8–9)</li>
</ul>
<div><b>Cost of faithfulness:</b> Daniel’s ordinary piety (prayer) becomes extraordinary courage when it brings him into mortal danger (Dan 6:10–17).<b>Consequence of compromise (shown around him):</b> Belshazzar’s pride and idolatry are judged (Dan 5:22–30), highlighting what Daniel refuses to become.</div>

<div><b><h2>9) Theology and Big Themes</h2></b></div>
<div><b><h2>God’s character revealed</h2></b></div>
<ul>
<li><b>Sovereign Lord of history:</b> He “removes kings and sets up kings” (Dan 2:21); his dominion is everlasting (Dan 4:34–35).</li>
<li><b>Holy Judge:</b> Babylon is “weighed… and found wanting” (Dan 5:27).</li>
<li><b>Revealer of mysteries:</b> God discloses what humans cannot access (Dan 2:28–30).</li>
<li><b>Deliverer and living God:</b> God rescues Daniel (Dan 6:22–23) and is confessed as “the living God… whose kingdom shall never be destroyed” (Dan 6:26–27).</li>
<li><b>Merciful and covenant-keeping:</b> Daniel appeals to God’s covenant love and mercy (Dan 9:4, 9, 18).</li>
</ul>
<div><b><h2>Covenant, exile, and holiness</h2></b></div>
<ul>
<li>Daniel embodies <b>covenant identity in displacement</b>—not assimilated, yet serving faithfully within pagan structures (Dan 1:8; 2:48; 6:1–3).</li>
<li>Exile is interpreted theologically: Jerusalem’s fall is not God’s defeat but part of God’s righteous dealings (Dan 9:11–14; cf. Dan 1:2).</li>
</ul>
<div><b><h2>Kingdom of God vs. kingdoms of man</h2></b></div>
<ul>
<li>Empires rise and fall, but God establishes an <b>everlasting kingdom</b> (Dan 2:44; 7:13–14, 27).</li>
<li>The “saints of the Most High” endure oppression but are ultimately vindicated (Dan 7:21–22, 25–27).</li>
</ul>
<div><b><h2>Worship and witness in a hostile world</h2></b></div>
<ul>
<li>Daniel’s life teaches worship as <b>public allegiance</b>: prayer and confession are acts of loyalty (Dan 6:10; 2:20–23).</li>
<li>Testimony reaches rulers: Nebuchadnezzar and Darius publicly acknowledge God’s greatness (Dan 4:34–37; 6:26–27).</li>
</ul>
<div><b><h2>Spiritual conflict behind political conflict</h2></b></div>
<ul>
<li>Daniel 10 portrays unseen conflict accompanying earthly events (Dan 10:12–14, 20–21), grounding hope not in human control but in God’s purposes.</li>
</ul>
<div><b><h2>Future hope: judgment and resurrection</h2></b></div>
<ul>
<li>Daniel gives one of the clearest OT statements of bodily resurrection and final destinies (Dan 12:2–3). History is moving toward consummation, not endless cycles.</li>
</ul>
<div><br></div>
<div><b><h2>10) How Daniel Points Us to Christ</h2></b></div>
<div><b><h2>1) The “Son of Man” kingdom fulfilled in Jesus</h2></b></div>
<div>Daniel sees “one like a son of man” receiving dominion, glory, and an everlasting kingdom (Dan 7:13–14). Jesus explicitly draws on this text in reference to himself (Matt 26:64; Mark 14:62), presenting his identity and reign in Danielic categories.</div>
<div><b><h2>2) The everlasting kingdom that crushes idols and empires</h2></b></div>
<div>The stone “cut out by no human hand” that becomes a mountain and fills the earth (Dan 2:34–35) corresponds to God’s kingdom that will never be destroyed (Dan 2:44–45). The NT proclaims Christ as the cornerstone and the inaugurator of God’s kingdom reign (themes resonant with Dan 2; cf. Matt 21:42–44 as broader “stone” kingdom imagery).</div>
<div><b><h2>3) Faithful obedience under trial (pattern fulfilled perfectly in Christ)</h2></b></div>
<div>Daniel’s righteousness under unjust condemnation (Dan 6:4–5, 10–17) anticipates the greater Righteous One, Jesus, condemned though innocent. Daniel is delivered from death; Jesus passes through death and is vindicated in resurrection—moving from typological pattern to ultimate fulfillment (cf. the logic of vindication and kingdom in Dan 7:13–14; and resurrection hope in Dan 12:2).</div>
<div><b><h2>4) Intercessory confession and the true Mediator</h2></b></div>
<div>Daniel intercedes for a sinful people, confessing “we have sinned” and pleading for mercy for God’s name (Dan 9:5, 18–19). Christ not only intercedes but <b>secures</b> redemption as the righteous mediator (NT development), fulfilling what Daniel’s prayer longs for: lasting righteousness and restored relationship with God (themes aligned with Dan 9:24).</div>
<div><i>(On Dan 9:24–27 specifically: Christians across traditions connect this passage to the Messiah and redemptive fulfillment, but details and timelines are debated. The text itself ties the vision to ending sin, atonement/iniquity resolution, and bringing everlasting righteousness (Dan 9:24), which the NT proclaims realized in Christ’s saving work.)</i><br></div>

<div><b><h2>11) Lessons and Applications</h2></b></div>
<div><b><h2>Examples to imitate</h2></b></div>
<ol>
<li><b>Resolve holiness before the test arrives</b> (Dan 1:8).</li>
<li><b>Seek God in prayer before you speak to power</b> (Dan 2:17–23).</li>
<li><b>Give God credit publicly</b> (Dan 2:27–28; 4:34–35).</li>
<li><b>Serve excellently without idolatry of success</b> (Dan 6:3–4).</li>
<li><b>Maintain ordinary spiritual disciplines even when costly</b> (Dan 6:10).</li>
<li><b>Tell the truth to leaders with sobriety and courage</b> (Dan 4:19–27; 5:22–23).</li>
<li><b>Confess sin with Scripture-shaped humility</b> (Dan 9:4–6).</li>
<li><b>Anchor hope in God’s kingdom, not political outcomes</b> (Dan 2:44; 7:27).</li>
<li><b>Expect unseen spiritual dimensions to visible trials</b> (Dan 10:12–14).</li>
<li><b>Live now in light of resurrection and final judgment</b> (Dan 12:2–3).</li>
</ol>
<div><b><h2>Warnings to heed</h2></b></div>
<ul>
<li><b>Pride is spiritually lethal</b> (Dan 4:30–37; 5:22–30).</li>
<li><b>Idolatry can look sophisticated (power, pleasure, self-glory)</b> but is judged by God (Dan 5:22–23, 27).</li>
<li><b>A “successful” exile can still be tested at the level of worship and allegiance</b> (Dan 6:7–10).</li>
</ul>
<div><b><h2>Application domains</h2></b></div>
<ul>
<li><b>Personal holiness:</b> choose faithfulness in small concessions (Dan 1:8–16).</li>
<li><b>Relationships/community:</b> pursue prayerful unity in crisis (Dan 2:17–18).</li>
<li><b>Prayer:</b> cultivate consistency that withstands pressure (Dan 6:10).</li>
<li><b>Suffering:</b> expect opposition for godliness and trust God’s vindication (Dan 6:16–23; 7:21–22).</li>
<li><b>Decision-making:</b> interpret events through God’s sovereignty (Dan 2:21; 4:35).</li>
<li><b>Leadership:</b> speak truth with humility and moral clarity (Dan 4:27; 5:22–23).</li>
</ul>

<div><b><h2>12) Discussion and Reflection Questions (10–20)</h2></b></div>
<div><b><h2>Personal reflection</h2></b></div>
<ol>
<li>What “defilements” (Dan 1:8) tempt you most to justify as harmless assimilation?</li>
<li>Where do you tend to seek control rather than seek mercy through prayer (Dan 2:17–18)?</li>
<li>How does Daniel’s habit of prayer (Dan 6:10) challenge your consistency?</li>
<li>Do you interpret success as proof of God’s pleasure, or as stewardship under God’s sovereignty (Dan 2:21; 6:3)?</li>
<li>Which is harder for you: telling truth to power (Dan 5:22–23) or confessing sin as “we” (Dan 9:5)?</li>
</ol>
<div><b><h2>Small group discussion</h2></b></div>
<ol>
<li>What does Daniel model about engaging culture without capitulating to it (Dan 1:8; 2:48; 6:1–3)?</li>
<li>How do Daniel 2 and Daniel 7 reshape the way Christians should think about nations and politics (Dan 2:44; 7:27)?</li>
<li>In what ways does pride show up like Nebuchadnezzar’s or Belshazzar’s in modern life (Dan 4:30; 5:22)?</li>
<li>What comforts you—and what unsettles you—about resurrection and final destinies (Dan 12:2–3)?</li>
<li>How should “spiritual conflict” language (Dan 10:12–14) shape prayer without breeding speculation?</li>
</ol>
<div><b><h2>Deep study (text-observation)</h2></b></div>
<ol>
<li>Trace how often Daniel explicitly attributes knowledge and power to God (Dan 2:20–23; 2:27–28; 4:34–35). What pattern emerges?</li>
<li>Compare Daniel’s confession in Dan 9:4–19 with covenant confession patterns elsewhere (e.g., Lev 26:40–42). What elements recur?</li>
<li>What does the narrative emphasize about Daniel’s innocence in Dan 6 (Dan 6:4–5, 22)? Why is that important?</li>
<li>In Dan 7:13–14, what is given to the “Son of Man,” and what is the scope/duration of his rule?</li>
<li>Observe Daniel’s physical responses to visions (Dan 7:28; 8:27; 10:8–9). What does that suggest about revelation’s weight?</li>
</ol>

Forgive us, for we have sinned and done wrong (Dan 9:5). For your name’s sake, show mercy, and turn our hearts from pride and idols (Dan 5:22–23). Fix our hope on your everlasting kingdom (Dan 2:44) and on the Son of Man whose dominion will never pass away (Dan 7:13–14). Keep us until the end, and grant us to stand in our allotted place by your grace (Dan 12:13). Amen.</div>
