<div><h1>Boaz</h1></div>
<div><br></div>
<div><b><h1>Boaz — Bible Character Study</h1></b></div>
<div><b><h2>1) Story Summary (chronological)</h2></b></div>
<div>Boaz enters the biblical narrative during the “days when the judges ruled” (Ruth 1:1), in Bethlehem of Judah (Ruth 1:1–2; 2:4). He is introduced as a “worthy” (or “prominent/valiant”) man of Elimelech’s clan (Ruth 2:1) who owns fields where the poor glean (Ruth 2:2–3). Ruth, a Moabite widow who has attached herself to Naomi and to the LORD (Ruth 1:16–17), “happens” to glean in Boaz’s field (Ruth 2:3). Boaz notices her, learns her story, and extends extraordinary protection and provision, grounding his kindness in the LORD’s covenant care and Ruth’s faithfulness (Ruth 2:8–12, 14–16).</div>
<div>As the plot tightens, Naomi recognizes Boaz as a “redeemer” (kinsman-redeemer; <i>go’el</i>) for their family (Ruth 2:20). Ruth approaches Boaz at the threshing floor according to Naomi’s plan, seeking redemption and protection under Boaz’s “wings” (Ruth 3:1–9). Boaz responds with integrity: he is willing to redeem, but insists on honoring the nearer redeemer’s legal right first (Ruth 3:10–13). He sends Ruth away provided for, protecting her reputation (Ruth 3:14–18).</div>
<div>In the city gate (the legal forum), Boaz publicly conducts the redemption. He offers the nearer redeemer the chance to redeem Naomi’s land, but clarifies that the redemption includes marrying Ruth to raise up the name of the dead on his inheritance; the nearer redeemer declines (Ruth 4:1–6). Boaz then redeems the property and takes Ruth as wife with witnesses and a formal declaration (Ruth 4:7–12). The LORD grants conception; Ruth bears a son, Obed (Ruth 4:13). The story closes by tying Boaz to the royal line: Obed fathers Jesse, and Jesse fathers David (Ruth 4:17, 22). Boaz’s story begins with his appearance in Ruth 2 and ends with the genealogy in Ruth 4.</div>
<div><b><h2>2) What He’s Known For</h2></b></div>
<div>Boaz is most known for faithful covenant-kindness expressed through integrity, generosity, and lawful redemption—becoming a key link in the lineage of David (and, by implication, the messianic line).</div>
<div><b>Headlines</b><br></div>
<ul>
<li>A “worthy” man in Bethlehem (Ruth 2:1).</li>
<li>Protector and provider for a vulnerable foreign widow (Ruth 2:8–9, 14–16).</li>
<li>Acknowledges and blesses Ruth’s faith and refuge in the LORD (Ruth 2:11–12).</li>
<li>A lawful “redeemer” who honors proper order and public witness (Ruth 3:12–13; 4:1–12).</li>
<li>A man of restraint and reputation—guards Ruth’s dignity (Ruth 3:14).</li>
<li>A bridge in redemptive history: ancestor of David (Ruth 4:17–22).</li>
</ul>
<div><b><h2>3) Where He Appears in the Bible</h2></b></div>
<div><b><h2>Primary narrative locations</h2></b></div>
<ul>
<li><b>Ruth — 2:1–23</b> — Boaz meets Ruth, shows protection and generous provision.</li>
<li><b>Ruth — 3:1–18</b> — Threshing-floor request; Boaz’s promise and integrity with the nearer redeemer.</li>
<li><b>Ruth — 4:1–17</b> — Legal redemption at the gate; marriage; Obed’s birth.</li>
</ul>
<div><b><h2>Secondary references (genealogies / later mentions)</h2></b></div>
<ul>
<li><b>Ruth — 4:18–22</b> — Genealogy from Perez to David includes Boaz.</li>
<li><b>1 Chronicles — 2:11–12</b> — Genealogy lists Boaz (as ancestor of David).</li>
<li><b>Matthew — 1:5–6</b> — Genealogy of Jesus names Boaz (with Rahab and Ruth in the line).</li>
</ul>
<div><b><h2>4) Vital Statistics</h2></b></div>
<ul>
<li><b>Historical setting:</b> “In the days when the judges ruled” (Ruth 1:1). This is Israel’s pre-monarchy period characterized by local governance and recurring spiritual/moral instability (cf. Judges 21:25).</li>
<li><b>Geography:</b><br></li>
<ul>
<li><b>Bethlehem (Judah)</b> — Boaz’s home region and the setting of the narrative’s legal and social life (Ruth 1:1–2; 2:4; 4:1–2).</li>
<li><b>Fields / threshing floor</b> near Bethlehem — key settings for provision and the redemption request (Ruth 2:3–4; 3:2).</li>
</ul>
<li><b>Family and relationships:</b><br></li>
<ul>
<li><b>Clan connection:</b> “of the clan of Elimelech” (Ruth 2:1).</li>
<li><b>Wife:</b> Ruth the Moabite (Ruth 4:10, 13).</li>
<li><b>Son:</b> Obed (Ruth 4:13, 17).</li>
<li><b>Extended line:</b> Obed → Jesse → David (Ruth 4:17, 22).</li>
<li><b>Allies/witnesses:</b> elders and townspeople at the gate (Ruth 4:2, 9–12).</li>
<li><b>“Nearer redeemer”:</b> unnamed kinsman with first right of redemption (Ruth 3:12; 4:1–6).</li>
</ul>
<li><b>Roles/offices:</b><br></li>
<ul>
<li>Not a prophet/judge/king. He is a <b>landowner</b> and <b>kinsman-redeemer</b> (<i>go’el</i>) acting within Israel’s covenant law and community structures (Ruth 2:1; 2:20; 3:9–13; 4:1–12).</li>
</ul>
<li><b>Key turning points:</b><br></li>
<ul>
<li><b>Recognition and favor toward Ruth</b> (Ruth 2:8–12).</li>
<li><b>Ruth’s redemption request at the threshing floor</b> (Ruth 3:9).</li>
<li><b>Boaz’s insistence on lawful order (nearer redeemer first)</b> (Ruth 3:12–13).</li>
<li><b>Public redemption and marriage covenant at the gate</b> (Ruth 4:1–12).</li>
<li><b>Birth of Obed and insertion into Davidic line</b> (Ruth 4:13, 17, 22).</li>
</ul>
</ul>

<div><b><span style="font-size: 16px">6) Strengths, Weaknesses, and Heart-Level Patterns</span></b></div>
<div><b><h2>Strengths (with textual grounding)</h2></b></div>
<ul>
<li><b>Covenant kindness expressed in practical action:</b> protection, provision, and advocacy for Ruth (Ruth 2:8–9, 14–16).</li>
<li><b>Spiritual discernment and God-centered speech:</b> he frames events in terms of the LORD’s blessing and refuge (Ruth 2:4, 12).</li>
<li><b>Respect for dignity and purity/reputation:</b> he ensures Ruth is not exposed to harm or gossip (Ruth 2:9; 3:14).</li>
<li><b>Integrity and legal faithfulness:</b> willing to redeem, but insists the nearer redeemer be consulted first (Ruth 3:12–13); conducts transaction publicly (Ruth 4:1–12).</li>
<li><b>Courage to bear cost for another’s restoration:</b> embraces the full terms of redemption including Ruth and the deceased’s name (Ruth 4:9–10).</li>
</ul>
<div><b><h2>Weaknesses / sins / blind spots (staying close to the text)</h2></b></div>
<div>Scripture does not record a moral failure for Boaz in Ruth. What we <i>can</i> say cautiously:</div>
<ul>
<li><b>Potential vulnerability to misunderstanding:</b> the threshing-floor scene could have been misconstrued socially, which is why Boaz acts quickly to protect Ruth’s reputation (Ruth 3:14). The text presents his choices as wise, not blameworthy.</li>
</ul>
<div><b><h2>Motives / fears / desires (careful inference)</h2></b></div>
<ul>
<li><b>Desire to honor the LORD through righteousness and kindness:</b> implied by his blessing language and his lawful, public handling (Ruth 2:12; 3:11–13; 4:9–12).</li>
<li><b>Concern for righteousness </b><b><i>and</i></b><b> community witness:</b> his insistence on proper order and witnesses suggests he values public integrity (Ruth 4:1–12).</li>
<li><b>Protective compassion for the vulnerable:</b> shown in proactive safeguards and generosity (Ruth 2:8–9, 15–16).</li>
</ul>
<div><b><h2>What changes (or doesn’t) over time?</h2></b></div>
<div>Boaz’s pattern is notably consistent: <b>kindness + integrity</b> from first encounter (Ruth 2) through private vulnerability (Ruth 3) to public legal action (Ruth 4). The setting shifts, but the character remains steady (Ruth 2:8–12; 3:10–13; 4:9–10).</div>
<div><b><h2>7) Victories and Challenges</h2></b></div>
<div><b><h2>Major “wins” (faithfulness and influence)</h2></b></div>
<ul>
<li><b>Protection and provision for Ruth in a dangerous social context</b> (Ruth 2:8–9, 14–16).</li>
<li><b>Theological encouragement:</b> recognizing Ruth’s faith and pronouncing blessing (Ruth 2:11–12).</li>
<li><b>Lawful redemption that restores Naomi’s family line and inheritance</b> (Ruth 4:9–10).</li>
<li><b>Becoming an instrument in God’s providence toward David’s line</b> (Ruth 4:17–22; cf. Matt 1:5–6).</li>
</ul>
<div><b><h2>Major challenges (cost, risk, complexity)</h2></b></div>
<ul>
<li><b>Navigating redemption lawfully with competing rights:</b> the nearer redeemer must be approached first (Ruth 3:12; 4:1–6).</li>
<li><b>Social/reputational risk in the threshing-floor encounter:</b> managed carefully to protect Ruth (Ruth 3:14).</li>
<li><b>The cost of redemption:</b> includes land, marriage, and raising up the dead man’s name (Ruth 4:5, 9–10).</li>
</ul>
<div><b>Cost of faithfulness:</b> Boaz embraces the full obligation when another man declines due to potential loss to his own inheritance (Ruth 4:6, 9–10). The narrative spotlights that redemption is costly—and Boaz accepts the cost.</div>

<div><b><h2>9) Theology and Big Themes</h2></b></div>
<ul>
<li><b>God’s covenant care mediated through faithful people:</b> The LORD’s kindness is expressed through Boaz’s tangible righteousness (Ruth 2:12, 20; 4:14).</li>
<li><b>Redemption as costly, public, and lawful:</b> Boaz’s redemption is not sentimental; it is legal, witnessed, and involves real cost (Ruth 4:1–12).</li>
<li><b>Inclusion of the outsider into God’s people and purposes:</b> Ruth the Moabite is honored, protected, and brought into the line leading to David (Ruth 2:10–12; 4:13–22).</li>
<li><b>Providence in the ordinary:</b> “happened” events (gleaning in the right field) become the hinge of redemptive history (Ruth 2:3; 4:17–22).</li>
<li><b>The LORD as ultimate Redeemer:</b> Human redemption points upward; the community blesses the LORD for restoring Naomi (Ruth 4:14).</li>
</ul>
<div><b><h2>10) How Boaz Points Us to Christ</h2></b></div>
<div>Boaz is not a messiah-figure in a simplistic sense, but the book of Ruth presents patterns that legitimately echo forward.</div>
<div><b><h2>Typology (careful and biblically grounded)</h2></b></div>
<ul>
<li><b>Kinsman-redeemer pattern:</b> Boaz functions as a <i>go’el</i> who willingly pays a cost to redeem and restore a vulnerable family line (Ruth 2:20; 4:9–10). This anticipates the broader biblical theme of redemption culminating in Christ, who redeems his people at great cost (Mark 10:45; Eph 1:7; 1 Pet 1:18–19).</li>
<li><b>Refuge and “wings” imagery:</b> Ruth seeks refuge under the LORD’s wings (Ruth 2:12) and then asks Boaz to “spread your wings” (Ruth 3:9). Boaz becomes an instrument of the LORD’s refuge—pointing to Christ as the ultimate shelter and protector for sinners (Matt 23:37; John 10:11).</li>
<li><b>Genealogical trajectory to David and Messiah:</b> Boaz is in the Davidic line (Ruth 4:17–22), and the NT explicitly includes Boaz in Jesus’ genealogy (Matt 1:5–6). The narrative shows God preparing the way for the kingdom and its King.</li>
</ul>
<div><b><h2>Gospel connections</h2></b></div>
<ul>
<li><b>Grace to outsiders:</b> Ruth, a foreigner, is received and honored—an enacted parable of Gentile inclusion by grace (Ruth 2:10–12; cf. Eph 2:12–13, 19).</li>
<li><b>Redemption creates a new future:</b> what was empty becomes full through lawful redemption and God’s gift of life (Ruth 1:21; 4:13–17), echoing how salvation in Christ reverses spiritual death into life (Eph 2:1–5).</li>
</ul>
<div><b><h2>11) Lessons and Applications</h2></b></div>
<ol>
<li><b>Faithfulness often looks like ordinary obedience in ordinary places</b> (Ruth 2:3–4).</li>
<li><b>Godliness includes how you treat the vulnerable</b>—especially those without power or status (Ruth 2:8–9; cf. Deut 24:19–22).</li>
<li><b>Generosity can exceed the minimum and still be righteous</b> (Ruth 2:14–16).</li>
<li><b>Blessing others includes interpreting life through God’s character, not luck</b> (Ruth 2:12).</li>
<li><b>Integrity keeps both compassion and boundaries together</b> (Ruth 3:10–14).</li>
<li><b>Do the right thing even when you could shortcut it privately</b> (Ruth 3:12–13; 4:1–2).</li>
<li><b>Public accountability protects people and honors God</b> (Ruth 4:9–12).</li>
<li><b>Redemption is costly—love that won’t pay a price isn’t redemption</b> (Ruth 4:5–6, 9–10).</li>
<li><b>God weaves individual faithfulness into redemptive history beyond what we can see</b> (Ruth 4:17–22).</li>
<li><b>The LORD often answers prayers through people who act righteously</b> (Ruth 2:12; 4:14).</li>
<li><b>A good reputation is guarded by wise, proactive choices</b> (Ruth 3:14).</li>
<li><b>The outsider who trusts the LORD belongs—and can bear lasting fruit</b> (Ruth 2:12; 4:13–17; Matt 1:5).</li>
</ol>
<div><b><h2>Warnings to heed</h2></b></div>
<ul>
<li>Don’t use power (economic, social, sexual) to exploit vulnerability; righteousness protects (Ruth 2:8–9).</li>
<li>Don’t evade costly obedience with self-preservation, like the nearer redeemer’s refusal (Ruth 4:6).</li>
</ul>
<div><b><h2>Examples to imitate</h2></b></div>
<ul>
<li>Protection + provision + integrity (Ruth 2:8–9, 14–16; 3:12–13; 4:9–10).</li>
</ul>
<div><b><h2>Targeted application areas</h2></b></div>
<ul>
<li><b>Personal holiness:</b> integrity in private and public (Ruth 3:12–14; 4:1–2).</li>
<li><b>Relationships:</b> kindness with clarity and honor (Ruth 2:8–12; 3:10–11).</li>
<li><b>Prayer:</b> bless others with God-centered words and then act in alignment (Ruth 2:12; 4:9–10).</li>
<li><b>Suffering:</b> God can restore “emptiness” through faithful steps (Ruth 1:21; 4:14–17).</li>
<li><b>Decision-making/leadership:</b> do what’s lawful, witnessed, and loving—not merely expedient (Ruth 4:1–12).</li>
</ul>
<div><b><h2>12) Discussion and Reflection Questions</h2></b></div>
<div><b><h2>Personal reflection</h2></b></div>
<ol>
<li>Where do you see Boaz combining generosity with boundaries? (Ruth 2:8–9; 3:10–14)</li>
<li>What does Boaz’s blessing reveal about how he interprets Ruth’s story? (Ruth 2:11–12)</li>
<li>In what ways do you have “field-owner” power—resources, influence, access—that can protect others? (Ruth 2:8–16)</li>
<li>What costs of obedience do you tend to avoid, and why? (Ruth 4:5–6, 9–10)</li>
<li>How does public accountability strengthen love and justice in your life? (Ruth 4:1–2, 9–12)</li>
</ol>
<div><b><h2>Small group discussion</h2></b></div>
<ol>
<li>Why is the gate scene (public witnesses) essential to the story’s theology? (Ruth 4:1–12)</li>
<li>Discuss the contrast between Boaz and the nearer redeemer. What do they each value? (Ruth 4:4–6, 9–10)</li>
<li>How does the book present God’s providence without constant miracles? (Ruth 2:3; 4:13–17)</li>
</ol>
<div><b><h2>Deep study (text observation)</h2></b></div>
<ol>
<li>Trace “wings/refuge” language across Ruth 2:12 and Ruth 3:9. What’s the narrative effect?</li>
<li>List Boaz’s commands to his men about Ruth. What do they reveal about his character and priorities? (Ruth 2:9, 15–16)</li>
<li>What repeated phrases highlight Ruth’s identity (“the Moabite”) and why might the author keep repeating them? (Ruth 2:2, 6, 21; 4:5, 10)</li>
<li>How do the women’s words in Ruth 4:14–17 interpret the whole story?</li>
<li>What legal customs appear in Ruth 4:7–8, and what do they contribute to the narrative?</li>
<li>How does the genealogy (Ruth 4:18–22) reshape the meaning of the earlier events?</li>
</ol>
<div><b><h2>Christ-focused reflection</h2></b></div>
<ol>
<li>In what ways does Boaz’s costly redemption help you understand redemption language in the NT? (Ruth 4:9–10; Mark 10:45; 1 Pet 1:18–19)</li>
<li>What does Ruth’s inclusion teach about grace and belonging? (Ruth 2:10–12; Eph 2:12–13, 19)</li>
<li>How does God’s work through ordinary faithfulness encourage you today? (Ruth 2:3; Rom 8:28)</li>
</ol>
<div><b><h2>13) Prayer Response</h2></b></div>
<div>Lord God of covenant mercy, thank you for your kindness to the vulnerable and for providing refuge under your wings (Ruth 2:12). Make us people of integrity like Boaz—quick to protect, eager to give, careful to do what is right in private and in public (Ruth 2:8–9; 3:12–13; 4:9–10). Guard us from self-preserving compromise and teach us to embrace the cost of faithful love (Ruth 4:6, 9–10). And as you wove Boaz’s obedience into your saving purposes, strengthen our trust that you are working through ordinary faithfulness for your glory and our good (Ruth 2:3; 4:17). Through Jesus our Redeemer, amen (Eph 1:7).</div>
