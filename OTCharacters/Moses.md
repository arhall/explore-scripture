<div><h1>Moses</h1></div>
<div><br></div>
<div><b><h2>Moses — Robust Bible Character Study (Scripture-Primary)</h2></b></div>
<div><br></div>
<div><b><h2>1) Story Summary (Chronological)</h2></b></div>
<div>Moses’ story begins under Israel’s oppression in Egypt, when a pharaoh decrees death for Hebrew male infants (Ex 1:8–22). Moses is born to Amram and Jochebed, hidden, then providentially adopted into Pharaoh’s household through Pharaoh’s daughter, while being nursed by his own mother (Ex 2:1–10; Ex 6:20; Num 26:59). As an adult, Moses identifies with his enslaved people, kills an Egyptian who is beating a Hebrew, and flees Egypt when his act becomes known (Ex 2:11–15).</div>
<div>In Midian, Moses marries Zipporah, daughter of Jethro (also called Reuel), and begins a long season of obscurity as a shepherd (Ex 2:16–22; Ex 3:1). At Horeb/Sinai, God appears to Moses in the burning bush, reveals His covenant name (YHWH), and calls Moses to confront Pharaoh and lead Israel out of Egypt (Ex 3:1–10, 13–15). Moses resists with fears and objections, but God provides signs and appoints Aaron as spokesman (Ex 3:11–4:17). Moses returns, challenges Pharaoh, and God brings escalating judgments—ten plagues—culminating in the Passover and the death of Egypt’s firstborn (Ex 5:1–12:30). Israel departs in haste, God delivers them through the Red Sea, and Pharaoh’s army is destroyed (Ex 12:31–42; Ex 14:13–31).</div>
<div>In the wilderness, Moses leads a newly freed but frequently grumbling people. God provides water, manna, and victory over Amalek (Ex 15:22–16:36; Ex 17:1–16). At Sinai, God enters covenant with Israel, gives the Ten Commandments and wider law, and commands the tabernacle’s construction—establishing Israel’s worship and God’s dwelling among them (Ex 19:1–20:21; Ex 24:3–8; Ex 25–31; Ex 40:34–38). Moses acts as mediator when Israel commits idolatry with the golden calf; he intercedes, pleads for God’s presence, and receives renewed covenant mercy and revelation of God’s character (Ex 32:1–14; Ex 33:12–23; Ex 34:5–9).</div>
<div>The narrative continues through the wilderness years: ordering of worship and holiness (Leviticus), repeated testing, rebellion, judgment, and perseverance (Numbers). Key crises include the people’s refusal to enter Canaan after the spies’ report at Kadesh, leading to forty years of wilderness wandering (Num 13–14; cf. Deut 1:19–46). Moses repeatedly intercedes during rebellions (Num 14:13–20; Num 16:41–50). Late in the journey, Moses sins at Meribah by speaking rashly and striking the rock, failing to “uphold” God’s holiness before the people; God declares Moses will not enter the land (Num 20:2–13; Ps 106:32–33).</div>
<div>In Deuteronomy, Moses delivers covenant sermons on the plains of Moab, recounting God’s works, expounding the law, calling Israel to love and obey the LORD, and preparing Joshua to lead (Deut 1–4; Deut 6:4–9; Deut 31:1–8). Moses blesses the tribes and ascends Mount Nebo, where God shows him the land from afar (Deut 32:48–52; Deut 33). Moses dies there at 120 years old, with undimmed vigor, and Israel mourns him; Scripture testifies that no prophet like Moses had arisen in Israel, whom the LORD knew “face to face” (Deut 34:5–12; cf. Ex 33:11).</div>
<div>Moses’ story ends with his death and burial in Deuteronomy (Deut 34:5–7), though he reappears later at the Transfiguration with Elijah, speaking with Jesus (Matt 17:1–5; Luke 9:28–31).</div>
<div><br></div>
<div><b><h2>2) What Moses Is Known For</h2></b></div>
<div>Moses is known as God’s chosen deliverer of Israel, covenant mediator, prophet, and the central human instrument through whom God formed Israel into a redeemed worshiping nation (Ex 3:7–10; Ex 19:3–6; Deut 34:10–12).</div>
<div><b>Headline descriptors</b><br></div>
<ul>
<li><b>Deliverer-leader of the Exodus</b> (Ex 14:30–31)</li>
<li><b>Covenant mediator at Sinai</b> (Ex 24:3–8; Gal 3:19)</li>
<li><b>Prophet “known by the LORD face to face”</b> (Deut 34:10; Ex 33:11)</li>
<li><b>Lawgiver and teacher of Torah</b> (Deut 4:1, 5–8; John 1:17)</li>
<li><b>Intercessor who stands in the breach</b> (Ex 32:11–14; Ps 106:23)</li>
<li><b>Humble/meek leader under immense pressure</b> (Num 12:3)</li>
<li><b>A flawed servant disciplined by God</b> (Num 20:10–12; Deut 32:51–52)</li>
</ul>
<div><br></div>
<div><b><h2>3) Where Moses Appears in the Bible</h2></b></div>
<div><b><h2>Primary narrative locations</h2></b></div>
<ul>
<li><b>Exodus — Ex 1–40</b> — Birth, call, plagues, Passover, Red Sea, Sinai covenant, tabernacle (Ex 2; Ex 3–4; Ex 7–14; Ex 19–24; Ex 32–34; Ex 40)</li>
<li><b>Leviticus — Lev 1–27</b> — Moses receives and communicates holiness/worship laws (Lev 1:1; Lev 26:46)</li>
<li><b>Numbers — Num 1–36</b> — Wilderness leadership, rebellions, judgments, Meribah (Num 11–14; Num 16–17; Num 20)</li>
<li><b>Deuteronomy — Deut 1–34</b> — Moses’ sermons, covenant renewal, succession, death (Deut 6; Deut 30; Deut 31; Deut 34)</li>
</ul>
<div><b><h2>Secondary references (later mentions, summaries, teachings)</h2></b></div>
<ul>
<li><b>Joshua — Josh 1:1–9; Josh 8:30–35; Josh 24:5</b> — Transition to Joshua; “law of Moses” read and obeyed</li>
<li><b>Judges — Judg 4:11</b> — Moses’ family line referenced (Hobab/Jethro connections; interpretive details debated)</li>
<li><b>1 Samuel — 1 Sam 12:6–8</b> — Samuel recounts Moses/Aaron as God’s agents of deliverance</li>
<li><b>Psalms — Ps 77:20; Ps 90 (title); Ps 99:6–7; Ps 106:23, 32–33</b> — Moses as shepherd-leader, intercessor; Meribah failure noted</li>
<li><b>Isaiah — Isa 63:11–14</b> — Exodus remembered; Moses associated with God’s leading by the Spirit</li>
<li><b>Jeremiah — Jer 15:1</b> — Moses named as a premier intercessor</li>
<li><b>Daniel — Dan 9:11–13</b> — “Law of Moses” referenced in confession</li>
<li><b>Malachi — Mal 4:4</b> — Call to remember the law of Moses</li>
<li><b>Gospels — Matt 5:17; Matt 17:1–5; Mark 9:2–8; Luke 16:29–31; John 1:17; John 5:45–47</b> — Moses and the law; Moses at Transfiguration; Moses’ writings testify of Christ</li>
<li><b>Acts — Acts 3:22–23; Acts 7:17–44; Acts 13:17–19</b> — Moses’ life summarized; Deut 18 applied to Jesus</li>
<li><b>Romans — Rom 10:5, 19</b> — Moses cited regarding law and Israel</li>
<li><b>2 Corinthians — 2 Cor 3:7–18</b> — Moses’ veil as covenantal imagery</li>
<li><b>Galatians — Gal 3:19–24</b> — Law “through angels by an intermediary”</li>
<li><b>Hebrews — Heb 3:1–6; Heb 11:23–29; Heb 12:21</b> — Moses as faithful servant; faith example; Sinai trembling</li>
<li><b>Jude — Jude 9</b> — Dispute over Moses’ body (brief allusion)</li>
<li><b>Revelation — Rev 15:3</b> — “Song of Moses” paired with the Lamb</li>
</ul>

<div><b><h2>4) Vital Statistics</h2></b></div>
<div><b><h2>Historical setting (as Scripture indicates)</h2></b></div>
<ul>
<li>Israel is enslaved in <b>Egypt</b> under a pharaoh who “did not know Joseph” (Ex 1:8–14).</li>
<li>Moses is <b>80</b> when he confronts Pharaoh (Ex 7:7) and <b>120</b> at death (Deut 34:7).</li>
<li>Israel’s wilderness period is <b>40 years</b> due to covenant unbelief at Kadesh (Num 14:33–34; Deut 2:7).</li>
</ul>
<div><i>(Exact absolute dating in BCE is debated; Scripture gives relative time markers, ages, and durations.)</i><br></div>
<div><b><h2>Geography (strong associations)</h2></b></div>
<ul>
<li><b>Egypt</b> (birth/adoption; Exodus confrontation) (Ex 2:1–10; Ex 5–12)</li>
<li><b>Midian</b> (exile, marriage, shepherding) (Ex 2:15–22; Ex 3:1)</li>
<li><b>Horeb/Sinai</b> (burning bush; covenant; tabernacle instructions) (Ex 3:1; Ex 19:1–2)</li>
<li><b>Wilderness regions</b>: Marah/Elim, Sin, Rephidim, Kadesh, etc. (Ex 15–17; Num 13–14; Num 20)</li>
<li><b>Plains of Moab / Mount Nebo</b> (final sermons; death) (Deut 1:5; Deut 34:1–6)</li>
</ul>
<div><b><h2>Family and relationships</h2></b></div>
<ul>
<li><b>Parents:</b> Amram and Jochebed (Ex 6:20; Num 26:59)</li>
<li><b>Siblings:</b> Aaron and Miriam (Ex 6:20; Num 26:59; Num 12:1)</li>
<li><b>Wife:</b> Zipporah (Ex 2:21; Ex 4:25)</li>
<li><b>Sons:</b> Gershom and Eliezer (Ex 2:22; Ex 18:3–4)</li>
<li><b>Father-in-law:</b> Jethro/Reuel (Ex 2:18; Ex 3:1; Ex 18:1)</li>
<li><b>Key allies:</b> Aaron (Ex 4:14–16), Joshua (Ex 17:9–14; Num 27:18–23), Caleb (Num 14:24)</li>
<li><b>Key opponents:</b> Pharaoh (Ex 5–14), Korah/Dathan/Abiram (Num 16), many within Israel’s murmuring generation (Num 14:1–4)</li>
</ul>
<div><b><h2>Roles/offices</h2></b></div>
<ul>
<li><b>Prophet</b> (Deut 34:10; Hos 12:13)</li>
<li><b>Covenant mediator / intercessor</b> (Ex 24:8; Ex 32:30–32)</li>
<li><b>Leader/judge over Israel</b> (Ex 18:13–26)</li>
<li><b>Lawgiver/teacher</b> (Deut 4:1, 5)</li>
<li><b>Wilderness shepherd of God’s flock</b> (Ps 77:20)</li>
</ul>
<div><b><h2>Key turning points (call, crisis, covenant moments)</h2></b></div>
<ul>
<li>Call at the burning bush (Ex 3–4)</li>
<li>Confrontation with Pharaoh and Passover (Ex 7–12)</li>
<li>Red Sea deliverance (Ex 14)</li>
<li>Sinai covenant and law (Ex 19–24)</li>
<li>Golden calf and renewed mercy (Ex 32–34)</li>
<li>Kadesh unbelief and forty-year sentence (Num 13–14)</li>
<li>Meribah failure and exclusion from land (Num 20:10–12)</li>
<li>Deuteronomy sermons and succession to Joshua (Deut 31)</li>
<li>Death on Nebo (Deut 34)</li>
</ul>

<div><b><h2>6) Strengths, Weaknesses, and Heart-Level Patterns</h2></b></div>
<div><b><h2>Strengths (with textual evidence)</h2></b></div>
<ul>
<li><b>Faith that chooses God’s people over status</b>: Moses refuses to be called Pharaoh’s daughter’s son, choosing reproach with God’s people (Heb 11:24–26; cf. Ex 2:11).</li>
<li><b>Courage and perseverance under confrontation</b>: Moses repeatedly returns to Pharaoh with God’s word despite setbacks (Ex 7:1–6; Ex 10:27–29).</li>
<li><b>Humility/meekness as a leader</b>: Moses is described as very meek/humble (Num 12:3), enduring criticism (Num 12:1–9).</li>
<li><b>Intercession rooted in God’s promises and glory</b>: He pleads God’s covenant name/reputation and promises (Ex 32:11–14; Num 14:13–19).</li>
<li><b>Intimacy with God’s presence</b>: The LORD speaks with Moses “face to face, as a man speaks to his friend” (Ex 33:11; cf. Deut 34:10).</li>
</ul>
<div><b><h2>Weaknesses / sins / blind spots (with brief context)</h2></b></div>
<ul>
<li><b>Impulsive violence and self-directed action</b>: Killing the Egyptian (Ex 2:11–12) leads to fear and flight (Ex 2:14–15).</li>
<li><b>Fearful reluctance and excuse-making at calling</b>: “Who am I…?” “What if they don’t believe me?” “I am not eloquent…” (Ex 3:11; Ex 4:1, 10–13).</li>
<li><b>Failure in household obedience (circumcision episode)</b>: The LORD confronts Moses on the way; Zipporah circumcises their son (Ex 4:24–26). The text is terse but signals serious covenant neglect.</li>
<li><b>Anger under pressure</b>: Moses’ frustration surfaces repeatedly (Num 11:10–15; cf. Ex 32:19).</li>
<li><b>Meribah disobedience and misrepresentation of God</b>: Moses speaks harshly (“Hear now, you rebels…”) and strikes the rock; God says, “you did not believe in me, to uphold me as holy” (Num 20:10–12; cf. Ps 106:32–33).</li>
</ul>
<div><b><h2>Motives / fears / desires (carefully inferred, anchored to text)</h2></b></div>
<ul>
<li><b>Fear of exposure and consequences</b> after the killing (Ex 2:14–15).</li>
<li><b>Desire to identify with God’s people</b> rather than Egypt’s privilege (Heb 11:24–26).</li>
<li><b>Deep burden for Israel’s survival</b> (Ex 32:31–32; Num 14:13–19).</li>
<li><b>Central longing for God’s presence and knowledge of His ways</b> (Ex 33:13–16, 18).</li>
</ul>
<div><b><h2>What changes (or doesn’t) over time?</h2></b></div>
<ul>
<li><b>Changes:</b> Moses moves from hesitant and self-focused objections (Ex 3–4) to God-centered intercession grounded in God’s name and promises (Ex 32–34; Num 14:13–19). He grows in settled dependence on God’s presence (Ex 33:15–16).</li>
<li><b>Doesn’t fully change:</b> Under sustained pressure, Moses still struggles with anger and rash speech/action (Num 20:10–12; Ps 106:32–33). Growth does not eliminate weakness; it often reveals how costly leadership can be.</li>
</ul>
<div><br></div>
<div><b><h2>7) Victories and Challenges</h2></b></div>
<div><b><h2>Major “wins” (deliverances, obedience, leadership, influence)</h2></b></div>
<ul>
<li><b>Instrument of deliverance from Egypt</b> (Ex 14:30–31; Ps 77:20)</li>
<li><b>Faithful mediator in receiving and teaching God’s law</b> (Ex 24:3–4; Deut 4:1–8)</li>
<li><b>Organizer of just leadership structures</b> (Ex 18:13–26)</li>
<li><b>Intercession that averts immediate judgment</b> (Ex 32:11–14; Num 14:19–20; Ps 106:23)</li>
<li><b>Perseverance in leading a difficult people for decades</b> (Deut 2:7; Deut 34:7)</li>
</ul>
<div><b><h2>Major challenges (temptations, failures, suffering, opposition, doubt)</h2></b></div>
<ul>
<li><b>Personal failure and exile</b> (Ex 2:11–15)</li>
<li><b>Pharaoh’s hard resistance and worsening conditions</b> (Ex 5:6–23; Ex 7:13)</li>
<li><b>Relentless grumbling and rebellion from Israel</b> (Ex 16:2–3; Num 14:1–4; Num 16:1–3)</li>
<li><b>Family/internal leadership conflict</b> (Num 12:1–2)</li>
<li><b>Crushing leadership burden</b> (Num 11:10–15)</li>
<li><b>Consequences of Meribah: barred from entering Canaan</b> (Num 20:12; Deut 32:51–52)</li>
</ul>
<div><b>Cost of faithfulness / consequences of compromise</b><br></div>
<ul>
<li>Moses bears the emotional and spiritual weight of mediation (Ex 32:30–32; Num 11:10–15).</li>
<li>Yet even Moses’ disobedience has consequences—God’s holiness is not negotiable in leadership (Num 20:10–12).</li>
</ul>

<div><b><h2>9) Theology and Big Themes</h2></b></div>
<div><b><h2>God’s character revealed through Moses’ life and ministry</h2></b></div>
<ul>
<li><b>Holiness:</b> God’s presence is dangerous to approach casually (Ex 3:5; Num 20:12).</li>
<li><b>Mercy and steadfast love:</b> God forgives and renews covenant after idolatry (Ex 34:6–7; Ex 34:9–10).</li>
<li><b>Sovereignty and power over gods/kingdoms:</b> Plagues and Red Sea display supremacy (Ex 7:5; Ex 12:12; Ex 14:31).</li>
<li><b>Patience and fatherly discipline:</b> Wilderness judgments are real, yet God sustains a stubborn people (Num 14:18–20; Deut 8:2–5).</li>
</ul>
<div><b><h2>Covenant, worship, and God dwelling with His people</h2></b></div>
<ul>
<li>Redemption is for worship and covenant identity (Ex 5:1; Ex 19:4–6).</li>
<li>The tabernacle is a theological center: God dwelling among His redeemed people (Ex 25:8; Ex 40:34–38).</li>
</ul>
<div><b><h2>Human nature: sin, unbelief, and the need for a mediator</h2></b></div>
<ul>
<li>Israel’s repeated murmuring and rebellion expose the heart (Ex 16:2–3; Num 14:1–4).</li>
<li>Moses’ mediation highlights both the necessity of an intercessor and the insufficiency of even the best human mediator (Ex 32:30–32; Num 20:12).</li>
</ul>
<div><b><h2>Law and grace in redemptive history</h2></b></div>
<ul>
<li>The law comes after redemption (Ex 20:2), shaping a redeemed people’s life.</li>
<li>Later Scripture contrasts Moses-as-servant with Christ-as-Son and the new covenant’s greater glory (2 Cor 3:7–18; Heb 3:1–6).</li>
</ul>
<div><br></div>
<div><b><h2>10) How Moses Points Us to Christ (Biblically Grounded)</h2></b></div>
<div><b><h2>Prophet like Moses</h2></b></div>
<ul>
<li>Moses explicitly foretells a coming <b>prophet like him</b> whom God’s people must listen to (Deut 18:15–19).</li>
<li>The New Testament identifies Jesus as the fulfillment of this expectation (Acts 3:22–23; cf. John 6:14).</li>
</ul>
<div><b><h2>Mediator of covenant → Christ the greater mediator</h2></b></div>
<ul>
<li>Moses mediates the Sinai covenant (Ex 24:3–8), but later Scripture teaches Christ mediates a <b>better covenant</b> (Heb 8:6; Heb 9:15).</li>
<li>Moses is faithful “as a servant,” but Christ is faithful “as a Son” over God’s house (Heb 3:1–6).</li>
</ul>
<div><b><h2>Exodus patterns fulfilled in Christ’s redemption</h2></b></div>
<ul>
<li><b>Passover:</b> Israel spared by blood (Ex 12:13) prefigures Christ our Passover (1 Cor 5:7).</li>
<li><b>Wilderness provision:</b> manna and water become signs pointing beyond themselves (Ex 16:4; Ex 17:6; cf. John 6:31–35; 1 Cor 10:4).</li>
<li><b>Bronze serpent:</b> lifted up for life (Num 21:8–9) explicitly applied to Jesus’ lifting up (John 3:14–15).</li>
<li><b>Transfiguration:</b> Moses appears with Elijah; the Father’s command is, “Listen to him” (Matt 17:1–5), echoing Deut 18’s listening theme.</li>
</ul>
<div><b><h2>Moses’ writings testify to Christ</h2></b></div>
<ul>
<li>Jesus says Moses wrote about Him, and unbelief toward Moses’ writings corresponds to unbelief toward Jesus (John 5:45–47).</li>
</ul>

<div><b><h2>11) Lessons and Applications (Scripture-Tethered)</h2></b></div>
<ol>
<li><b>God’s call rests on His presence, not your competence</b> (Ex 3:12; Ex 4:10–12).</li>
<li><b>Trying to accomplish God’s purposes in the flesh can create avoidable wounds</b> (Ex 2:11–15; cf. Ex 3:10–12).</li>
<li><b>Leadership requires shared burdens and wise structures</b> (Ex 18:17–23).</li>
<li><b>Redemption is for worship and holiness, not merely freedom from trouble</b> (Ex 19:4–6; Lev 19:2).</li>
<li><b>Intercession appeals to God’s promises, name, and character</b> (Ex 32:11–14; Num 14:13–19).</li>
<li><b>Knowing God’s ways matters more than merely receiving His gifts</b> (Ex 33:13–16).</li>
<li><b>Idolatry is always a present danger—even right after spiritual highs</b> (Ex 32:1–8).</li>
<li><b>Unbelief can forfeit blessings and shape decades</b> (Num 14:22–23, 33–34).</li>
<li><b>Anger and rash words can sabotage faithful service</b> (Num 20:10–12; Ps 106:32–33).</li>
<li><b>God’s servants are honored, but God’s mission outlasts them</b> (Deut 34:5–9; Josh 1:1–9).</li>
<li><b>The law exposes need and points beyond itself to a greater Mediator</b> (Gal 3:19–24; Heb 3:1–6).</li>
<li><b>Listen to the greater Moses—Jesus</b> (Deut 18:15; Matt 17:5; Acts 3:22–23).</li>
</ol>
<div><b><h2>Warnings to heed</h2></b></div>
<ul>
<li>Don’t harden your heart like the wilderness generation (Num 14:1–4; cf. Heb 3:7–19).</li>
<li>Don’t presume on leadership status; holiness matters (Num 20:12; Jas 3:1 echoes the weight of teaching).</li>
</ul>
<div><b><h2>Examples to imitate</h2></b></div>
<ul>
<li>Meekness under criticism (Num 12:3).</li>
<li>Prayerful mediation for others (Ex 32:30–32; Num 14:19).</li>
<li>Choosing God over status (Heb 11:24–26).</li>
</ul>
<div><b>Application areas</b><br></div>
<ul>
<li><b>Personal holiness:</b> respond to God’s presence with reverence (Ex 3:5; Lev 19:2).</li>
<li><b>Relationships:</b> respond to conflict without grasping for vindication (Num 12:1–9, 13).</li>
<li><b>Prayer:</b> plead God’s promises and character (Ex 32:11–14; Ex 34:6–7).</li>
<li><b>Suffering:</b> endure with faith in unseen reward (Heb 11:26–27).</li>
<li><b>Decision-making:</b> remember past redemption as fuel for obedience (Deut 8:2; Deut 6:20–25).</li>
<li><b>Leadership:</b> practice shared burden, patience, and God-centered speech (Ex 18:21–23; Num 20:10–12; Ex 33:15–16).</li>
</ul>

<div><b><h2>12) Discussion and Reflection Questions</h2></b></div>
<div><b><h2>Personal reflection</h2></b></div>
<ol>
<li>Where do you most resonate with Moses’ objections (“Who am I?” “I’m not eloquent”)—and what does God answer? (Ex 3:11–12; Ex 4:10–12)</li>
<li>What “Egypt” status or comfort competes with identifying with God’s people? (Heb 11:24–26)</li>
<li>How does Moses’ insistence on God’s presence reshape your priorities? (Ex 33:15–16)</li>
<li>Where has anger or rash speech endangered your faithfulness? (Num 20:10–12; Ps 106:32–33)</li>
<li>What past deliverance should you remember to strengthen present obedience? (Deut 8:2; Ex 20:2)</li>
</ol>
<div><b><h2>Small group discussion</h2></b></div>
<ol>
<li>Why do you think God allows Moses a long hidden season in Midian? What does it produce? (Ex 2:15–22; Ex 3:1)</li>
<li>How do the golden calf and Meribah together warn leaders and congregations? (Ex 32; Num 20:10–12)</li>
<li>What does Moses teach us about praying for difficult people? (Num 14:13–19; Ex 32:11–14)</li>
<li>How should we think about consequences for sin even after long faithful service? (Num 20:12; Deut 34:5–7)</li>
</ol>
<div><b><h2>Deep study (text-observation)</h2></b></div>
<ol>
<li>In Exodus 3–4, list every objection Moses makes and every divine provision God gives. (Ex 3:11–4:17)</li>
<li>In Exodus 32:11–14, what arguments does Moses use? Promises? God’s reputation? (Ex 32:11–13)</li>
<li>Compare Exodus 34:6–7 with Numbers 14:18–19—how does Moses reuse God’s revealed character in prayer? (Ex 34:6–7; Num 14:18–19)</li>
<li>In Numbers 20:2–13, what exactly does God command, and what exactly does Moses do? (Num 20:8–11)</li>
<li>In Deuteronomy 6:4–9, how is love for God meant to permeate daily life? (Deut 6:6–9)</li>
<li>How do Hebrews 3:1–6 and 2 Corinthians 3:7–18 assess Moses’ role relative to Christ and the new covenant? (Heb 3:1–6; 2 Cor 3:7–18)</li>
<li>How does the Transfiguration scene echo Deuteronomy 18? (Matt 17:5; Deut 18:15)</li>
</ol>

Through Jesus Christ, our Mediator and Redeemer, amen (Heb 9:15; 1 Cor 5:7).</div>
