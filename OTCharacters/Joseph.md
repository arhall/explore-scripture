<div><h1>Joseph</h1></div>
<div><br></div>
<div><b><h1>Joseph (son of Jacob) — Robust Bible Character Study</h1></b></div>
<div><b>Scope note:</b> This study is about <b>Joseph the patriarch</b>, son of <b>Jacob/Israel</b> (Genesis 37–50), not Joseph the husband of Mary (Matthew 1–2; Luke 1–2).</div>
<div><br></div>
<div><b><h2>1) Story Summary</h2></b></div>
<div>Joseph’s story begins in the <b>patriarchal narratives</b> as the favored son of Jacob, marked by God-given dreams and hated by his brothers (Genesis <b>37:2–11</b>). Their jealousy turns violent: they conspire to kill him, then sell him into slavery, and deceive their father into believing Joseph is dead (Genesis <b>37:18–36</b>).</div>
<div>In Egypt Joseph serves in <b>Potiphar’s house</b>, where the Lord’s presence makes him successful and trustworthy (Genesis <b>39:1–6</b>). He refuses sexual sin with Potiphar’s wife (Genesis <b>39:7–12</b>) and is falsely accused and imprisoned (Genesis <b>39:13–20</b>). Even in prison the Lord remains with him, granting favor and administrative responsibility (Genesis <b>39:21–23</b>). Joseph interprets dreams for Pharaoh’s officials (Genesis <b>40:5–22</b>), but is forgotten for two years (Genesis <b>40:23; 41:1</b>).</div>
<div>When Pharaoh is troubled by dreams, Joseph is summoned; he attributes interpretation to God (Genesis <b>41:16, 25, 32</b>) and offers wise counsel for crisis planning (Genesis <b>41:33–36</b>). Pharaoh elevates him to a position of sweeping authority—second only to Pharaoh (Genesis <b>41:39–44</b>). Joseph oversees preparation and distribution through seven years of abundance and seven years of famine (Genesis <b>41:47–57</b>), drawing even the nations to Egypt for food (Genesis <b>41:57</b>).</div>
<div>The famine brings Joseph’s brothers to him. After a prolonged, morally searching process that exposes guilt and tests whether they have changed (Genesis <b>42:21–24; 44:16–34</b>), Joseph reveals himself, comforts them, and interprets the whole tragedy through God’s providence: “God sent me before you… to preserve life” (Genesis <b>45:5–8</b>). Jacob’s family relocates to Egypt under Pharaoh’s favor (Genesis <b>46:1–7; 47:5–12</b>).</div>
<div>Near the end of Genesis, Joseph receives Jacob’s blessing and adopts Joseph’s sons Ephraim and Manasseh into the tribal inheritance (Genesis <b>48:5–20</b>). Joseph hears Jacob’s prophetic words over the tribes, including a distinctive blessing on Joseph (Genesis <b>49:22–26</b>). After Jacob dies, Joseph leads the burial in Canaan (Genesis <b>50:1–14</b>) and reassures his fearful brothers with one of Scripture’s clearest providence statements: “You meant evil against me, but God meant it for good” (Genesis <b>50:20</b>). Joseph dies in Egypt, expressing faith that God will bring Israel up from Egypt and requesting his bones be carried up (Genesis <b>50:24–26</b>).</div>
<div><b>Where the story begins:</b> Genesis <b>37:2</b><br><b>Where the story ends:</b> Genesis <b>50:26</b> (with later fulfillment in Exodus <b>13:19</b>; Joshua <b>24:32</b>)</div>
<div><br></div>
<div><b><h2>2) What He’s Known For</h2></b></div>
<div>Joseph is most known as the <b>suffering righteous man</b> whom God exalts, using him to <b>preserve the covenant family</b> and bless the nations during famine (Genesis <b>45:5–7; 50:20</b>).</div>
<div><b>Headline descriptors</b><br></div>
<ul>
<li><b>Beloved son / hated brother</b> (Genesis <b>37:3–4</b>)</li>
<li><b>Dreamer whose visions prove true</b> (Genesis <b>37:5–11; 41:25–32</b>)</li>
<li><b>Faithful under injustice</b> (Genesis <b>39:20–23</b>)</li>
<li><b>Resists sexual temptation</b> (Genesis <b>39:7–12</b>)</li>
<li><b>Wise administrator and “savior” through provision</b> (Genesis <b>41:39–41, 53–57</b>)</li>
<li><b>Agent of reconciliation and forgiveness</b> (Genesis <b>45:1–15; 50:15–21</b>)</li>
<li><b>Model of providence-shaped theology</b> (Genesis <b>45:5–8; 50:20</b>)</li>
</ul>
<div><br></div>
<div><b><h2>3) Where He Appears in the Bible</h2></b></div>
<div><b><h2>Primary narrative locations</h2></b></div>
<ul>
<li><b>Genesis — 30:22–24</b> — Joseph’s birth to Rachel; named with hope.</li>
<li><b>Genesis — 37:1–36</b> — Favored son, dreams, betrayal, slavery.</li>
<li><b>Genesis — 39:1–23</b> — Potiphar, temptation, false accusation, prison.</li>
<li><b>Genesis — 40:1–23</b> — Interprets dreams for cupbearer/baker.</li>
<li><b>Genesis — 41:1–57</b> — Interprets Pharaoh’s dreams; exalted; famine planning.</li>
<li><b>Genesis — 42:1–45:28</b> — Brothers come; testing; revelation; forgiveness.</li>
<li><b>Genesis — 46:1–47:12</b> — Family migrates to Egypt; Joseph provides.</li>
<li><b>Genesis — 48:1–22</b> — Jacob blesses Ephraim/Manasseh; adoption into tribes.</li>
<li><b>Genesis — 50:1–26</b> — Jacob’s burial; Joseph forgives; Joseph’s death &amp bones.</li>
</ul>
<div><b><h2>Secondary references (later mentions)</h2></b></div>
<ul>
<li><b>Exodus — 1:8</b> — “A new king… who did not know Joseph.”</li>
<li><b>Exodus — 13:19</b> — Moses takes Joseph’s bones (fulfilling Genesis <b>50:25</b>).</li>
<li><b>Joshua — 24:32</b> — Joseph’s bones buried at Shechem.</li>
<li><b>Psalm — 105:16–22</b> — Poetic retelling: suffering, then authority.</li>
<li><b>Acts — 7:9–16</b> — Stephen summarizes Joseph as rejected yet exalted.</li>
<li><b>Hebrews — 11:22</b> — Joseph’s faith shown in speaking of the exodus and his bones.</li>
</ul>
<div><br></div>
<div><b><h2>4) Vital Statistics</h2></b></div>
<div><b>Historical setting (as Scripture indicates)</b><br></div>
<ul>
<li><b>Patriarchal era</b> (Abraham → Isaac → Jacob), prior to Israel’s enslavement in Egypt (Genesis <b>37–50</b>; Exodus <b>1:8–14</b>). Scripture does not supply firm calendar dates.</li>
</ul>
<div><b>Geography</b><br></div>
<ul>
<li><b>Canaan</b> (Hebron region; Shechem/Dothan area implied in his capture) (Genesis <b>37:12–17</b>).</li>
<li><b>Egypt</b> (Potiphar’s house; prison; Pharaoh’s court; Goshen for family) (Genesis <b>39:1–2; 41:14; 47:6</b>).</li>
<li><b>Shechem</b> (eventual burial of bones) (Joshua <b>24:32</b>).</li>
</ul>
<div><b>Family &amp relationships</b><br></div>
<ul>
<li>Father: <b>Jacob/Israel</b> (Genesis <b>37:3; 46:29</b>).</li>
<li>Mother: <b>Rachel</b> (Genesis <b>30:22–24; 35:24</b>).</li>
<li>Siblings: <b>11 brothers</b> incl. <b>Benjamin</b> (full brother) (Genesis <b>35:24; 37:3</b>).</li>
<li>Wife: <b>Asenath</b>, daughter of Potiphera priest of On (Genesis <b>41:45</b>).</li>
<li>Sons: <b>Manasseh</b> and <b>Ephraim</b> (Genesis <b>41:50–52</b>).</li>
<li>Key figures: <b>Potiphar</b> (Genesis <b>39:1</b>), <b>Pharaoh</b> (Genesis <b>41:14, 39–44</b>), <b>chief cupbearer</b> (Genesis <b>40:1, 23</b>), <b>Judah</b> (Genesis <b>44:18–34</b>).</li>
</ul>
<div><b>Roles/offices</b><br></div>
<ul>
<li>Household steward/administrator (Genesis <b>39:4–6</b>).</li>
<li>Prison administrator (Genesis <b>39:22–23</b>).</li>
<li>Royal counselor/dream interpreter (Genesis <b>41:15–16, 25</b>).</li>
<li>National administrator/vizier over Egypt’s provisioning (Genesis <b>41:40–44</b>).</li>
</ul>
<div><b>Key turning points</b><br></div>
<ul>
<li>God-given dreams &amp family hostility (Genesis <b>37:5–11</b>).</li>
<li>Sold into slavery; descent into suffering (Genesis <b>37:28</b>).</li>
<li>Moral crisis: temptation refused (Genesis <b>39:9–12</b>).</li>
<li>Unjust imprisonment yet divine favor (Genesis <b>39:20–23</b>).</li>
<li>Remembered at Pharaoh’s crisis; exaltation (Genesis <b>41:9–14, 39–44</b>).</li>
<li>Reconciliation with brothers; providence clarified (Genesis <b>45:4–8; 50:20</b>).</li>
<li>Faith in God’s future deliverance (Genesis <b>50:24–25</b>).</li>
</ul>

<div><b><h2>6) Strengths, Weaknesses, and Heart-Level Patterns</h2></b></div>
<div><b><h2>Strengths (with textual evidence)</h2></b></div>
<ul>
<li><b>God-centered integrity under pressure:</b> “How then can I do this great wickedness and sin against God?” (Genesis <b>39:9</b>).</li>
<li><b>Faith that attributes power to God, not self:</b> “Do not interpretations belong to God?” (Genesis <b>40:8</b>); “It is not in me; God will give Pharaoh a favorable answer” (Genesis <b>41:16</b>).</li>
<li><b>Diligence and administrative wisdom:</b> Entrusted over Potiphar’s house (Genesis <b>39:4–6</b>); then over Egypt’s grain strategy (Genesis <b>41:33–41, 47–49</b>).</li>
<li><b>Perseverance in suffering with evidence of God’s favor:</b> “The LORD was with Joseph” (Genesis <b>39:2, 21, 23</b>).</li>
<li><b>Mercy and reconciliation:</b> He weeps, embraces, provides, and speaks kindly (Genesis <b>45:14–15; 50:21</b>).</li>
</ul>
<div><b><h2>Weaknesses / blind spots (carefully, from the text)</h2></b></div>
<ul>
<li><b>Youthful insensitivity or naiveté:</b> Joseph reports negatively on brothers (Genesis <b>37:2</b>) and recounts dreams that intensify hostility (Genesis <b>37:5–11</b>). Scripture doesn’t explicitly label this sin, but it does show relational fallout (Genesis <b>37:8, 11</b>).</li>
<li><b>Dependence on human remembrance (possible):</b> Joseph asks the cupbearer to remember him (Genesis <b>40:14–15</b>). This is not condemned, but the narrative contrasts human forgetting (Genesis <b>40:23</b>) with God’s perfect timing (Genesis <b>41:1, 14</b>).</li>
</ul>
<div><b><h2>Motives, fears, desires (anchored to text)</h2></b></div>
<ul>
<li><b>Desire for holiness and fear of sinning against God</b> (Genesis <b>39:9</b>).</li>
<li><b>Desire for justice/deliverance</b> evidenced by requesting remembrance from prison (Genesis <b>40:14–15</b>).</li>
<li><b>Deep longing for family reconciliation</b> seen in repeated weeping and restraint (Genesis <b>42:24; 43:30–31; 45:1–2</b>).</li>
</ul>
<div><b><h2>What changes (or doesn’t) over time?</h2></b></div>
<ul>
<li><b>Consistent:</b> God-consciousness and integrity (Genesis <b>39:9; 40:8; 41:16</b>).</li>
<li><b>Deepens:</b> His providence-shaped interpretation of suffering grows clearer at the end: “God sent me…” (Genesis <b>45:5–8</b>) and “God meant it for good” (Genesis <b>50:20</b>).</li>
<li><b>Relational posture matures:</b> From youthful family tension (Genesis <b>37:2–11</b>) to deliberate reconciliation and sustained kindness (Genesis <b>45:4–15; 50:21</b>).</li>
</ul>

<div><b><h2>9) Theology and Big Themes</h2></b></div>
<div><b><h2>God’s character revealed</h2></b></div>
<ul>
<li><b>Sovereign providence over human evil:</b> Human intent is real (“you meant evil,” Genesis <b>50:20</b>), yet God’s intent governs the outcome (“God meant it for good,” Genesis <b>50:20</b>).</li>
<li><b>God’s presence with the righteous sufferer:</b> The repeated refrain “the LORD was with Joseph” anchors the story (Genesis <b>39:2, 21, 23</b>).</li>
<li><b>God as the true revealer:</b> Joseph insists interpretation belongs to God (Genesis <b>40:8; 41:16, 25, 32</b>).</li>
</ul>
<div><b><h2>Covenant preservation and mission</h2></b></div>
<ul>
<li><b>Preserving the seed/family line:</b> Joseph explicitly frames his role as preserving a remnant (Genesis <b>45:7</b>), which safeguards the Abrahamic promises (cf. Genesis <b>12:1–3</b>).</li>
<li><b>Blessing to the nations through Israel:</b> Egypt becomes a storehouse for “all the earth” (Genesis <b>41:57</b>), previewing the outward-facing blessing theme in Genesis (Genesis <b>12:3</b>).</li>
</ul>
<div><b><h2>Human nature: sin, repentance-like change, grace</h2></b></div>
<ul>
<li><b>Sin’s destructiveness:</b> Jealousy leads to betrayal and deceit (Genesis <b>37:4, 18–36</b>).</li>
<li><b>Conscience awakened:</b> The brothers interpret calamity as linked to guilt (Genesis <b>42:21</b>).</li>
<li><b>Transformation evidenced:</b> Judah’s self-offering contrasts earlier cruelty (Genesis <b>44:33–34</b> vs. Genesis <b>37:26–27</b>).</li>
<li><b>Grace-driven reconciliation:</b> Joseph’s kindness is grounded in God’s purposes, not their merit (Genesis <b>45:5–8; 50:21</b>).</li>
</ul>
<div><br></div>
<div><b><h2>10) How Joseph Points Us to Christ</h2></b></div>
<div><b><h2>Typology (careful, text-anchored patterns)</h2></b></div>
<div>Joseph is not Jesus, but his life <b>foreshadows</b> recurring biblical patterns fulfilled in Christ:</div>
<ul>
<li><b>Beloved son rejected by his own:</b> Joseph is loved by his father yet rejected by brothers (Genesis <b>37:3–4</b>). Jesus is the beloved Son (Matthew <b>3:17</b>) rejected by “his own” (John <b>1:11</b>).</li>
<li><b>Suffering of the righteous leading to exaltation:</b> Joseph suffers unjustly then is exalted to the right hand of power in Egypt (Genesis <b>39:20–23; 41:39–44</b>). Jesus suffers and is exalted (Philippians <b>2:8–11</b>).</li>
<li><b>“Sent ahead” to preserve life:</b> Joseph: “God sent me before you… to preserve life” (Genesis <b>45:5</b>). Christ is sent by the Father to give life (John <b>6:38–40</b>).</li>
<li><b>Bread for the world:</b> In famine, “all the earth” comes for grain (Genesis <b>41:57</b>). Jesus identifies himself as the true bread that gives life to the world (John <b>6:35</b>).</li>
<li><b>Forgiveness extended to wrongdoers:</b> Joseph forgives those who harmed him (Genesis <b>50:17–21</b>). Jesus prays for his enemies (Luke <b>23:34</b>) and secures forgiveness through the cross (Ephesians <b>1:7</b>).</li>
</ul>
<div><b><h2>Gospel connections</h2></b></div>
<ul>
<li>Joseph’s climactic confession—God turning evil into saving good (Genesis <b>50:20</b>)—prepares us to recognize the cross: human evil acts in history, yet God accomplishing salvation (Acts <b>2:23</b>).</li>
</ul>

<div><b><h2>11) Lessons and Applications</h2></b></div>
<div><b><h2>Examples to imitate (tied to Scripture)</h2></b></div>
<ol>
<li><b>Flee temptation, don’t negotiate with it</b> (Genesis <b>39:10–12</b>).</li>
<li><b>Define sin vertically (“against God”)</b> (Genesis <b>39:9</b>).</li>
<li><b>Give God credit for gifts and insight</b> (Genesis <b>40:8; 41:16</b>).</li>
<li><b>Be faithful in “small” assignments</b> (Genesis <b>39:4–6; 39:22–23</b>).</li>
<li><b>Endure long delays without abandoning faith</b> (Genesis <b>40:23; 41:1</b>).</li>
<li><b>Practice wisdom that blesses others in crisis</b> (Genesis <b>41:33–36, 47–49</b>).</li>
<li><b>Pursue reconciliation with truth, not denial</b> (Genesis <b>42:21–24; 45:4–8</b>).</li>
<li><b>Forgive while naming wrongdoing honestly</b> (Genesis <b>50:17–21</b>).</li>
<li><b>Interpret life with providence, not bitterness</b> (Genesis <b>45:5–8; 50:20</b>).</li>
<li><b>Live with future faith, not present fear</b> (Genesis **50:24–25; Hebrews <b>11:22</b>).</li>
</ol>
<div><b><h2>Warnings to heed</h2></b></div>
<ul>
<li><b>Favoritism and unchecked envy fracture families</b> (Genesis <b>37:3–4</b>).</li>
<li><b>Sin often multiplies through deception</b> (Genesis <b>37:31–35</b>).</li>
<li><b>Righteousness can be costly in the short term</b> (Genesis <b>39:9–20</b>)—but God is not absent (Genesis <b>39:21</b>).</li>
</ul>
<div><b><h2>Application arenas</h2></b></div>
<ul>
<li><b>Personal holiness:</b> flight + God-centered conscience (Genesis <b>39:9–12</b>).</li>
<li><b>Relationships:</b> forgiveness anchored in God’s purposes (Genesis <b>50:20–21</b>).</li>
<li><b>Prayer/faith posture:</b> confess God as the giver, not self (Genesis <b>41:16</b>).</li>
<li><b>Suffering:</b> God’s presence is real even when circumstances worsen (Genesis <b>39:20–23</b>).</li>
<li><b>Decision-making/leadership:</b> wise planning serves life and neighbor (Genesis <b>41:33–36</b>).</li>
</ul>

<li>What evidence shows the brothers changed (Genesis <b>44:33–34</b>), and what does that teach about repentance bearing fruit?</li>
<li>Joseph forgives, yet the wrong is named plainly (Genesis <b>50:17–21</b>). What does that model for conflict today?</li>
</ol>

<li>Compare Joseph’s two statements about interpretation (Genesis <b>40:8</b> and <b>41:16</b>). What do they reveal about his theology?</li>
<li>Why does Genesis slow down the narrative to show Joseph “weeping” repeatedly (Genesis <b>42:24; 43:30; 45:1–2</b>)? What does that communicate?</li>
<li>In Genesis <b>45:5–8</b>, list Joseph’s claims about God’s actions. How many times does he mention God, and to what end?</li>
<li>Read Genesis <b>50:15–21</b> carefully. What fears drive the brothers? What words does Joseph use to comfort them (v. 21)?</li>
<li>How does Joseph’s request about his bones function as faith (Genesis **50:24–25; Hebrews <b>11:22</b>)?</li>
</ol>
