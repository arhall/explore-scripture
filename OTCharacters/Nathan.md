<div><h1>Nathan</h1></div>
<div><br></div>
<div><b><h1>Nathan (the Prophet) — Bible Character Study</h1></b></div>
<div><b>Disambiguation:</b> This study is about <b>Nathan the prophet</b> in King David’s court (2 Samuel; 1 Kings; 1 Chronicles), not <b>Nathan the son of David</b> (e.g., 2 Samuel 5:14; Luke 3:31).</div>
<div><br></div>
<div><b><h2>1) Story Summary (Chronological)</h2></b></div>
<div>Nathan enters the biblical narrative during David’s reign, functioning as a <b>court prophet</b>—a covenant messenger who brings the word of the LORD to the king.</div>
<ol>
<li><b>David’s temple desire and God’s covenant promise:</b> David shares his desire to build a “house” (temple) for the LORD. Nathan initially approves—then receives a correcting word from the LORD that <b>David will not build the temple</b>, but God will instead build David a “house” (dynasty), promising an enduring throne (2 Samuel 7:1–17; 1 Chronicles 17:1–15).</li>
<li><b>Confronting David’s sin with Bathsheba:</b> After David’s adultery and arranged death of Uriah, the LORD sends Nathan with a parable that exposes David’s guilt. Nathan declares God’s judgment and names the sin directly (“You are the man!”). David confesses, and Nathan announces forgiveness—yet also proclaims consequences, including the death of the child (2 Samuel 12:1–15, 18).</li>
<li><b>Solomon’s birth and divine favor:</b> David comforts Bathsheba; she bears Solomon. The LORD loves Solomon, and Nathan delivers a message, naming him <b>Jedidiah</b> (“beloved of the LORD”) (2 Samuel 12:24–25).</li>
<li><b>Securing Solomon’s succession:</b> Near David’s death, Adonijah exalts himself as king. Nathan alerts Bathsheba and orchestrates an appeal to David. David publicly confirms Solomon as heir; Nathan participates in Solomon’s anointing alongside Zadok the priest, and the kingdom recognizes Solomon (1 Kings 1:8–45).</li>
</ol>
<div><b>Where Nathan’s story begins/ends:</b><br></div>
<ul>
<li>Begins prominently in <b>2 Samuel 7</b> (also 1 Chronicles 17).</li>
<li>Ends with his last narrative appearance in <b>1 Kings 1</b>; later texts mention records associated with him (e.g., 1 Chronicles 29:29; 2 Chronicles 9:29).</li>
</ul>
<div><br></div>
<div><b><h2>2) What He’s Known For</h2></b></div>
<div>Nathan is most known as the prophet who <b>(1) delivered the Davidic covenant promise</b> and <b>(2) confronted David’s gravest moral failure</b>, calling him to repentance.</div>
<div><b>Headlines (3–7):</b><br></div>
<ul>
<li><b>Court prophet</b> to David who speaks God’s word with authority (2 Samuel 7:4–5).</li>
<li><b>Correctable messenger</b>—willing to reverse his own initial counsel when God corrects him (2 Samuel 7:3–4).</li>
<li><b>Fearless confronter of royal sin</b> (“You are the man!”) (2 Samuel 12:7).</li>
<li><b>Interpreter of covenant consequences</b>—forgiveness and discipline held together (2 Samuel 12:13–14).</li>
<li><b>Instrument in Solomon’s establishment</b> as the legitimate king (1 Kings 1:11–14, 38–40).</li>
<li><b>Link to Israel’s royal theology</b> through the Davidic covenant (2 Samuel 7:12–16).</li>
</ul>
<div><br></div>
<div><b><h2>3) Where He Appears in the Bible</h2></b></div>
<div><b><h2>Primary narrative locations</h2></b></div>
<ul>
<li><b>2 Samuel — 7:1–17</b> — Nathan delivers God’s word about David and the promised dynasty.</li>
<li><b>2 Samuel — 12:1–25</b> — Nathan confronts David’s sin; announces judgment and mercy; names Solomon Jedidiah.</li>
<li><b>1 Kings — 1:8–45</b> — Nathan helps secure Solomon’s succession and participates in his anointing.</li>
</ul>
<div><b><h2>Secondary references (mentions/writings)</h2></b></div>
<ul>
<li><b>1 Chronicles — 17:1–15</b> — Parallel account of Nathan’s message about the “house” (dynasty/temple).</li>
<li><b>1 Chronicles — 29:29</b> — David’s acts recorded in writings associated with Samuel, Nathan, and Gad.</li>
<li><b>2 Chronicles — 9:29</b> — Solomon’s acts recorded in writings associated with Nathan (and others).</li>
<li><b>2 Chronicles — 12:15</b> — Rehoboam’s acts recorded in writings associated with Shemaiah and Iddo (Nathan not here, but this shows the broader “prophetic records” pattern).</li>
<li><b>2 Chronicles — 13:22</b> — Acts of Abijah recorded in the “story” (midrash/record) of Iddo (again, similar record-keeping context; Nathan specifically in 9:29).</li>
</ul>
<div><i>(Note: the Bible references “the book/prophecy/acts of Nathan” as a source; we do not possess it as a separate canonical book today—so we stick to canonical texts that mention him.)</i> (1 Chronicles 29:29; 2 Chronicles 9:29)</div>
<div><br></div>
<div><b><h2>4) Vital Statistics</h2></b></div>
<ul>
<li><b>Historical setting:</b> United Monarchy—primarily the reign of <b>David</b>, and the transition to <b>Solomon</b> (2 Samuel 7; 2 Samuel 12; 1 Kings 1).</li>
<li><b>Geography:</b> David’s royal context—Jerusalem/Israel’s political-religious center (implied in 2 Samuel 7:1–2; 1 Kings 1:28–39).</li>
<li><b>Family/relationships (explicit):</b><br></li>
<ul>
<li>Closely connected to <b>David</b> (2 Samuel 7:2–17; 12:1–14).</li>
<li>Works with <b>Bathsheba</b> in the succession crisis (1 Kings 1:11–14).</li>
<li>Collaborates with <b>Zadok the priest</b> and others in Solomon’s anointing (1 Kings 1:32–39).</li>
<li>Connected to <b>Solomon</b> via the naming “Jedidiah” (2 Samuel 12:24–25).</li>
</ul>
<li><b>Roles/offices:</b> <b>Prophet</b>—messenger of the LORD with authority to correct king and nation (2 Samuel 7:4–5; 12:1, 7).</li>
<li><b>Key turning points:</b><br></li>
<ul>
<li>Receives and delivers the LORD’s corrective revelation about the temple/dynasty (2 Samuel 7:4–17).</li>
<li>Confronts David’s sin; announces both forgiveness and discipline (2 Samuel 12:7–14).</li>
<li>Acts decisively to ensure Solomon’s rightful enthronement (1 Kings 1:11–40).</li>
</ul>
</ul>

<div><b><h2>6) Strengths, Weaknesses, and Heart-Level Patterns</h2></b></div>
<div><b><h2>Strengths (with textual evidence)</h2></b></div>
<ul>
<li><b>Submission to God’s word over his own instincts:</b> Nathan’s initial “Go, do all that is in your heart” is reversed when the LORD speaks (2 Samuel 7:3–4). He faithfully delivers the corrected message (2 Samuel 7:5–17).</li>
<li><b>Courage to confront power:</b> Nathan directly indicts David’s abuse of authority and hidden sin (2 Samuel 12:7–9).</li>
<li><b>Moral clarity and pastoral precision:</b> Nathan uses a parable that exposes sin and leads to confession (2 Samuel 12:1–6, 13).</li>
<li><b>Commitment to covenant order and public righteousness:</b> In the succession crisis, Nathan pursues a lawful, public resolution in line with David’s oath and God’s purposes (1 Kings 1:11–14, 29–35, 38–40).</li>
</ul>
<div><b><h2>Weaknesses / sins / blind spots (what Scripture shows—and doesn’t)</h2></b></div>
<ul>
<li><b>No explicit moral failure is recorded</b> for Nathan in the canonical narrative. That doesn’t mean he was sinless, only that Scripture’s focus is his prophetic function in key covenant moments.</li>
<li>A potential limitation we can observe <b>without accusing sin</b>: Nathan initially speaks from prudence rather than revelation (2 Samuel 7:3), reminding us that even godly leaders must distinguish “wise opinion” from “Thus says the LORD” (2 Samuel 7:4–5).</li>
</ul>
<div><b><h2>Motives / fears / desires (careful inferences anchored to text)</h2></b></div>
<ul>
<li><b>Desire for God’s honor and right worship:</b> His readiness to support David’s temple desire (2 Samuel 7:3) suggests alignment with worship priorities—though corrected in timing and agent (2 Samuel 7:4–13).</li>
<li><b>Desire for justice and covenant integrity:</b> His direct indictment of David’s injustice toward Uriah and Bathsheba (2 Samuel 12:7–9) reflects commitment to God’s righteousness.</li>
<li><b>Protective concern for the legitimate heir and stability:</b> His warning to Bathsheba (1 Kings 1:11–14) implies concern that covenant purposes and lives are at stake (1 Kings 1:12).</li>
</ul>
<div><b><h2>What changes (or doesn’t) over time?</h2></b></div>
<ul>
<li><b>Consistent pattern:</b> Nathan remains a man under the authority of God’s word—whether delivering promise (2 Samuel 7) or rebuke (2 Samuel 12) or guiding political-theological crisis (1 Kings 1).</li>
<li><b>Maturing expression:</b> The narrative shows a prophet who can <b>counsel</b>, <b>correct</b>, <b>confront</b>, and <b>strategize</b>—different expressions of the same faithful office (2 Samuel 7:4–5; 12:1; 1 Kings 1:11–14).</li>
</ul>
<div><br></div>
<div><b><h2>7) Victories and Challenges</h2></b></div>
<div><b><h2>Major “wins” (obedience, influence, faithfulness)</h2></b></div>
<ul>
<li><b>Delivered the Davidic covenant promise faithfully</b> (2 Samuel 7:4–17; 1 Chronicles 17:3–15).</li>
<li><b>Brought David to repentance</b> through wise confrontation (2 Samuel 12:1–13).</li>
<li><b>Upheld both mercy and justice</b> in proclaiming forgiveness and consequences (2 Samuel 12:13–14).</li>
<li><b>Helped secure Solomon’s enthronement</b> against a rival claim (1 Kings 1:11–40).</li>
</ul>
<div><b><h2>Major challenges (opposition, risk, costly faithfulness)</h2></b></div>
<ul>
<li><b>Confronting a powerful king</b> after grievous abuse of authority—socially and personally dangerous work (2 Samuel 12:7–9).</li>
<li><b>Announcing painful consequences</b> even after repentance—bearing the weight of truthful proclamation (2 Samuel 12:14–18).</li>
<li><b>Navigating a volatile succession crisis</b> where political missteps could lead to death and civil fracture (1 Kings 1:11–21).</li>
</ul>
<div><b>Cost of faithfulness / consequences of compromise (highlight):</b>Nathan’s ministry shows that <i>faithfulness</i> requires saying hard things to powerful people (2 Samuel 12:7–12) and that even forgiven sin can carry real temporal consequences (2 Samuel 12:13–14).</div>

<div><b><h2>9) Theology and Big Themes</h2></b></div>
<div><b><h2>God’s character revealed</h2></b></div>
<ul>
<li><b>Sovereign, initiating grace:</b> God takes David from shepherd to prince and establishes covenant promise (2 Samuel 7:8–9, 12–16).</li>
<li><b>Holiness and justice:</b> God does not overlook royal sin; He exposes it and judges it (2 Samuel 12:7–12).</li>
<li><b>Mercy and covenant faithfulness:</b> God forgives David upon confession (2 Samuel 12:13) and continues His redemptive plan through Solomon (2 Samuel 12:24–25).</li>
</ul>
<div><b><h2>Covenant, kingdom, worship</h2></b></div>
<ul>
<li><b>Covenant shapes history:</b> The “house” promise frames Israel’s royal hope (2 Samuel 7:12–16).</li>
<li><b>Worship must follow God’s way:</b> David’s temple desire is good, but God determines timing and agent (2 Samuel 7:4–13).</li>
<li><b>The kingdom is not mere politics:</b> Solomon’s enthronement is bound up with divine promise and legitimate succession (1 Kings 1:29–35, 38–40).</li>
</ul>
<div><b><h2>Human nature: sin, repentance, consequences</h2></b></div>
<ul>
<li><b>Sin deceives and abuses power:</b> David’s actions against Uriah show how desire can become exploitation (2 Samuel 12:7–9).</li>
<li><b>Repentance is real and decisive:</b> “I have sinned against the LORD” is met with real forgiveness (2 Samuel 12:13).</li>
<li><b>Consequences remain instructive:</b> Discipline underscores God’s holiness and trains His people (2 Samuel 12:14).</li>
</ul>
<div><br></div>
<div><b><h2>10) How Nathan Points Us to Christ</h2></b></div>
<div><b><h2>Office/pattern: prophet as covenant messenger</h2></b></div>
<div>Nathan embodies the prophetic role: speaking God’s word into the life of the king and the kingdom (2 Samuel 7:4–5; 12:1). This anticipates the need for a <b>greater Prophet</b> who speaks God’s final word with perfect authority.</div>
<ul>
<li><b>Prophet expectation:</b> Deuteronomy anticipates a prophet like Moses (Deuteronomy 18:15–19).</li>
<li><b>NT fulfillment:</b> The NT identifies Jesus in this prophetic fulfillment stream (Acts 3:22–23).</li>
</ul>
<div><b><h2>Promise trajectory: Davidic covenant to the Messiah</h2></b></div>
<div>Nathan delivers the covenant promise of an enduring Davidic throne (2 Samuel 7:12–16). The NT explicitly locates Jesus in the line and fulfillment of David’s royal promise:</div>
<ul>
<li><b>Jesus receives David’s throne:</b> Luke 1:32–33.</li>
<li><b>Jesus as promised Savior from David’s line:</b> Acts 13:22–23.</li>
</ul>
<div><b><h2>Gospel connection: grace without denial of holiness</h2></b></div>
<div>Nathan’s confrontation shows the gospel-shaped reality that:</div>
<ul>
<li>Sin must be named truthfully (2 Samuel 12:7–9),</li>
<li>Repentance matters (2 Samuel 12:13),</li>
<li>Forgiveness is real (2 Samuel 12:13),</li>
<li>And God’s holiness still trains His people through consequences (2 Samuel 12:14; cf. Hebrews 12:5–11).</li>
</ul>
<div>Nathan does not “save” David—God does. But Nathan is the instrument God uses to bring David into the light, preparing us to see our need for the One who not only exposes sin but bears it.</div>
<div><br></div>
<div><b><h2>11) Lessons and Applications (8–15, each tied to Scripture)</h2></b></div>
<ol>
<li><b>Don’t confuse wise advice with God’s revealed will.</b> Nathan’s good-sounding counsel needed correction by the LORD (2 Samuel 7:3–5).</li>
<li><b>God’s “no” can carry a deeper “yes” of grace.</b> David won’t build the temple, but God promises an enduring dynasty (2 Samuel 7:12–16).</li>
<li><b>Even the powerful are accountable to God’s word.</b> Nathan confronts the king under divine authority (2 Samuel 12:7–9).</li>
<li><b>Sin is best exposed indirectly sometimes—but must be named directly eventually.</b> Parable (2 Samuel 12:1–6) leads to direct indictment (2 Samuel 12:7).</li>
<li><b>True repentance is simple, honest, and immediate.</b> David confesses plainly (2 Samuel 12:13).</li>
<li><b>Forgiveness is real, and discipline can still follow.</b> “Put away your sin” and “nevertheless…” (2 Samuel 12:13–14).</li>
<li><b>God’s purposes continue through broken people—but never excuse sin.</b> Solomon’s favor follows David’s repentance and discipline (2 Samuel 12:24–25).</li>
<li><b>Courageous truth-telling is a form of love.</b> Nathan risks much to rescue David from self-deception (2 Samuel 12:7–13).</li>
<li><b>Leadership transitions require integrity and clarity, not ambition.</b> Nathan acts against Adonijah’s self-exaltation (1 Kings 1:5–6, 11–14, 29–35).</li>
<li><b>Public righteousness matters for public office.</b> Solomon’s anointing is public and orderly (1 Kings 1:38–40).</li>
<li><b>God’s kingdom is advanced through both proclamation and prudent action.</b> Nathan speaks God’s word (2 Samuel 7; 12) and acts wisely in crisis (1 Kings 1:11–40).</li>
</ol>
<div><b>Warnings to heed:</b> hidden sin + power is spiritually lethal (2 Samuel 12:7–9).<b>Examples to imitate:</b> truthful confrontation, submission to correction, and commitment to covenant order (2 Samuel 7:4–5; 12:1–13; 1 Kings 1:11–40).</div>

<div><b><h2>Personal reflection</h2></b></div>
<ol>
<li>Where are you tempted to say “God is surely with you” (2 Samuel 7:3) when you haven’t actually sought God’s word?</li>
<li>How do you respond when God corrects your confident opinion (2 Samuel 7:4–5)?</li>
<li>What does David’s confession teach you about honest repentance (2 Samuel 12:13)?</li>
</ol>
<div><b><h2>Small group discussion</h2></b></div>
<ol>
<li>Why do you think Nathan used a parable before a direct accusation (2 Samuel 12:1–7)?</li>
<li>In what ways does the Davidic covenant shape the whole storyline of the Bible (2 Samuel 7:12–16; Luke 1:32–33)?</li>
<li>How should Christians hold together forgiveness and consequences (2 Samuel 12:13–14)?</li>
</ol>
<div><b><h2>Deep study (text-observation)</h2></b></div>
<ol>
<li>Trace the repeated “house” language in 2 Samuel 7. What does it mean in each context (2 Samuel 7:5–7, 11, 16)?</li>
<li>In 2 Samuel 12:7–9, list the LORD’s “I gave… I delivered… I anointed…” statements. What do they reveal about God’s generosity?</li>
<li>What exactly does Nathan say will happen as a consequence (2 Samuel 12:10–12, 14)? What does he <b>not</b> say?</li>
<li>Compare 2 Samuel 7 and 1 Chronicles 17. What emphases are similar/different? (2 Samuel 7:12–16; 1 Chronicles 17:11–14)</li>
<li>In 1 Kings 1, what actions show Nathan’s strategic wisdom without manipulation (1 Kings 1:11–14, 22–27)?</li>
<li>Why is public anointing significant in 1 Kings 1:38–40?</li>
</ol>
<div><b><h2>Christ-centered synthesis</h2></b></div>
<ol>
<li>How does Nathan’s covenant message set up messianic hope (2 Samuel 7:12–16; Acts 13:22–23)?</li>
<li>How does Nathan’s role as prophet help you understand Jesus’ prophetic ministry (Deuteronomy 18:15–19; Acts 3:22–23)?</li>
<li>Where do you most need God’s word to confront, comfort, or redirect you right now (2 Samuel 7:4–5; 12:7, 13)?</li>
</ol>
