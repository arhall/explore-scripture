<div><h1>Samson</h1></div>
<div><br></div>
<div><b><h1>Samson — Bible Character Study (Judges)</h1></b></div>
<div><b><h2>1) Story Summary (Chronological)</h2></b></div>
<div>Samson is introduced in the era of the judges, during a time when Israel repeatedly falls into sin and is oppressed by surrounding peoples—here, especially the Philistines (Judg 13:1). His story begins with an angelic announcement to a barren woman that she will bear a son who will be a Nazirite “from the womb,” set apart to God, and who will “begin to save Israel from the hand of the Philistines” (Judg 13:2–5). Samson’s birth and calling are framed as divine initiative: God gives the child, sets the terms of his consecration, and empowers him by the Spirit (Judg 13:24–25).</div>
<div>As Samson grows, his life becomes a paradox of God-given strength and self-driven desire. He insists on marrying a Philistine woman from Timnah against his parents’ counsel (Judg 14:1–3). On the way, he tears a lion apart “as one tears a young goat” when the Spirit of the LORD rushes upon him (Judg 14:5–6). He later uses honey from the lion’s carcass, leading into a riddle at his wedding feast and a cycle of conflict, retaliation, and escalating violence between Samson and the Philistines (Judg 14:8–20).</div>
<div>Betrayed by his Timnite wife under Philistine pressure, Samson responds with vengeance: he strikes down thirty men at Ashkelon to pay his wager (Judg 14:19), then later burns Philistine crops by tying torches to foxes (Judg 15:1–5). The Philistines retaliate by burning his wife and her father (Judg 15:6). Samson’s reprisal intensifies; Judah’s men bind Samson to hand him over, but Samson breaks free when the Spirit rushes upon him and kills a thousand men with a donkey’s jawbone (Judg 15:9–15). Afterwards, he cries to God for water, and the LORD provides (Judg 15:18–19). The narrative summarizes: “He judged Israel twenty years in the days of the Philistines” (Judg 15:20).</div>
<div>Samson then goes to Gaza, spends the night with a prostitute, and escapes an ambush by ripping up the city gates and carrying them away (Judg 16:1–3). His downfall centers on Delilah in the valley of Sorek. Pressed repeatedly, Samson toys with deception about the source of his strength, until he finally reveals that his Nazirite consecration is symbolized in his uncut hair (Judg 16:4–17). Delilah lulls him to sleep; his hair is cut; “the LORD left him,” and the Philistines seize him, gouge out his eyes, and force him to grind in prison (Judg 16:18–21). Yet the text notes his hair begins to grow again (Judg 16:22).</div>
<div>In the finale, during a Philistine celebration to Dagon, Samson is brought out to entertain. He prays: “O Lord GOD, please remember me… strengthen me only this once” (Judg 16:28). He braces the temple’s central pillars and pulls it down, killing many Philistines and himself: “So the dead whom he killed at his death were more than those whom he had killed during his life” (Judg 16:29–30). His family buries him in the tomb of his father Manoah (Judg 16:31). Samson’s story ends where it began: God’s purpose to oppose Philistine oppression advances, though through a deeply flawed deliverer.</div>
<div><b>Where his story begins and ends</b><br></div>
<ul>
<li><b>Begins:</b> Angelic announcement and Nazirite calling (Judg 13:1–5)</li>
<li><b>Ends:</b> Death at the Philistine temple and burial (Judg 16:28–31)</li>
</ul>
<div><br></div>
<div><b><h2>2) What He’s Known For</h2></b></div>
<div>Samson is most known as a Spirit-empowered Nazirite judge whose extraordinary strength is matched by moral weakness—yet God uses him to strike the Philistines and “begin” Israel’s deliverance (Judg 13:5; 14:6; 15:14–15; 16:30).</div>
<div><b>Headline descriptors</b><br></div>
<ul>
<li>Nazirite “from the womb” (Judg 13:5, 7)</li>
<li>Spirit-empowered strength against Philistines (Judg 14:6; 15:14)</li>
<li>A judge in the days of Philistine oppression (Judg 15:20)</li>
<li>Driven by desire; compromised by Philistine entanglements (Judg 14:1–3; 16:1, 4)</li>
<li>A deliverer whose life is marked by vengeance cycles (Judg 14:19; 15:5, 7–8)</li>
<li>Brought low through betrayal; blinded and enslaved (Judg 16:18–21)</li>
<li>Final prayer and death that topples a pagan temple (Judg 16:28–30)</li>
</ul>
<div><br></div>
<div><b><h2>3) Where He Appears in the Bible</h2></b></div>
<div><b><h2>Primary narrative locations</h2></b></div>
<ul>
<li><b>Judges 13:1–25</b> — Birth announcement; Nazirite requirements; Spirit begins to stir him.</li>
<li><b>Judges 14:1–20</b> — Timnah marriage pursuit; lion; honey; riddle; betrayal; first major clash.</li>
<li><b>Judges 15:1–20</b> — Burning fields; escalation; jawbone slaughter; God provides water; judges 20 years.</li>
<li><b>Judges 16:1–31</b> — Gaza gates; Delilah; loss of strength; imprisonment; final prayer and death.</li>
</ul>
<div><b><h2>Secondary references</h2></b></div>
<ul>
<li><b>Hebrews 11:32</b> — Listed among examples of faith (with other judges/kings).</li>
<li><b>1 Samuel 12:11</b> — “Jerubbaal… Bedan… Jephthah… Samuel” (textual questions around “Bedan”; sometimes linked with Samson/Dan—uncertain; do not build doctrine on it).</li>
<li><b>Judges 2:16–19 (the judge-cycle frame)</b> — Interprets the era Samson belongs to (contextual, not a direct mention).</li>
</ul>

<div><b><h2>4) Vital Statistics</h2></b></div>
<ul>
<li><b>Historical setting:</b> Period of the Judges; Israel oppressed by Philistines (Judg 13:1). Samson’s judging: <b>20 years</b> “in the days of the Philistines” (Judg 15:20; 16:31).</li>
<li><b>Geography (key places):</b><br></li>
<ul>
<li><b>Zorah</b> (Samson’s family region; burial; Spirit stirs him) (Judg 13:2, 25; 16:31)</li>
<li><b>Eshtaol / between Zorah and Eshtaol</b> (Spirit stirring; burial region) (Judg 13:25; 16:31)</li>
<li><b>Timnah</b> (Philistine area; intended marriage; wedding) (Judg 14:1–2)</li>
<li><b>Sorek Valley</b> (Delilah) (Judg 16:4)</li>
<li><b>Gaza</b> (prostitute episode; city gates) (Judg 16:1–3)</li>
</ul>
<li><b>Family and relationships:</b><br></li>
<ul>
<li><b>Father:</b> Manoah (Judg 13:2, 8–12; 16:31)</li>
<li><b>Mother:</b> unnamed; barren; receives angelic announcement (Judg 13:2–7)</li>
<li><b>Wife (Timnah):</b> unnamed; given to another man; dies by Philistine violence (Judg 14:1–2; 15:2, 6)</li>
<li><b>Delilah:</b> lover whose betrayal leads to his capture (Judg 16:4–21)</li>
<li><b>Others:</b> Judah’s men who bind him (Judg 15:9–13); Philistine lords/rulers (Judg 16:5, 18)</li>
</ul>
<li><b>Roles/offices:</b> <b>Judge</b> (Judg 15:20; 16:31); <b>Nazirite-consecrated</b> (Judg 13:5, 7).</li>
<li><b>Key turning points:</b><br></li>
<ul>
<li>Spirit begins to stir him (Judg 13:25)</li>
<li>Timnah desire and marriage pursuit (Judg 14:1–3)</li>
<li>Repeated Spirit-empowered victories (Judg 14:6, 19; 15:14–15)</li>
<li>Prayer for water (dependence moment) (Judg 15:18–19)</li>
<li>Revealing his consecration secret to Delilah (Judg 16:17)</li>
<li>“The LORD left him” and captivity (Judg 16:20–21)</li>
<li>Final prayer and temple collapse (Judg 16:28–30)</li>
</ul>
</ul>

<div><b><h2>6) Strengths, Weaknesses, and Heart-Level Patterns</h2></b></div>
<div><b><h2>Strengths (evidenced in text)</h2></b></div>
<ul>
<li><b>Spirit-empowered courage and capacity for deliverance</b> (Judg 14:6; 15:14–15). The repeated phrase “the Spirit of the LORD rushed upon him” underscores divine enablement.</li>
<li><b>Decisiveness in conflict (bold action, not passivity)</b> (Judg 15:4–5; 16:3). He acts aggressively against oppression, even if motives are mixed.</li>
<li><b>Ability to cry to God in crisis</b> (Judg 15:18; 16:28). Samson prays when desperate—showing he knows where strength ultimately comes from.</li>
<li><b>Endurance in humiliation leading to a final act of deliverance</b> (Judg 16:21–30). Suffering does not erase God’s ability to use him.</li>
</ul>
<div><b><h2>Weaknesses / sins / blind spots (with context)</h2></b></div>
<ul>
<li><b>Lust-driven choices and boundary-crossing</b>: insisting on a Philistine wife (Judg 14:1–3); visiting a prostitute (Judg 16:1); entanglement with Delilah (Judg 16:4).</li>
<li><b>Flirtation with secrecy and manipulation</b>: riddle and wager dynamics that spiral into violence (Judg 14:12–19); repeated deceptive “tests” with Delilah (Judg 16:7–15).</li>
<li><b>Vengeance logic that dominates his leadership</b>: “I will be avenged on you” (Judg 15:7) and retaliation escalating after retaliation (Judg 14:19; 15:5–8).</li>
<li><b>Treating consecration casually</b>: he reveals what should be guarded, and his confidence becomes presumption (“I will go out as at other times…”) (Judg 16:17, 20).</li>
</ul>
<div><b><h2>Motives / fears / desires (careful inferences anchored to the text)</h2></b></div>
<ul>
<li><b>Desire for what is pleasing to his eyes</b> is explicit: “she is right in my eyes” (Judg 14:3). This echoes the Judges theme of doing what is right in one’s own eyes (cf. Judg 21:25).</li>
<li><b>A taste for thrill/risk and control</b> is suggested by repeated “game-playing” with enemies and with Delilah (Judg 14:12–14; 16:7–15).</li>
<li><b>A late-emerging desire for vindication of God’s name and personal wrongs</b> appears in the final scene: his prayer targets the Philistines who credit Dagon and humiliate him (Judg 16:23–28).</li>
</ul>
<div><b><h2>What changes (or doesn’t) over time?</h2></b></div>
<ul>
<li><b>Doesn’t change much:</b> the pattern of desire → compromise → conflict persists (Timnah, Gaza, Sorek) (Judg 14:1–3; 16:1, 4).</li>
<li><b>A real shift:</b> the end of the story moves Samson from self-confidence to <b>explicit dependence</b>—he prays for strength “only this once” (Judg 16:28). Even this prayer includes personal vengeance (“for my two eyes”), so the change is partial, but it is still a return to God.</li>
</ul>
<div><br></div>
<div><b><h2>7) Victories and Challenges</h2></b></div>
<div><b><h2>Major “wins” (deliverance, influence, faith)</h2></b></div>
<ul>
<li><b>Lion slain by the Spirit</b> (Judg 14:6) — early sign of divine empowerment.</li>
<li><b>Strikes down thirty Philistines at Ashkelon</b> (Judg 14:19) — first major blow against Philistines.</li>
<li><b>Burns Philistine fields</b> (Judg 15:4–5) — crippling economic sabotage (though morally complicated).</li>
<li><b>Kills a thousand with a donkey’s jawbone</b> (Judg 15:14–15) — dramatic deliverance episode under Spirit empowerment.</li>
<li><b>Escapes Gaza by carrying away city gates</b> (Judg 16:3) — symbolic humiliation of Philistine security.</li>
<li><b>Final deliverance through temple collapse</b> (Judg 16:28–30) — his greatest strike against Philistines occurs at his death.</li>
</ul>
<div><b><h2>Major challenges (temptations, failures, suffering, opposition)</h2></b></div>
<ul>
<li><b>Internal compromise:</b> pursuing Philistine relationships (Judg 14:1–3; 16:1, 4).</li>
<li><b>External pressure and betrayal:</b> Philistines coerce his wife (Judg 14:15–17) and bribe Delilah (Judg 16:5).</li>
<li><b>Isolation from Israel:</b> Judah hands him over (Judg 15:12–13), highlighting Israel’s weakness and Samson’s loneliness.</li>
<li><b>Consequences of presumption:</b> “the LORD left him,” leading to blindness and slavery (Judg 16:20–21).</li>
<li><b>Suffering and humiliation:</b> forced labor and public mockery (Judg 16:21, 25).</li>
</ul>
<div><b>Cost / consequence note:</b> Samson’s compromises cost him freedom, sight, and life (Judg 16:20–30). Yet God’s mercy still operates—he hears Samson’s prayer and uses his death to strike the oppressors (Judg 16:28–30).</div>

<div><b><h2>9) Theology and Big Themes</h2></b></div>
<div><b><h2>God’s character revealed</h2></b></div>
<ul>
<li><b>Sovereign initiative and providence:</b> God begins salvation through a miraculous birth and a set-apart calling (Judg 13:3–5).</li>
<li><b>Patience and mercy toward flawed servants:</b> God empowers Samson repeatedly and even hears him in desperation (Judg 14:6; 15:14; 15:18–19; 16:28).</li>
<li><b>Holiness and discipline:</b> consecration matters; when Samson’s calling is violated and presumed upon, “the LORD left him,” and judgment follows (Judg 16:20–21).</li>
<li><b>Justice over idolatry and oppression:</b> Philistines attribute victory to Dagon (Judg 16:23–24), but God’s final act topples their celebration (Judg 16:28–30).</li>
</ul>
<div><b><h2>Deliverance in the Judges pattern</h2></b></div>
<div>Samson’s narrative sits inside the Judges cycle: Israel sins → oppression → the LORD raises a deliverer (Judg 13:1–5; cf. Judg 2:16–19). Yet Samson shows an intensified problem: the deliverer himself embodies Israel’s divided heart—God’s gifts mixed with compromise.</div>
<div><b><h2>Human nature: gifting without maturity</h2></b></div>
<div>Samson is a case study in how <b>calling and gifting</b> do not equal holiness. He is empowered externally by the Spirit (Judg 14:6; 15:14), yet repeatedly unmanaged desire erodes his life (Judg 14:3; 16:1, 4).</div>
<div><b><h2>Suffering as judgment and as instrument</h2></b></div>
<div>Samson’s blindness and bondage are clearly consequences (Judg 16:20–21), yet in God’s providence they become the stage for a final deliverance through prayer (Judg 16:28–30).</div>
<div><br></div>
<div><b><h2>10) How Samson Points Us to Christ (Careful, Text-Grounded)</h2></b></div>
<div><b><h2>Office / pattern: Spirit-empowered deliverer</h2></b></div>
<div>Samson functions as a <b>deliverer-judge</b> raised up by God in Israel’s distress (Judg 13:5). This anticipates the need for a greater Deliverer—one who not only fights enemies but also renews hearts (a need Judges itself highlights).</div>
<div><b><h2>A contrast that sharpens the gospel</h2></b></div>
<ul>
<li>Samson is set apart from birth yet compromised by desire (Judg 13:5; 14:3; 16:1, 4).</li>
<li>Jesus is consecrated and obedient without sin (Heb 4:15), resisting temptation where others fall (Matt 4:1–11). Samson shows that Israel needs more than raw power; it needs a righteous Savior-King.</li>
</ul>
<div><b><h2>Death that brings victory (limited type, real pattern)</h2></b></div>
<div>Samson’s final act brings deliverance through his death: “Let me die with the Philistines” (Judg 16:30). There is a <b>pattern</b> of victory through death, but the differences matter:</div>
<ul>
<li>Samson dies to destroy enemies and includes personal vengeance (Judg 16:28–30).</li>
<li>Christ dies to defeat sin, death, and the devil, offering forgiveness to enemies (Rom 5:8–10; Col 2:13–15). So Samson can be read as a shadowy, imperfect pointer: deliverance comes at a cost, but only Christ delivers without moral failure and without mixed motives.</li>
</ul>
<div><br></div>
<div><b><h2>11) Lessons and Applications (Scripture-Tethered)</h2></b></div>
<ol>
<li><b>God can begin deliverance through unlikely, flawed instruments</b> (Judg 13:5; 14:6) — but do not confuse usefulness with approval.</li>
<li><b>Consecration is not optional decoration; it’s identity</b> (Judg 13:5, 13–14; 16:17).</li>
<li><b>“Right in my eyes” is a spiritual warning label</b> (Judg 14:3; cf. Judg 21:25).</li>
<li><b>Small compromises normalize bigger ones</b> (Judg 14:1–3 → 16:1 → 16:4–17).</li>
<li><b>The Spirit’s empowerment is not a license for recklessness</b> (Judg 14:6; 16:20).</li>
<li><b>Isolation is dangerous—Samson repeatedly stands alone</b> (Judg 15:12–13). Seek covenant community rather than operating as a solo hero (principle consistent with Israel’s life together).</li>
<li><b>Prayer in desperation is still prayer—cry out to God</b> (Judg 15:18–19; 16:28).</li>
<li><b>Sin blinds and enslaves</b> (Judg 16:20–21) — sometimes literally, always spiritually (principle).</li>
<li><b>Idolatry celebrates false victories, but God’s justice stands</b> (Judg 16:23–24, 30).</li>
<li><b>God’s purposes advance even when leaders are deeply imperfect</b> (Judg 13:5; 16:30) — which should humble us, not embolden sin.</li>
<li><b>Consequences can be severe, even for the called</b> (Judg 16:20–21, 30).</li>
<li><b>Finish matters:</b> Samson’s last scene shows a return to dependence (Judg 16:28). Don’t let earlier failure have the final word if repentance and faith are possible.</li>
</ol>
<div><b><h2>Warnings to heed</h2></b></div>
<ul>
<li>Presumption: “I will go out as at other times” (Judg 16:20).</li>
<li>Playing with temptation and secrecy (Judg 16:7–15).</li>
<li>Treating calling as negotiable (Judg 16:17).</li>
</ul>
<div><b><h2>Examples to imitate (carefully)</h2></b></div>
<ul>
<li>Crying to God for help (Judg 15:18; 16:28).</li>
<li>Acknowledging God as the true source of strength (implied in his prayer) (Judg 16:28).</li>
</ul>
<div><b>Application areas</b><br></div>
<ul>
<li><b>Personal holiness:</b> guard consecration; don’t flirt with what destroys (Judg 13:5; 16:17).</li>
<li><b>Relationships:</b> desire without discernment leads to bondage (Judg 14:3; 16:4–21).</li>
<li><b>Prayer:</b> pray before crisis, not only after; but even in crisis, pray (Judg 15:18–19; 16:28).</li>
<li><b>Decision-making:</b> resist “right in my eyes” logic; submit desires to God’s word (Judg 14:3).</li>
<li><b>Leadership:</b> gifting must be paired with character; otherwise the leader becomes the liability (Judg 15:20; 16:20–21).</li>
</ul>

</ol>

</ol>

<li>Observe the role of Philistine coercion (Judg 14:15–17; 16:5). How does pressure shape the narrative?</li>
<li>What repeated words/images stand out (eyes, binding, gates, hair, pillars)? How do they develop themes (Judg 14:3; 16:21; 16:3; 16:17; 16:29)?</li>
<li>Compare Samson’s “water” episode (Judg 15:18–19) with his “strength” episode (Judg 16:28). What do these prayers reveal?</li>
<li>What is the significance of Samson’s story beginning with promised deliverance and ending with death (Judg 13:5; 16:30)? What does it suggest about the need for a better judge/king?</li>
</ol>
