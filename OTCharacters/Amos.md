<div><h1>Amos</h1></div>
<div><br></div>
<div><b><h1>Amos — Bible Character Study</h1></b></div>
<div><b><h2>1) Story Summary (chronological)</h2></b></div>
<div>Amos appears as a prophet unexpectedly raised up from ordinary work, sent with a severe message to the <b>Northern Kingdom (Israel)</b> during a season of prosperity and outward religious activity. The book opens by locating Amos historically: he is “among the shepherds of Tekoa,” prophesying “in the days of Uzziah king of Judah and in the days of Jeroboam son of Joash king of Israel,” “two years before the earthquake” (Amos 1:1).</div>
<div>Amos is not presented as a “career prophet” from a prophetic guild. He identifies himself as a shepherd and a dresser of sycamore figs, and he insists that <b>the LORD took him</b> from following the flock and commissioned him: “Go, prophesy to my people Israel” (Amos 7:14–15). His message begins with judgment oracles against surrounding nations (Amos 1:3–2:3), then turns sharply to Judah (Amos 2:4–5) and especially Israel (Amos 2:6–16), exposing <b>covenant-breaking expressed in injustice, exploitation, sexual immorality, and corrupt courts</b> (Amos 2:6–8; 5:10–12).</div>
<div>He confronts Israel’s false confidence: they presumed God’s favor because of election and worship, but Amos declares that election increases accountability—“You only have I known… therefore I will punish you” (Amos 3:1–2). He unmasks empty ritual: God rejects their feasts and songs when they refuse righteousness, calling instead for justice to “roll down like waters” (Amos 5:21–24). He warns that the “day of the LORD” they desire will be darkness, not light, for an unrepentant people (Amos 5:18–20).</div>
<div>A pivotal narrative episode occurs when <b>Amaziah</b>, priest of Bethel, opposes Amos, accuses him of conspiracy, and orders him to flee (Amos 7:10–13). Amos answers with his call testimony and delivers a severe word of judgment against Amaziah and Israel (Amos 7:14–17). The latter part of the book contains a sequence of visions (locusts, fire, plumb line, summer fruit, the LORD beside the altar) emphasizing the inevitability of judgment (Amos 7:1–9; 8:1–3; 9:1–4), including the terrifying “famine… of hearing the words of the LORD” (Amos 8:11–12).</div>
<div>Yet Amos ends with hope: after purging judgment, the LORD will <b>restore “the booth of David,”</b> repair ruins, and bring lasting planting and peace (Amos 9:11–15).<b>Where his story begins and ends in the Bible:</b> Amos’s story begins in the superscription and call narrative in <b>Amos 1:1; 7:14–15</b> and ends with the restoration promise in <b>Amos 9:11–15</b>.</div>
<div><br></div>
<div><b><h2>2) What He’s Known For</h2></b></div>
<div>Amos is most known for being a <b>shepherd-prophet</b> sent to confront Israel’s injustice and religious hypocrisy, proclaiming unavoidable judgment—and concluding with a promise of Davidic restoration.</div>
<div><b>Headlines</b><br></div>
<ul>
<li><b>Shepherd from Tekoa turned prophet by divine call</b> (Amos 1:1; 7:14–15)</li>
<li><b>Prophet of justice (righteousness over ritual)</b> (Amos 5:21–24)</li>
<li><b>Covenant accountability: election heightens responsibility</b> (Amos 3:1–2)</li>
<li><b>Bold confrontation of Bethel’s establishment</b> (Amos 7:10–17)</li>
<li><b>Warnings about the “day of the LORD” and false security</b> (Amos 5:18–20; 6:1)</li>
<li><b>Visions that seal the certainty of judgment</b> (Amos 7:1–9; 8:1–3; 9:1–4)</li>
<li><b>Hope of restored Davidic rule and renewed creation blessing</b> (Amos 9:11–15)</li>
</ul>
<div><br></div>
<div><b><h2>3) Where He Appears in the Bible</h2></b></div>
<div><b>Primary narrative locations (main story)</b></div>
<ul>
<li><b>Amos — 1:1–9:15</b> — Amos’s prophecies, visions, confrontation, and restoration hope.</li>
</ul>
<div><b>Secondary references (later mentions / interpretive use)</b></div>
<ul>
<li><b>Acts — 15:13–18</b> — James cites <b>Amos 9:11–12</b> to explain Gentile inclusion under David’s restored reign through Christ.</li>
<li><b>(Theme echoes, not direct mentions)</b> Many prophets echo Amos’s justice-without-hypocrisy emphasis (e.g., Isaiah 1:10–17; Micah 6:6–8), but Amos himself is explicitly quoted in Acts 15.</li>
</ul>
<div><br></div>
<div><b><h2>4) Vital Statistics</h2></b></div>
<ul>
<li><b>Historical setting (as Scripture indicates):</b><br></li>
<ul>
<li>Reigns of <b>Uzziah (Judah)</b> and <b>Jeroboam II (Israel)</b>; “two years before the earthquake” (Amos 1:1).</li>
</ul>
<li><b>Geography:</b><br></li>
<ul>
<li><b>Tekoa</b> (Amos’s home region; Amos 1:1)</li>
<li><b>Bethel</b> (royal sanctuary; center of confrontation; Amos 7:10–13)</li>
<li><b>Samaria</b> / Israel’s strongholds and “winter house… summer house” (symbols of wealth; Amos 3:15)</li>
<li>Broader focus: <b>Israel</b> and surrounding nations (Amos 1:3–2:16).</li>
</ul>
<li><b>Family and relationships (explicit in Scripture):</b><br></li>
<ul>
<li>No parents/spouse/children named for Amos.</li>
<li>Key opponent: <b>Amaziah, priest of Bethel</b> (Amos 7:10–17).</li>
<li>Audience: “my people Israel” (Amos 7:15).</li>
</ul>
<li><b>Roles/offices:</b><br></li>
<ul>
<li>Prophet by divine summons (Amos 7:14–15).</li>
<li>Occupations: <b>shepherd</b> and <b>dresser of sycamore figs</b> (Amos 7:14; cf. 1:1).</li>
</ul>
<li><b>Key turning points:</b><br></li>
<ul>
<li>Divine call from ordinary labor to prophetic mission (Amos 7:14–15).</li>
<li>Public clash with Bethel’s priesthood/power (Amos 7:10–17).</li>
<li>Transition into judgment-visions that emphasize inevitability (Amos 7:1–9; 8:1–3; 9:1–4).</li>
<li>Final turn to restoration promise (Amos 9:11–15).</li>
</ul>
</ul>

<div><b><h2>6) Strengths, Weaknesses, and Heart-Level Patterns</h2></b></div>
<div><b>Strengths (evidenced in the text)</b></div>
<ul>
<li><b>Courage to confront power and popular religion</b> (Amos 7:10–17).</li>
<li><b>Conviction that God’s word must be spoken</b> (“the Lord GOD has spoken; who can but prophesy?”) (Amos 3:8).</li>
<li><b>Moral clarity about justice and righteousness</b> (Amos 5:14–15, 24).</li>
<li><b>Humility about his own status</b>—not leveraging credentials, but pointing to God’s call (Amos 7:14–15).</li>
<li><b>Theological depth:</b> God’s sovereignty over nations and nature, and covenant seriousness (Amos 3:1–2; 4:13; 9:5–6).</li>
</ul>
<div><b>Weaknesses / sins / blind spots (what Scripture actually shows)</b></div>
<ul>
<li>The book does <b>not</b> narrate a moral failure of Amos the way some biographies do (e.g., David, Jonah). The emphasis is on Israel’s sin and Amos’s faithful delivery of God’s message (Amos 7:14–15; 8:11–12).</li>
<li>A careful reading should avoid inventing faults not stated.</li>
</ul>
<div><b>Motives / fears / desires (careful inference anchored to text)</b></div>
<ul>
<li><b>Fear of God more than fear of man:</b> Amos speaks despite institutional threat (Amos 7:12–17).</li>
<li><b>Desire for true covenant life:</b> his repeated calls to “seek the LORD and live” imply longing for repentance and life, not mere doom (Amos 5:4–6, 14–15).</li>
<li><b>Grief over judgment’s necessity:</b> the earlier visions show Amos interceding (“O Lord GOD, please forgive… please cease”) before the plumb line vision signals the end of relenting (Amos 7:2–6; 7:7–9).</li>
</ul>
<div><b>What changes (or doesn’t) over time?</b></div>
<ul>
<li><b>Doesn’t change:</b> the core message—God hates oppression and hypocritical worship; covenant people must repent (Amos 2:6–8; 5:21–24).</li>
<li><b>Changes:</b> the tone moves from <b>warning + intercession</b> (Amos 7:1–6) toward <b>inevitability</b> (Amos 7:7–9; 9:1–4), yet ends with <b>restoration hope</b> (Amos 9:11–15).</li>
</ul>
<div><br></div>
<div><b><h2>7) Victories and Challenges</h2></b></div>
<div><b>Major “wins” (obedience, influence, faithfulness)</b></div>
<ul>
<li><b>Faithfully proclaimed God’s word despite being an outsider</b> (Amos 7:14–15).</li>
<li><b>Spoke truth at the royal sanctuary</b>—a high-risk context (Amos 7:10–13).</li>
<li><b>Left a canonical witness</b> that shaped later church discernment about Gentiles (Amos 9:11–12; Acts 15:13–18).</li>
</ul>
<div><b>Major challenges (opposition, cost, consequences)</b></div>
<ul>
<li><b>Opposition and attempted exile:</b> “O seer, go, flee away…” (Amos 7:12–13).</li>
<li><b>Accusations of political treason:</b> Amaziah reports Amos to the king as a threat (Amos 7:10–11).</li>
<li><b>The burden of proclaiming unavoidable judgment</b> after repeated refusal to repent (Amos 7:7–9; 8:1–3).</li>
<li><b>Spiritual tragedy described:</b> a coming “famine… of hearing the words of the LORD” (Amos 8:11–12).</li>
</ul>
<div><b>Cost of faithfulness:</b> Amos’s fidelity brings conflict with religious authorities and social pressure (Amos 7:10–17).<b>Consequences of compromise (in Israel):</b> the nation’s luxury and ease mask rot and invite ruin (Amos 6:1–7; 8:4–6).</div>

<div><br></div>
<div><b><h2>9) Theology and Big Themes</h2></b></div>
<ul>
<li><b>God’s holiness and justice:</b> God judges nations and His own people; sin is real, public, and accountable (Amos 1:3–2:16; 5:21–24).</li>
<li><b>Covenant seriousness:</b> Israel’s election is not a shield from discipline; it heightens responsibility (Amos 3:1–2).</li>
<li><b>True worship vs. religious performance:</b> God rejects liturgy divorced from righteousness and love of neighbor (Amos 5:21–24; 8:4–6).</li>
<li><b>Social ethics are theological:</b> oppression is not merely “political”; it is covenant violation against the LORD (Amos 2:6–8; 5:10–12).</li>
<li><b>Divine sovereignty over creation and history:</b> doxological interludes emphasize God’s rule (Amos 4:13; 5:8–9; 9:5–6).</li>
<li><b>Judgment and mercy:</b> severe judgment is announced, yet the book concludes with restorative promise (Amos 9:11–15).</li>
</ul>
<div><br></div>
<div><b><h2>10) How Amos Points Us to Christ</h2></b></div>
<ul>
<li><b>The prophetic office fulfilled in Christ:</b> Amos speaks God’s word with uncompromising truth; Jesus is the ultimate Prophet who embodies and reveals God’s word (cf. Amos 3:7–8; Hebrews 1:1–2).</li>
<li><b>Exposure of hypocrisy:</b> Amos condemns worship without justice (Amos 5:21–24). Jesus likewise confronts religious hypocrisy and calls for weightier matters—justice and mercy (Matthew 23:23).</li>
<li><b>Davidic restoration fulfilled in the Messiah:</b> The climactic hope—restoring “the booth of David” (Amos 9:11–12)—is explicitly applied by the apostles to the messianic era and the inclusion of the Gentiles through Jesus (Acts 15:13–18).</li>
<li><b>Gospel pattern:</b> judgment is real, but God preserves a people and promises restoration (Amos 9:8–15). In Christ, the kingdom promise extends to the nations, forming one redeemed people (Acts 15:13–18; Ephesians 2:11–22).</li>
</ul>
<div><br></div>
<div><b><h2>11) Lessons and Applications (tied to Scripture)</h2></b></div>
<div><b>Examples to imitate</b></div>
<ol>
<li><b>Obey God’s call even without institutional status</b> (Amos 7:14–15).</li>
<li><b>Speak truth when it is costly</b> (Amos 7:10–17).</li>
<li><b>Let worship produce righteousness and justice</b> (Amos 5:21–24).</li>
<li><b>Seek the LORD Himself, not merely “religious places”</b> (Amos 5:4–6).</li>
<li><b>Measure prosperity by faithfulness, not comfort</b> (Amos 6:1–7).</li>
</ol>
<div><b>Warnings to heed</b></div>
<ol>
<li><b>Election/privilege does not cancel accountability</b> (Amos 3:1–2).</li>
<li><b>Religious activity can be an offense to God when paired with injustice</b> (Amos 5:21–24).</li>
<li><b>Do not desire the “day of the LORD” while resisting repentance</b> (Amos 5:18–20).</li>
<li><b>Economic exploitation is spiritual rebellion</b> (Amos 8:4–6).</li>
<li><b>Ignoring God’s word can lead to losing it</b> (Amos 8:11–12).</li>
</ol>
<div><b>Application areas</b></div>
<ul>
<li><b>Personal holiness:</b> repent of hidden compromises; “seek me and live” (Amos 5:4).</li>
<li><b>Relationships and community:</b> pursue fairness, truth-telling, and neighbor-love that aligns with justice (Amos 5:14–15).</li>
<li><b>Prayer:</b> plead for mercy and return to God while there is opportunity (Amos 7:2–6; 5:6).</li>
<li><b>Leadership:</b> don’t build “success” on exploitation; God sees and judges (Amos 5:10–12; 8:4–6).</li>
<li><b>Suffering/pressure:</b> be faithful under opposition; God’s call outweighs human threats (Amos 7:12–15).</li>
</ul>

<div><b><h2>12) Discussion and Reflection Questions</h2></b></div>
<div><b>Personal reflection</b></div>
<ol>
<li>Where am I tempted to substitute religious habits for obedience (Amos 5:21–24)?</li>
<li>What does it look like, concretely, for me to “seek the LORD and live” (Amos 5:4–6)?</li>
<li>What “false securities” am I leaning on (status, comfort, prosperity) (Amos 6:1)?</li>
<li>How do I respond when confronted—defensiveness like Amaziah, or repentance (Amos 7:10–13; 5:15)?</li>
</ol>
<div><b>Small group discussion</b></div>
<ol>
<li>Why does Amos connect worship with justice so directly (Amos 5:21–24; 8:4–6)?</li>
<li>What modern equivalents of “selling the righteous for silver” might exist (Amos 2:6)?</li>
<li>How should Christians hold together God’s justice and God’s mercy in preaching and practice (Amos 1–2; 9:11–15)?</li>
<li>How does Acts 15 use Amos 9, and what does that teach about reading the OT as fulfilled in Christ (Acts 15:13–18)?</li>
</ol>
<div><b>Deep study (text observation)</b></div>
<ol>
<li>Track the “therefore” logic in Amos: what sins lead to what judgments (e.g., Amos 2:6–16; 5:10–13)?</li>
<li>What do the visions in Amos 7–9 communicate about timing and inevitability?</li>
<li>What repeated phrases or themes appear (e.g., “seek… and live,” “woe,” “for three transgressions and for four”)? (Amos 1:3; 5:4; 6:1).</li>
<li>Compare Amos 5:21–24 with Isaiah 1:10–17—what overlaps and what unique emphases appear?</li>
<li>What is the function of the doxologies (Amos 4:13; 5:8–9; 9:5–6) in the argument of the book?</li>
</ol>

Teach us to seek You and live—turn us from false security and comfortable sin (Amos 5:4–6; 6:1). Make our worship true, our repentance sincere, and our lives marked by justice, mercy, and integrity (Amos 5:14–15, 24). Thank You that You do not leave judgment as the final word, but promise restoration through the Son of David, and mercy that reaches the nations (Amos 9:11–15; Acts 15:13–18). Keep us faithful to Your word while it may be heard, and shape us into a people who love what You love. Amen.</div>
