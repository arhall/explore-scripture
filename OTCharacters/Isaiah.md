<div><h1>Isaiah</h1></div>
<div><br></div>
<div><b><h1>Isaiah — Bible Character Study</h1></b></div>
<div><b><h2>1) Story Summary (chronological)</h2></b></div>
<div>Isaiah is the major prophet most closely associated with <b>Judah and Jerusalem</b> in the generations leading up to the Babylonian exile. His book opens by identifying him as <b>“the son of Amoz”</b> and locating his prophetic ministry across multiple Judean reigns: <b>Uzziah, Jotham, Ahaz, and Hezekiah</b> (Isa 1:1). His message is simultaneously <b>covenant lawsuit</b> (exposing Judah’s sin and announcing judgment) and <b>covenant hope</b> (promising purification, a faithful remnant, a coming Davidic king, the LORD’s Servant, and final renewal).</div>
<div>Isaiah’s ministry is framed by a defining encounter with the LORD’s holiness. In <b>the year King Uzziah died</b>, Isaiah sees the LORD “high and lifted up,” hears the seraphim cry “Holy, holy, holy,” confesses his own uncleanness, is cleansed, and is commissioned (“Here am I! Send me”) to preach to a people who will largely refuse to hear (Isa 6:1–13). In the days of <b>Ahaz</b>, Isaiah confronts royal fear and political unbelief, calling the king to quiet trust rather than alliances, and giving the sign of <b>Immanuel</b> (Isa 7:1–14; cf. 7:9). Isaiah’s own household becomes part of his prophetic sign-work: his son <b>Shear-jashub</b> (“A remnant shall return”) accompanies him, and later another son <b>Maher-shalal-hash-baz</b> (“Swift to the spoil, speedy to the prey”) is named as a living warning about imminent judgment (Isa 7:3; 8:1–4). Isaiah also refers to his wife as “the prophetess” (Isa 8:3).</div>
<div>In the days of <b>Hezekiah</b>, Isaiah appears in narrative episodes that overlap with Kings and Chronicles: he delivers the LORD’s word during the Assyrian crisis, announces God’s defense of Jerusalem, and the angel of the LORD strikes the Assyrian camp (Isa 36–37; cf. 2 Kgs 19). He also speaks to Hezekiah about his illness and recovery, including a confirming sign (Isa 38; cf. 2 Kgs 20:1–11). Finally, when Babylonian envoys visit, Isaiah confronts Hezekiah’s display of treasures and announces that <b>Babylon will one day carry Judah into exile</b> (Isa 39:1–7; cf. 2 Kgs 20:12–18). After this, Isaiah’s personal storyline fades from view; the book continues with sweeping oracles of comfort, the Servant’s work, the ingathering of the nations, and the promise of new creation (e.g., Isa 40–66).</div>
<div><b>Where Isaiah’s story begins and ends in Scripture:</b><br></div>
<ul>
<li>Begins: identification and early ministry in Judah (Isa 1:1), with his call narrated in detail (Isa 6:1–13).</li>
<li>Ends: last narrative appearance confronting Hezekiah after the Babylonian envoys (Isa 39:1–8).</li>
</ul>
<div><b>Note on uncertain traditions:</b> Later Jewish/Christian tradition associates Isaiah with martyrdom under Manasseh, but <b>Scripture itself does not narrate Isaiah’s death</b>. Stick to what is written (Isa 1:1; 39:8).</div>
<div><br></div>
<div><b><h2>2) What He’s Known For</h2></b></div>
<div>Isaiah is most known as the prophet of <b>the LORD’s holiness</b> and <b>the coming salvation of God</b>—judgment and hope held together under God’s covenant faithfulness. He is also the prophet most frequently echoed in the New Testament regarding <b>Messiah, the kingdom, the Servant, and the gospel going to the nations</b>.</div>
<div><b>Headline descriptors (3–7):</b><br></div>
<ul>
<li><b>“Holy, holy, holy” prophet</b> of the LORD’s majesty and purity (Isa 6:1–3).</li>
<li><b>Covenant prosecutor</b> exposing empty worship and social injustice (Isa 1:10–17; 5:8–23).</li>
<li><b>Court-facing counselor</b> confronting kings with the call to faith (Isa 7:1–9; 37:5–7).</li>
<li><b>Prophet of the Remnant</b>—judgment will come, yet God preserves a people (Isa 1:9; 6:13; 10:20–22).</li>
<li><b>Herald of the Davidic King</b> and the Spirit-anointed reign (Isa 9:6–7; 11:1–10).</li>
<li><b>Voice of comfort and new exodus hope</b> for God’s people (Isa 40:1–11; 43:1–7).</li>
<li><b>Witness to the Servant</b> who suffers to save (Isa 52:13–53:12).</li>
</ul>
<div><br></div>
<div><b><h2>3) Where He Appears in the Bible</h2></b></div>
<div><b><h2>Primary narrative locations (main storyline)</h2></b></div>
<ul>
<li><b>Isaiah 1 —</b> Title and setting of Isaiah’s ministry (Isa 1:1).</li>
<li><b>Isaiah 6 —</b> Call/commission; cleansing; message of hardening; hope of a holy seed (Isa 6:1–13).</li>
<li><b>Isaiah 7–8 —</b> Ahaz crisis; Immanuel sign; Shear-jashub; Maher-shalal-hash-baz; “God is with us” theme (Isa 7:1–14; 8:1–10).</li>
<li><b>Isaiah 20 —</b> Isaiah’s embodied sign against relying on Egypt/Cush (Isa 20:1–6).</li>
<li><b>Isaiah 36–39 —</b> Hezekiah narratives: Assyria, deliverance, Hezekiah’s illness, Babylon envoys, exile prophecy (Isa 36–39).</li>
</ul>
<div><b><h2>Secondary references (later mentions / parallel histories)</h2></b></div>
<ul>
<li><b>2 Kings 19–20 —</b> Isaiah’s counsel during Assyrian invasion; Hezekiah’s sickness; Babylon envoys (2 Kgs 19:1–37; 20:1–19).</li>
<li><b>2 Chronicles 26:22 —</b> Notes Isaiah wrote about Uzziah’s acts (2 Chr 26:22).</li>
<li><b>2 Chronicles 32:32 —</b> Mentions “the vision of Isaiah” concerning Hezekiah (2 Chr 32:32).</li>
<li><b>Ezra 1:1–4 —</b> Cyrus proclamation echoes Isaiah’s themes (cf. Isa 44:28; 45:1), though Isaiah is not named here.</li>
<li><b>New Testament usage (themes of Isaiah’s life/message):</b><br></li>
<ul>
<li><b>John 12:38–41 —</b> Cites Isaiah and says he saw Jesus’ glory (Isa 53:1; 6:10).</li>
<li><b>Acts 8:30–35 —</b> Isaiah 53 explained as fulfilled in Jesus.</li>
<li><b>Romans 10:16–21 —</b> Isaiah used to explain gospel reception and Gentile inclusion (Isa 53:1; 65:1–2).</li>
<li><b>Matthew 1:22–23 —</b> Isaiah 7:14 applied to Jesus’ birth.</li>
</ul>
</ul>
<div><br></div>
<div><b><h2>4) Vital Statistics</h2></b></div>
<ul>
<li><b>Historical setting (as Scripture indicates):</b><br></li>
<ul>
<li>Ministry spans the reigns of <b>Uzziah, Jotham, Ahaz, Hezekiah</b> (Isa 1:1).</li>
<li>Call vision dated to <b>“the year that King Uzziah died”</b> (Isa 6:1).</li>
<li>Major geopolitical pressure: <b>Assyria</b> (Isa 7; 36–37) and the looming rise of <b>Babylon</b> (Isa 39:6–7).</li>
</ul>
<li><b>Geography (strong associations):</b><br></li>
<ul>
<li><b>Judah/Jerusalem/Zion</b> as central focus (Isa 1:1; 2:1–4).</li>
<li>Broader nations in view: <b>Assyria, Egypt, Cush, Babylon</b> and many others (e.g., Isa 19; 20; 39).</li>
</ul>
<li><b>Family and relationships:</b><br></li>
<ul>
<li><b>Father:</b> Amoz (Isa 1:1).</li>
<li><b>Wife:</b> “the prophetess” (Isa 8:3).</li>
<li><b>Children:</b> Shear-jashub (Isa 7:3); Maher-shalal-hash-baz (Isa 8:3).</li>
<li><b>Key relationships:</b> Kings <b>Ahaz</b> (Isa 7) and <b>Hezekiah</b> (Isa 36–39); royal officials who consult him (Isa 37:2).</li>
</ul>
<li><b>Roles/offices:</b><br></li>
<ul>
<li><b>Prophet</b> (Isa 6:8–9).</li>
<li>Functions as a <b>public preacher</b>, <b>sign-actor</b> (Isa 20:2–4), and <b>royal counselor</b> (Isa 7:3–9; 37:5–7).</li>
</ul>
<li><b>Key turning points:</b><br></li>
<ul>
<li><b>Call &amp cleansing</b> in the throne-room vision (Isa 6:1–8).</li>
<li><b>Commission to a resistant audience</b> (Isa 6:9–13).</li>
<li><b>Ahaz crisis</b>: trust vs. fear; Immanuel sign (Isa 7:1–14).</li>
<li><b>Hezekiah crisis</b>: Assyria threatens; God delivers Jerusalem (Isa 36–37).</li>
<li><b>Babylon turning point</b>: prophecy of exile after Hezekiah’s misstep (Isa 39:5–7).</li>
</ul>
</ul>

<div><b><h2>6) Strengths, Weaknesses, and Heart-Level Patterns</h2></b></div>
<div><b><h2>Strengths (with textual evidence)</h2></b></div>
<ul>
<li><b>Awe-filled God-centeredness:</b> Isaiah’s ministry begins with the vision of God’s holiness and glory (Isa 6:1–3).</li>
<li><b>Humility and repentance:</b> He does not posture; he confesses personally (“Woe is me…”) (Isa 6:5).</li>
<li><b>Readiness to serve:</b> “Here am I! Send me.” (Isa 6:8).</li>
<li><b>Courage to confront power:</b> He speaks hard truth to kings and courts (Isa 7:3–9; 39:5–7).</li>
<li><b>Theological clarity about idols and trust:</b> He repeatedly exposes false refuges—alliances, wealth, ritual—calling for trust in the LORD (Isa 30:1–5, 15; 31:1).</li>
<li><b>Compassionate proclamation of comfort:</b> He speaks tenderly to God’s people with rich promises of redemption (Isa 40:1–2; 43:1–7).</li>
</ul>
<div><b><h2>Weaknesses / sins / blind spots (carefully, anchored to text)</h2></b></div>
<div>Scripture does <b>not</b> record a scandal or moral collapse in Isaiah’s life comparable to some other figures. What we <i>can</i> say from the narrative:</div>
<ul>
<li><b>He identifies as a sinner needing cleansing:</b> “I am a man of unclean lips… I dwell in the midst of a people of unclean lips” (Isa 6:5). This is not a “failure episode,” but it is an explicit admission of impurity before a holy God.</li>
<li><b>Costly obedience required public vulnerability:</b> The LORD commands Isaiah to walk barefoot and stripped as a sign (Isa 20:2–4). The text doesn’t present this as Isaiah’s weakness, but it highlights how prophetic ministry can include humiliating assignments.</li>
</ul>
<div><b><h2>Motives / fears / desires (careful inference)</h2></b></div>
<ul>
<li><b>Governing desire:</b> that God be seen as holy and trusted above all political strategies (Isa 6:3; 7:9; 30:15).</li>
<li><b>Governing fear:</b> not mainly fear of man, but fear of the LORD—reverent awe shaping bold speech (Isa 6:1–8).</li>
<li><b>Pastoral burden:</b> anguish over a people who refuse to hear, yet hope in God’s preserving “holy seed” (Isa 6:9–13; 1:9).</li>
</ul>
<div><b><h2>What changes (or doesn’t) over time?</h2></b></div>
<ul>
<li><b>Doesn’t change:</b> Isaiah’s message consistently holds <b>judgment and hope</b> together: sin must be addressed, yet God will save for his name’s sake (Isa 1:18–20; 6:9–13; 37:35; 40:1–2).</li>
<li><b>Develops in emphasis:</b> As the storyline moves toward exile (Isa 39:6–7), the book’s horizon expands to profound promises of <b>comfort, the Servant’s work, and global redemption</b> (Isa 40–55), culminating in final renewal (Isa 65:17–25; 66:22–23).</li>
</ul>
<div><br></div>
<div><b><h2>7) Victories and Challenges</h2></b></div>
<div><b><h2>Major “wins” (obedience, influence, deliverance)</h2></b></div>
<ul>
<li><b>Faithful acceptance of a hard calling</b> (Isa 6:8–13).</li>
<li><b>Calling rulers to faith instead of fear</b> (Isa 7:3–9; 30:15).</li>
<li><b>Instrumental prophetic leadership during national crisis</b> (Isa 37:21–35).</li>
<li><b>Witnessing and announcing God’s deliverance of Jerusalem</b> (Isa 37:36–38).</li>
<li><b>Clear-eyed confrontation of pride and future exile</b> (Isa 39:5–7)—truth spoken even when unwelcome.</li>
</ul>
<div><b><h2>Major challenges (opposition, suffering, rejection)</h2></b></div>
<ul>
<li><b>Commissioned to preach to people who will not listen</b> (Isa 6:9–10).</li>
<li><b>Bearing the shame of sign-acts</b> (Isa 20:2–4).</li>
<li><b>Living and preaching amid geopolitical terror</b> (Isa 36:1–22).</li>
<li><b>The grief of long-term covenant unfaithfulness in Judah</b> (Isa 1:2–4; 5:1–7).</li>
</ul>
<div><b>Cost of faithfulness:</b> Isaiah’s calling includes rejection (Isa 6:9–13) and personal humiliation (Isa 20:2–4), underscoring that prophetic ministry often means suffering for truth.</div>

<div><b><h2>9) Theology and Big Themes</h2></b></div>
<ul>
<li><b>God’s holiness and glory:</b> The LORD is morally perfect, utterly distinct, and worthy of fear and worship (Isa 6:1–3). Isaiah’s favorite title, “the Holy One of Israel,” captures this (e.g., Isa 1:4; 30:11–15).</li>
<li><b>Covenant faithfulness: judgment and mercy:</b> Judah’s sin brings real judgment (Isa 1:2–9; 5:1–7), yet God purifies and preserves (Isa 1:18–20; 6:13; 10:20–22).</li>
<li><b>The remnant and faithful seed:</b> God’s saving purposes continue through a preserved people (Isa 1:9; 6:13).</li>
<li><b>True worship and justice:</b> God rejects worship that masks oppression; holiness must show up in mercy and righteousness (Isa 1:10–17; 58:1–12).</li>
<li><b>Trust as the heart of obedience:</b> Isaiah relentlessly opposes salvation-by-strategy (alliances, horses, wealth) and calls for quiet confidence in God (Isa 7:9; 30:15; 31:1).</li>
<li><b>The Davidic kingdom hope:</b> God will raise up a righteous King from David’s line whose reign extends to the nations (Isa 9:6–7; 11:1–10).</li>
<li><b>The Servant as the mechanism of salvation:</b> The Servant bears sin, justifies many, and brings light to the nations (Isa 42:1–7; 49:5–6; 52:13–53:12).</li>
<li><b>Global mission and new creation:</b> Zion’s hope ultimately expands to worldwide worship and final renewal (Isa 2:2–4; 56:6–8; 65:17–25; 66:18–23).</li>
</ul>
<div><br></div>
<div><b><h2>10) How Isaiah Points Us to Christ (OT ↔ NT)</h2></b></div>
<div><b><h2>1) Isaiah as a prophet (office) and pattern</h2></b></div>
<div>Isaiah embodies the prophetic office: <b>God’s word to God’s people</b>, often resisted, delivered with personal cost (Isa 6:9–13; 20:2–4). Jesus stands in the line of prophets and is greater than them—God’s final Word (cf. Heb 1:1–2), and his ministry likewise meets rejection (cf. John 12:37–41 citing Isa 53:1 and Isa 6:10).</div>
<div><b><h2>2) Promises fulfilled in Jesus</h2></b></div>
<ul>
<li><b>Immanuel promise:</b> Isa 7:14 is explicitly connected to Jesus’ birth (Matt 1:22–23).</li>
<li><b>Messianic light and kingdom:</b> Isaiah’s hope for light and righteous rule (Isa 9:1–7; 11:1–10) is taken up in Jesus’ ministry and identity (cf. Matt 4:15–16; Luke 1:32–33; Rom 15:12).</li>
<li><b>Spirit-anointed proclamation:</b> Isa 61:1–2 is read by Jesus as fulfilled in himself (Luke 4:16–21).</li>
</ul>
<div><b><h2>3) The Suffering Servant and the gospel core</h2></b></div>
<div>Isaiah’s Servant songs culminate in substitutionary suffering and justification (Isa 52:13–53:12). The NT repeatedly treats this as directly fulfilled in Christ:</div>
<ul>
<li><b>Acts 8:30–35</b> interprets Isa 53 as Jesus.</li>
<li><b>1 Peter 2:22–25</b> echoes Isa 53’s language to explain atonement and healing.</li>
<li><b>John 12:38–41</b> ties Isaiah’s visions and words to Jesus’ glory and rejection.</li>
</ul>
<div><b><h2>4) New creation hope in Christ</h2></b></div>
<div>Isaiah’s “new heavens and new earth” (Isa 65:17; 66:22) provides a major OT horizon for final redemption, echoed in <b>Revelation 21:1–5</b>, where God’s dwelling with his people is realized fully in Christ.</div>
<div><br></div>
<div><b><h2>11) Lessons and Applications (8–15, Scripture-tethered)</h2></b></div>
<ol>
<li><b>Holiness begins with God, not us.</b> Let God’s majesty define reality (Isa 6:1–3).</li>
<li><b>True ministry flows from repentance and cleansing.</b> Confession precedes commissioning (Isa 6:5–8).</li>
<li><b>Faithfulness is obedience, not visible “success.”</b> Isaiah is sent to a hard audience (Isa 6:9–13).</li>
<li><b>Fear makes bad political theology.</b> “If you are not firm in faith…” (Isa 7:9).</li>
<li><b>Don’t replace God with strategies.</b> Quiet trust is the path of salvation (Isa 30:15; 31:1).</li>
<li><b>God hates empty religion.</b> Worship without justice is hypocrisy (Isa 1:10–17; 58:6–7).</li>
<li><b>God preserves a remnant by grace.</b> When collapse seems total, God keeps a holy seed (Isa 1:9; 6:13).</li>
<li><b>God rules nations and history.</b> Assyria’s threat is real, but not ultimate (Isa 37:21–36).</li>
<li><b>Prayer matters in crisis.</b> God responds to Hezekiah’s prayer and sends Isaiah’s answer (Isa 37:14–21; 38:5).</li>
<li><b>Pride invites future ruin.</b> Hezekiah’s display becomes a prophecy of exile (Isa 39:5–7).</li>
<li><b>The gospel is substitutionary at its center.</b> The Servant bears sin (Isa 53:4–6).</li>
<li><b>Hope is as big as new creation.</b> God’s endgame is renewal, not mere survival (Isa 65:17–25).</li>
</ol>
<div><b>Warnings to heed</b><br></div>
<ul>
<li>Hardened hearing is a terrifying judgment (Isa 6:9–10).</li>
<li>Religious performance can provoke God’s disgust when paired with injustice (Isa 1:11–15).</li>
<li>Trusting “Egypt” (whatever your functional savior is) is spiritual adultery (Isa 31:1).</li>
</ul>
<div><b>Examples to imitate</b><br></div>
<ul>
<li>Humble confession and readiness (Isa 6:5–8).</li>
<li>Courage to speak truth to power (Isa 7:3–9; 39:5–7).</li>
<li>Steady hope that God will keep his promises despite collapse (Isa 10:20–22; 40:1–2).</li>
</ul>
<div>Applications to key areas:</div>
<ul>
<li><b>Personal holiness:</b> begin with God’s holiness; repent concretely (Isa 6:1–7; 1:16–17).</li>
<li><b>Relationships:</b> pursue justice, mercy, and integrity, not spiritual show (Isa 1:17).</li>
<li><b>Prayer:</b> bring fear and crisis to God first (patterned in Isa 37; 38).</li>
<li><b>Suffering:</b> expect cost in obedience; God is still sovereign (Isa 20:2–4; 37:36).</li>
<li><b>Decision-making/leadership:</b> choose trust over panic-driven alliances (Isa 7:9; 30:15).</li>
</ul>

<div><b><h2>Personal reflection</h2></b></div>
<ol>
<li>Where do you most need the vision of God’s holiness to reshape your fears (Isa 6:1–3)?</li>
<li>What does Isaiah’s “unclean lips” confession suggest about sin’s reach (Isa 6:5)?</li>
<li>What “coal-to-the-lips” realities of grace does Scripture give believers today (cf. Isa 6:6–7)?</li>
<li>What is your functional “Egypt”—the thing you instinctively trust for security (Isa 31:1)?</li>
</ol>
<div><b><h2>Small group discussion</h2></b></div>
<ol>
<li>How do we distinguish true worship from religious performance (Isa 1:10–17; 58:1–7)?</li>
<li>How can Isaiah’s hard commission (Isa 6:9–13) help when ministry feels fruitless?</li>
<li>What does “If you are not firm in faith…” look like in real decisions (Isa 7:9)?</li>
<li>In the Assyrian crisis, what contrasts do you see between human threat and divine sovereignty (Isa 36–37)?</li>
</ol>
<div><b><h2>Deep study (text-observation)</h2></b></div>
<ol>
<li>Trace the movement in Isaiah 6: <b>vision → confession → cleansing → commission</b>. What does each step teach?</li>
<li>In Isaiah 7, what exactly is Ahaz afraid of, and what does Isaiah command him to do (Isa 7:2–9)?</li>
<li>How do Isaiah’s children’s names function as theology-in-miniature (Isa 7:3; 8:3–4)?</li>
<li>What is the logic of Isaiah 53: why does the Servant suffer, and what results follow (Isa 53:4–12)?</li>
<li>Compare Isaiah 1:16–20 with Isaiah 55:6–7. What stays constant about repentance and mercy?</li>
<li>What pictures of God’s tenderness appear in Isaiah 40:1–11 alongside his power in 40:12–31?</li>
</ol>
<div><b><h2>Christ-focused</h2></b></div>
<ol>
<li>Why do you think the NT returns so often to Isaiah to explain Jesus (e.g., Isa 53; Isa 61; John 12:38–41)?</li>
<li>How does the Servant’s substitution (Isa 53:5–6) shape assurance and humility?</li>
<li>What does “new heavens and new earth” do to your present hope and perseverance (Isa 65:17–25)?</li>
</ol>

Give us hearts that love justice and mercy, not empty religion (Isa 1:16–17). And fix our hope on your Servant who was pierced for our transgressions and bore our iniquity (Isa 53:5–6). Keep us as your remnant by grace, until the day you make all things new (Isa 10:20–22; 65:17). Amen.</div>
