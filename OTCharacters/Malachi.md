<div><h1>Malachi</h1></div>
<div><br></div>
<div><b><h2>Malachi — Bible Character Study</h2></b></div>
<div><b>Note on genre:</b> Malachi is presented almost entirely through his <b>prophetic messages</b> rather than biographical narrative. Scripture gives us <b>very little personal detail</b> about the man; what we know with confidence comes from the book that bears his name (Malachi 1:1).</div>
<div><br></div>
<div><b><h2>1) Story Summary</h2></b></div>
<div>Malachi appears as the named messenger of the LORD to the post-exilic community of Judah. The book opens with a superscription—“The oracle of the word of the LORD to Israel by Malachi” (Malachi 1:1)—and then unfolds as a series of disputations: God speaks, the people (and especially priests) respond skeptically, and the LORD answers with covenant charges and promised intervention (e.g., Malachi 1:2, 1:6; 2:17; 3:7–8, 3:13).</div>
<div>Chronologically, Malachi’s ministry fits <b>after the return from exile</b>, when the <b>temple services are active</b> (offerings, altar, priests functioning; Malachi 1:6–10; 2:1–9) yet the community has slid into <b>spiritual apathy</b> and <b>covenant compromise</b> (Malachi 1:13–14; 2:10–16; 3:8–10). The LORD confronts:</div>
<ul>
<li><b>Corrupt worship</b> and contempt for God’s name among priests and people (Malachi 1:6–14; 2:1–9).</li>
<li><b>Covenant unfaithfulness</b> in marriage, including treachery and divorce (Malachi 2:10–16).</li>
<li><b>Cynicism about God’s justice</b> (Malachi 2:17; 3:13–15).</li>
<li><b>Robbing God</b> through withholding tithes/offerings (Malachi 3:8–10).</li>
</ul>
<div>The book culminates with the promise that the LORD will <b>come in purifying judgment and saving healing</b>: a messenger will prepare the way, “the Lord…will suddenly come to his temple,” and a refining work will sift true worshipers (Malachi 3:1–5). The final words anticipate the “great and awesome day of the LORD” and the coming of “Elijah the prophet” to turn hearts (Malachi 4:1–6).</div>
<div><b>Where his story begins and ends:</b> Malachi’s recorded ministry begins at <b>Malachi 1:1</b> and ends at <b>Malachi 4:6</b>.</div>
<div><br></div>
<div><b><h2>2) What He’s Known For</h2></b></div>
<div>Malachi is most known for delivering God’s covenant lawsuit against post-exilic Judah and for closing the Old Testament with forward-looking promises that frame the coming of John the Baptist and Jesus.</div>
<div><b>Headline descriptors (3–7):</b><br></div>
<ul>
<li><b>Covenant prosecutor</b> exposing half-hearted worship and priestly corruption (Malachi 1:6–14; 2:1–9).</li>
<li><b>Voice against spiritual apathy</b> (“You say…”) calling for repentance (Malachi 3:7).</li>
<li><b>Defender of covenant faithfulness in marriage</b> (Malachi 2:14–16).</li>
<li><b>Announcer of the coming Messenger and the Lord’s temple visitation</b> (Malachi 3:1).</li>
<li><b>Herald of refining judgment and true worship</b> (Malachi 3:2–4).</li>
<li><b>Final OT prophet</b> pointing toward “Elijah” and the Day of the LORD (Malachi 4:1–6).</li>
<li><b>Anchor text for NT fulfillment</b> (e.g., Malachi 3:1; 4:5–6 echoed in the Gospels).</li>
</ul>
<div><br></div>
<div><b><h2>3) Where He Appears in the Bible</h2></b></div>
<div><b><h2>Primary narrative location</h2></b></div>
<ul>
<li><b>Malachi — 1:1–4:6</b> — The oracle(s) of the LORD delivered “by Malachi” (Malachi 1:1).</li>
</ul>
<div><b><h2>Secondary references (later mentions / NT echoes of Malachi’s message)</h2></b></div>
<div>Malachi (as a person) is not clearly mentioned elsewhere by name, but <b>Malachi’s prophecies</b> are repeatedly referenced:</div>
<ul>
<li><b>Romans — 9:13</b> — Paul cites Malachi 1:2–3 (“Jacob…Esau…”) in discussing God’s saving purpose.</li>
<li><b>Matthew — 11:10</b> — Jesus cites Malachi 3:1 regarding the messenger preparing the way (cf. Malachi 3:1).</li>
<li><b>Mark — 1:2</b> — Malachi 3:1 is cited in connection with John the Baptist and the coming of the Lord (cf. Malachi 3:1).</li>
<li><b>Luke — 1:17</b> — John’s ministry described in “the spirit and power of Elijah,” echoing Malachi 4:5–6.</li>
</ul>
<div>(These are references to <b>Malachi’s words</b>, not additional biography of Malachi.)</div>
<div><br></div>
<div><b><h2>4) Vital Statistics</h2></b></div>
<div><b><h2>Historical setting (as Scripture indicates)</h2></b></div>
<ul>
<li><b>Post-exilic Judah with functioning temple worship</b> (priests, altar, offerings ongoing; Malachi 1:6–10; 2:1–9).</li>
<li>The people are back in the land, but spiritual life is marked by <b>weariness</b>, <b>cynicism</b>, and <b>compromise</b> (Malachi 1:13; 2:17; 3:13–15).</li>
<li><b>Dating is debated</b>: Many place Malachi near the era reflected in Ezra–Nehemiah because of similar issues (intermarriage, priestly negligence, tithes; cf. Malachi 2:10–16; 3:8–10). However, <b>Malachi itself does not provide an explicit king/date marker</b> (Malachi 1:1), so this remains <b>inferred</b>, not certain.</li>
</ul>
<div><b><h2>Geography (strongly associated places)</h2></b></div>
<ul>
<li><b>Judah / Jerusalem / the temple</b> are central (Malachi 1:8, 1:10; 2:11; 3:1).</li>
<li>The message addresses “Israel” in covenant terms (Malachi 1:1), but the concrete setting is the restored worshiping community.</li>
</ul>
<div><b><h2>Family and relationships</h2></b></div>
<ul>
<li><b>Unknown.</b> Scripture gives no parents, spouse, children, or personal network for Malachi (Malachi 1:1).</li>
</ul>
<div><b><h2>Roles/offices</h2></b></div>
<ul>
<li><b>Prophet / messenger of the LORD</b> delivering an “oracle” (Malachi 1:1).</li>
<li>Functionally: <b>covenant enforcer</b> calling priests and people back to faithful worship and obedience (Malachi 2:1–9; 3:7).</li>
</ul>
<div><b><h2>Key turning points (call, crisis, covenant moments)</h2></b></div>
<div>Because Malachi’s biography is not narrated, the “turning points” are the <b>major covenant confrontations and promises</b> within the oracle:</div>
<ul>
<li>Confrontation of contempt for God’s name and corrupt sacrifices (Malachi 1:6–14).</li>
<li>Priestly warning and reminder of the covenant with Levi (Malachi 2:1–9).</li>
<li>Covenant faithlessness in marriage addressed (Malachi 2:10–16).</li>
<li>Promise of the messenger and the Lord’s purifying coming (Malachi 3:1–5).</li>
<li>Call to return and the tithes challenge (Malachi 3:7–12).</li>
<li>Separation of the righteous and the wicked; “book of remembrance” (Malachi 3:16–18).</li>
<li>Final Day-of-the-LORD warning and Elijah promise (Malachi 4:1–6).</li>
</ul>

<div><b><h2>6) Strengths, Weaknesses, and Heart-Level Patterns</h2></b></div>
<div><b><h2>Strengths (evidenced in passages)</h2></b></div>
<div>Because Malachi’s personal actions are not narrated, we observe strengths primarily through the faithful delivery of God’s message:</div>
<ul>
<li><b>Courage to confront corrupt worship and leadership</b> (Malachi 1:6–14; 2:1–9).</li>
<li><b>Clarity on God’s holiness and honor</b>—God’s “name” and “glory” matter (Malachi 1:6, 1:11; 2:2).</li>
<li><b>Covenant-mindedness</b>: repeated appeal to covenant categories—Fatherhood of God, covenant with Levi, marriage covenant, Torah remembrance (Malachi 2:10; 2:4–5; 2:14; 4:4).</li>
<li><b>Pastoral differentiation</b>: not all are condemned; the LORD recognizes those who fear Him (Malachi 3:16–18).</li>
</ul>
<div><b><h2>Weaknesses / sins / blind spots</h2></b></div>
<ul>
<li><b>None explicitly attributed to Malachi</b> in Scripture. The text does not narrate failures, doubts, or sins of the prophet (Malachi 1:1–4:6).</li>
</ul>
<div><b><h2>Motives / fears / desires (careful inference, anchored to text)</h2></b></div>
<ul>
<li><b>Desire for God’s honor in worship</b> (inferred from the centrality of God’s name being despised and restored; Malachi 1:6, 1:11; 2:2).</li>
<li><b>Desire for covenant integrity among God’s people</b> (inferred from sustained covenant charges and calls to return; Malachi 2:10–16; 3:7). These are best framed not as “Malachi’s inner psychology” (which Scripture doesn’t disclose), but as the <b>prophetic burden</b> the LORD places on him (Malachi 1:1).</li>
</ul>
<div><b><h2>What changes (or doesn’t) over time?</h2></b></div>
<ul>
<li>The <b>people’s posture</b> is confronted repeatedly: skepticism (“How…?”), weariness, and cynical speech (Malachi 1:2, 1:6–7; 2:17; 3:13–15).</li>
<li>The <b>LORD’s character</b> is steady: covenant faithfulness and moral constancy (Malachi 3:6).</li>
<li>The book moves toward an <b>eschatological resolution</b>: God will come, purify, judge, and vindicate the faithful (Malachi 3:1–5; 3:16–18; 4:1–3).</li>
</ul>
<div><br></div>
<div><b><h2>7) Victories and Challenges</h2></b></div>
<div><b><h2>Major “wins” (obedience, influence, faithful witness)</h2></b></div>
<ul>
<li><b>Faithful proclamation of the LORD’s covenant charges and promises</b> (Malachi 1:1; 3:1–5; 4:4–6).</li>
<li><b>Re-centering worship on God’s worth</b> (Malachi 1:11–14) and calling for honored priestly ministry (Malachi 2:7–9).</li>
<li><b>Strengthening the faithful remnant</b> by assuring them God sees and will vindicate them (Malachi 3:16–18; 4:2).</li>
</ul>
<div><b><h2>Major challenges (opposition, hardness, spiritual climate)</h2></b></div>
<ul>
<li><b>Entrenched apathy and contempt in worship</b> (“What a weariness this is”; Malachi 1:13).</li>
<li><b>Priestly corruption and leadership failure</b> that causes people to stumble (Malachi 2:8–9).</li>
<li><b>Community cynicism</b> that questions God’s justice and dismisses repentance as pointless (Malachi 2:17; 3:14–15).</li>
<li><b>Cost of faithful truth-telling</b> is implied by the repeated disputation style (“You say…”), suggesting resistance and rationalization (Malachi 1:2, 1:6–7; 3:7–8; 3:13).</li>
</ul>
<div><b>Cost of faithfulness / consequences of compromise:</b><br></div>
<ul>
<li>Compromise brings covenant curse and judgment (Malachi 2:2–3; 3:5; 4:1).</li>
<li>Faithfulness is remembered and rewarded (Malachi 3:16–18; 4:2).</li>
</ul>

<div><b><h2>8) Essential Verses and Key Cross-References (Organized by Themes)</h2></b></div>
<div><b><h2>God’s covenant love and election</h2></b></div>
<ul>
<li><b>Malachi 1:2–3</b> — “I have loved you…” (echoed in <b>Romans 9:13</b>).</li>
<li><b>Malachi 3:6</b> — “I the LORD do not change…”</li>
</ul>
<div><b>Cross-references:</b> Deuteronomy 7:6–8 (covenant love/election); James 1:17 (God’s unchanging goodness).</div>
<div><b><h2>Worship, the priesthood, and honoring God’s name</h2></b></div>
<ul>
<li><b>Malachi 1:6</b> — A son honors his father… yet God’s name is despised.</li>
<li><b>Malachi 1:10–11</b> — God’s name honored among nations; worship not to be treated lightly.</li>
<li><b>Malachi 2:7–9</b> — Priestly role: guard knowledge; corrupt priests cause stumbling.</li>
</ul>
<div><b>Cross-references:</b> Leviticus 22:17–25 (acceptable offerings); 1 Samuel 2:30 (honoring God); Isaiah 1:11–17 (empty worship).</div>
<div><b><h2>Covenant faithfulness in marriage and community</h2></b></div>
<ul>
<li><b>Malachi 2:14</b> — Marriage described as covenant; treachery condemned.</li>
<li><b>Malachi 2:15–16</b> — God seeks godly offspring; hatred of faithless divorce/treachery (carefully read in context).</li>
</ul>
<div><b>Cross-references:</b> Genesis 2:24 (marriage design); Deuteronomy 24:1–4 (divorce legislation backdrop); Matthew 19:4–6 (Jesus on creation intent).</div>
<div><b><h2>Repentance and covenant obedience (including stewardship)</h2></b></div>
<ul>
<li><b>Malachi 3:7</b> — “Return to me… and I will return to you.”</li>
<li><b>Malachi 3:8–10</b> — “Will man rob God?”; call to bring full tithes.</li>
<li><b>Malachi 3:11–12</b> — Promise of protection/blessing as covenant mercy.</li>
</ul>
<div><b>Cross-references:</b> Deuteronomy 28 (blessings/curses); Proverbs 3:9–10 (honor the LORD with wealth).</div>
<div><b><h2>The coming Messenger, the Lord’s coming, and purification</h2></b></div>
<ul>
<li><b>Malachi 3:1</b> — Messenger prepares the way; the Lord comes to his temple.</li>
<li><b>Malachi 3:2–3</b> — Refiner’s fire; purifier of worship.</li>
<li><b>Malachi 3:5</b> — Judgment against oppression and covenant-breaking sins.</li>
</ul>
<div><b>Cross-references / NT echoes:</b><br></div>
<ul>
<li>Mark 1:2; Matthew 11:10 (Malachi 3:1 applied to John preparing for Jesus).</li>
<li>Isaiah 40:3 (paired in Gospel citations with Malachi 3:1).</li>
<li>Luke 3:4–6 (preparation theme).</li>
</ul>
<div><b><h2>The Day of the LORD: judgment and healing</h2></b></div>
<ul>
<li><b>Malachi 4:1</b> — The day burns like an oven; arrogant consumed.</li>
<li><b>Malachi 4:2</b> — “Sun of righteousness” rising with healing for those who fear the LORD.</li>
<li><b>Malachi 4:5–6</b> — Elijah promise; turning hearts to avert covenant curse.</li>
</ul>
<div><b>Cross-references / NT echoes:</b><br></div>
<ul>
<li>Luke 1:17 (Elijah-like ministry applied to John).</li>
<li>Joel 2:31 (day of the LORD theme).</li>
<li>2 Thessalonians 1:7–10 (final judgment/vindication patterns).</li>
</ul>
<div><br></div>
<div><b><h2>9) Theology and Big Themes</h2></b></div>
<div><b><h2>God’s character revealed</h2></b></div>
<ul>
<li><b>Covenant love and faithfulness:</b> God begins with love and election (Malachi 1:2–5).</li>
<li><b>Holiness and honor:</b> God’s name must be revered; casual worship is covenant contempt (Malachi 1:6, 1:14).</li>
<li><b>Immutability / steadfastness:</b> “I the LORD do not change” grounds hope and warning (Malachi 3:6).</li>
<li><b>Justice:</b> God hears the complaint about justice and promises decisive action (Malachi 2:17; 3:5; 4:1–3).</li>
</ul>
<div><b><h2>Covenant, worship, and priestly leadership</h2></b></div>
<ul>
<li>Worship is covenantal—offerings and priestly teaching must align with God’s holiness (Malachi 1:7–10; 2:7–9).</li>
<li>Leaders are accountable; corrupt leadership multiplies stumbling (Malachi 2:8).</li>
</ul>
<div><b><h2>Human nature: apathy, self-justification, cynicism</h2></b></div>
<ul>
<li>The repeated “<b>You say…</b>” exposes our tendency to rationalize sin and minimize God’s claims (Malachi 1:2, 1:6–7; 2:17; 3:7–8; 3:13).</li>
<li>People can be outwardly religious yet inwardly weary and transactional (Malachi 1:13; 3:14).</li>
</ul>
<div><b><h2>Remnant hope and eschatological resolution</h2></b></div>
<ul>
<li>God differentiates between those who fear Him and the arrogant (Malachi 3:16–18; 4:1–2).</li>
<li>The book leans forward to God’s climactic intervention: purification, judgment, and healing (Malachi 3:1–4; 4:2).</li>
</ul>
<div><br></div>
<div><b><h2>10) How Malachi Points Us to Christ</h2></b></div>
<div><b><h2>Promises and offices fulfilled</h2></b></div>
<ul>
<li><b>The Messenger who prepares the way</b> (Malachi 3:1) is applied in the Gospels to <b>John the Baptist</b> preparing for Jesus (Mark 1:2; Matthew 11:10; cf. Malachi 3:1).</li>
<li><b>“The Lord…will suddenly come to his temple”</b> (Malachi 3:1) aligns with Jesus’ coming and His temple-focused ministry (e.g., His zeal and purifying actions in the temple resonate with Malachi’s purification theme; cf. Malachi 3:2–3).</li>
</ul>
<div><b><h2>Elijah motif and gospel preparation</h2></b></div>
<ul>
<li>Malachi ends with the promise of <b>Elijah</b> coming to turn hearts (Malachi 4:5–6). Luke explicitly connects this to John’s ministry “in the spirit and power of Elijah” (Luke 1:17), which prepares people for the Lord.</li>
</ul>
<div><b><h2>Refining and judgment themes completed in Christ</h2></b></div>
<ul>
<li>Malachi’s “refiner” imagery (Malachi 3:2–3) anticipates God’s purifying work that Christ brings—both through His searching holiness and through the new-covenant cleansing and formation of a worshiping people (cf. Malachi 3:3–4).</li>
<li>The Day of the LORD contrast—judgment for the arrogant, healing for those who fear God (Malachi 4:1–2)—finds ultimate clarity in the NT’s final reckoning and salvation (cf. themes echoed across the NT; e.g., final judgment/vindication patterns consistent with 2 Thessalonians 1:7–10).</li>
</ul>
<div><i>(Typology caution: Malachi is not portrayed as a type of Christ in a narrative-office sense like king/priest patterns; rather, </i><b><i>his message</i></b><i> is a canonical bridge to Christ’s advent and the forerunner.)</i><br></div>
<div><br></div>
<div><b><h2>11) Lessons and Applications</h2></b></div>
<ol>
<li><b>God’s love is real even when you can’t “feel” it</b>—measure by covenant truth, not mood (Malachi 1:2–5).</li>
<li><b>Half-hearted worship is a heart diagnosis</b> (Malachi 1:7–8, 1:13–14).</li>
<li><b>Leaders are accountable for doctrinal and moral integrity</b> (Malachi 2:7–9; 2:1–2).</li>
<li><b>Covenant faithfulness belongs in the home as much as the temple</b> (Malachi 2:14–16).</li>
<li><b>Cynicism is not “honest doubt” when it accuses God’s justice while excusing our sin</b> (Malachi 2:17; 3:13–15).</li>
<li><b>Repentance is returning—concrete, not vague</b> (Malachi 3:7).</li>
<li><b>Stewardship is spiritual</b>; withholding from God is framed as covenant robbery (Malachi 3:8–10).</li>
<li><b>God sees the faithful remnant</b> even when culture rewards arrogance (Malachi 3:16–18).</li>
<li><b>God’s coming is both purifying and judging</b>—good news for the repentant, terror for the proud (Malachi 3:2–5; 4:1–2).</li>
<li><b>Remember the Torah</b>: spiritual renewal is tied to God’s revealed word (Malachi 4:4).</li>
<li><b>Preparation matters</b>: God sends a forerunner before decisive redemptive action (Malachi 3:1; 4:5–6).</li>
<li><b>Hope includes healing</b>: reverent fear of God isn’t grim—it leads to joy and restoration (Malachi 4:2–3).</li>
</ol>
<div><b><h2>Warnings to heed</h2></b></div>
<ul>
<li>Treating worship as <b>inconvenient</b> or <b>transactional</b> (Malachi 1:13–14; 3:14).</li>
<li>Leadership that causes others to stumble (Malachi 2:8).</li>
<li>Hardening into “God doesn’t notice / it doesn’t matter” speech (Malachi 3:13–15).</li>
</ul>
<div><b><h2>Examples to imitate</h2></b></div>
<ul>
<li><b>Those who feared the LORD and spoke with one another</b>—reverent community in a cynical age (Malachi 3:16).</li>
<li><b>Returning</b> when confronted (Malachi 3:7).</li>
</ul>

<div><b><h2>12) Discussion and Reflection Questions (10–20)</h2></b></div>
<div><b><h2>Personal reflection</h2></b></div>
<ol>
<li>Where do you hear yourself saying “How have we…?”—defensiveness that resists God’s diagnosis (Malachi 1:2; 3:7–8)?</li>
<li>What does your “offering” look like—firstfruits or leftovers (Malachi 1:8, 1:13–14)?</li>
<li>In what ways have you slipped into cynicism about God’s justice or goodness (Malachi 2:17; 3:14–15)?</li>
<li>What would “return to me” require specifically in your life right now (Malachi 3:7)?</li>
<li>Do you live as if God keeps a “book of remembrance,” or as if faithfulness is unseen (Malachi 3:16–18)?</li>
</ol>
<div><b><h2>Small group discussion</h2></b></div>
<ol>
<li>Why does Malachi treat worship problems as contempt for God’s “name” (Malachi 1:6)?</li>
<li>What makes corrupt leadership so destructive in Malachi’s logic (Malachi 2:7–9)?</li>
<li>How do Malachi’s marriage warnings connect to covenant faithfulness more broadly (Malachi 2:10–16)?</li>
<li>What’s the difference between lamenting injustice and accusing God of being unjust (Malachi 2:17)?</li>
<li>How do Malachi 3:1–5 and 4:1–2 hold together judgment and hope?</li>
</ol>
<div><b><h2>Deep study (text-observation)</h2></b></div>
<ol>
<li>Trace the repeated phrase “You say…”: what patterns of self-justification emerge (Malachi 1:2, 1:6–7; 2:17; 3:7–8; 3:13)?</li>
<li>List the sins named in Malachi 3:5. What do they have in common socially and spiritually?</li>
<li>In Malachi 3:16–18, what marks the faithful? What does God promise?</li>
<li>Observe the imagery in Malachi 3:2–3 (fire/soap). What is being purified, and why?</li>
<li>How does Malachi 4:4 connect Torah remembrance with end-times hope?</li>
</ol>
<div><b><h2>Christ-connection study</h2></b></div>
<ol>
<li>How do the Gospels use Malachi 3:1, and what does that imply about Jesus’ identity and mission (Mark 1:2; Matthew 11:10; cf. Malachi 3:1)?</li>
<li>How does Luke 1:17 interpret Malachi 4:5–6, and what “heart turning” looks like in Luke’s narrative (Luke 1:17; cf. Malachi 4:5–6)?</li>
</ol>

Forgive us for despising your name in subtle ways (Malachi 1:6) and for speaking cynically about your justice (Malachi 2:17; 3:14). Turn our hearts back to you—teach us to return in real repentance (Malachi 3:7), to honor you in worship, integrity, and faithfulness (Malachi 2:14–16). Thank you that you see those who fear you (Malachi 3:16), and that you promise healing to those who revere your name (Malachi 4:2). Prepare us by your word (Malachi 4:4), and make us ready for the Lord who comes to purify his people (Malachi 3:1–3). In Jesus’ name, amen.</div>
