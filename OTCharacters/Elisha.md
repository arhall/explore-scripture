<div><h1>Elisha</h1></div>
<div><br></div>
<div><b><h1>Elisha — Bible Character Study (Scripture-First)</h1></b></div>
<div><b><h2>1) Story Summary (chronological)</h2></b></div>
<div>Elisha enters the biblical narrative near the end of Elijah’s ministry. God commands Elijah to anoint (appoint) Elisha as prophet in his place (1 Kgs 19:16). Elijah finds Elisha plowing; Elisha responds decisively—leaving his former life and following Elijah as his attendant (1 Kgs 19:19–21).</div>
<div>Elisha’s public ministry begins as Elijah is taken up to heaven. Elisha asks for a “double portion” of Elijah’s spirit (the inheritance portion of a firstborn—signaling succession), receives it, and immediately demonstrates prophetic authority as he parts the Jordan and is recognized by the sons of the prophets (2 Kgs 2:1–15). From there, Elisha serves as a central prophetic witness in the Northern Kingdom (Israel), repeatedly confronting idolatry, advising kings, protecting prophetic communities, and displaying the living God’s power through signs of judgment and mercy.</div>
<div>Across 2 Kings 2–13, Elisha’s ministry includes: purifying waters (2 Kgs 2:19–22), judgment on mocking contempt (2 Kgs 2:23–25), provision for widows and prophetic students (2 Kgs 4:1–7; 6:1–7), miraculous feeding (2 Kgs 4:42–44), raising a child (2 Kgs 4:32–37), revealing enemy plans and protecting Israel (2 Kgs 6:8–23), prophecy during famine and siege (2 Kgs 6:24–7:20), the healing of Naaman the Syrian and the exposure of Gehazi’s greed (2 Kgs 5:1–27), and involvement in major political transitions (anointing Jehu through a messenger; 2 Kgs 9:1–13; plus revelations about Hazael; 2 Kgs 8:7–15).</div>
<div>Elisha’s story ends with his death and burial, yet Scripture records one final sign: a dead man is revived upon touching Elisha’s bones—an unusual testimony to God’s life-giving power even after the prophet’s death (2 Kgs 13:20–21). His ministry is later referenced by Jesus as an example of God’s sovereign mercy extending beyond Israel (Luke 4:27).</div>
<div><b>Where his story begins:</b> 1 Kings 19:16–21 (his call and departure to follow Elijah).<b>Where his story ends:</b> 2 Kings 13:20–21 (death, burial, posthumous sign).</div>
<div><br></div>
<div><b><h2>2) What He’s Known For</h2></b></div>
<div>Elisha is most known as <b>Elijah’s successor</b>—a prophet through whom God repeatedly displays covenant faithfulness, power over creation, and compassion for the needy, while also executing sobering judgments.</div>
<div><b>Headline descriptors</b><br></div>
<ul>
<li><b>Successor-prophet</b> who inherits Elijah’s mantle and ministry (2 Kgs 2:9–15)</li>
<li><b>Miracle-bearing prophet</b>: provision, healing, resurrection, deliverance (2 Kgs 4–6)</li>
<li><b>Covenant witness in Israel</b> during moral/spiritual decline (2 Kgs 3; 6–8)</li>
<li><b>Instrument of mercy to outsiders</b> (Naaman) and Israel’s remnant (2 Kgs 5:1–19)</li>
<li><b>Fearless truth-teller to kings</b>—guidance and rebuke (2 Kgs 3:13–19; 6:8–12; 7:1–2)</li>
<li><b>Teacher and protector</b> of prophetic communities (“sons of the prophets”) (2 Kgs 4:38–41; 6:1–7)</li>
<li><b>Sign of God’s living power</b> even in death (2 Kgs 13:20–21)</li>
</ul>
<div><br></div>

</ul>
<div><b><h2>Secondary references (later mentions)</h2></b></div>
<ul>
<li><b>Luke — 4:27</b> — Jesus cites Naaman’s cleansing as an example of God’s sovereign mercy beyond Israel</li>
<li><b>(Implicit thematic echoes)</b> Prophetic ministry patterns recur across Scripture (e.g., prophet as covenant prosecutor: Deut 18:15–22; 1 Kgs 18; 2 Kgs 17:13), though Elisha is not frequently named outside Kings and Luke.</li>
</ul>

<div><b><h2>4) Vital Statistics</h2></b></div>
<div><b>Historical setting (Scripture-based, relative)</b><br></div>
<ul>
<li>Elisha is called during Elijah’s ministry (1 Kgs 19:16–21) and ministers through multiple kings of Israel, notably <b>Joram/Jehoram</b> (2 Kgs 3), <b>Jehu</b> (2 Kgs 9–10 context), <b>Jehoahaz</b> (2 Kgs 13:1), and <b>Joash/Jehoash</b> (2 Kgs 13:10–19). His ministry spans a significant portion of Israel’s divided-kingdom era (2 Kgs 2–13).</li>
</ul>
<div><b>Geography (strongly associated)</b><br></div>
<ul>
<li><b>Abel-meholah</b> (home region) (1 Kgs 19:16)</li>
<li><b>Jordan River</b> (succession sign) (2 Kgs 2:14)</li>
<li><b>Gilgal, Bethel, Jericho</b> (prophetic circuits) (2 Kgs 2:1–5)</li>
<li><b>Samaria</b> (capital; siege narrative) (2 Kgs 6:24; 7:1)</li>
<li><b>Shunem</b> (Shunammite household) (2 Kgs 4:8)</li>
<li><b>Carmel</b> (frequent travel base) (2 Kgs 4:25)</li>
<li><b>Dothan</b> (surrounded by Arameans; angelic protection) (2 Kgs 6:13–17)</li>
<li><b>Damascus</b> (Hazael episode) (2 Kgs 8:7–15)</li>
</ul>
<div><b>Family and relationships</b><br></div>
<ul>
<li><b>Father:</b> Shaphat (1 Kgs 19:16, 19)</li>
<li><b>Mentor:</b> Elijah (1 Kgs 19:19–21; 2 Kgs 2:1–15)</li>
<li><b>Servant/assistant:</b> Gehazi (2 Kgs 4:12; 5:20–27)</li>
<li><b>Allies:</b> “sons of the prophets” (2 Kgs 2:3, 5, 7; 4:38; 6:1)</li>
<li><b>Key beneficiaries:</b> widow (2 Kgs 4:1–7), Shunammite family (2 Kgs 4:8–37), Naaman (2 Kgs 5:1–19)</li>
<li><b>Key opponents:</b> Aramean forces and hostile skeptics (2 Kgs 6–7); contemptuous mockers (2 Kgs 2:23–25)</li>
</ul>
<div><b>Roles/offices</b><br></div>
<ul>
<li><b>Prophet of Yahweh</b> (1 Kgs 19:16; 2 Kgs 2:15)</li>
<li><b>Prophetic counselor to rulers</b> (2 Kgs 3:13–19; 6:8–12; 7:1–2; 13:14–19)</li>
<li><b>Leader among prophetic communities</b> (2 Kgs 4:38–41; 6:1–7)</li>
</ul>
<div><b>Key turning points</b><br></div>
<ul>
<li><b>Call and decisive break with former life</b> (1 Kgs 19:19–21)</li>
<li><b>Succession: mantle, double portion, Jordan crossing</b> (2 Kgs 2:9–15)</li>
<li><b>Public recognition as prophet</b> (2 Kgs 2:15)</li>
<li><b>Major demonstrations of mercy and judgment</b> (2 Kgs 2:19–25; 5:1–27; 6–7)</li>
<li><b>Final prophecy and death</b> (2 Kgs 13:14–21)</li>
</ul>

<div><b><h2>6) Strengths, Weaknesses, and Heart-Level Patterns</h2></b></div>
<div><b><h2>Strengths (with textual evidence)</h2></b></div>
<ul>
<li><b>Decisive obedience to calling:</b> leaves livelihood and follows immediately (1 Kgs 19:20–21).</li>
<li><b>Desire for spiritual inheritance and capacity:</b> asks for a “double portion” (2 Kgs 2:9).</li>
<li><b>Confidence in God’s unseen protection:</b> “Those who are with us are more than those who are with them” (2 Kgs 6:16–17).</li>
<li><b>Compassion for the vulnerable:</b> widows and the poor are repeatedly helped (2 Kgs 4:1–7; 4:38–41).</li>
<li><b>Fearless truthfulness before power:</b> confronts kings and skeptics with God’s word (2 Kgs 3:13–19; 7:1–2).</li>
<li><b>Integrity about grace:</b> refuses payment for Naaman’s healing (2 Kgs 5:16).</li>
<li><b>Pastoral presence:</b> persistent involvement with prophetic communities and families (2 Kgs 4:8–10; 6:1–7).</li>
</ul>
<div><b><h2>Weaknesses / sins / blind spots (careful, text-anchored)</h2></b></div>
<div>Scripture records <b>no explicit moral scandal</b> attributed to Elisha (unlike many kings). Still, there are <b>sobering episodes</b> that can tempt readers to misread the text; we must stay with what is written.</div>
<ul>
<li><b>Severe judgments associated with his prophetic authority</b> (not necessarily “personal vindictiveness” stated as sin):</li>
<ul>
<li>The bear incident follows public contempt (“Go up, you baldhead!”) and is narrated as a judgment connected to the prophet’s office (2 Kgs 2:23–25). The text does not call Elisha sinful here, but it does present the fearsome seriousness of treating God’s prophet with contempt.</li>
<li>Gehazi’s judgment is pronounced by Elisha after deceit and greed (2 Kgs 5:20–27). Again, the text frames this as covenantal/prophetic judgment, not as Elisha’s personal profiteering (contrast Gehazi).</li>
</ul>
</ul>
<div><b><h2>Motives / fears / desires (inferred cautiously)</h2></b></div>
<ul>
<li><b>Desire:</b> to faithfully carry forward Elijah’s prophetic work—hence the “double portion” request (2 Kgs 2:9).</li>
<li><b>Desire:</b> that people trust God’s word rather than panic at visible threats (2 Kgs 6:16–17; 7:1–2).</li>
<li><b>Concern:</b> guarding the holiness of God’s name and prophetic office amid widespread apostasy (implied across confrontations: 2 Kgs 3:13–14; 5:15–16; 7:1–2).</li>
</ul>
<div><b><h2>What changes (or doesn’t) over time?</h2></b></div>
<ul>
<li><b>Changes:</b> Elisha shifts from <b>disciple/attendant</b> (1 Kgs 19:21) to <b>recognized national prophet</b> influencing kings and international affairs (2 Kgs 3; 6–8; 13).</li>
<li><b>Doesn’t change:</b> the consistent pattern that <b>God’s word governs reality</b>—deliverance, judgment, provision, and revelation hinge on what the Lord has spoken (2 Kgs 7:1, 16–20; 6:12).</li>
</ul>
<div><br></div>
<div><b><h2>7) Victories and Challenges</h2></b></div>
<div><b><h2>Major “wins” (deliverances, obedience, influence)</h2></b></div>
<ul>
<li><b>Succession secured; prophetic continuity preserved</b> (2 Kgs 2:9–15).</li>
<li><b>Life-giving mercy to communities</b> (healed waters: 2 Kgs 2:19–22; multiplied oil: 2 Kgs 4:1–7).</li>
<li><b>Power over death displayed</b> (resurrection of Shunammite’s son: 2 Kgs 4:32–37).</li>
<li><b>Grace to a Gentile commander</b> (Naaman cleansed; confession of Yahweh: 2 Kgs 5:14–15).</li>
<li><b>Protection from enemy aggression and fear</b> (angelic armies: 2 Kgs 6:15–17; merciful handling of captives: 2 Kgs 6:21–23).</li>
<li><b>Truth upheld amid famine and disbelief</b> (deliverance prophecy fulfilled: 2 Kgs 7:1, 16–20).</li>
<li><b>Final strengthening of Israel’s hope</b> through last counsel (2 Kgs 13:14–19).</li>
</ul>
<div><b><h2>Major challenges (opposition, suffering, compromise around him)</h2></b></div>
<ul>
<li><b>Contempt and rejection culture:</b> mocked and scorned (2 Kgs 2:23–24).</li>
<li><b>Serving amid compromised leadership:</b> kings seek help without true covenant faithfulness (2 Kgs 3:13; 6:31).</li>
<li><b>National catastrophe context:</b> famine, siege, cannibalism horror—spiritual and social collapse (2 Kgs 6:24–29).</li>
<li><b>Corruption near ministry:</b> Gehazi’s greed attempts to monetize grace (2 Kgs 5:20–27).</li>
<li><b>Physical decline and death:</b> even a great prophet is mortal (2 Kgs 13:14, 20).</li>
</ul>
<div><b>Cost of faithfulness:</b> Elisha stands as God’s representative in a hostile, unstable environment; the narrative repeatedly shows that speaking God’s word invites conflict, misunderstanding, and proximity to suffering (2 Kgs 6:31–33; 7:2).</div>

<div><b><h2>9) Theology and Big Themes</h2></b></div>
<div><b><h2>God’s character revealed</h2></b></div>
<ul>
<li><b>Sovereign power over nature, nations, and life/death:</b> Jordan parted (2 Kgs 2:14), enemy plans revealed (2 Kgs 6:8–12), death reversed (2 Kgs 4:32–37; 13:21).</li>
<li><b>Mercy and compassion:</b> widows, hungry communities, and even an enemy commander receive help (2 Kgs 4:1–7; 4:42–44; 5:14–15).</li>
<li><b>Holiness and justice:</b> contempt, deceit, and covenant-breaking do not go unanswered (2 Kgs 2:23–25; 5:20–27; 7:2, 17–20).</li>
<li><b>Faithful word-keeping:</b> prophecy and fulfillment are repeatedly paired (2 Kgs 7:1, 16–20; 8:10–15).</li>
</ul>
<div><b><h2>Covenant, kingdom, and worship</h2></b></div>
<ul>
<li>Elisha functions as a <b>covenant witness</b> in Israel’s idolatrous monarchy—God preserves a prophetic voice even when the throne is unstable (2 Kgs 3; 6–8).</li>
<li>His miracles are not “party tricks” but <b>signs</b> that Yahweh alone is God and that covenant life depends on hearing His word (2 Kgs 5:15–16; 7:1).</li>
</ul>
<div><b><h2>Human nature: sin, grace, repentance, perseverance</h2></b></div>
<ul>
<li><b>Pride vs humble faith:</b> Naaman must submit to God’s simple command (2 Kgs 5:10–14).</li>
<li><b>Greed corrupts ministry:</b> Gehazi shows how proximity to holy things can coexist with a covetous heart (2 Kgs 5:20–27).</li>
<li><b>Fear is defeated by spiritual sight:</b> the servant’s panic yields to confidence when God opens his eyes (2 Kgs 6:15–17).</li>
<li><b>Unbelief can be sophisticated and fatal:</b> the officer doubts God’s promise and suffers the consequence (2 Kgs 7:2, 17–20).</li>
</ul>

<div><b><h2>10) How Elisha Points Us to Christ (careful, Scripture-linked)</h2></b></div>
<div><b><h2>Prophet office and the Word of the Lord</h2></b></div>
<div>Elisha stands in the stream of God’s prophets who speak God’s word with authority (2 Kgs 7:1). This anticipates Christ, who is more than a prophet yet truly prophetic—He speaks God’s words as the Son (John 12:49; Heb 1:1–2).</div>
<div><b><h2>Patterns of mercy that echo Jesus’ ministry</h2></b></div>
<div>Several Elisha episodes form <b>credible typological patterns</b> (repeated themes, not forced allegory):</div>
<ul>
<li><b>Feeding miracle:</b> Elisha feeds many with limited bread (2 Kgs 4:42–44), echoing Jesus feeding the multitudes (Matt 14:13–21).</li>
<li><b>Cleansing of a “leper”:</b> Naaman is cleansed by God’s command received in faith (2 Kgs 5:14), echoing Jesus cleansing lepers (Mark 1:40–42). Jesus explicitly references Naaman to teach sovereign grace (Luke 4:27).</li>
<li><b>Raising the dead:</b> Elisha raises the Shunammite’s son (2 Kgs 4:32–37), echoing Christ’s resurrection power (Luke 7:11–15; John 11:43–44).</li>
<li><b>Opening eyes to unseen reality:</b> Elisha’s servant’s eyes are opened (2 Kgs 6:17), anticipating the broader biblical theme of spiritual illumination fulfilled in Christ (2 Cor 4:6).</li>
</ul>
<div><b><h2>Gospel connections</h2></b></div>
<div>Elisha’s ministry repeatedly shows that <b>salvation help comes from the Lord</b>, not from human power, money, or status:</div>
<ul>
<li>Naaman cannot buy grace (2 Kgs 5:15–16) and must receive cleansing by humble trust (2 Kgs 5:14). This coheres with the NT pattern: grace received by faith, not purchased (Eph 2:8–9).</li>
<li>God’s mercy extending beyond Israel (Naaman; Luke 4:27) foreshadows the worldwide scope of the gospel (Acts 1:8).</li>
</ul>

<div><b><h2>11) Lessons and Applications (tied to Scripture)</h2></b></div>
<div><b><h2>Examples to imitate</h2></b></div>
<ol>
<li><b>Respond to God’s call decisively</b> (1 Kgs 19:20–21).</li>
<li><b>Seek spiritual capacity for faithfulness, not personal glory</b> (2 Kgs 2:9).</li>
<li><b>Anchor courage in God’s unseen realities</b> (2 Kgs 6:16–17).</li>
<li><b>Practice compassionate, practical mercy</b> toward the vulnerable (2 Kgs 4:1–7).</li>
<li><b>Honor integrity in ministry—don’t monetize grace</b> (2 Kgs 5:16).</li>
<li><b>Hold tightly to God’s promises when circumstances scream the opposite</b> (2 Kgs 7:1).</li>
<li><b>Cultivate humble obedience—God’s commands may offend pride</b> (2 Kgs 5:10–14).</li>
<li><b>Lead others toward faith, not panic</b> (2 Kgs 6:16).</li>
<li><b>Trust God’s word over human skepticism</b> (2 Kgs 7:2, 16–20).</li>
</ol>
<div><b><h2>Warnings to heed</h2></b></div>
<ol>
<li><b>Don’t treat holy things with contempt</b> (2 Kgs 2:23–25).</li>
<li><b>Beware greed near sacred work</b>—Gehazi is a perpetual warning (2 Kgs 5:20–27).</li>
<li><b>Unbelief can harden into mockery and end in loss</b> (2 Kgs 7:2, 17–20).</li>
</ol>
<div><b><h2>Application domains</h2></b></div>
<ul>
<li><b>Personal holiness:</b> resist covetousness; honor God’s name (2 Kgs 5:20–27; 2 Kgs 2:23–25).</li>
<li><b>Relationships:</b> practice mercy and hospitality like the Shunammite (2 Kgs 4:8–10).</li>
<li><b>Prayer:</b> ask God for opened eyes and fearless faith (2 Kgs 6:17).</li>
<li><b>Suffering:</b> cling to God’s word in famine-like seasons (2 Kgs 7:1).</li>
<li><b>Decision-making/leadership:</b> lead from God’s promises, not public pressure or fear (2 Kgs 6:16–17; 7:1).</li>
</ul>
