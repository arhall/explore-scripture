<div><h1>Abraham</h1></div>
<div><br></div>
<div><b><h1>Abraham — Robust Bible Character Study (Scripture-anchored)</h1></b></div>
<div><b><h2>1) Story Summary (chronological)</h2></b></div>
<div>Abraham (first called <i>Abram</i>) enters the biblical story as a man living among the peoples of Mesopotamia, descended from Shem through Terah (Gen 11:10–27). His narrative begins when God calls him to leave his country and kindred, promising to make of him a great nation, to bless him, and to bless the nations through him (Gen 12:1–3). Abram obeys, traveling into Canaan with Sarai (later <i>Sarah</i>) and Lot (Gen 12:4–5).</div>
<div>His life then unfolds as a pilgrim-journey of faith marked by altars, promises, fear, failures, and covenant renewal. In Canaan he experiences famine and goes down to Egypt, where fear leads him to misrepresent Sarai (Gen 12:10–20). God nonetheless preserves him and continues to reaffirm the promise of offspring and land (Gen 13:14–18). Abram’s faith and leadership appear in rescuing Lot from regional kings (Gen 14:13–16), and in his priestly encounter with Melchizedek, to whom Abram gives a tenth (Gen 14:18–20).</div>
<div>God formally binds Himself to Abram in covenant, promising descendants as numerous as the stars; Abram “believed the LORD, and he counted it to him as righteousness” (Gen 15:5–6). Yet Abram and Sarai attempt to secure the promise through Hagar, producing Ishmael and introducing deep household conflict (Gen 16:1–6, 11–16). God later renames Abram as Abraham (“father of a multitude”) and institutes covenant circumcision as the sign (Gen 17:1–14), reiterating that Sarah will bear the promised son (Gen 17:15–21).</div>
<div>God visits Abraham and Sarah, announcing Isaac’s birth; Abraham intercedes for Sodom, revealing both compassion and reverence for God’s justice (Gen 18:16–33). After Sodom’s destruction and Lot’s deliverance (Gen 19:29), Abraham again repeats his earlier fear-driven pattern with Abimelech regarding Sarah (Gen 20:1–18). God keeps His word: Isaac is born, bringing laughter and fulfillment (Gen 21:1–7). Conflict culminates in Hagar and Ishmael being sent away—yet God also provides for them (Gen 21:8–21). Abraham’s faith reaches its most severe test when God commands him to offer Isaac; Abraham obeys, and God provides a substitute ram, reaffirming covenant blessing and worldwide promise (Gen 22:1–19).</div>
<div>Later, Abraham secures a burial place for Sarah in Canaan (a tangible “down payment” on the land promise) (Gen 23:1–20). He arranges Isaac’s marriage to Rebekah, seeking continuity of the covenant line (Gen 24:1–67). Abraham has additional children by Keturah, but Isaac remains the heir of promise (Gen 25:1–6). Abraham dies “in a good old age,” and is buried by Isaac and Ishmael (Gen 25:7–10). His story begins at <b>Genesis 11:26–32 / 12:1</b> and ends at <b>Genesis 25:7–11</b>, with significant later interpretation across Scripture (e.g., Rom 4; Gal 3; Heb 11; Jas 2).</div>
<div><br></div>
<div><b><h2>2) What He’s Known For</h2></b></div>
<div>Abraham is known as the covenant patriarch—called by God, justified by faith, and appointed as a conduit of blessing to the nations (Gen 12:1–3; 15:6; 17:4–8). He is repeatedly held up as a model of faith and as the foundational figure in the unfolding covenant story.</div>
<div><b>Headline descriptors</b><br></div>
<ul>
<li><b>Called pilgrim</b> who left homeland to follow God’s promise (Gen 12:1–4; Heb 11:8–10).</li>
<li><b>Covenant patriarch</b> of Israel and “father of a multitude” (Gen 17:4–7).</li>
<li><b>Justified by faith</b>—belief counted as righteousness (Gen 15:6; Rom 4:1–5).</li>
<li><b>Intercessor</b> who pleads for the righteous and wrestles with God’s justice (Gen 18:22–33).</li>
<li><b>Tested worshiper</b>—the binding of Isaac and God’s provision (Gen 22:1–14; Heb 11:17–19).</li>
<li><b>Imperfect man of faith</b> with recurring fear and compromise (Gen 12:11–13; 20:2).</li>
<li><b>Blessing conduit</b>—promises aimed at all families of the earth (Gen 12:3; Gal 3:8).</li>
</ul>
<div><br></div>
<div><b><h2>3) Where He Appears in the Bible</h2></b></div>
<div><b><h2>Primary narrative locations</h2></b></div>
<ul>
<li><b>Genesis — 11:26–25:11</b> — Abraham’s call, covenant, family, tests, and death.</li>
<ul>
<li><b>Gen 12:1–9</b> — Call and entry into Canaan; promises restated.</li>
<li><b>Gen 13:14–18</b> — Land promise clarified after separation from Lot.</li>
<li><b>Gen 15:1–21</b> — Covenant ceremony; faith counted as righteousness.</li>
<li><b>Gen 17:1–27</b> — Name change; covenant sign (circumcision); Isaac promised.</li>
<li><b>Gen 18:1–33</b> — Promise of Isaac; intercession for Sodom.</li>
<li><b>Gen 21:1–21</b> — Isaac born; Hagar and Ishmael sent away.</li>
<li><b>Gen 22:1–19</b> — Isaac “offered”; God provides; covenant oath renewed.</li>
<li><b>Gen 23</b> — Sarah’s death; purchase of burial field.</li>
<li><b>Gen 24</b> — Isaac’s marriage arranged.</li>
<li><b>Gen 25:7–11</b> — Abraham’s death and burial.</li>
</ul>
</ul>
<div><b><h2>Secondary references (later mentions/interpretations)</h2></b></div>
<ul>
<li><b>Exodus — 2:24; 3:6, 15–16; 6:2–8</b> — God remembers covenant with Abraham; “God of Abraham.”</li>
<li><b>Leviticus — 26:42</b> — Covenant remembrance.</li>
<li><b>Deuteronomy — 1:8; 6:10; 9:5; 34:4</b> — Land promise grounded in oath to Abraham.</li>
<li><b>Joshua — 24:2–4</b> — Summary: God called Abraham from beyond the River.</li>
<li><b>Nehemiah — 9:7–8</b> — Confession recounts call and covenant.</li>
<li><b>Psalm — 105:6–11</b> — Covenant promises celebrated.</li>
<li><b>Isaiah — 41:8; 51:1–2</b> — Abraham as God’s friend and progenitor.</li>
<li><b>Micah — 7:20</b> — God’s steadfast love shown in promise to Abraham.</li>
<li><b>Matthew — 1:1–2</b> — Genealogy: Jesus as son of Abraham.</li>
<li><b>Luke — 1:54–55, 72–73; 3:34</b> — Promise to Abraham in salvation history.</li>
<li><b>John — 8:39–58</b> — True children of Abraham defined; Abraham “rejoiced.”</li>
<li><b>Acts — 3:25; 7:2–8; 13:26</b> — Abrahamic promise framed as fulfilled in Christ.</li>
<li><b>Romans — 4; 9:6–13</b> — Abraham as paradigmatic for justification and promise.</li>
<li><b>Galatians — 3:6–29; 4:21–31</b> — Faith as true sonship; Hagar/Sarah typology.</li>
<li><b>Hebrews — 6:13–20; 11:8–19</b> — Promise, oath, pilgrimage, and offering Isaac.</li>
<li><b>James — 2:21–23</b> — Abraham’s faith shown by works (Isaac event).</li>
</ul>
<div><br></div>
<div><b><h2>4) Vital Statistics</h2></b></div>
<div><b>Historical setting (as Scripture indicates)</b><br></div>
<ul>
<li>Patriarchal era, pre-Mosaic, before Israel becomes a nation (Gen 12–25). Exact dating is debated; Scripture anchors him within genealogical flow from Shem to Terah to Abram (Gen 11:10–27).</li>
</ul>
<div><b>Geography</b><br></div>
<ul>
<li><b>Ur of the Chaldeans</b> and/or Mesopotamian region associated with Terah’s household (Gen 11:28, 31).</li>
<li><b>Haran</b> (Gen 11:31–12:5).</li>
<li><b>Canaan</b>: Shechem, Bethel/Ai, Hebron (Mamre), Negeb (Gen 12:6–9; 13:3–4, 18).</li>
<li><b>Egypt</b> (Gen 12:10).</li>
<li><b>Gerar</b> (region of Abimelech) (Gen 20:1).</li>
<li><b>Moriah</b> (binding of Isaac) (Gen 22:2).</li>
<li><b>Machpelah (near Hebron)</b> burial site purchase (Gen 23:17–20).</li>
</ul>
<div><b>Family and relationships</b><br></div>
<ul>
<li>Father: <b>Terah</b> (Gen 11:27).</li>
<li>Wife: <b>Sarai/Sarah</b> (Gen 11:29; 17:15).</li>
<li>Nephew: <b>Lot</b> (Gen 11:27; 12:4–5).</li>
<li>Children:</li>
<ul>
<li><b>Ishmael</b> by Hagar (Gen 16:15).</li>
<li><b>Isaac</b> by Sarah (Gen 21:2–3).</li>
<li>Additional sons by <b>Keturah</b> (Gen 25:1–2).</li>
</ul>
<li>Key associates:</li>
<ul>
<li><b>Melchizedek</b> (Gen 14:18–20).</li>
<li><b>Abimelech</b> (Gen 20; 21:22–34).</li>
<li><b>Mamre, Eshcol, Aner</b> (allies in rescue of Lot) (Gen 14:13, 24).</li>
<li><b>Eliezer of Damascus</b> implied chief servant before Isaac (Gen 15:2); unnamed chief servant in Gen 24.</li>
</ul>
</ul>
<div><b>Roles/offices</b><br></div>
<ul>
<li><b>Patriarch / covenant head</b> (Gen 17:4–8).</li>
<li><b>Prophet</b> (explicitly called so) (Gen 20:7).</li>
<li><b>Intercessor</b> (Gen 18:22–33).</li>
<li><b>Sojourner/pilgrim</b> (Gen 23:4; Heb 11:9–10).</li>
</ul>
<div><b>Key turning points</b><br></div>
<ul>
<li>Call to leave and go to Canaan (Gen 12:1–4).</li>
<li>Covenant ratification and justification-by-faith statement (Gen 15:6–18).</li>
<li>Hagar/Ishmael episode (Gen 16).</li>
<li>Covenant renewal: new names + circumcision; Isaac promised (Gen 17).</li>
<li>Intercession for Sodom; destruction of cities (Gen 18–19).</li>
<li>Isaac’s birth (Gen 21:1–7).</li>
<li>Testing at Moriah; divine provision; covenant oath (Gen 22).</li>
<li>Purchase of Machpelah (Gen 23).</li>
<li>Isaac’s marriage arranged (Gen 24).</li>
<li>Death and burial (Gen 25:7–10).</li>
</ul>

<div><b><h2>6) Strengths, Weaknesses, and Heart-Level Patterns</h2></b></div>
<div><b><h2>Strengths (with textual evidence)</h2></b></div>
<ul>
<li><b>Obedient faith</b>: Leaves homeland at God’s word (Gen 12:4). Hebrews highlights his forward-looking trust (Heb 11:8–10).</li>
<li><b>Worshipful orientation</b>: Builds altars and calls on the LORD (Gen 12:7–8; 13:4, 18).</li>
<li><b>Courage and leadership</b>: Pursues and defeats kings to rescue Lot (Gen 14:14–16).</li>
<li><b>Generosity and peace-making</b>: Offers Lot the first choice to avoid strife (Gen 13:8–9).</li>
<li><b>God-centered integrity in conflict</b>: Refuses the king of Sodom’s enrichment so God gets the glory (Gen 14:22–23).</li>
<li><b>Intercessory compassion</b>: Pleads for the righteous in Sodom (Gen 18:23–33).</li>
<li><b>Persevering trust under trial</b>: Offers Isaac in obedience; NT interprets this as faith in God’s power even over death (Gen 22:1–12; Heb 11:17–19).</li>
</ul>
<div><b><h2>Weaknesses / sins / blind spots (with brief context)</h2></b></div>
<ul>
<li><b>Fear-driven deception (repeated)</b>:</li>
<ul>
<li>Egypt: “Say you are my sister” (Gen 12:11–13).</li>
<li>Gerar: same pattern with Abimelech (Gen 20:2, 11). The text shows recurring anxiety about personal safety that endangers Sarah and compromises witness (Gen 12:14–20; 20:9–10).</li>
</ul>
<li><b>Impatience / grasping at promise</b>:</li>
<ul>
<li>Hagar episode originates in Sarai’s proposal and Abram’s compliance (Gen 16:1–4). Consequences include contempt, affliction, and lasting familial conflict (Gen 16:4–6; 21:9–10).</li>
</ul>
<li><b>Household leadership complexity</b>:</li>
<ul>
<li>Abraham is not portrayed as uniformly decisive; he “listens” to Sarah in the Hagar matter (Gen 16:2) and later in sending Hagar away (Gen 21:12–14). The latter is commanded by God, but still grievous (Gen 21:11–12).</li>
</ul>
</ul>
<div><b><h2>Motives / fears / desires (careful inference anchored to text)</h2></b></div>
<ul>
<li><b>Fear of death / vulnerability as a sojourner</b> motivates the “sister” strategy (Gen 12:12; 20:11).</li>
<li><b>Desire for an heir</b> and anxiety about barrenness surfaces in his lament (Gen 15:2–3) and his willingness to accept a human workaround (Gen 16:2–4).</li>
<li><b>Desire to honor God’s word</b> grows in prominence: he continues to obey amid long delays (Gen 12:4; 17:23; 22:3).</li>
</ul>
<div><b><h2>What changes (or doesn’t) over time?</h2></b></div>
<ul>
<li><b>Growth</b>: Abraham increasingly responds to God’s word with decisive obedience (contrast Gen 12:10–20 with Gen 22:1–3). His intercession in Gen 18 also shows mature engagement with God’s justice and mercy.</li>
<li><b>Persistent pattern</b>: Fear remains a recurring temptation (Gen 12; Gen 20), reminding readers that sanctification in Scripture can be real yet uneven.</li>
<li><b>Promise stability</b>: God’s commitment is unwavering across Abraham’s failures and delays (Gen 12:1–3; 15:18; 17:7; 22:16–18).</li>
</ul>
<div><br></div>
<div><b><h2>7) Victories and Challenges</h2></b></div>
<div><b><h2>Major “wins” (faith, deliverance, influence)</h2></b></div>
<ul>
<li><b>Obeys the call</b> and enters Canaan (Gen 12:4–5).</li>
<li><b>Worships and marks the land</b> with altars (Gen 12:7–8; 13:18).</li>
<li><b>Rescues Lot</b> and defeats oppressors (Gen 14:14–16).</li>
<li><b>Refuses corrupt gain</b> to protect God’s glory (Gen 14:22–23).</li>
<li><b>Believes God</b>—faith counted as righteousness (Gen 15:6).</li>
<li><b>Receives covenant sign</b> and leads his household in circumcision (Gen 17:23–27).</li>
<li><b>Intercedes</b> and is heard in the narrative (Lot is delivered; Gen 19:29 connects to Abraham’s role).</li>
<li><b>Endures the climactic test</b> at Moriah; God provides (Gen 22:1–14).</li>
<li><b>Secures Machpelah</b> (first owned land in Canaan) (Gen 23:17–20).</li>
<li><b>Ensures covenant continuity</b> through Isaac’s marriage (Gen 24:3–4, 67).</li>
</ul>
<div><b><h2>Major challenges (temptations, failures, suffering, opposition, doubt)</h2></b></div>
<ul>
<li><b>Famine displacement</b> and vulnerability in Egypt (Gen 12:10).</li>
<li><b>Endangering Sarah</b> through deception (Gen 12:11–20; 20:2–18).</li>
<li><b>Long delay of promise</b> (implicit across Gen 12–21; explicit longing Gen 15:2–3).</li>
<li><b>Hagar/Ishmael conflict</b> and its grief (Gen 16:4–6; 21:11).</li>
<li><b>Sodom’s moral crisis</b> and the limits of intercession (Gen 18:32–19:29).</li>
<li><b>The command to offer Isaac</b>—the severest cost of faithfulness (Gen 22:1–2).</li>
</ul>
<div><b>Cost and consequence highlights</b><br></div>
<ul>
<li>Compromise driven by fear brings reproach from outsiders and relational harm (Gen 12:18–19; 20:9–10).</li>
<li>Faithfulness can involve profound sorrow and sacrifice (Gen 21:11–14; 22:2–3), yet God provides and confirms His promises (Gen 21:17–19; 22:12–14, 16–18).</li>
</ul>

<div><b><h2>9) Theology and Big Themes</h2></b></div>
<div><b><h2>God’s character revealed</h2></b></div>
<ul>
<li><b>Sovereign caller and promise-maker</b>: God initiates the relationship (Gen 12:1–3).</li>
<li><b>Faithful covenant-keeper</b> despite human weakness: He preserves Sarah and advances promise even after Abraham’s failures (Gen 12:17–20; 20:3–7; 21:1–2).</li>
<li><b>Patient and instructive</b>: Repeats and clarifies promise over years (Gen 13:14–17; 15:1–6; 17:1–8).</li>
<li><b>Just Judge and merciful rescuer</b>: Sodom narrative holds both justice and deliverance (Gen 18:25; 19:29).</li>
<li><b>Provider</b>: Moriah crystallizes God’s provision (Gen 22:14).</li>
</ul>
<div><b><h2>Covenant, kingdom, mission</h2></b></div>
<ul>
<li><b>Covenant as the backbone of redemption history</b>: Abraham is the hinge between primeval history (Gen 1–11) and patriarchal/national history (Gen 12 onward).</li>
<li><b>Universal mission</b>: Blessing is aimed outward to “all families of the earth” (Gen 12:3), later read as gospel advance to the Gentiles (Gal 3:8).</li>
<li><b>Land-seed-blessing</b>: The triad shapes Torah, prophets, and NT fulfillment patterns (Gen 12:1–3; 15:18; 17:7).</li>
</ul>
<div><b><h2>Human nature: faith, sin, grace, perseverance</h2></b></div>
<ul>
<li><b>Justification by faith</b>: Abraham is declared righteous by believing God (Gen 15:6), later foundational for Pauline theology (Rom 4:3).</li>
<li><b>Mixed obedience</b>: Fear and faith coexist; Scripture portrays a real believer with real weakness (Gen 12; 20).</li>
<li><b>Waiting as obedience</b>: Barrenness and delay become the arena where faith is refined (Gen 15:2–6; 21:1–2; Heb 11:11–12).</li>
</ul>
<div><br></div>
<div><b><h2>10) How Abraham Points Us to Christ (OT↔NT, biblically grounded)</h2></b></div>
<div><b><h2>Promise fulfillment: “seed” and worldwide blessing</h2></b></div>
<ul>
<li>The Abrahamic promise explicitly targets <b>all nations</b> (Gen 12:3; 22:18). The NT interprets this as the gospel announced beforehand and fulfilled in Christ (Gal 3:8, 16). Christ is the climactic “seed” through whom blessing extends to Gentiles (Gal 3:14).</li>
</ul>
<div><b><h2>Justification by faith</h2></b></div>
<ul>
<li>Abraham’s righteousness comes through faith, not works of law (Gen 15:6; Rom 4:1–5). This anticipates the gospel logic: sinners are counted righteous by trusting God’s promise fulfilled in Christ (Rom 4:23–25).</li>
</ul>
<div><b><h2>The “beloved son” pattern and provision on Moriah (careful typology)</h2></b></div>
<ul>
<li>Abraham is told to offer his “son…whom you love” (Gen 22:2), and God provides a substitute (Gen 22:13–14). The NT echoes the pattern: God “did not spare his own Son” (Rom 8:32). The typological movement is not “Isaac = Jesus” in every detail, but a covenantal pattern: <b>the father’s giving, the beloved son, and God’s provision</b> culminating in Christ’s true sacrifice.</li>
</ul>
<div><b><h2>Pilgrimage and the better country</h2></b></div>
<ul>
<li>Abraham lives as a sojourner, seeking a city with foundations (Heb 11:9–10). This points beyond Canaan to the eschatological inheritance secured in Christ (Heb 11:13–16), aligning Abraham’s life with the pilgrim nature of Christian discipleship (1 Pet 2:11, theme).</li>
</ul>
<div><b><h2>True children of Abraham</h2></b></div>
<ul>
<li>Physical descent is redefined: those who share Abraham’s faith are his true offspring (Gal 3:7, 29). This centers Christ as the locus of covenant belonging.</li>
</ul>

<div><b><h2>11) Lessons and Applications (Scripture-tethered)</h2></b></div>
<ol>
<li><b>God’s call often requires costly leaving</b> (Gen 12:1–4) — obedience may disrupt security and familiarity.</li>
<li><b>Faith is counted righteous because it trusts God’s promise</b> (Gen 15:6; Rom 4:3–5).</li>
<li><b>Waiting is not wasted</b>—delay is a school for trust (Gen 15:2–6; 21:1–2; Heb 11:11–12).</li>
<li><b>Fear can make believers pragmatic and deceptive</b> (Gen 12:11–13; 20:11) — identify recurring fear patterns.</li>
<li><b>God’s faithfulness outlasts our failures</b> (Gen 12:17–20; 20:3–7; 21:1–2).</li>
<li><b>Peacemaking and generosity can reflect confidence in God’s provision</b> (Gen 13:8–12).</li>
<li><b>Integrity about wealth and influence matters</b> (Gen 14:22–23) — resist entangling alliances that steal God’s glory.</li>
<li><b>Intercession should be humble, persistent, and God-centered</b> (Gen 18:23–33).</li>
<li><b>Trying to “help” God’s promises can create lasting harm</b> (Gen 16:1–6; 21:9–11).</li>
<li><b>Covenant obedience includes embodied signs and household leadership</b> (Gen 17:9–14, 23–27).</li>
<li><b>God may test what we treasure most</b> (Gen 22:1–2) — faith submits even beloved gifts to the Giver.</li>
<li><b>God provides what He requires</b> (Gen 22:13–14) — obedience is paired with divine provision.</li>
<li><b>Faith takes tangible steps in God’s promises</b> (Gen 23:17–20) — invest in what God says is real, even if not yet fully seen.</li>
<li><b>Covenant purpose is outward-facing blessing</b> (Gen 12:3; Gal 3:8) — the people of God exist for the nations.</li>
</ol>
<div><b><h2>Warnings to heed</h2></b></div>
<ul>
<li>Don’t let fear reshape truth-telling or marital faithfulness (Gen 12:11–20; 20:9–13).</li>
<li>Don’t shortcut God’s timing with human schemes (Gen 16:1–6).</li>
<li>Don’t confuse blessing with worldly leverage or corrupt gain (Gen 14:21–24).</li>
</ul>
<div><b><h2>Examples to imitate</h2></b></div>
<ul>
<li>Respond quickly to God’s word (Gen 12:4; 22:3).</li>
<li>Intercede with reverent boldness (Gen 18:27, 32).</li>
<li>Refuse compromised prosperity (Gen 14:22–23).</li>
</ul>
<div><b>Application arenas</b><br></div>
<ul>
<li><b>Personal holiness</b>: Identify “repeat sins” (fear) and bring them into the light (Gen 20:11).</li>
<li><b>Relationships</b>: Practice peacemaking and generosity (Gen 13:8–9).</li>
<li><b>Prayer</b>: Learn intercession that appeals to God’s justice and mercy (Gen 18:25).</li>
<li><b>Suffering &amp delay</b>: Hold to promise when circumstances contradict it (Gen 15:2–6).</li>
<li><b>Decision-making &amp leadership</b>: Protect covenant priorities, even in family planning and household direction (Gen 17:23–27; 24:1–4).</li>
</ul>

<div><b><h2>12) Discussion and Reflection Questions (10–20)</h2></b></div>
<div><b><h2>Personal reflection</h2></b></div>
<ol>
<li>Where is God calling you to “leave” something for the sake of obedience (Gen 12:1–4)?</li>
<li>What fears tend to drive your decision-making, and what compromises do they produce (Gen 12:12; 20:11)?</li>
<li>In what area are you tempted to “help God out” rather than wait (Gen 16:1–4)?</li>
</ol>
<div><b><h2>Small group discussion</h2></b></div>
<ol>
<li>How do you reconcile Abraham’s real faith with his repeated failures (Gen 15:6; 20:2)?</li>
<li>What does Abraham’s refusal of Sodom’s wealth teach about integrity and witness (Gen 14:22–23)?</li>
<li>How does Genesis 15:6 shape how we talk about salvation (Rom 4:3–5)?</li>
<li>What do you learn about prayer from Abraham’s intercession (Gen 18:23–33)?</li>
</ol>
<div><b><h2>Deep study (text-observation)</h2></b></div>
<ol>
<li>Track the repetitions of God’s promises to Abraham (Gen 12; 13; 15; 17; 22). What changes each time?</li>
<li>In Genesis 16, what exactly is Abraham’s role in the conflict? What is Sarai’s? What does the narrator emphasize (Gen 16:1–6)?</li>
<li>Compare Genesis 12 and Genesis 20. What is similar, and what differs in Abraham’s explanations (Gen 12:11–13; 20:11–13)?</li>
<li>In Genesis 22, note every verb describing Abraham’s actions. What does the pacing suggest about the test (Gen 22:3–10)?</li>
<li>What is the significance of “The LORD will provide” in context (Gen 22:14)?</li>
<li>Why does Genesis spend a full chapter on purchasing Machpelah (Gen 23)? What theological point might it underscore about promise and land?</li>
</ol>
<div><b><h2>Christ and the gospel</h2></b></div>
<ol>
<li>How does Paul use Abraham to argue for justification by faith (Rom 4:1–5)?</li>
<li>How does Galatians connect “blessing to nations” to Christ and the Spirit (Gal 3:8, 14)?</li>
<li>What does it mean to be a “child of Abraham” according to Jesus and Paul (John 8:39–40; Gal 3:7)?</li>
</ol>
