<div><h1>Micah</h1></div>
<div><br></div>
<div><b><h2>Micah (the prophet, “the Morashtite”)</h2></b></div>
<div><b>Disambiguation:</b> “Micah” can refer to multiple people in Scripture (e.g., the Micah of Judges 17–18). This study focuses on <b>Micah the prophet</b>, identified as <b>“Micah of Moresheth”</b> and author/voice of the book of <b>Micah</b> (Micah 1:1).</div>
<div><br></div>
<div><b><h2>1) Story Summary (chronological)</h2></b></div>
<div>Micah is a prophet to <b>Judah</b> (and also speaks against <b>Samaria/Israel</b>) during the reigns of <b>Jotham, Ahaz, and Hezekiah</b> (Micah 1:1). The book presents his prophetic ministry as a series of oracles that expose covenant-breaking—especially <b>idolatry, injustice, corrupt leadership, and exploitative economics</b>—and announce coming <b>judgment</b> on both <b>Samaria</b> and <b>Jerusalem</b> (Micah 1:2–7; 3:9–12; 6:9–16).</div>
<div>Yet Micah’s message is not only judgment. In the midst of severe indictments, he proclaims God’s future <b>restoration</b>, the exaltation of the Lord’s reign, and a coming <b>ruler from Bethlehem</b> whose “goings forth are from of old” (Micah 4:1–5; 4:6–7; 5:2–5). The book culminates in hope anchored in God’s character: the Lord’s unique mercy, compassion, and covenant faithfulness—“Who is a God like you…?” (Micah 7:18–20).</div>
<div><b>Where his story begins and ends in the Bible:</b><br></div>
<ul>
<li>Begins: <b>Micah 1:1</b> (identification and timeframe)</li>
<li>Ends: <b>Micah 7:20</b> (doxology of covenant mercy)</li>
</ul>
<div><br></div>
<div><b><h2>2) What He’s Known For</h2></b></div>
<div>Micah is most known for proclaiming God’s <b>covenant lawsuit</b> against His people, condemning injustice and corrupt leadership, and announcing both <b>judgment</b> and <b>messianic hope</b>.</div>
<div><b>Headlines:</b><br></div>
<ul>
<li><b>Prophet from the countryside</b> (“Morashtite”) speaking to national power centers (Micah 1:1; 3:1, 9).</li>
<li><b>Bold accuser of exploitation</b>—land-grabbing, predatory power, and economic oppression (Micah 2:1–2).</li>
<li><b>Indictor of corrupt leaders and “prophets for hire”</b> (Micah 3:5–11).</li>
<li><b>Herald of Zion’s ruin</b> (Micah 3:12).</li>
<li><b>Vision-caster of the latter-day kingdom</b> (Micah 4:1–5).</li>
<li><b>Foreteller of the Bethlehem ruler</b> (Micah 5:2; cf. Matt. 2:5–6).</li>
<li><b>Summarizer of true covenant piety</b> (Micah 6:6–8).</li>
</ul>
<div><br></div>
<div><b><h2>3) Where He Appears in the Bible</h2></b></div>
<div><b><h2>Primary narrative locations (main story)</h2></b></div>
<ul>
<li><b>Micah — Micah 1:1–7:20 —</b> Micah’s prophetic oracles: judgment, hope, Messiah, covenant faithfulness.</li>
</ul>
<div><b><h2>Secondary references (later mentions/interpretations)</h2></b></div>
<ul>
<li><b>Jeremiah — Jer. 26:18–19 —</b> Elders cite <b>Micah 3:12</b> as a precedent; Hezekiah feared the LORD and sought His favor rather than killing Micah.</li>
<li><b>Matthew — Matt. 2:5–6 —</b> Micah’s <b>Bethlehem</b> prophecy applied to Jesus (drawing from Micah 5:2).</li>
<li><b>John — John 7:42 —</b> The Bethlehem expectation echoes <b>Micah 5:2</b> in the debate about Jesus’ origins.</li>
<li><b>Isaiah — Isa. 2:2–4 (parallel to Micah 4:1–3) —</b> Shared latter-days vision of Zion and the nations.</li>
</ul>
<div><br></div>
<div><b><h2>4) Vital Statistics</h2></b></div>
<ul>
<li><b>Historical setting (as Scripture indicates):</b><br></li>
<ul>
<li>Active during the kings <b>Jotham, Ahaz, Hezekiah</b> of Judah (Micah 1:1). This places him in the era of Assyrian pressure and turmoil (implicit backdrop; Scripture’s explicit anchor is the kings listed).</li>
</ul>
<li><b>Geography:</b><br></li>
<ul>
<li><b>Moresheth / Moresheth-gath region</b> (Micah “the Morashtite,” Micah 1:1).</li>
<li>Speaks about <b>Samaria</b> (Micah 1:6) and <b>Jerusalem/Zion</b> (Micah 3:12; 4:2).</li>
</ul>
<li><b>Family and relationships:</b><br></li>
<ul>
<li>Scripture gives <b>no explicit family line</b> (parents/spouse/children not named). <i>Uncertain beyond what is written.</i><br></li>
</ul>
<li><b>Roles/offices:</b><br></li>
<ul>
<li><b>Prophet</b>—speaks the word of the LORD; confronts rulers, priests, prophets (Micah 1:1; 3:1, 5, 11).</li>
<li>Self-description of prophetic empowerment: “filled with power… to declare… transgression” (Micah 3:8).</li>
</ul>
<li><b>Key turning points (text-driven):</b><br></li>
<ul>
<li><b>Commission/stance:</b> empowered to confront sin (Micah 3:8).</li>
<li><b>Public, high-stakes oracle:</b> Zion’s destruction announced (Micah 3:12), later remembered as historically weighty (Jer. 26:18–19).</li>
<li><b>Hope pivot:</b> the latter-days kingdom and the Bethlehem ruler (Micah 4:1–5; 5:2–5).</li>
<li><b>Final confidence in God’s mercy:</b> doxology of forgiveness and covenant fidelity (Micah 7:18–20).</li>
</ul>
</ul>

<div><b><h2>6) Strengths, Weaknesses, and Heart-Level Patterns</h2></b></div>
<div><b><h2>Strengths (with textual evidence)</h2></b></div>
<ul>
<li><b>Courageous truth-telling to power:</b> confronts rulers and religious leaders directly (Micah 3:1–3, 9–11).</li>
<li><b>Prophetic integrity and empowerment:</b> “filled with power… to declare… transgression” (Micah 3:8).</li>
<li><b>Moral clarity about justice:</b> condemns exploitation and land theft (Micah 2:1–2).</li>
<li><b>Theological depth and hope:</b> can announce judgment without losing sight of restoration (Micah 4:1–7; 7:18–20).</li>
<li><b>Tender grief and empathy:</b> laments publicly (Micah 1:8–9; 7:1).</li>
</ul>
<div><b><h2>Weaknesses/sins/blind spots (what Scripture shows)</h2></b></div>
<div>Micah’s personal sins are <b>not recorded</b>. The text focuses on his message, not moral failures. <i>So we should not invent weaknesses beyond the text.</i><br></div>
<div><b><h2>Motives/fears/desires (careful, text-anchored inference)</h2></b></div>
<ul>
<li><b>Desire for covenant faithfulness:</b> he prosecutes God’s case against idolatry and injustice (Micah 1:2–5; 6:1–5).</li>
<li><b>Grief over communal collapse:</b> his lament suggests an ache for righteousness and truth in society (Micah 7:1–2).</li>
<li><b>Confidence in God’s mercy:</b> he ends not with despair but worship (Micah 7:18–20).</li>
</ul>
<div><b><h2>What changes (or doesn’t) over time?</h2></b></div>
<ul>
<li><b>Consistent pattern:</b> judgment and hope remain intertwined throughout (Micah 1–3; 4–5; 6–7).</li>
<li><b>Escalation in focus:</b> from immediate indictments (Micah 2–3; 6:9–16) to sweeping future hope (Micah 4:1–7; 5:2–5) and final doxology (Micah 7:18–20).</li>
</ul>
<div><br></div>
<div><b><h2>7) Victories and Challenges</h2></b></div>
<div><b><h2>Major “wins” (faithful obedience and impact)</h2></b></div>
<ul>
<li><b>Faithful proclamation despite opposition:</b> he continues speaking hard truth (Micah 2:6–11; 3:8).</li>
<li><b>Lasting influence remembered in Judah:</b> Micah’s Zion-ruin prophecy is cited generations later; Hezekiah’s response is held up as fearing the LORD rather than silencing the prophet (Micah 3:12; Jer. 26:18–19).</li>
<li><b>Hope-giving clarity about God’s future kingdom:</b> vision that steadies God’s people (Micah 4:1–5; 7:7–9).</li>
</ul>
<div><b><h2>Major challenges (cost of faithfulness)</h2></b></div>
<ul>
<li><b>Social pressure to stop preaching:</b> “Do not preach…” (Micah 2:6), with pushback against his message.</li>
<li><b>Ministering amid widespread corruption:</b> leaders “hate the good and love the evil” (Micah 3:2); even close relationships become unsafe (Micah 7:5–6).</li>
<li><b>Bearing grief over judgment:</b> Micah’s lament shows the emotional burden of announcing disaster (Micah 1:8–9).</li>
</ul>

<li>Ex. 34:6–7 (God’s mercy and justice); Ps. 103:8–12 (forgiveness).</li>
<li>Luke 1:72–73 (covenant mercy language echoes OT covenant themes).</li>
</ul>
<div><br></div>
<div><b><h2>9) Theology and Big Themes</h2></b></div>
<ul>
<li><b>God’s holiness and justice:</b> the LORD rises to testify against covenant breach (Micah 1:2–5; 6:1–5).</li>
<li><b>God’s concern for the oppressed:</b> exploitation and theft are treated as spiritual rebellion (Micah 2:1–2; 3:1–3).</li>
<li><b>Corruption of leadership is catastrophic:</b> rulers, priests, prophets distort justice and worship (Micah 3:9–11).</li>
<li><b>Covenant lawsuit structure:</b> God recounts His saving acts and charges His people (Micah 6:1–5).</li>
<li><b>True worship integrates ethics and humility:</b> ritual cannot replace obedience (Micah 6:6–8).</li>
<li><b>Remnant and restoration:</b> God gathers the broken and makes them His people (Micah 4:6–7).</li>
<li><b>Kingdom hope:</b> the nations will learn God’s ways; peace flows from His rule (Micah 4:1–4).</li>
<li><b>Mercy triumphing in God’s character:</b> the finale exalts forgiveness rooted in covenant promises (Micah 7:18–20).</li>
</ul>
<div><br></div>
<div><b><h2>10) How Micah Points Us to Christ</h2></b></div>
<div><b><h2>Prophetic office and pattern</h2></b></div>
<div>Micah functions as a covenant prosecutor and herald—exposing sin and calling for true repentance (Micah 6:1–8). In the NT, Jesus likewise confronts hypocritical worship and injustice (e.g., Matt. 23:23 echoes the weightier matters of the law—justice/mercy/faithfulness).</div>
<div><b><h2>The Bethlehem promise (explicit OT↔NT link)</h2></b></div>
<ul>
<li><b>Micah 5:2</b> foretells a ruler from <b>Bethlehem</b>.</li>
<li>The NT explicitly applies this to <b>Jesus</b> (Matt. 2:5–6; cf. John 7:42).</li>
</ul>
<div><b><h2>Shepherd-king and “peace”</h2></b></div>
<ul>
<li>Micah describes the ruler who “shall stand and shepherd… and they shall dwell secure” and “he shall be their peace” (Micah 5:4–5).</li>
<li>Jesus identifies Himself as the <b>good shepherd</b> (John 10:11) and is explicitly called <b>our peace</b> (Eph. 2:14).</li>
</ul>
<div><b><h2>Mercy and covenant faithfulness fulfilled</h2></b></div>
<div>Micah ends with God pardoning iniquity and showing steadfast love, “as you have sworn… to Abraham” (Micah 7:18–20). The NT presents Christ’s saving work as the climactic expression of God’s covenant mercy (Luke 1:72–73; Rom. 15:8).</div>
<div><br></div>
<div><b><h2>11) Lessons and Applications (tied to Scripture)</h2></b></div>
<ol>
<li>God sees and judges public injustice as covenant sin (Micah 2:1–2).</li>
<li>Spiritual leadership must not be for sale (Micah 3:11).</li>
<li>Courage to speak truth is a God-given empowerment, not personality (Micah 3:8).</li>
<li>A society can be religious and still be rotten at its core (Micah 3:9–11).</li>
<li>God’s future kingdom reshapes present hope and priorities (Micah 4:1–4).</li>
<li>God gathers the weak and outcast; restoration begins with compassion (Micah 4:6–7).</li>
<li>True worship is not primarily performance but a life of justice, love, humility (Micah 6:6–8).</li>
<li>When faithfulness is rare, resolve to “look to the LORD” (Micah 7:7).</li>
<li>God’s mercy is not sentimental—He pardons and remains faithful to covenant promises (Micah 7:18–20).</li>
<li>Messianic hope is concrete: God’s salvation enters history in a promised ruler (Micah 5:2; Matt. 2:5–6).</li>
</ol>
<div><b><h2>Warnings to heed</h2></b></div>
<ul>
<li>Don’t confuse ritual with obedience (Micah 6:6–8).</li>
<li>Don’t use spiritual words to cover greed (Micah 3:11).</li>
<li>Don’t normalize exploitation as “just business” (Micah 2:1–2).</li>
</ul>
<div><b><h2>Examples to imitate</h2></b></div>
<ul>
<li>Lament sin’s damage rather than excusing it (Micah 1:8–9).</li>
<li>Persist in hope: “my God will hear me” (Micah 7:7).</li>
<li>Worship God for His mercy and covenant faithfulness (Micah 7:18–20).</li>
</ul>

<div><b><h2>12) Discussion and Reflection Questions (10–20)</h2></b></div>
<div><b><h2>Personal reflection</h2></b></div>
<ol>
<li>Where am I tempted to substitute religious activity for obedience (Micah 6:6–8)?</li>
<li>Do I treat injustice as “someone else’s issue,” or as covenant seriousness (Micah 2:1–2)?</li>
<li>What does “walk humbly with your God” look like in my current season (Micah 6:8)?</li>
<li>When life feels morally confusing, do I default to Micah 7:7 or to self-reliance?</li>
</ol>
<div><b><h2>Small group discussion</h2></b></div>
<ol>
<li>Why does Micah spend so much time confronting leaders (Micah 3:1–11)?</li>
<li>What modern equivalents of “preaching for pay” might we need to guard against (Micah 3:11)?</li>
<li>How should Micah 4:1–4 reshape the church’s hope and posture toward the nations?</li>
<li>How do we hold together judgment (Micah 3:12) and mercy (Micah 7:18–20) without weakening either?</li>
</ol>
<div><b><h2>Deep study (text-observation)</h2></b></div>
<ol>
<li>Trace the courtroom language in Micah 1:2–5 and 6:1–5. Who is speaking, who is addressed, who are the witnesses?</li>
<li>In Micah 2:1–2, what is the progression from desire → plan → abuse of power?</li>
<li>In Micah 3:9–11, list each group (rulers/priests/prophets) and their specific corruption.</li>
<li>Compare Micah 4:1–3 with Isaiah 2:2–4. What’s identical, and what nuances differ?</li>
<li>In Micah 5:2, what phrases emphasize both humility (Bethlehem) and ancient purpose (“from of old”)?</li>
<li>In Micah 6:6–8, what is contrasted (offerings vs. obedience), and why?</li>
<li>In Micah 7:18–20, what verbs describe God’s mercy, and what covenant roots are named?</li>
</ol>

Teach us to do justice, to love steadfast love, and to walk humbly with you (Micah 6:8). When righteousness feels scarce, keep us looking to you with confidence that you hear your people (Micah 7:7).
