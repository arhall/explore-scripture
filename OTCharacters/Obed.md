<div><h1>Obed</h1></div>
<div><br></div>
<div><b><h1>Obed — Bible Character Study</h1></b></div>
<div><b><h2>1) Story Summary (chronological)</h2></b></div>
<div>Obed’s story is brief in narrative detail but massive in covenant significance. He is introduced at the close of Ruth as the child born to Boaz and Ruth, given to Naomi’s household as a “redeemer” (in the sense of family restoration), and placed into the genealogy that leads to David—and ultimately to the Messiah.</div>
<ul>
<li><b>Where his story begins:</b> at his birth and naming in <b>Bethlehem</b> after Boaz marries Ruth (Ruth 4:13–17).</li>
<li><b>Where his story ends (in Scripture’s narrative focus):</b> in genealogies that trace Israel’s royal line—Obed as father of Jesse and grandfather of David (Ruth 4:17, 21–22; 1 Chron 2:12; Matt 1:5–6; Luke 3:32).</li>
</ul>
<div>Because Scripture gives no episodes of Obed’s adult life, the Bible’s emphasis is not on what Obed did, but on what <b>God did through Obed’s place in the covenant line</b> (Ruth 4:14–17; Matt 1:5–16).</div>
<div><br></div>
<div><b><h2>2) What He’s Known For</h2></b></div>
<div>Obed is most known for being a <b>providential link</b> in the line from Judah to David and to Jesus, born through the “kinsman-redeemer” resolution of Ruth’s story.</div>
<div><b>Headlines</b><br></div>
<ul>
<li><b>Son of Boaz and Ruth</b> (Ruth 4:13, 17)</li>
<li><b>Instrument of Naomi’s restoration</b> (“a son has been born to Naomi”) (Ruth 4:14–17)</li>
<li><b>Father of Jesse; grandfather of David</b> (Ruth 4:17, 21–22; 1 Chron 2:12)</li>
<li><b>Named in the Messiah’s genealogy</b> (Matt 1:5–6; Luke 3:32)</li>
<li><b>A witness to God’s covenant providence in the era of the Judges</b> (Ruth 1:1; 4:13–17)</li>
</ul>
<div><br></div>
<div><b><h2>3) Where He Appears in the Bible</h2></b></div>
<div><b>Primary narrative locations</b><br></div>
<ul>
<li><b>Ruth — 4:13–17</b> — Obed is born to Ruth and Boaz; women of Bethlehem name him; Naomi nurses him.</li>
<li><b>Ruth — 4:18–22</b> — Genealogy from Perez to David includes Obed as a key link.</li>
</ul>
<div><b>Secondary references</b><br></div>
<ul>
<li><b>1 Chronicles — 2:12</b> — Genealogy of Judah: Boaz → Obed → Jesse.</li>
<li><b>Matthew — 1:5–6</b> — Genealogy of Jesus: Boaz → Obed → Jesse → David.</li>
<li><b>Luke — 3:32</b> — Genealogy tracing back through David includes Obed.</li>
</ul>
<div><br></div>
<div><b><h2>4) Vital Statistics</h2></b></div>
<ul>
<li><b>Historical setting:</b> “In the days when the judges ruled” (Ruth 1:1). Obed is born at the end of Ruth’s account, still within that period (Ruth 4:13–17).</li>
<li><b>Geography:</b> <b>Bethlehem</b> (implied throughout Ruth’s closing scene; Ruth 4:11, 13–17).</li>
<li><b>Family and relationships</b><br></li>
<ul>
<li><b>Father:</b> Boaz (Ruth 4:13, 17)</li>
<li><b>Mother:</b> Ruth the Moabite (Ruth 4:13, 17; cf. Ruth 1:4; 2:2)</li>
<li><b>Adoptive/household focus:</b> Naomi (Ruth 4:16–17)</li>
<li><b>Son:</b> Jesse (Ruth 4:17, 22; 1 Chron 2:12)</li>
<li><b>Grandson:</b> David (Ruth 4:17, 22; Matt 1:6)</li>
</ul>
<li><b>Roles/offices:</b> No office is stated (not described as prophet/judge/king). His “role” is genealogical and covenant-historical: <b>ancestor in the Davidic/Messianic line</b> (Ruth 4:17–22; Matt 1:5–6).</li>
<li><b>Key turning points (as Scripture presents them)</b><br></li>
<ul>
<li><b>Birth through a redemption-marriage outcome</b> (Ruth 4:9–13, 17)</li>
<li><b>Named and celebrated as Naomi’s restoration</b> (Ruth 4:14–17)</li>
<li><b>Placed in the genealogy leading to David</b> (Ruth 4:18–22)</li>
</ul>
</ul>

<div><b><h2>6) Strengths, Weaknesses, and Heart-Level Patterns</h2></b></div>
<div><b><h2>Strengths (text-grounded)</h2></b></div>
<div>Because the Bible gives no direct actions or sayings of Obed, we must be careful: <b>Scripture does not attribute personal virtues to him explicitly.</b> What we <i>can</i> affirm is the theological framing around his birth and placement.</div>
<ul>
<li><b>He stands as evidence of God’s providence and covenant faithfulness.</b> The narrative stresses that the LORD “gave” conception (Ruth 4:13) and that the community interprets Obed’s arrival as the LORD’s kindness to Naomi (Ruth 4:14–15).</li>
<li><b>He embodies the fruit of faithful “ordinary” obedience in God’s people.</b> Ruth’s loyal love and Boaz’s integrity lead to a covenantally significant outcome (Ruth 1:16–17; 2:11–12; 3:10–13; 4:9–13), culminating in Obed (Ruth 4:13–17).</li>
</ul>
<div><b><h2>Weaknesses / sins / blind spots</h2></b></div>
<ul>
<li><b>None are recorded.</b> Scripture is silent about Obed’s character failures or moral struggles.</li>
</ul>
<div><b><h2>Motives / fears / desires (careful inference)</h2></b></div>
<ul>
<li><b>No direct psychological inferences are possible</b> because Obed has no narrated decisions or dialogue.</li>
<li>The <i>community’s</i> desire is clear: preservation of family name and blessing in Israel (Ruth 4:11–12, 14–15). Obed exists within that covenant hope.</li>
</ul>
<div><b><h2>What changes (or doesn’t) over time?</h2></b></div>
<ul>
<li>Obed’s arc is presented in a <b>single moment</b> (birth/naming) and then as a <b>fixed genealogical link</b> (Ruth 4:17–22; 1 Chron 2:12; Matt 1:5–6). Scripture gives no “development narrative” for him.</li>
</ul>
<div><br></div>
<div><b><h2>7) Victories and Challenges</h2></b></div>
<div><b><h2>Major “wins” (as the text frames them)</h2></b></div>
<ul>
<li><b>Birth as an act of divine provision:</b> “the LORD gave her conception” (Ruth 4:13).</li>
<li><b>Agent of Naomi’s restoration (familial and social):</b> the women declare the child will “renew your life” and “nourish you in your old age” (Ruth 4:14–15); Naomi takes and nurses him (Ruth 4:16).</li>
<li><b>Covenant-historical significance:</b> Obed becomes the father of Jesse and grandfather of David (Ruth 4:17, 22), later embedded in the Messianic genealogy (Matt 1:5–6; Luke 3:32).</li>
</ul>
<div><b><h2>Major challenges (what we can responsibly say)</h2></b></div>
<ul>
<li><b>Narrative silence:</b> Scripture does not present personal trials; the “challenge” for interpretation is resisting speculation and letting the canonical emphasis stand—God’s providence through seemingly small lives (Ruth 4:13–17).</li>
<li><b>Born into a complex family story marked by loss and outsider inclusion:</b> The story that leads to Obed includes famine, death, widowhood, and Ruth’s status as a Moabite (Ruth 1:1–5; 2:2, 6; 4:13–17). Obed’s very existence testifies that God brings life out of emptiness (Ruth 1:20–21; 4:14–17).</li>
</ul>
<div><b>Cost of faithfulness / consequences of compromise (in context):</b><br></div>
<ul>
<li>Ruth and Boaz’s faithful actions are highlighted as exemplary and fruitful (Ruth 2:11–12; 3:10–13; 4:9–13), contrasted implicitly with the fragility of life in the Judges era (Ruth 1:1) and the losses Naomi endured (Ruth 1:3–5).</li>
</ul>
<div><br></div>
<div><b><h2>8) Essential Verses and Key Cross-References (organized by themes)</h2></b></div>
<div><b><h2>A) Obed directly named (core texts)</h2></b></div>
<ul>
<li>Ruth 4:13 — LORD gives conception; a son is born.</li>
<li>Ruth 4:16–17 — Naomi takes the child; women name him Obed; “he was the father of Jesse, the father of David.”</li>
<li>Ruth 4:21–22 — “Boaz fathered Obed, Obed fathered Jesse, and Jesse fathered David.”</li>
<li>1 Chron 2:12 — “Boaz fathered Obed; Obed fathered Jesse.”</li>
<li>Matt 1:5–6 — “Boaz the father of Obed by Ruth… Jesse the father of David the king.”</li>
<li>Luke 3:32 — Obed listed within the Davidic line.</li>
</ul>
<div><b><h2>B) Providence and the LORD’s kindness</h2></b></div>
<ul>
<li>Ruth 1:20–21 — Naomi’s emptiness sets the stage for restoration.</li>
<li>Ruth 2:12 — blessing language: the LORD rewarding Ruth.</li>
<li>Ruth 4:14–15 — the LORD praised for not leaving Naomi without a “redeemer”; child as renewal.</li>
</ul>
<div><b><h2>C) Redemption, inheritance, and covenant continuity</h2></b></div>
<ul>
<li>Ruth 4:9–10 — Boaz’s legal act to preserve the name and inheritance.</li>
<li>Deut 25:5–10 — background for levirate-type concerns (name preservation), relevant to Ruth’s legal/covenantal resolution (cf. Ruth 4:5, 10).</li>
<li>Num 27:8–11 — inheritance preservation principles (broader Torah background for family lines).</li>
</ul>
<div><b><h2>D) Davidic and Messianic trajectory</h2></b></div>
<ul>
<li>Gen 49:10 — Judah’s scepter promise (broad trajectory toward kingship).</li>
<li>2 Sam 7:12–16 — Davidic covenant (Obed sits upstream of this line; Ruth 4:17, 22).</li>
<li>Isa 11:1 — “stump of Jesse” (Obed → Jesse; Ruth 4:17).</li>
<li>Mic 5:2 — Bethlehem as messianic setting (Ruth’s Bethlehem context; Ruth 4:11, 13–17).</li>
<li>Gal 4:4–5 — “born… to redeem” (theological echo of God’s redemptive timing).</li>
</ul>
<div><i>(Note: these OT/NT links do not claim Obed personally fulfills them; they show how Obed’s recorded place in the line participates in the larger canonical storyline.)</i><br></div>
<div><br></div>
<div><b><h2>9) Theology and Big Themes</h2></b></div>
<div><b><h2>God’s character revealed</h2></b></div>
<ul>
<li><b>Providence and life-giving power:</b> The LORD “gave” conception (Ruth 4:13), showing God as the decisive giver of life and the director of outcomes beyond human planning.</li>
<li><b>Covenant kindness (ḥesed) working through people:</b> Ruth’s loyal love and Boaz’s integrity become instruments of restoration (Ruth 1:16–17; 2:11–12; 3:10–13; 4:9–13), culminating in Obed (Ruth 4:13–17).</li>
</ul>
<div><b><h2>Covenant, kingdom, and redemption</h2></b></div>
<ul>
<li><b>Family redemption and name preservation:</b> Boaz’s act explicitly aims to “perpetuate the name of the dead… that the name… may not be cut off” (Ruth 4:10). Obed is the concrete outcome of that redemption.</li>
<li><b>From Judges-chaos to kingly hope:</b> Ruth begins in the era of the Judges (Ruth 1:1) and ends by pointing to David (Ruth 4:17, 22), showing God quietly preparing kingship and covenant stability.</li>
<li><b>Inclusion of the outsider:</b> Ruth is repeatedly identified as a Moabite (Ruth 2:2, 6; 4:5, 10), yet her child stands inside the royal/Messianic line (Matt 1:5–6).</li>
</ul>
<div><b><h2>Human nature: loss, hope, and God’s reversal</h2></b></div>
<ul>
<li>Naomi’s bitterness and emptiness (Ruth 1:20–21) is met with renewed life through the child (Ruth 4:14–17). Obed’s birth is part of God’s pattern of <b>reversal</b>—bringing fullness from emptiness.</li>
</ul>
<div><br></div>
<div><b><h2>10) How Obed Points Us to Christ</h2></b></div>
<div><b><h2>A) Genealogical promise and redemptive continuity</h2></b></div>
<div>Obed is a <b>named link</b> between Ruth’s redemption story and the Davidic monarchy (Ruth 4:17, 22), and then between David and Jesus (Matt 1:5–6; Luke 3:32). The NT makes explicit that God’s salvation plan moves through real families, real histories, and covenant promises.</div>
<div><b><h2>B) Patterns of redemption (careful typology)</h2></b></div>
<ul>
<li><b>Kinsman-redeemer themes (Boaz) culminate in a child who embodies restoration.</b> While Obed is not the redeemer himself, his birth is celebrated in redemption language—Naomi is not left “without a redeemer,” and her life is “renewed” (Ruth 4:14–15). This anticipates the fuller redemption God accomplishes in Christ (cf. Gal 4:4–5).</li>
<li><b>The “servant” motif (name resonance):</b> Obed’s name is commonly connected to the Hebrew root for “serve” (cf. “servant” language elsewhere). Scripture does not explain the name’s meaning in the text, so treat this as <b>probable etymology</b>, not a doctrinal point. Even so, the Messiah’s line culminating in the true Servant-King is a major biblical theme (Isa 42:1; Mark 10:45).</li>
</ul>
<div><b><h2>C) Gospel connections</h2></b></div>
<ul>
<li><b>Grace includes the outsider:</b> Ruth the Moabite enters the messianic genealogy (Matt 1:5), foreshadowing the gospel’s widening mercy to the nations (cf. Gen 12:3; Eph 2:12–13).</li>
<li><b>God brings life out of death and emptiness:</b> The movement from famine and graves (Ruth 1:1–5) to birth and blessing (Ruth 4:13–17) echoes the gospel pattern of resurrection life.</li>
</ul>
<div><br></div>
<div><b><h2>11) Lessons and Applications (tied to Scripture)</h2></b></div>
<ol>
<li><b>God works through “ordinary” lives to accomplish extraordinary purposes</b> (Ruth 4:13–17; Matt 1:5–6).</li>
<li><b>Providence is often visible only in hindsight</b>—Ruth’s closing genealogy reframes the whole story (Ruth 4:18–22).</li>
<li><b>Family faithfulness matters, even when the world is unstable</b> (“days of the judges”) (Ruth 1:1; 4:13–17).</li>
<li><b>God’s kindness can restore what bitterness and loss have hollowed out</b> (Ruth 1:20–21; 4:14–17).</li>
<li><b>Redemption has legal, relational, and communal dimensions</b>—Boaz redeems publicly, not privately (Ruth 4:1–12).</li>
<li><b>Integrity in small decisions can shape generations</b> (Ruth 3:10–13; 4:9–10; 4:17).</li>
<li><b>The covenant community should learn to bless God for His work in others’ lives</b> (Ruth 4:14–15).</li>
<li><b>Outsiders are not beyond God’s covenant mercy</b> (Ruth 2:10–12; Matt 1:5).</li>
<li><b>Your story may be told in ways you never see</b>—Obed is known mainly through what comes after him (Ruth 4:17, 22).</li>
<li><b>Scripture teaches humility in interpretation:</b> where God is silent, we must not invent (contrast the text’s specificity in Ruth 4:13–17 with its silence elsewhere).</li>
</ol>
<div><b>Warnings to heed</b><br></div>
<ul>
<li>Don’t despise “genealogical” passages; they often carry covenant theology and gospel trajectories (Ruth 4:18–22; Matt 1:1–6).</li>
</ul>
<div><b>Examples to imitate (from the context that produces Obed)</b><br></div>
<ul>
<li>Ruth’s loyal love and trust under uncertainty (Ruth 1:16–17; 2:11–12).</li>
<li>Boaz’s integrity and covenant responsibility (Ruth 2:1; 3:10–13; 4:9–10).</li>
</ul>

<div><b><h2>12) Discussion and Reflection Questions (10–20)</h2></b></div>
<div><b><h2>Personal reflection</h2></b></div>
<ol>
<li>Naomi says, “I went away full, and the LORD has brought me back empty” (Ruth 1:21). How does Ruth 4:14–17 answer that lament?</li>
<li>What does Ruth 4:13 teach you about God’s involvement in life’s outcomes?</li>
<li>Where are you tempted to assume God is absent because life feels “ordinary”? How do genealogies challenge that (Ruth 4:18–22; Matt 1:5–6)?</li>
<li>How do you respond when God’s kindness comes through other people’s faithfulness (Ruth 2:11–12; 4:14–15)?</li>
<li>What losses or disappointments in your life most resemble Naomi’s emptiness (Ruth 1:20–21), and what would “renewal” look like (Ruth 4:15)?</li>
</ol>
<div><b><h2>Small group discussion</h2></b></div>
<ol>
<li>Why does Ruth end with a genealogy (Ruth 4:18–22)? What would be lost without it?</li>
<li>In Ruth 4:14–15 the women praise the LORD and speak of a “redeemer.” What do they mean in context, and how does that language prepare us for bigger redemption themes?</li>
<li>How does Ruth’s identity as “the Moabite” (Ruth 4:5, 10) deepen the grace displayed in Matt 1:5?</li>
</ol>
<div><b><h2>Deep study / text-observation</h2></b></div>
<ol>
<li>Trace all the speakers in Ruth 4:11–17 (elders, women, narrator). What theological interpretation does the community provide?</li>
<li>What repeated words/ideas link Ruth 4:9–10 with 4:14–17 (name, restoration, blessing, renewal)?</li>
<li>Compare Ruth 4:17 with 4:21–22. Why does the author state the David connection twice?</li>
<li>Read Isa 11:1 alongside Ruth 4:17. What does it mean that messianic hope is expressed through family lines?</li>
</ol>
