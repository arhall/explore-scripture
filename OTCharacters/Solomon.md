<div><h1>Solomon</h1></div>
<div><br></div>
<div><b><h1>Solomon Bible Character Study</h1></b></div>
<div><b><h2>1) Story Summary (chronological)</h2></b></div>
<div>Solomon enters the biblical story first as <b>the son born to David and Bathsheba</b>, named <i>Solomon</i> (and also called <i>Jedidiah</i>, “beloved of the LORD”) after the death of their first child (2 Sam 12:24–25). As David nears death, a succession crisis erupts when <b>Adonijah</b> exalts himself as king, but Nathan and Bathsheba secure David’s public confirmation that <b>Solomon will reign</b> (1 Kgs 1:5–53). David charges Solomon to <b>walk in the LORD’s ways</b> and to deal wisely with unresolved matters in the kingdom (1 Kgs 2:1–9). Solomon’s reign begins with consolidating rule and removing threats (1 Kgs 2:10–46).</div>
<div>Early in his reign, Solomon worships at <b>Gibeon</b>, and the LORD appears to him in a dream, inviting him to ask what he wants. Solomon asks for <b>wisdom to govern</b>—“an understanding mind” to discern good and evil—rather than long life or riches. God grants him wisdom and also gives wealth and honor (1 Kgs 3:4–14; 2 Chr 1:7–12). Solomon’s wisdom becomes widely known, illustrated immediately in the famous judgment between two women disputing a child (1 Kgs 3:16–28). His kingdom experiences order, prosperity, and international fame (1 Kgs 4:20–34).</div>
<div>Solomon then undertakes his central royal work: <b>building the temple</b> in Jerusalem, fulfilling David’s desire and aligning with the LORD’s covenant purposes (1 Kgs 5–6; 1 Chr 22:6–10). After finishing the temple and palace projects (1 Kgs 6–7), Solomon brings the ark into the temple and prays a theologically rich <b>dedication prayer</b>, asking God to hear from heaven, forgive, and uphold covenant mercy—while warning that sin brings discipline and exile (1 Kgs 8; 2 Chr 5–7). The LORD appears again, affirming the temple and reiterating the conditional warning: if Solomon or Israel turns to other gods, judgment will come (1 Kgs 9:1–9; 2 Chr 7:12–22).</div>
<div>As Solomon’s reign progresses, Scripture emphasizes extraordinary wealth, building, trade, and renown—climaxing in the visit of the <b>queen of Sheba</b>, who praises the LORD for placing Solomon on the throne (1 Kgs 10:1–13). Yet the narrative turns dark: Solomon accumulates <b>gold, horses, and many wives</b>, and “his wives turned away his heart after other gods” (1 Kgs 10:14–29; 11:1–8). The LORD raises adversaries against him, declares that the kingdom will be torn from his son (though not in Solomon’s days, for David’s sake), and Jeroboam is marked as a future instrument of division (1 Kgs 11:9–40). Solomon reigns forty years and dies; his son <b>Rehoboam</b> succeeds him, and the kingdom soon divides (1 Kgs 11:41–43; 12:1–20).</div>
<div><b>Where his story begins and ends:</b>Begins: his birth and naming (2 Sam 12:24–25). Ends: his death and transition to Rehoboam (1 Kgs 11:41–43; 2 Chr 9:29–31).</div>
<div><br></div>
<div><b><h2>2) What He’s Known For</h2></b></div>
<div>Solomon is most known for embodying the heights—and tragic vulnerabilities—of the Davidic monarchy: <b>wisdom, temple-building, prosperity, and later spiritual compromise</b> (1 Kgs 3–11).</div>
<div><b>Headline descriptors</b><br></div>
<ul>
<li><b>The wisest king of Israel</b> (1 Kgs 3:12; 4:29–34)</li>
<li><b>Builder of the first temple</b> (1 Kgs 6:1; 8:1–21)</li>
<li><b>The “golden age” monarch of Israel’s united kingdom</b> (1 Kgs 4:20–25; 10:14–29)</li>
<li><b>A king whose heart was later turned by idolatry</b> (1 Kgs 11:4–8)</li>
<li><b>A son of David who receives covenant promises and warnings</b> (2 Sam 7:12–16; 1 Kgs 9:4–9)</li>
<li><b>A benchmark for glory and wisdom surpassed by Christ</b> (Matt 12:42; 6:29)</li>
</ul>
<div><br></div>

<div><b><h2>4) Vital Statistics</h2></b></div>
<div><b>Historical setting (as Scripture indicates)</b><br></div>
<ul>
<li>The reign of <b>Solomon</b> occurs <b>after David</b> and <b>before the kingdom divides</b> into northern Israel and southern Judah (1 Kgs 11:41–43; 12:1–20).</li>
<li>Scripture gives the length of his reign: <b>40 years</b> (1 Kgs 11:42).</li>
</ul>
<div><b>Geography strongly associated</b><br></div>
<ul>
<li><b>Jerusalem</b> (temple, palace, throne) (1 Kgs 8:1; 10:26)</li>
<li><b>Gibeon</b> (early worship; dream) (1 Kgs 3:4–5)</li>
<li><b>Lebanon / Tyre</b> (cedars; Hiram alliance) (1 Kgs 5:1–10)</li>
<li><b>Ezion-geber</b> (Red Sea fleet) (1 Kgs 9:26–28)</li>
<li><b>Sheba</b> (queen’s visit) (1 Kgs 10:1–13)</li>
</ul>
<div><b>Family and relationships</b><br></div>
<ul>
<li>Father: <b>David</b> (2 Sam 12:24; 1 Kgs 2:1)</li>
<li>Mother: <b>Bathsheba</b> (2 Sam 12:24; 1 Kgs 1:11–17)</li>
<li>Rival/brother: <b>Adonijah</b> (1 Kgs 1:5–10; 2:13–25)</li>
<li>Prophetic counsel: <b>Nathan</b> (1 Kgs 1:11–27)</li>
<li>Priestly support: <b>Zadok</b> (1 Kgs 1:32–35)</li>
<li>International ally: <b>Hiram king of Tyre</b> (1 Kgs 5:1–12)</li>
<li>Son and successor: <b>Rehoboam</b> (1 Kgs 11:43; 14:21)</li>
<li>Wives/concubines: many, including foreign wives; pivotal in his later downfall (1 Kgs 11:1–4)</li>
</ul>
<div><b>Roles/offices</b><br></div>
<ul>
<li><b>King of Israel</b> (1 Kgs 2:12)</li>
<li><b>Temple-builder</b> (1 Kgs 6:1; Acts 7:47)</li>
<li><b>Wisdom teacher/authorial figure (ascribed)</b> (Prov 1:1; Eccl 1:1; Song 1:1)</li>
</ul>
<div><b>Key turning points</b><br></div>
<ul>
<li>Call/commission: David’s charge to obey the LORD (1 Kgs 2:1–4)</li>
<li>Covenant moment: the LORD’s gift of wisdom at Gibeon (1 Kgs 3:5–14)</li>
<li>Worship apex: temple completion and dedication prayer (1 Kgs 8)</li>
<li>Warning renewed: the LORD’s conditional promise and threat (1 Kgs 9:4–9)</li>
<li>Crisis/decline: love of many foreign women and idolatry (1 Kgs 11:1–8)</li>
<li>Judgment pronounced: kingdom torn from his house (delayed to his son’s days) (1 Kgs 11:9–13)</li>
</ul>

<div><b><h2>6) Strengths, Weaknesses, and Heart-Level Patterns</h2></b></div>
<div><b><h2>Strengths (with textual evidence)</h2></b></div>
<ul>
<li><b>Humility and dependence (early):</b> Solomon confesses his inexperience—“I am but a little child”—and asks for discernment to govern well (1 Kgs 3:7–9).</li>
<li><b>Love for the LORD (early):</b> “Solomon loved the LORD, walking in the statutes of David his father” (with the noted “except” regarding high places) (1 Kgs 3:3).</li>
<li><b>God-given wisdom and judicial insight:</b> God grants “a wise and discerning mind,” and Solomon exercises wise justice (1 Kgs 3:12, 28).</li>
<li><b>Worship-centered leadership:</b> He prioritizes building the LORD’s house and leads national worship with sacrifices and prayer (1 Kgs 6:1; 8:22–61).</li>
<li><b>Theological clarity in prayer:</b> Solomon’s temple prayer affirms God’s uniqueness, covenant steadfast love, the need for forgiveness, and the reality of discipline/exile (1 Kgs 8:23–53).</li>
<li><b>Administrative competence:</b> The kingdom’s provision and structure are described in detail, emphasizing stability and order (1 Kgs 4:1–19, 27–28).</li>
</ul>
<div><b><h2>Weaknesses / sins / blind spots (with context)</h2></b></div>
<ul>
<li><b>Compromised worship early (“high places”):</b> Even while loving the LORD, Solomon sacrifices at high places (1 Kgs 3:2–3). The text flags this as a problem area, even if the temple was not yet built (1 Kgs 3:2).</li>
<li><b>Accumulative drift (Torah warnings echoed):</b> Solomon multiplies <b>horses</b>, <b>gold</b>, and <b>wives</b>—patterns explicitly warned against for kings (1 Kgs 10:26–29; 11:1–3; cf. Deut 17:16–17).</li>
<li><b>Heart turned to idolatry:</b> “His heart was not wholly true to the LORD” and he follows other gods due to his wives (1 Kgs 11:4–8).</li>
<li><b>Failure to heed direct divine warning:</b> The LORD had told Israel (and Solomon) not to go after other gods; Solomon does (1 Kgs 11:9–10).</li>
<li><b>Burdening the people (implied downstream):</b> After Solomon, the people plead for relief from the “heavy yoke” of his rule (1 Kgs 12:4). The narrative ties this social cost to the kingdom’s fracture.</li>
</ul>
<div><b><h2>Motives / fears / desires (careful inferences anchored to text)</h2></b></div>
<ul>
<li><b>Desire to rule well (early):</b> Explicit in his request for wisdom to judge God’s people (1 Kgs 3:9).</li>
<li><b>Desire for greatness/renown (later drift):</b> The narrative’s focus on wealth, prestige, and international display (1 Kgs 10:14–29) alongside Torah-prohibited accumulation (Deut 17:16–17) suggests a heart increasingly shaped by <b>security and status</b> rather than simple covenant trust. (This is an inference from narrative emphasis, not a stated motive.)</li>
<li><b>Desire expressed in disordered love:</b> “Solomon loved many foreign women” (explicit) and this love reorients his worship (1 Kgs 11:1–4).</li>
</ul>
<div><b><h2>What changes (or doesn’t) over time?</h2></b></div>
<ul>
<li><b>Changes:</b> Early humility and wholehearted pursuit of wisdom give way to patterns of <b>accumulation</b> and <b>divided worship</b> (1 Kgs 3:7–14 → 11:1–8).</li>
<li><b>Doesn’t change:</b> God remains faithful to Davidic promises even while disciplining Solomon—judgment is delayed “for the sake of David” (1 Kgs 11:12–13), showing covenant faithfulness alongside holiness.</li>
</ul>
<div><br></div>
<div><b><h2>7) Victories and Challenges</h2></b></div>
<div><b><h2>Major “wins” (obedience, leadership, influence)</h2></b></div>
<ul>
<li><b>Choosing wisdom over self-serving gifts:</b> Solomon asks for discernment; God is pleased (1 Kgs 3:9–13).</li>
<li><b>Just leadership that produces holy fear:</b> The people “stood in awe” because they saw God’s wisdom in him (1 Kgs 3:28).</li>
<li><b>Temple built for the LORD’s name:</b> The enduring symbol of God’s dwelling (in covenant terms) among his people (1 Kgs 6:1; 8:16–21).</li>
<li><b>A model prayer of covenant theology:</b> Solomon appeals to God’s steadfast love and forgiveness (1 Kgs 8:23–53).</li>
<li><b>Global testimony:</b> Nations come to hear Solomon’s wisdom; the queen of Sheba blesses the LORD (1 Kgs 4:34; 10:6–9).</li>
</ul>
<div><b><h2>Major challenges (temptations, failures, consequences)</h2></b></div>
<ul>
<li><b>Syncretism pressure (high places):</b> Early compromise in worship practice (1 Kgs 3:2–3).</li>
<li><b>Temptation of power and wealth:</b> Horses, chariots, and gold accumulate (1 Kgs 10:26–29).</li>
<li><b>Sexual/relational compromise leading to spiritual compromise:</b> Many wives turn his heart (1 Kgs 11:1–4).</li>
<li><b>Idolatry and covenant breach:</b> Building high places for other gods (1 Kgs 11:5–8).</li>
<li><b>God’s disciplinary response:</b> Adversaries arise; kingdom fracture is decreed (1 Kgs 11:9–13, 14–40).</li>
</ul>
<div><b>Cost and consequence:</b> Solomon’s compromises do not remain “private.” They shape national future—division and instability emerge as covenant judgment, even if delayed (1 Kgs 11:11–13; 12:1–20).</div>
<div><br></div>
<div><b><h2>8) Essential Verses and Key Cross-References (organized by themes)</h2></b></div>
<div><b><h2>Wisdom and prayer</h2></b></div>
<ul>
<li><b>1 Kgs 3:9</b> — “Give your servant…an understanding mind…to discern between good and evil.”</li>
<li><b>1 Kgs 3:12</b> — God grants a wise and discerning mind unmatched.</li>
<li><b>1 Kgs 4:29–31</b> — God gives Solomon exceedingly great wisdom.</li>
<li><b>James 1:5</b> — Wisdom asked from God (echoes the posture Solomon models early).</li>
</ul>
<div><b><h2>Temple, worship, and covenant</h2></b></div>
<ul>
<li><b>1 Kgs 6:1</b> — The temple building begins (historical hinge).</li>
<li><b>1 Kgs 8:23</b> — God’s uniqueness and steadfast love in Solomon’s prayer.</li>
<li><b>1 Kgs 8:27</b> — “Heaven…cannot contain you”—right theology of God’s transcendence.</li>
<li><b>1 Kgs 9:4–9</b> — Promise and warning: fidelity vs. apostasy and ruin.</li>
<li><b>2 Sam 7:12–16</b> — Davidic covenant foundation for Solomon’s role.</li>
<li><b>Acts 7:47–50</b> — Solomon built the house; God not contained by buildings.</li>
</ul>
<div><b><h2>Kingship under Torah (warnings fulfilled)</h2></b></div>
<ul>
<li><b>Deut 17:16–17</b> — King must not multiply horses or wives, nor greatly multiply silver and gold.</li>
<li><b>1 Kgs 10:26–29</b> — Horses/chariots multiplied.</li>
<li><b>1 Kgs 11:3–4</b> — Many wives; heart turned away.</li>
</ul>
<div><b><h2>Apostasy and its aftermath</h2></b></div>
<ul>
<li><b>1 Kgs 11:4</b> — Heart not wholly true; wives turn him after other gods.</li>
<li><b>1 Kgs 11:9–13</b> — Judgment: kingdom torn (delayed for David’s sake).</li>
<li><b>Neh 13:26</b> — Later reformers cite Solomon as a cautionary example.</li>
</ul>
<div><b><h2>Solomon as signpost to Christ</h2></b></div>
<ul>
<li><b>Matt 12:42 / Luke 11:31</b> — “Something greater than Solomon is here.”</li>
<li><b>Matt 6:29 / Luke 12:27</b> — Solomon’s splendor relativized by God’s providence.</li>
<li><b>Matt 1:6–7</b> — Solomon in the messianic line, yet not the final Son.</li>
</ul>
<div><i>(Note on “Solomonic books”): Proverbs is explicitly attributed to Solomon in key headings (Prov 1:1; 10:1; 25:1). Ecclesiastes and Song of Songs present Solomonic attribution in their openings (Eccl 1:1, 12; Song 1:1). The extent and manner of Solomon’s direct authorship is debated, but the canonical text itself makes these claims.)</i><br></div>

<div><b><h2>9) Theology and Big Themes</h2></b></div>
<ul>
<li><b>God’s generosity and sovereignty:</b> Wisdom is granted by God, not achieved by human brilliance (1 Kgs 3:12; 4:29).</li>
<li><b>Covenant faithfulness and conditional kingship:</b> Blessing is tied to walking before God “as David…walked,” while apostasy invites covenant judgment (1 Kgs 9:4–9).</li>
<li><b>Worship at the center of kingdom life:</b> The temple and dedication prayer show the kingdom exists for God’s name, presence, and forgiveness (1 Kgs 8:16–21, 30).</li>
<li><b>The danger of divided hearts:</b> Solomon’s downfall is described primarily as <b>a heart problem</b>—love misdirected leading to idolatry (1 Kgs 11:4).</li>
<li><b>Human glory vs. God’s glory:</b> Solomon’s splendor is real but ultimately relativized—God clothes lilies more beautifully (Matt 6:29).</li>
<li><b>Sin’s corporate consequences:</b> A king’s disobedience shapes a nation’s future; Solomon’s compromise contributes to the later tearing of the kingdom (1 Kgs 11:11–13; 12:1–20).</li>
<li><b>The temple as both gift and sign:</b> Solomon recognizes God cannot be contained (1 Kgs 8:27), pointing beyond the building to the God who hears from heaven (1 Kgs 8:30).</li>
</ul>
<div><br></div>
<div><b><h2>10) How Solomon Points Us to Christ</h2></b></div>
<ul>
<li><b>Son of David, but not the final Son:</b> Solomon is a Davidic king within the line that leads to Jesus (Matt 1:6–7). Yet his failures show that Israel needs a truer King.</li>
<li><b>Temple-builder pointing to a greater dwelling:</b> Solomon builds the house for God (1 Kgs 6:1; Acts 7:47), but God is not contained (1 Kgs 8:27). The trajectory pushes forward to God’s ultimate presence with his people in Christ (cf. the NT’s emphasis that God’s dwelling is realized beyond a building; Acts 7:48–50).</li>
<li><b>Wisdom king surpassed by Christ:</b> Jesus explicitly places himself above Solomon—greater wisdom, greater authority, greater revelation (Matt 12:42). Solomon’s wisdom foreshadows the need for perfect wisdom embodied in the Messiah.</li>
<li><b>A kingdom of peace that exposes our need:</b> Solomon’s reign pictures peace and international acknowledgment (1 Kgs 4:20–25; 10:6–9), but it is fragile because the king’s heart is divided (1 Kgs 11:4). Christ’s kingdom does not collapse under temptation; Solomon’s collapse functions as contrast and longing.</li>
</ul>

<div><b><h2>11) Lessons and Applications</h2></b></div>
<div><b><h2>Examples to imitate</h2></b></div>
<ol>
<li><b>Ask God for wisdom with humble realism.</b> Solomon’s request models dependence (1 Kgs 3:7–9).</li>
<li><b>Prioritize discernment for serving others, not self-advancement.</b> He asks to judge well (1 Kgs 3:9).</li>
<li><b>Let worship shape leadership.</b> Solomon’s dedication prayer shows governance under God (1 Kgs 8:22–61).</li>
<li><b>Pray with covenant categories: sin, forgiveness, discipline, mercy.</b> Solomon prays expecting real repentance and real forgiveness (1 Kgs 8:33–40).</li>
<li><b>Hold prosperity loosely and gratefully—don’t confuse blessing with spiritual safety.</b> Solomon’s prosperity precedes moral collapse (1 Kgs 10:14–29; 11:4).</li>
</ol>
<div><b><h2>Warnings to heed</h2></b></div>
<ol>
<li><b>Small compromises in worship can normalize bigger drift.</b> “High places” appear early as an “except” (1 Kgs 3:3).</li>
<li><b>Don’t build a life that contradicts God’s explicit commands.</b> Deut 17:16–17 → 1 Kgs 10–11 shows how disobedience can be gradual and catastrophic.</li>
<li><b>Guard the heart; loves reorder worship.</b> The text locates downfall in the heart turned away (1 Kgs 11:4).</li>
<li><b>The “successful” life can still be spiritually hollow.</b> Solomon has glory and renown, yet ends compromised (1 Kgs 10:23–24; 11:4–8).</li>
<li><b>Your private choices can shape public outcomes.</b> Solomon’s sin contributes to national fracture (1 Kgs 11:11–13; 12:4).</li>
<li><b>Covenant warnings are real—God’s holiness is not suspended by privilege.</b> The LORD’s warning is direct and fulfilled in trajectory (1 Kgs 9:6–9; 11:9–13).</li>
<li><b>Marriage and spiritual allegiance are inseparable.</b> Scripture explicitly ties Solomon’s marriages to idolatry (1 Kgs 11:1–4; cf. Neh 13:26).</li>
</ol>
<div><b><h2>Application focus areas</h2></b></div>
<ul>
<li><b>Personal holiness:</b> Identify “high place” compromises—permitted habits that erode exclusive devotion (1 Kgs 3:3; 11:4).</li>
<li><b>Relationships:</b> Treat spiritual unity as weighty; love can disciple the heart toward or away from God (1 Kgs 11:4).</li>
<li><b>Prayer:</b> Ask for wisdom for your callings and responsibilities (1 Kgs 3:9; Jas 1:5).</li>
<li><b>Decision-making:</b> Measure choices against explicit Scripture, not merely outcomes or opportunities (Deut 17:16–17).</li>
<li><b>Leadership:</b> Remember the people bear the cost of leaders’ sin and pride (1 Kgs 12:4).</li>
</ul>

<div><b><h2>12) Discussion and Reflection Questions (10–20)</h2></b></div>
<div><b><h2>Personal reflection</h2></b></div>
<ol>
<li>In <b>1 Kings 3:7–9</b>, what does Solomon’s self-description reveal about humility? Where do you resist that posture?</li>
<li>What do you tend to ask God for first—wisdom, comfort, control, success? Compare with <b>1 Kings 3:11–13</b>.</li>
<li>Identify one “high place” compromise in your life—something “mostly good” that subtly competes with wholehearted worship (<b>1 Kgs 3:3</b>).</li>
<li>What forms of “accumulation” tempt you (money, status, experiences, control)? How does <b>Deut 17:16–17</b> diagnose that?</li>
<li>Where do your relationships most shape your spiritual trajectory (for good or ill)? Reflect on <b>1 Kgs 11:4</b>.</li>
</ol>
<div><b><h2>Small group discussion</h2></b></div>
<ol>
<li>Why does Scripture put such weight on the king’s heart being “wholly true” (<b>1 Kgs 11:4</b>) rather than only listing political failures?</li>
<li>In Solomon’s temple prayer (<b>1 Kgs 8</b>), what themes of confession and restoration should shape church prayer today?</li>
<li>How do we hold together God’s generosity (wisdom/wealth given) with God’s discipline (kingdom torn) (<b>1 Kgs 3:12–13; 11:11–13</b>)?</li>
<li>Discuss how prosperity can become spiritually dangerous even when it’s a gift (compare <b>1 Kgs 10</b> with <b>1 Kgs 11</b>).</li>
</ol>
<div><b><h2>Deep study (text observation)</h2></b></div>
<ol>
<li>Track the repeated “heart” language in <b>1 Kings 11:1–10</b>. What exactly turns Solomon, and how does the text describe the process?</li>
<li>Compare <b>1 Kgs 8:27</b> with <b>Acts 7:48–50</b>. What do both passages teach about God’s presence and “holy spaces”?</li>
<li>Compare the Torah ideal for kingship (<b>Deut 17:14–20</b>) with Solomon’s portrait (<b>1 Kgs 10–11</b>). Where is the contrast sharpest?</li>
<li>In <b>Matt 12:42</b>, why does Jesus emphasize “something greater than Solomon”? What does that imply about where true wisdom is found?</li>
<li>In <b>1 Kgs 9:4–9</b>, list the conditional statements and outcomes. How does the passage shape your view of covenant responsibility?</li>
<li>How does <b>Neh 13:26</b> use Solomon’s story pastorally for Israel’s later reforms?</li>
</ol>

<div><b><h2>13) Prayer Response (Scripture-shaped)</h2></b></div>
<div>Lord God, giver of wisdom, we confess that we often ask for lesser things when you invite us to ask for what we need to serve faithfully. Give us discerning hearts to know good from evil (1 Kgs 3:9), and keep our hearts wholly true to you (1 Kgs 11:4). Guard us from loving what pulls us toward idols, and from building lives of accumulation that contradict your Word (Deut 17:16–17). Teach us to pray with repentance and confidence in your mercy, as Solomon prayed for forgiveness and restoration (1 Kgs 8:33–40). Fix our eyes on the One greater than Solomon (Matt 12:42), and make our worship undivided, our leadership humble, and our obedience steady—by your grace. Amen.</div>
