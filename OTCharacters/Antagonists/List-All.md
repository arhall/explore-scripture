<div><h1>List-All</h1></div>
<div><br></div>
<div>Below is a <b>broad, fairly comprehensive “working list” of major Old Testament antagonists</b>—i.e., named people (especially <b>kings/rulers</b>, commanders, priests, and plotters) who <b>oppose God’s purposes, God’s people, or God’s prophets</b> in the narrative. (Plenty of opponents are <b>unnamed</b>—e.g., “Pharaoh” in Exodus—so those are noted where relevant.)</div>
<div><br></div>
<div><b><h2>Primeval and Patriarchal Era (Genesis)</h2></b></div>
<div><b><h2>Early narrative antagonists</h2></b></div>
<ul>
<li><b>The Serpent</b> — Eden tempter — <i>Gen 3</i> — opposes God’s word and leads to the fall.</li>
<li><b>Cain</b> — first murderer — <i>Gen 4</i> — kills Abel; embodies rebellion.</li>
<li><b>Nimrod</b> (often treated as a “proto-tyrant”) — mighty hunter/kingdom-builder — <i>Gen 10:8–12</i> — tied to early imperial power (interpretive).</li>
<li><b>Kings of the eastern coalition</b>: <b>Chedorlaomer</b> (Elam), <b>Amraphel</b> (Shinar), <b>Arioch</b> (Ellasar), <b>Tidal</b> (Goiim) — war against local kings — <i>Gen 14</i> — take Lot captive; opposed by Abram.</li>
<li><b>Abimelech</b> (king of Gerar) — conflict with Abraham/Isaac — <i>Gen 20; 26</i> — antagonistic moments (though also restrained by God).</li>
<li><b>Laban</b> — opposes Jacob’s departure — <i>Gen 29–31</i> — internal/family antagonist (non-king).</li>
</ul>
<div><br></div>
<div><b><h2>Exodus / Wilderness (Exodus–Deuteronomy)</h2></b></div>
<div><b><h2>Egypt</h2></b></div>
<ul>
<li><b>Pharaoh of the Exodus</b> (unnamed) — king of Egypt — <i>Ex 1–14</i> — enslaves Israel; opposes Yahweh’s command through Moses.</li>
</ul>
<div><b><h2>Internal rebels (Israel)</h2></b></div>
<ul>
<li><b>Korah</b> — Levite rebel — <i>Num 16</i> — challenges Moses/Aaron’s God-given leadership.</li>
<li><b>Dathan &amp Abiram</b> (and <b>On</b>, named with them) — rebels — <i>Num 16</i> — join Korah’s revolt.</li>
<li><b>Golden calf leaders/instigators</b> (esp. <b>Aaron’s failure</b>) — <i>Ex 32</i> — covenant-breaking episode (not a “villain” arc, but antagonist moment).</li>
</ul>
<div><b><h2>Foreign kings and hostile leaders encountered</h2></b></div>
<ul>
<li><b>Amalek</b> (as a people) — first major military attacker — <i>Ex 17</i> (king later: Agag).</li>
<li><b>Arad, king of Canaan</b> — attacks Israel — <i>Num 21:1–3</i>.</li>
<li><b>Sihon, king of the Amorites</b> — refuses passage; fights Israel — <i>Num 21:21–31; Deut 2</i>.</li>
<li><b>Og, king of Bashan</b> — fights Israel — <i>Num 21:33–35; Deut 3</i>.</li>
<li><b>Balak, king of Moab</b> — hires a curse — <i>Num 22–24; Deut 23:3–6; Josh 24:9–10</i>.</li>
<li><b>Balaam</b> — diviner for hire — <i>Num 22–24; 31:8, 16; Deut 23:4–5; Josh 13:22</i>.</li>
<li><b>Midianite leaders</b>: <b>Zur</b> (noted as a Midianite “chief”; father of Cozbi) — <i>Num 25:15; 31:8</i>.</li>
<li><b>Edom’s refusal</b> (king unnamed) — denies passage — <i>Num 20:14–21</i>.</li>
</ul>
<div><br></div>
<div><b><h2>Conquest (Joshua)</h2></b></div>
<div><b><h2>Major Canaanite kings opposed in Joshua</h2></b></div>
<div><b>Southern coalition (Joshua 10)</b><br></div>
<ul>
<li><b>Adoni-zedek</b>, king of Jerusalem — <i>Josh 10</i><br></li>
<li><b>Hoham</b>, king of Hebron — <i>Josh 10</i><br></li>
<li><b>Piram</b>, king of Jarmuth — <i>Josh 10</i><br></li>
<li><b>Japhia</b>, king of Lachish — <i>Josh 10</i><br></li>
<li><b>Debir</b>, king of Eglon — <i>Josh 10</i><br></li>
</ul>
<div><b>Northern coalition (Joshua 11)</b><br></div>
<ul>
<li><b>Jabin</b>, king of Hazor — <i>Josh 11</i> (distinct from later Jabin in Judges)</li>
<li>Plus allied northern kings (often unnamed individually) — <i>Josh 11–12</i><br></li>
</ul>
<div><b>Other notable kings/cities</b><br></div>
<ul>
<li><b>King of Jericho</b> — <i>Josh 6</i><br></li>
<li><b>King of Ai</b> — <i>Josh 7–8</i><br></li>
<li>Many listed “kings defeated” — <i>Josh 12</i> (a long roster; often used as a canonical list of conquest antagonists).</li>
</ul>
<div><b><h2>Notable non-royal antagonistic actors</h2></b></div>
<ul>
<li><b>Achan</b> — covenant-breaker whose sin brings defeat — <i>Josh 7</i>.</li>
</ul>
<div><br></div>
<div><b><h2>Judges Era (Judges)</h2></b></div>
<div><b><h2>Foreign oppressors / rulers / commanders</h2></b></div>
<ul>
<li><b>Cushan-rishathaim</b>, king of Mesopotamia/Aram-naharaim — <i>Judg 3:8–11</i><br></li>
<li><b>Eglon</b>, king of Moab — <i>Judg 3:12–30</i><br></li>
<li><b>Jabin</b>, king of Canaan (Hazor) — <i>Judg 4–5</i><br></li>
<li><b>Sisera</b>, commander of Jabin’s army — <i>Judg 4–5</i><br></li>
<li><b>Midianite oppression</b> (leaders named):</li>
<ul>
<li><b>Oreb</b> and <b>Zeeb</b> (princes) — <i>Judg 7:25; 8:3</i><br></li>
<li><b>Zebah</b> and <b>Zalmunna</b> (kings) — <i>Judg 8:5–21</i><br></li>
</ul>
<li><b>Abimelech</b> (Israelite usurper-king) — <i>Judg 9</i> — internal tyrant; murders brothers.</li>
<li><b>Ammonite king</b> (unnamed) — conflict with Jephthah — <i>Judg 11</i><br></li>
<li><b>Philistines</b> (often “lords of the Philistines” unnamed) — major oppressors — <i>Judg 13–16</i><br></li>
<ul>
<li><b>Delilah</b> — betrays Samson — <i>Judg 16</i> (non-royal antagonist).</li>
</ul>
</ul>
<div><br></div>
<div><b><h2>United Monarchy (1 Samuel–1 Kings 2)</h2></b></div>
<div><b><h2>Saul–David conflict and Philistine threats</h2></b></div>
<ul>
<li><b>Goliath of Gath</b> — Philistine champion — <i>1 Sam 17</i><br></li>
<li><b>Achish</b>, king of Gath — <i>1 Sam 21; 27–29</i> (ambiguous, but often adversarial context)</li>
<li><b>Saul</b> (as antagonist to David) — <i>1 Sam 18–31</i> — attempts to kill David.</li>
<li><b>Doeg the Edomite</b> — Saul’s agent — <i>1 Sam 21–22</i> — massacre of priests.</li>
<li><b>Nabal</b> — opposes David — <i>1 Sam 25</i><br></li>
<li><b>Agag</b>, king of the Amalekites — <i>1 Sam 15</i> — enemy king spared then executed.</li>
</ul>
<div><b><h2>David’s internal rebellions and rival leaders</h2></b></div>
<ul>
<li><b>Abner</b> (as rival general early) — <i>2 Sam 2–3</i> (complex figure, not purely antagonist).</li>
<li><b>Ish-bosheth</b> (rival claimant) — <i>2 Sam 2–4</i><br></li>
<li><b>Absalom</b> — son who leads coup — <i>2 Sam 15–18</i><br></li>
<li><b>Ahithophel</b> — Absalom’s counselor — <i>2 Sam 15–17</i><br></li>
<li><b>Sheba son of Bichri</b> — rebel — <i>2 Sam 20</i><br></li>
<li><b>Shimei</b> — curses David; later conflict — <i>2 Sam 16; 1 Kgs 2</i> (limited antagonist role)</li>
</ul>
<div><b><h2>Foreign kings opposing David</h2></b></div>
<ul>
<li><b>Hanun</b>, king of Ammon — <i>2 Sam 10</i><br></li>
<li><b>Hadadezer</b>, king of Zobah — <i>2 Sam 8; 10</i><br></li>
<li><b>Rezon</b> (adversary arising in Solomon’s days) — <i>1 Kgs 11:23–25</i><br></li>
<li><b>Hadad the Edomite</b> (adversary to Solomon) — <i>1 Kgs 11:14–22</i><br></li>
</ul>
<div><br></div>
<div><b><h2>Divided Kingdom: Israel &amp Judah (1 Kings 12–2 Kings)</h2></b></div>
<div><b><h2>Northern Kingdom (Israel): dynastic/political antagonists and idolatrous kings</h2></b></div>
<div>Key kings frequently framed as covenant-opponents:</div>
<ul>
<li><b>Jeroboam I</b> — establishes rival worship — <i>1 Kgs 12–14</i><br></li>
<li><b>Nadab</b> — <i>1 Kgs 15:25–31</i><br></li>
<li><b>Baasha</b> — <i>1 Kgs 15:32–16:7</i><br></li>
<li><b>Omri</b> — <i>1 Kgs 16:21–28</i><br></li>
<li><b>Ahab</b> — intensifies Baal worship — <i>1 Kgs 16:29–22</i><br></li>
<li><b>Jezebel</b> (queen) — persecutes prophets — <i>1 Kgs 18–21; 2 Kgs 9</i><br></li>
<li><b>Ahaziah</b> — <i>1 Kgs 22:51–2 Kgs 1</i><br></li>
<li><b>Joram/Jeho­ram</b> — <i>2 Kgs 3; 8–9</i><br></li>
<li><b>Jehu</b> (judgment instrument, but also complex) — <i>2 Kgs 9–10</i><br></li>
<li>Later Israelite kings often condemned: <b>Jehoahaz, Jehoash/Joash, Jeroboam II, Zechariah, Shallum, Menahem, Pekahiah, Pekah, Hoshea</b> — <i>2 Kgs 13–17</i>.</li>
</ul>
<div><b><h2>Southern Kingdom (Judah): notable antagonistic rulers and usurpers</h2></b></div>
<ul>
<li><b>Rehoboam</b> (folly leading to division) — <i>1 Kgs 12; 2 Chr 10–12</i><br></li>
<li><b>Asa’s opponents</b> include foreign threats (see below), but Asa largely positive.</li>
<li><b>Ahaz</b> — deepens idolatry; appeals to Assyria — <i>2 Kgs 16</i><br></li>
<li><b>Manasseh</b> — paradigmatic wicked king — <i>2 Kgs 21</i><br></li>
<li><b>Amon</b> — <i>2 Kgs 21:19–26</i><br></li>
<li><b>Jehoiakim</b> — opposes Jeremiah; burns scroll — <i>2 Kgs 23:34–24:7; Jer 36</i><br></li>
<li><b>Zedekiah</b> — rebellion/capitulation failures in last days — <i>2 Kgs 24–25; Jer 37–39</i><br></li>
<li><b>Athaliah</b> (queen usurper) — murders royal heirs — <i>2 Kgs 11</i>.</li>
</ul>
<div><b><h2>Major foreign kings/empires opposing Israel/Judah (big “antagonist list”)</h2></b></div>
<div><b>Egypt</b><br></div>
<ul>
<li><b>Shishak</b> (often identified with Shoshenq I) — attacks Judah — <i>1 Kgs 14:25–26</i><br></li>
<li><b>Pharaoh Neco/Necho II</b> — kills Josiah; controls Judah briefly — <i>2 Kgs 23:29–35</i><br></li>
</ul>
<div><b>Aram/Syria</b><br></div>
<ul>
<li><b>Ben-hadad I</b> — <i>1 Kgs 15:18–20</i><br></li>
<li><b>Ben-hadad II</b> — wars with Ahab — <i>1 Kgs 20; 2 Kgs 6–7</i><br></li>
<li><b>Hazael</b> — aggressive Aramean king — <i>2 Kgs 8–13</i><br></li>
<li><b>Rezin</b> — Aramean king in Syro-Ephraimite crisis — <i>2 Kgs 15–16; Isa 7</i><br></li>
</ul>
<div><b>Moab</b><br></div>
<ul>
<li><b>Mesha</b>, king of Moab — rebels against Israel — <i>2 Kgs 3</i><br></li>
</ul>
<div><b>Assyria</b><br></div>
<ul>
<li><b>Tiglath-pileser III</b> — invades; deports — <i>2 Kgs 15–16</i><br></li>
<li><b>Shalmaneser V</b> — siege of Samaria — <i>2 Kgs 17</i><br></li>
<li><b>Sargon II</b> (implied by “king of Assyria” taking Samaria) — context for Samaria’s fall — <i>2 Kgs 17</i> (extra-biblical naming, but commonly linked historically)</li>
<li><b>Sennacherib</b> — invades Judah; threatens Jerusalem — <i>2 Kgs 18–19; Isa 36–37</i><br></li>
<ul>
<li><b>Rabshakeh</b> (field commander/spokesman) — <i>2 Kgs 18; Isa 36</i><br></li>
</ul>
<li><b>Esarhaddon</b> — resettlement policy referenced — <i>2 Kgs 19:37; Ezra 4:2</i><br></li>
<li><b>Ashurbanipal/Osnappar</b> (name form appears as <b>Osnappar</b>) — <i>Ezra 4:10</i> (post-conquest resettlement context)</li>
</ul>
<div><b>Babylon</b><br></div>
<ul>
<li><b>Nebuchadnezzar II</b> — destroys Jerusalem/temple; exile — <i>2 Kgs 24–25; Jer; Dan 1–4</i><br></li>
<li><b>Nebuzaradan</b> (captain of the guard) — executes judgment — <i>2 Kgs 25; Jer 39–40</i><br></li>
<li><b>“Kings of Babylon”</b> in Daniel include:</li>
<ul>
<li><b>Belshazzar</b> — <i>Dan 5</i> (technically co-regency context; narrative antagonist)</li>
<li><b>Darius the Mede</b> (in Daniel’s narrative frame) — <i>Dan 6</i> (not always “antagonist,” but his officials are)</li>
<li><b>Satraps/officials</b> who plot against Daniel — <i>Dan 6</i> (mostly unnamed)</li>
</ul>
</ul>
<div><br></div>

<li><b>Pashhur</b> — priest/official who strikes Jeremiah — <i>Jer 20</i><br></li>
<li><b>Shemaiah</b> — opposing Jeremiah’s message — <i>Jer 29</i><br></li>
<li><b>Amaziah</b> (priest of Bethel) — opposes Amos — <i>Amos 7:10–17</i><br></li>
<li><b>False prophets of Ahab</b> (notably <b>Zedekiah son of Chenaanah</b>) — <i>1 Kgs 22</i><br></li>
</ul>
<div><b><h2>“End-time / symbolic” hostile leaders</h2></b></div>
<ul>
<li><b>Gog</b> (of Magog) — invader figure — <i>Ezek 38–39</i><br></li>
</ul>
<div><br></div>
<div><b><h2>Exile and Post-Exile (Ezra–Nehemiah–Esther)</h2></b></div>
<div><b><h2>Ezra–Nehemiah opposition</h2></b></div>
<ul>
<li><b>Rehum</b> and <b>Shimshai</b> — officials opposing rebuilding — <i>Ezra 4</i><br></li>
<li><b>Tattenai</b> (Tatnai) — governor who investigates — <i>Ezra 5–6</i> (more “obstacle” than villain; included for completeness)</li>
<li><b>Sanballat the Horonite</b> — mocks/plots against rebuilding — <i>Neh 2; 4; 6</i><br></li>
<li><b>Tobiah the Ammonite</b> — ally of Sanballat; internal sabotage — <i>Neh 2; 4; 6; 13</i><br></li>
<li><b>Geshem the Arab</b> — opposition leader — <i>Neh 2; 6</i><br></li>
</ul>
<div><b><h2>Esther</h2></b></div>
<ul>
<li><b>Haman the Agagite</b> — genocidal plotter — <i>Est 3–9</i><br></li>
<li><b>Zeresh</b> (Haman’s wife; advisor) — <i>Est 5–6</i> (supporting antagonist role)</li>
</ul>

<li><b>Satan</b> standing to accuse Joshua the high priest — <i>Zech 3:1–2</i> (accuser role)</li>

</ul>
